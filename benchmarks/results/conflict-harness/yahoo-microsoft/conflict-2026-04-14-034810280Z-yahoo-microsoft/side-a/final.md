# Reopen Negotiations, but Sell Only Above a Higher Reservation Price

Yahoo should reopen negotiations with Microsoft, but it should not treat a sale as inherently superior simply because it owns valuable minority stakes. The correct conclusion is narrower and more defensible: Yahoo should pursue a sale only if Microsoft is willing to pay a price that clearly compensates shareholders for three things at once: the core operating business, Microsoft's strategic need for search scale, and the embedded option value of Yahoo Japan and Alibaba. On the evidence available in May 2008, the board should prefer a negotiated sale over a pure independence strategy only if Microsoft returns with a meaningfully improved bid above $33 and closer to, or above, the board's stated $37 threshold. The central strategic fact remains unchanged: Yahoo's operating trajectory is deteriorating in the segment that matters most. Yahoo still has major consumer reach and the leading US display position, but the value pool in internet advertising is shifting toward search, where Google has roughly 63% share, Yahoo is near 20% and declining, and Microsoft is near 10%. Yahoo's Panama platform improved revenue per search by 15-20%, which shows execution progress, but improved monetization without share stabilization does not restore strategic parity. Yahoo's 2007 revenue was $6.97B with only $695M of operating income, versus Google's $16.6B revenue and $5.1B operating income. That gap suggests a structural disadvantage in search economics rather than a short-term execution miss. [ctx-2] [ctx-5] [ctx-7]

That said, the minority stakes cut against any rushed sale. Yahoo Japan is worth roughly $8-10B and Alibaba roughly $3-4B on 2008 marks, which means $11-14B of value sits outside the core operating business. Those holdings give Yahoo a real fallback and raise the reservation price for any transaction. They are not evidence that Yahoo should sell quickly; they are evidence that Yahoo can refuse an inadequate bid. The proper use of these assets in the board's reasoning is to set a tougher negotiating floor, not to rationalize accepting Microsoft's framing. [ctx-8] [ctx-9]

The independent alternative is still weak enough that the board should test Microsoft's willingness to pay. Yahoo's display leadership is vulnerable to attention fragmentation as Facebook scales rapidly from roughly 100M to 200M users in 2008. The Google search partnership may improve short-term monetization, but DOJ review makes it too fragile to anchor a multiyear independence case. If the Google deal is blocked or heavily constrained, Yahoo is left with declining search share, weaker bargaining power, and a cost base that has expanded to roughly 14,300 employees from 11,400 in 2006. Independence is therefore a fallback, not the preferred path, unless management can show a credible 12-24 month plan to stabilize search share and materially improve margins without relying on Google. Microsoft's strategic need still matters, but it should be used more carefully than in the first draft. Microsoft does need search scale badly, which is why Yahoo should reopen negotiations. But that need does not by itself prove Microsoft will pay $35-37. The board should therefore avoid treating that range as analytically secure. Instead, it should enter negotiations with a clear reservation price anchored in sum-of-the-parts logic and strategic control value, while recognizing that Microsoft's true walk-away point remains unknown. If Microsoft will not bridge meaningfully above $33, Yahoo should be prepared to remain independent temporarily rather than transfer the option value of Alibaba and Yahoo Japan too cheaply. ## Decision Frame

Decision: Reopen negotiations with Microsoft immediately, but authorize a sale only at a clearly improved price that compensates shareholders for the core business plus the option value of Yahoo Japan and Alibaba. On current evidence, that means the board should reject a return to $33 and use $37 as a defensible negotiating threshold rather than an assumed achievable outcome. [ctx-1] [ctx-8] [ctx-9]

Why this clears the bar:
- Yahoo's standalone operating outlook is weakening in search, the highest-growth and highest-margin battleground. [ctx-2] [ctx-5] [ctx-7]
- The Google partnership is too exposed to DOJ intervention to serve as a reliable cornerstone of independence. [ctx-3]
- The minority stakes materially strengthen Yahoo's fallback and therefore justify a higher reservation price, not a hurried sale. [ctx-8] [ctx-9]
- Microsoft's strategic need justifies reopening talks, but not assuming it will meet Yahoo's price without testing. [ctx-11]

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- Microsoft's actual walk-away price above $33.
- A cleaner sum-of-the-parts valuation separating Yahoo's core operations from Yahoo Japan and Alibaba.
- Probability-weighted DOJ outcomes for the Google-Yahoo partnership.
- Management's internal 12-24 month plan for stabilizing search share and improving margins without Google.
- The degree to which Alibaba's 2008 mark understates long-term upside versus the certainty of cash in a sale.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass conditions for this recommendation:
- The board can reopen talks quickly and test whether Microsoft will pay materially above $33.
- Directors align on a hard reservation price informed by asset value rather than symbolic defense of independence.
- Management can present independence as a credible fallback, not as the primary recommendation.

Fail conditions:
- Microsoft refuses to improve meaningfully above $33.
- DOJ unexpectedly clears a broad Google partnership that materially strengthens standalone economics.
- Management produces credible new evidence that Panama plus cost discipline can stabilize search share and expand margins within 12-24 months.
