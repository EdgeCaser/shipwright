# Strategic Recommendation: Reopen Negotiations with Microsoft

**To:** Yahoo Board of Directors  
**From:** Strategy Advisory  
**Date:** May 2008  
**Re:** Microsoft Acquisition — Board Decision

---

## Decision Frame

**Position: Reopen negotiations with Microsoft at a price in the $36–37 range.**

The board's rejection of $33/share was not wrong in principle — the offer likely undervalues Yahoo's assets. The error is treating that rejection as a strategy rather than a negotiating move. Microsoft signaled willingness to return. The board should use that opening now, before three structural forces further erode Yahoo's negotiating position.

**Why independence is not a viable alternative:**

**1. Search decline is structural, not cyclical.**  
Yahoo Search holds approximately 20% share and is falling. Google commands approximately 63%. Panama improved revenue-per-search by 15–20% since launch — a genuine operational achievement — but it has not reversed share loss. Search is the highest-growth, highest-margin segment in digital advertising. The gap in query volume and monetization efficiency is compounding against Yahoo, not stabilizing. There is no evidence in the current record that Yahoo has a credible path to reversing this trajectory independently.

**2. The Google partnership is the independence strategy's load-bearing wall — and it is likely to collapse.**  
The board's primary defensive play after rejecting Microsoft was the Google search advertising partnership. DOJ antitrust review is expected to block or severely limit that deal's scope. If the partnership fails, Yahoo will have surrendered negotiating leverage with Microsoft while receiving nothing operational in return. The board cannot credibly anchor an independent strategy on a deal regulators are likely to kill.

**3. Display advertising leadership is real today — and that is exactly why the board should sell now.**  
Yahoo holds the #1 US display share position (15.6% of total digital ad spend). This is a genuine, current asset. The evidence does not establish that Yahoo's display revenue is already eroding in May 2008, and the board should not pretend otherwise. What the evidence does establish is a directional threat: Facebook grew from 100 million to 200 million users in 2008, fragmenting the display attention pool toward social platforms. The correct reading of that signal is not that the moat has already collapsed — it is that display leadership has a finite shelf life and is worth more today than it will be in two or three years. Selling from a position of demonstrated strength is better than waiting until the erosion is measurable. Display dominance is a reason to command a strong price from Microsoft now, not a reason to hold out indefinitely.

**4. Asian stakes are financial holdings, not operating levers.**  
Yahoo Japan (~$8–10B estimated) and the Alibaba stake (~$3–4B estimated) are real and significant. They do not fix search share, do not address the forward pressure on display from social media growth, and do not improve Yahoo's competitive position in the US core business. They are the strongest argument for pushing the sale price above $33 — not arguments against selling. The board should use them as pricing evidence in negotiation, not as a rationale for independence.

**5. The market and activist investors have already discounted the board's position.**  
Yahoo's stock settled at approximately $24/share post-rejection — well below the $33 Microsoft offer. Carl Icahn has acquired a 5% stake and is publicly pushing for the deal. Both signals indicate the board's $37 floor lacks credibility in the market. Holding the $37 line risks an Icahn proxy fight that removes board control before any deal is reached, destroying the board's ability to extract a premium.

**The strongest counterargument — and why it does not change the conclusion:**  
Yahoo is probably worth more than $33/share. The Yahoo Japan and Alibaba stakes alone represent $11–14B of the $47.5B offer price, and the display business and Mail user base (260M+ users, largest globally) carry real strategic value to any acquirer. The $37 board minimum is defensible as a negotiating anchor. It is not defensible as a walkaway point when the alternative is a structurally weakening independent company with a search business in secular decline, a defensive partnership under antitrust review, and a display position whose forward trajectory is threatened by social media platforms Yahoo does not own. The question is not whether Yahoo is worth $37 in isolation — it is whether Yahoo's trajectory as an independent company justifies the premium over the certain $33 (or $35–37 with re-engagement) that Microsoft is willing to pay.

**Recommended action:** Authorize back-channel contact with Microsoft. Signal board willingness to engage seriously at $36–37/share. Emphasize the Yahoo Japan stake, the Alibaba stake, and the Mail user base as pricing justification. Microsoft's strategic need for search scale is genuine — they hold approximately 10% search share and are losing ground to Google. Yahoo at ~20% search share plus a 260M+ user email platform is a scale acquisition Microsoft cannot replicate organically. The board has leverage. That leverage diminishes each week the Google partnership review drags on.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

1. **Microsoft's revised bid ceiling.** Ballmer signaled willingness to return but gave no revised number. It is unknown whether Microsoft will move meaningfully above $33. If Microsoft's ceiling is $34, the gap may be unbridgeable without significant board concession — and the board must know this before re-engaging publicly.

2. **DOJ timeline and resolution scope for the Google-Yahoo review.** The review could resolve with a partial deal (some search revenue preserved) or a full block. This is the single largest variable in the independence strategy's viability. The outcome should be treated as binary: if the deal survives at meaningful scale, revisit; if blocked, the independence strategy has no engine.

3. **Yahoo display share trend data.** The packet confirms Yahoo's current #1 position (15.6%), but does not provide trend data showing whether share is stable, growing, or declining heading into 2008. The forward pressure from Facebook is real as a directional signal, but the near-term trajectory of Yahoo's display revenue is not established. This is a gap the board should close before finalizing any negotiating floor.

4. **Alibaba pre-IPO valuation precision.** The $3–4B figure is a 2008 estimate for a pre-IPO company. The actual trajectory and IPO timeline are not knowable with confidence. The stake could be worth substantially more — but it is illiquid and cannot be monetized on any near-term horizon relevant to this decision.

5. **Yahoo's internal execution capacity for an independent turnaround.** Panama demonstrates product capability, but the 10% operating margin versus Google's 31% suggests structural cost inefficiency. Headcount grew from 11,400 (2006) to 14,300 (2008) while search share declined — a concerning signal about whether Yahoo can close this gap without Microsoft's capital and distribution.

6. **Icahn's escalation timeline.** If Icahn moves to a proxy fight before re-engagement with Microsoft is completed, board composition could change mid-negotiation. The timeline for a potential Icahn action is not established in the current record.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**Recommended decision:** Reopen negotiations with Microsoft.  
**Confidence:** Medium.

The independence strategy fails on its two most operative pillars: search is in structural decline, and the Google partnership is likely blocked by DOJ review. Display leadership is real but finite as a standalone competitive moat, and the forward threat from social media makes today a stronger selling position than tomorrow. The board's $37 minimum is a defensible negotiating position; it is not a defensible operating alternative.

This recommendation passes the decision-usefulness threshold: it specifies a concrete action (back-channel re-engagement, $36–37 signal), identifies the key risk that would invalidate it (Microsoft ceiling at or below $34), and acknowledges the strongest counterargument (Yahoo's display position is currently real, not yet eroding) without allowing it to function as an excuse for inaction.

**Conditions that would require revisiting this recommendation:**
- DOJ clears the Google partnership at substantial scale, providing a credible search monetization bridge for 3+ years.
- Microsoft confirms it will not move above $33, in which case the board must pivot to a genuine restructuring plan — not the current defensive posture — including asset divestitures and margin improvement targeting 20%+.
- Display share trend data reveals Yahoo's position is stable or growing, which would materially strengthen the case for a higher walkaway price (though not necessarily for independence).

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Negotiation brief: Microsoft re-engagement parameters.**

Scope: Define Yahoo's walkaway price with asset-by-asset justification (Yahoo Japan stake, Alibaba stake, Mail user base, display inventory). Establish a back-channel contact protocol that does not trigger public disclosure obligations prematurely. Include a contingency restructuring path — specifically, a plan for divesting non-core assets and reducing headcount to improve operating margin toward 20%+ — to be activated if Microsoft declines to return above $34. The board should not enter re-engagement without both documents in hand.
