# Recommendation: Reopen negotiations with Microsoft and pursue a sale at $37/share or better

Yahoo should not continue as an independent company on the current plan. The board should immediately reopen negotiations with Microsoft, anchor at the board's stated $37/share minimum, and seek a tightly bounded final process focused on price, deal certainty, and employee retention. If Microsoft will not meet an acceptable range promptly, the board should still treat a sale as the preferred path and avoid doubling down on a stand-alone strategy built on the Google partnership.

The core issue is not whether Yahoo owns valuable assets. It does. The issue is whether Yahoo can convert those assets into a credible path to value creation that beats a $33 cash offer today and likely beats an improved bid in the mid-to-high $30s. On the available evidence, that case is weak. Yahoo remains strong in display and audience reach, but the industry profit pool is shifting toward search, where Yahoo has roughly 20% share and is losing ground to Google. Panama improved monetization, but it has not reversed share decline. Meanwhile, Google operates at a radically superior margin profile, showing that scale economics in search are compounding against Yahoo rather than converging.

The board's independent case relies on three pillars, and each is structurally fragile. First, display leadership is real but increasingly less defensible as social platforms fragment attention; Facebook's rapid user growth is an early warning that premium portal aggregation is weakening. Second, the Google search partnership is not a durable strategic answer because it effectively concedes Yahoo's own search economics while facing material DOJ antitrust risk. Third, the Alibaba and Yahoo Japan stakes are valuable, but they strengthen the argument that Microsoft is underpaying only modestly, not that Yahoo should remain independent. Those holdings are easier to monetize or preserve inside a sale process than to use as justification for continued operating underperformance.

Microsoft's strategic need materially improves Yahoo's negotiating leverage right now. Microsoft is the subscale player in search and has a pressing strategic requirement for audience, queries, and advertiser volume to challenge Google. That makes Yahoo more valuable to Microsoft than to most other buyers and increases the odds of a revised bid. Carl Icahn's activism adds urgency: a prolonged stand-off risks governance distraction, employee attrition, and value leakage while the operating business remains exposed. The board should use Microsoft's need, not independence rhetoric, as the lever to maximize shareholder value.

The strongest argument for staying independent is that $33 undervalues Yahoo's sum-of-the-parts: the core media properties, the #1 US display position, 260M+ Mail users, and the Asian equity stakes. That critique is directionally fair. But it supports negotiating harder, not walking away. The post-rejection trading level near $24 shows that public markets do not credit management's stand-alone thesis anywhere close to the board's implied intrinsic value. If the independent plan were genuinely compelling and executable, the gap between rejection and trading price would be narrower. Instead, the market is signaling skepticism that Yahoo can realize this value on its own within a reasonable timeframe.

The second major counterargument is that Panama may still close the monetization gap and that Yahoo can pair better search economics with display leadership to recover strategic relevance. But the evidence here is backward-looking and partial. A 15-20% lift in revenue per search is meaningful operationally, yet it has not halted share losses in the segment that matters most. In a scale-driven market, monetization improvement without query-share stabilization is not enough. Yahoo would need both better economics and regained usage momentum, and the packet provides no strong evidence that this turnaround is underway.

The board therefore should authorize a structured reengagement with Microsoft immediately. The mandate should be: secure a price increase toward at least $37/share, insist on clear diligence boundaries and timing, and prepare a clean communication package for employees, regulators, and investors. The negotiating position should explicitly frame Yahoo's audience assets, media properties, and Asian stakes as reasons for a higher price, while privately recognizing that those same assets do not solve the core strategic deterioration of the operating business.

**Decision Frame**

Recommendation: sell, not stay independent.

Why this clears the bar:
- The offered premium is already substantial relative to Yahoo's unaffected price.
- Yahoo's strongest operating franchise is in a slower-changing, increasingly fragmented display market, while search economics are consolidating around Google.
- The Google partnership is a defensive bridge with serious antitrust risk, not a durable strategy.
- Microsoft's strategic urgency creates unusually favorable leverage for Yahoo to improve terms now.

What to do next:
- Reopen talks immediately.
- Set an explicit acceptable range around the board's $37/share position.
- Run a short deadline-driven process to reduce uncertainty and attrition.

**Unknowns & Evidence Gaps**

- The packet does not provide a full sum-of-the-parts valuation for Yahoo's core business plus Asian holdings.
- There is no detailed forecast for Panama's impact on search margin over the next 2-3 years.
- We do not have current internal retention data, product roadmap confidence, or scenario analysis for a failed Google partnership.
- Regulatory certainty for either a Microsoft transaction or the Google deal is not quantified.

These gaps matter for price optimization, but they do not overturn the directional conclusion that independence carries higher execution risk than a negotiated sale.

**Pass/Fail Readiness**

Pass conditions for sale path:
- Microsoft signals willingness to return within a defined price band above $33.
- Board aligns around a short, non-drifting process.
- Management can retain key leaders through transaction signing with targeted packages.

Fail conditions for independence path:
- Google partnership is blocked or materially constrained.
- Search share continues declining despite Panama gains.
- Activist pressure escalates into a prolonged governance fight.
- Display leadership erodes as attention shifts to social platforms.

Readiness verdict: pass for reopening sale talks now; fail for relying on the current independent plan as the primary strategy.
