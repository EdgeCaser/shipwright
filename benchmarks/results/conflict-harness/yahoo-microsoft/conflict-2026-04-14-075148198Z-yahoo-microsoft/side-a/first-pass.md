# Recommendation: Reopen negotiations with Microsoft and pursue a sale at a higher price

Yahoo should not continue as an independent company on the current plan. The board should reopen negotiations with Microsoft immediately and seek a transaction in the $35-37 per share range, with $37 as the target and anything materially below $33 as a fail condition. The core reason is simple: Yahoo's strategic value is real, but its standalone path is weak where industry value is concentrating most rapidly: search. The Microsoft bid already recognizes substantial strategic value, while the independent alternative depends on a Google partnership that is likely to face antitrust limits and does not solve Yahoo's long-term control problem.

Yahoo still has major assets: the leading US display position, 260M+ Mail users, strong engagement properties such as Finance, Sports, and Flickr, and highly valuable stakes in Yahoo Japan and Alibaba. Those assets justify pressing for a better price than the original $33 offer. But they do not justify rejecting a credible buyer that has a demonstrated strategic need for Yahoo's scale and can likely extract more value from Yahoo's search and ad assets than Yahoo can on its own.

## Why sell now

First, Yahoo's market position is deteriorating in the segment that matters most. Search is the highest-growth and highest-margin part of digital advertising, yet Google holds roughly 63% share while Yahoo is around 20% and declining. Panama improved monetization by 15-20%, but better monetization without stabilizing share is not a turnaround; it is margin repair on a shrinking base. Meanwhile, Google generated far stronger economics in 2007, with $16.6B revenue and $5.1B operating income versus Yahoo's $6.97B revenue and $695M operating income. That gap is not a temporary execution miss. It reflects structural disadvantage at scale. [ctx-1] [ctx-2] [ctx-3] [ctx-4]

Second, the standalone defense is fragile. The Google-Yahoo search partnership is not a durable independence strategy because it is both externally constrained and strategically compromising. The DOJ is already reviewing it and is likely to block or narrow it. Even if it survives in limited form, outsourcing search ads to Google for 3+ years would improve near-term economics while effectively conceding control of Yahoo's core search monetization engine to its strongest competitor. That may defend quarterly results, but it weakens Yahoo's strategic independence rather than strengthens it. [ctx-5] [ctx-6]

Third, Microsoft's need creates leverage now that may not exist later. Microsoft is strategically behind in search, with only about 10% share, and needs Yahoo's scale to compete credibly with Google. That urgency gives Yahoo a narrow negotiating window to convert strategic scarcity into price. If Yahoo waits and its operating position weakens further, Microsoft may return on worse terms or not at all. Carl Icahn's 5% stake and public pressure increase the likelihood that the market will punish continued delay absent a clearly superior alternative. [ctx-7] [ctx-8] [ctx-9]

Fourth, the premium on the table already exceeds what the market believes Yahoo can achieve independently in the near term. Microsoft offered $33 per share, a 62% premium to the roughly $20 pre-offer price. After rejection, the stock traded around $24, indicating investors did not assign full value to the board's independence case. The board's view that Yahoo is worth at least $37 may be directionally reasonable when including strategic synergies and hidden asset value, but the observable market evidence says Yahoo should monetize that value through negotiation, not through a prolonged standalone bet. ## Strongest counterarguments, and why they do not change the decision

The strongest argument for staying independent is that Yahoo owns a rare portfolio of under-monetized assets. Yahoo is still #1 in US display, has one of the largest global audience footprints through Mail and media properties, and holds stakes in Yahoo Japan and Alibaba worth roughly $11-14B combined. On a sum-of-the-parts basis, the company may indeed be worth more than Microsoft's first offer. [ctx-12] [ctx-13] [ctx-14] [ctx-15]

That argument supports holding out for a better price, not holding out for independence. The issue is not whether Yahoo has assets; it is whether management has a credible path to compound their value faster than competitive erosion destroys it. The evidence points the other way. Headcount has risen from about 11,400 to 14,300 without commensurate operating performance, search share continues to fall, and the display business faces attention fragmentation as Facebook scales from roughly 100M to 200M users in 2008. Yahoo's best assets are real, but the market context is becoming less forgiving, not more. [ctx-16] [ctx-17]

A second counterargument is that Panama shows Yahoo can still close the monetization gap and that more time could unlock meaningful upside. But Panama has already produced measurable monetization gains without reversing the more important trend: user and query share loss. In platform markets, share loss to a dominant leader usually compounds. Operational improvement absent distribution or product leadership is unlikely to restore strategic parity with Google. ## Recommendation

Reopen talks with Microsoft immediately. Anchor at $37 per share, signal willingness to transact in a narrow band above the original offer, and treat the Google partnership only as negotiating leverage, not as the board's true standalone plan. If Microsoft refuses to improve terms meaningfully above $33, the board can still reassess. But the default should be to convert Yahoo's strategic assets into a negotiated exit while those assets still command scarcity value.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Reopen negotiations with Microsoft and pursue a sale.

Why now: Yahoo's standalone strategy depends on a regulatorily vulnerable Google partnership and a turnaround in search that current evidence does not support.

Target outcome: Secure a revised price in the $35-37 range, reflecting Yahoo's strategic assets and Microsoft's need for scale.

Walk-away condition: Do not accept a materially sub-$33 outcome absent a severe new deterioration in operating outlook.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

The exact sustainable earnings power of Yahoo's display business under rising social fragmentation remains uncertain.

The true realizable value of Yahoo Japan and Alibaba in a control transaction may differ from quoted standalone estimates.

Microsoft's actual ceiling price is unknown; its urgency is evident, but willingness to bridge to $37 is not proven.

The DOJ's final posture on the Google-Yahoo agreement is not yet resolved, though current signals make heavy reliance on it risky.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if the board is willing to treat sum-of-the-parts value as a pricing argument for sale rather than as proof of standalone viability.

Pass if management can enter negotiations quickly with clear price discipline and limited dependence on the Google deal.

Fail if the board's decision standard is preserving independence absent a hard, quantified standalone plan to stabilize search share and margins.

Fail if Yahoo continues using a contested Google partnership as its primary defense despite antitrust and strategic-control risk.
