# Critique: finding-2

- Target Side: side_a
- Target Claim IDs: side_a-claim-1, side_a-claim-3
- Attack Type: evidence_gap
- Severity: high

## Claim Under Attack
Yahoo should reopen negotiations and sell because the Microsoft offer reflects more realizable value than Yahoo's standalone operating trajectory, and strategic assets justify a higher price but not independence.

## Evidence Or Reason
Side A anchors its entire valuation argument on the gap between Yahoo's ~$24 post-rejection trading price and Microsoft's $33 offer, treating the market price as a reliable proxy for standalone value. This is a critical evidence gap: the memo never performs or references an actual sum-of-the-parts valuation despite acknowledging in its own Unknowns section that 'the precise standalone sum-of-the-parts value under realistic tax and execution assumptions is not fully established.' The Alibaba stake alone (~40% ownership, valued at ~$3-4B in 2008) would eventually be worth over $80B by IPO — Side A dismisses this as a 'financial asset' with 'uncertain timing and liquidity' but provides zero analysis of Alibaba's growth trajectory, revenue acceleration, or the Chinese e-commerce market expansion that was already visible by 2008. Similarly, the Yahoo Japan stake ($8-10B) is waved away without modeling its income stream or strategic optionality. A board memo that recommends selling the entire company while admitting it hasn't established what the parts are worth independently is recommending action before completing the analysis its own framework requires. The $33 offer may or may not exceed standalone value — but Side A hasn't shown the math, only asserted the conclusion.
