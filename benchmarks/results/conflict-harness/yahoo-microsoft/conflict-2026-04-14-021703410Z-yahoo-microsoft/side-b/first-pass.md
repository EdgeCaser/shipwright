# Reopen Negotiations and Sell to Microsoft

Yahoo should reopen negotiations with Microsoft and seek a transaction in the $35 to $37 per share range, with strong employee retention protections and clear treatment of the Alibaba and Yahoo Japan stakes. The core reason is simple: Yahoo's strongest assets are real, but they do not add up to a credible stand-alone path to close the strategic gap in search before market structure hardens further.

Yahoo still has major audience scale: leading US display share, 260M+ Mail users, and durable properties such as Finance, Sports, and Flickr. It also holds valuable stakes in Yahoo Japan and Alibaba. But the board's decision is not whether Yahoo has assets. It is whether those assets can compound faster as an independent operating company than as part of a larger platform with the capital, engineering base, and distribution leverage to contest Google. On that question, the evidence points toward sale.

First, the Microsoft offer already captures a level of value the public market does not believe Yahoo can realize alone. Microsoft offered $33 per share, a 62% premium to Yahoo's roughly $20 pre-offer price. After rejection, the stock traded closer to $24, which is the market's revealed view of Yahoo's stand-alone prospects under the board's preferred plan. A board can reject a first offer if it is genuinely inadequate, but it should not confuse strategic pride with bargaining leverage. The right move is not to hold out for independence; it is to use Microsoft's strategic need for search scale to improve price modestly and close.

Second, Yahoo's operating profile shows a company with valuable traffic but weak monetization relative to the market leader. Yahoo produced $6.97B of 2007 revenue but only $695M of operating income, roughly a 10% margin. Google produced $16.6B of revenue and $5.1B of operating income, about a 31% margin. That gap matters because search is the highest-growth, highest-margin segment, and Yahoo is losing share there despite the Panama improvements. A 15-20% revenue-per-search gain from Panama is meaningful, but it has not reversed the core problem: Google has about 63% share, Yahoo is around 20% and declining, and Microsoft is near 10%. Scale in search improves relevance, advertiser demand, monetization, and data feedback loops. Yahoo is on the wrong side of that flywheel.

Third, the independent plan is weaker than it appears. The Google search partnership is not a stable foundation for independence because the DOJ is already reviewing it and is likely to block or sharply limit it. Even if approved in diluted form, outsourcing search ads to Google for multiple years would effectively concede control of Yahoo's most strategically important monetization layer. That may boost near-term cash flow, but it would further reduce Yahoo's ability to rebuild differentiated search economics over time. Independence supported by a likely-constrained Google deal is not true independence.

Fourth, the non-core asset stakes strengthen the case for sale rather than independence. Yahoo Japan and Alibaba together may be worth roughly $11B to $14B on a stand-alone basis. That is a material source of hidden value, but it also means a large share of Yahoo's value is coming from assets not created by improving the core US operating business. If the board believes Microsoft undervalues those stakes, the answer is to negotiate explicit recognition of them in price or structure. It is not to keep running a core business that is losing strategic position in search while social platforms like Facebook are beginning to fragment attention in display.

The strongest argument for staying independent is that Yahoo still owns premier consumer destinations and remains the leader in US display advertising. That argument deserves respect. Display, homepage traffic, communications, and content brands still matter, and Yahoo could plausibly remain a meaningful media company. But this is exactly the problem: remaining meaningful is not the same as maximizing value. The industry profit pool is shifting toward search and performance advertising, while audience attention is becoming more fragmented. Yahoo's display leadership is an asset under pressure, not a protected moat.

The second-best argument against selling is that Microsoft is strategically desperate and therefore should pay more. That is partly correct. Microsoft does need search scale badly, and Yahoo should exploit that urgency. But the implication is to negotiate harder now, not to walk away indefinitely. The board's stated floor of $37 per share is aggressive but not absurd if linked to clean terms, break protections, and asset treatment. The correct recommendation is a controlled reopening aimed at extracting a final price improvement, with a willingness to accept a deal in the mid-to-high $30s if certainty is high.

The practical recommendation is: reopen talks immediately; anchor at $37; be prepared to settle if value and certainty are superior to the stand-alone path; and avoid relying on the Google partnership as the central defense. If Microsoft will not return above $33 with sufficient certainty, the board can revisit independence, but only with a restructuring-grade plan that reduces headcount growth, sharpens capital allocation, and treats the Asian stakes as monetizable strategic resources rather than passive consolation.

## Decision Frame

Recommendation: sell, by reopening negotiations with Microsoft and targeting a transaction in the $35 to $37 range.

Why this is the best decision:
- The offer already far exceeds the market's stand-alone valuation.
- Yahoo's search position is structurally deteriorating in the segment that matters most.
- Panama improved monetization but did not fix share loss.
- The Google partnership is unlikely to survive intact and weakens strategic control even if approved.
- Yahoo's hidden asset value is best used as negotiating leverage for sale terms, not as justification to keep operating independently.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- The precise standalone after-tax value realizable from partial or full monetization of Yahoo Japan and Alibaba is uncertain.
- The likelihood, timing, and exact scope of DOJ action on the Google partnership are not fully known.
- Microsoft's true willingness to pay above $33, and whether it would structure around the Asian stakes, remains untested after withdrawal.
- The near-term cost synergies and integration risks of a Microsoft-Yahoo combination need tighter quantification.
- Yahoo management has not yet shown a detailed independent plan with explicit margin, share, and headcount targets sufficient to justify rejecting a premium bid.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass conditions for reopening and selling:
- Microsoft indicates credible willingness to re-engage quickly.
- Yahoo can secure improved price, stronger certainty, or better treatment of strategic stakes.
- The board aligns around value maximization rather than symbolic independence.

Fail conditions that would justify reverting to independence:
- Microsoft refuses to improve economics or offers weak transaction certainty.
- Regulatory or structural terms destroy too much of the acquisition value.
- Yahoo can produce a credible 24-month independent plan with measurable commitments on search economics, margin expansion, and capital discipline that clearly exceeds the expected value of sale.

Current judgment: pass for reopening sale talks; fail for relying on the current independent defense plan.
