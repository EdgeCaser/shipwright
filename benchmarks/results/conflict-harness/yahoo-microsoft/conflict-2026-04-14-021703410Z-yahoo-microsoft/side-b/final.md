# Reopen Negotiations, but Sell Only if the Asian Stakes Are Explicitly Protected

Yahoo should reopen negotiations with Microsoft, but the board should sell only if the final deal either: (1) delivers clear value in the board's stated $37 per share range, or (2) separately preserves or explicitly credits the value of the Yahoo Japan and Alibaba stakes. If Microsoft will not do that, Yahoo should remain independent rather than accept a blended price that transfers too much upside from those assets to the buyer.

The central case for a sale still holds. Microsoft's $33 offer represented a 62% premium to Yahoo's roughly $20 pre-offer price, and Yahoo traded closer to $24 after rejection. That gap matters because it is the market's revealed judgment on Yahoo's stand-alone operating trajectory. Yahoo remains a major internet franchise with the leading US display position, 260M+ Mail users, and strong properties such as Finance, Sports, and Flickr. But the core operating problem is that the industry's profit pool is shifting toward search and performance advertising, and Yahoo is losing ground there. Yahoo generated $6.97B of 2007 revenue but only $695M of operating income, roughly a 10% margin, versus Google's $16.6B of revenue and $5.1B of operating income, roughly a 31% margin. Panama improved revenue-per-search by 15-20%, but Yahoo's search share remained around 20% and declining while Google held roughly 63%. That is not a profile that supports confidence in an independent turnaround.

The Google partnership does not solve this. The DOJ review creates a real risk that the arrangement is blocked or sharply narrowed, and even if it survives, outsourcing search monetization to Google would improve near-term economics by conceding long-term strategic control. An independence case built on a constrained Google deal is weak.

The critique is correct, however, that Yahoo's Asian stakes cannot simply be treated as generic negotiating leverage. Yahoo Japan and Alibaba are worth roughly $11B to $14B combined on the facts available here, which is a large share of the $47.5B offer value. If Microsoft will not pay for that value explicitly, then a straight sale at or near $33 risks forcing shareholders to give up high-optionality assets at a blended price that reflects the weakness of the core business more than the upside of those stakes. That changes the recommendation materially: the board should not accept a conventional whole-company deal unless those stakes are separately recognized in price or structure.

That does not overturn the sale recommendation; it sharpens it. Microsoft's strategic need for search scale is still Yahoo's strongest source of bargaining power. The board should use that leverage to demand one of three outcomes: a higher headline price, explicit contractual treatment for the Alibaba and Yahoo Japan stakes, or a structure that leaves some of that upside with Yahoo shareholders. If Microsoft refuses all three, then the board should reject the bid and pursue independence, not because the core operating case is strong, but because the stakes create a floor of retained optionality that a low blended acquisition would misprice.

The strongest counterargument is that Yahoo's display leadership, audience scale, and non-core assets together justify staying independent now. That argument is stronger after accounting properly for the Asian stakes. But it is still not strong enough to make independence the preferred base case, because the operating business continues to deteriorate in the segment that matters most, and the defensive Google path is unstable. The right conclusion is conditional but clear: sell if, and only if, Microsoft pays for the full asset mix rather than merely the troubled core.

## Decision Frame

Recommendation: reopen negotiations with Microsoft and pursue a sale only on terms that explicitly protect or fully reflect the value of Yahoo Japan and Alibaba.

Why this is the best decision:
- The core business does not have a credible stand-alone path to close the search gap with Google.
- The market has already signaled skepticism toward Yahoo's independent plan through the post-rejection trading level.
- The Google partnership is unlikely to provide a durable foundation for independence.
- The Asian stakes are too valuable to be silently absorbed into an undifferentiated $33-style blended bid.
- Microsoft's strategic urgency creates the best available chance to convert Yahoo's asset base into realizable shareholder value.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- Microsoft's actual willingness to raise headline price or structure around the Yahoo Japan and Alibaba stakes remains unproven.
- The exact after-tax realizable value and monetization paths for those stakes are not specified.
- The likely timing and scope of DOJ action on the Google-Yahoo partnership remain uncertain.
- The standalone upside of retaining Alibaba and Yahoo Japan versus selling now is directionally important but not fully quantified here.
- Yahoo management still has not shown a detailed 24-month operating plan that would justify rejecting a premium bid absent stake protection.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass conditions for selling:
- Microsoft re-engages quickly.
- Yahoo secures either a price in the board's stated range or explicit structural recognition of the Asian stakes.
- Transaction certainty is high enough that the premium is realizable.

Fail conditions for selling:
- Microsoft insists on a blended whole-company price that does not adequately credit Yahoo Japan and Alibaba.
- Regulatory or structural terms materially reduce acquisition value.
- Yahoo can demonstrate that retained stake optionality plus a credible operating plan exceeds the expected value of Microsoft's best final offer.

Current judgment: pass for reopening negotiations; conditional pass for a sale only if the Asian stakes are explicitly protected or fully valued.
