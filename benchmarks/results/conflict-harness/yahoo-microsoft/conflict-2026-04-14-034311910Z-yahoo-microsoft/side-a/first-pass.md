# Recommendation Memo: Reopen Negotiations and Sell to Microsoft

Yahoo should reopen negotiations with Microsoft and seek a price improvement toward the board's stated $37 per share threshold, but it should not pursue a stand-alone strategy as the base case. The core reason is simple: Yahoo's most strategic business, search, is structurally weakening at the exact moment scale is becoming decisive, while the alternative defense plan depends on a Google partnership that is likely to be blocked or curtailed by antitrust review. If Yahoo remains independent, it is left owning strong audience properties and valuable minority stakes, but without a credible path to restore competitiveness in the highest-growth profit pool.

The $33 offer already represents a 62% premium to Yahoo's pre-offer price of roughly $20, and the stock's post-rejection level near $24 suggests the market does not believe Yahoo can independently create equivalent near-term value. That gap matters. Yahoo's 2007 revenue was $6.97B, but operating income was only $695M, about a 10% margin, far below Google's 31% margin on $16.6B of revenue. Panama improved monetization by 15-20%, but it did not stop share losses. Yahoo remains strong in display, email, finance, sports, and Flickr, yet those assets do not solve the central issue that search scale, data, and advertiser liquidity are compounding in Google's favor.

Microsoft's strategic need increases Yahoo's leverage, not Yahoo's standalone viability. Microsoft is under-scaled in search at roughly 10% share and needs Yahoo's roughly 20% share to approach relevance against Google's 60%+ position. That means Yahoo should negotiate hard on price and terms, but from a posture of selling, not of indefinite independence. A rational objective is to use Microsoft's urgency, Carl Icahn's pressure, and the fragility of Yahoo's alternatives to extract a better bid, potentially with protections around employee retention, asset treatment, and execution certainty.

The strongest argument for independence is that Yahoo still owns scarce strategic assets: the #1 US display ad position, 260M+ Mail users, major consumer destinations, and valuable stakes in Yahoo Japan and Alibaba worth roughly $11-14B combined. But this argument overstates how much those assets can offset deterioration in the operating business. Display leadership is exposed to audience fragmentation as Facebook rapidly scales from 100M toward 200M users in 2008. The minority stakes are real sources of hidden value, but they are not evidence that the core operating company should remain independent; if anything, they strengthen the case that Microsoft can afford to pay more while Yahoo shareholders should realize value now rather than continue to bear execution risk.

The second major counterargument is that Panama and a Google search deal could buy time to stabilize the business. That is too fragile to anchor board strategy. Panama improved revenue-per-search, but not competitive position. The Google outsourcing partnership may raise monetization in the short run, but the DOJ review creates a serious probability that the deal is blocked or materially limited. A defense that depends on a likely-constrained transaction is not a board-grade alternative to a fully financed acquisition proposal.

Yahoo should therefore pursue a controlled re-engagement with Microsoft immediately. The message should be: Yahoo is prepared to transact at an improved price that better reflects strategic assets and Microsoft's need for scale, but it will not continue with a standalone strategy absent a clearly superior, executable plan that does not depend on the Google partnership. On the evidence available, that superior plan does not exist.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Reopen negotiations with Microsoft and position for a sale at an improved price, rather than commit to independence.

Why this is the right call:
- Yahoo's core competitive problem is in search, where share is declining despite Panama improvements.
- Search is the highest-growth, highest-margin segment, and Yahoo lacks a credible path to regain scale against Google.
- The independence plan relies too heavily on the Google partnership, which faces serious DOJ antitrust risk.
- The current offer already far exceeds the market's implied standalone value, while Microsoft's strategic need creates room to negotiate higher.
- Yahoo's non-core and minority-stake assets support price improvement, but do not justify remaining independent.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- The exact standalone sum-of-the-parts value under realistic, not aspirational, execution assumptions.
- The probability-weighted outcome of DOJ review on the Google partnership, including likely scope restrictions.
- Whether Microsoft would pay at or above $37 per share, and under what deal conditions.
- The degree to which Yahoo's audience properties can sustain display leadership as attention shifts toward Facebook and other social platforms.
- Integration-dis-synergy risk: whether Microsoft can preserve Yahoo's traffic and talent well enough to justify a higher bid.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if:
- Microsoft signals willingness to improve economics meaningfully above $33 per share.
- Yahoo can secure acceptable treatment for key assets, employees, and execution risk.
- The board confirms that the Google partnership cannot be relied on as a clean, durable alternative.

Fail if:
- Microsoft will not improve terms at all and diligence shows the sum-of-the-parts value clearly exceeds the likely transaction range on a near-term basis.
- Yahoo can present a standalone plan with quantified milestones showing search stabilization, not just better monetization, without depending on Google.
- Regulatory or transaction-certainty risk on a Microsoft deal rises materially.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A board decision memo with a probability-weighted options model comparing:
1. Microsoft sale at $33, $35, and $37 per share.
2. Independence with and without the Google partnership.
3. Sum-of-the-parts downside protection from Alibaba and Yahoo Japan stakes.

That artifact should force the board to compare executable shareholder outcomes rather than strategic narratives.
