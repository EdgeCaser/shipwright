# Critique: finding-2

- Target Side: side_a
- Target Claim IDs: side_a-claim-2, side_a-claim-3
- Attack Type: evidence_gap
- Severity: high

## Claim Under Attack
The post-rejection trading price of ~$24 suggests the market does not support Yahoo's higher standalone valuation, and Yahoo's equity stakes justify a higher price but not independence.

## Evidence Or Reason
Side A uses the $24 post-rejection price as a market verdict against independence, but this is circular reasoning during a hostile takeover context — the stock reflects merger arbitrage dynamics and uncertainty, not a clean fundamental valuation of Yahoo standalone. More critically, Side A acknowledges Yahoo Japan (~$8-10B) and Alibaba (~$3-4B) stakes total $11-14B, yet never completes the arithmetic: at ~1.38B shares outstanding, these stakes alone represent $8-10/share, meaning the market is valuing Yahoo's entire core operating business (display #1, 260M Mail users, Finance, Sports, Flickr, $7B revenue) at only $14-16/share. Side A's own evidence actually strengthens the case that $33 or even $37 dramatically undervalues Yahoo when you properly account for the option value of Alibaba pre-IPO (which Side A prices at only $3-4B against what became a $230B+ IPO). The memo identifies this gap in Unknowns but then proceeds to recommend selling anyway without resolving it — the sum-of-the-parts analysis Side A admits is missing is precisely the analysis that would determine whether selling is correct. Recommending a sale while acknowledging you lack the valuation to justify it is not evidence discipline; it is premature closure.
