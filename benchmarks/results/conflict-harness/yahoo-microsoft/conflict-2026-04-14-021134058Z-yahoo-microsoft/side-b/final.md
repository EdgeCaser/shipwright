# Revised artifact

## Reopen Negotiations and Sell to Microsoft

### Recommendation
Yahoo should reopen negotiations with Microsoft and pursue a sale, targeting at least the board's stated $37 per share threshold while prioritizing deal certainty over a prolonged independence plan. The revised case for selling is not that Yahoo lacks valuable assets. It is that much of Yahoo's value already sits in non-operating stakes and audience properties whose full value may be difficult to realize through continued independence, while the core operating business faces mounting strategic pressure in search and rising risk in display.

### Why the Sale Case Still Holds

#### 1. The right question is not premium versus unaffected price alone, but whether Yahoo can realize more value on its own than Microsoft can crystallize now
The critique is correct that the offer should be decomposed. Yahoo's Yahoo Japan stake is worth roughly $8-10B and its Alibaba stake roughly $3-4B, so $11-14B of value is non-operating. Against Microsoft's $47.5B offer, that implies the market is effectively valuing the rest of Yahoo at about $33.5-36.5B, and a $37/share outcome would imply somewhat more. That is not obviously trivial. But it also means the board's independence case depends on proving that Yahoo can preserve and expand the value of its operating business on top of those embedded stakes. The evidence packet does not show that with enough confidence.

#### 2. Yahoo's operating problem is strategic quality, not simply low margin versus Google
The critique is also correct that a direct Yahoo-versus-Google margin comparison can overstate the weakness of Yahoo's standalone model because Yahoo is more exposed to display and portal economics while Google is more concentrated in search. The stronger argument is narrower: the highest-growth, highest-margin segment in digital advertising is search, and Yahoo has about 20% share that is declining while Google holds more than 60%. Panama improved revenue per search by 15-20%, but the packet gives no evidence that it stopped share loss. That matters because a display-led company can remain valuable, but in 2008 the strategic center of gravity is moving toward search scale and performance advertising.

#### 3. Yahoo's strongest independent assets do not fully solve the forward-looking operating risk
Yahoo still has genuine strengths: #1 US display share at 15.6% of digital ad spend, 260M+ Mail users, and high-engagement properties including Finance, Sports, and Flickr. Those assets justify pushing Microsoft above $33. They do not by themselves justify rejecting a bid if the alternative is an independence thesis built on three simultaneous bets: defending display leadership as Facebook fragments attention, stabilizing or offsetting search decline, and navigating a Google partnership that DOJ review is likely to block or materially narrow.

#### 4. The Google partnership is a weak foundation for independence
Even if the Google deal improved near-term monetization, it would do so by outsourcing Yahoo's search ads for 3+ years. That is a monetization bridge, not a durable strategic rebuild. If the DOJ blocks or constrains the arrangement, the independence case loses a major support. If the DOJ allows it in substantial form, Yahoo still becomes more dependent on its largest rival in search economics. Either way, it is not a strong basis for rejecting an acquirer that urgently needs Yahoo's scale.

#### 5. Microsoft's urgency is the key leverage point, and Yahoo should use it now
Microsoft still needs search scale to compete with Google. That strategic need is what gives Yahoo negotiating power. The board should use that leverage to demand a higher bid, not to assume time is on Yahoo's side. Delay increases the odds that search keeps eroding, display leadership becomes less defensible, activist pressure intensifies, and the board is later forced to negotiate from a weaker position.

### Strongest Counterarguments

#### Counterargument: Yahoo's sum-of-the-parts value suggests the offer still undervalues the company
This is the best objection. The embedded value of Yahoo Japan and Alibaba, plus Yahoo's audience franchises, means the board is right to reject $33 if it believes Microsoft can pay more. But that supports renegotiation, not independence. The packet does not provide enough evidence that Yahoo can independently unlock that sum-of-the-parts value faster or with less risk than a negotiated sale.

#### Counterargument: Yahoo can remain a valuable display-led media and advertising company without winning in search
Possibly, but the burden is not to prove Yahoo can remain relevant. The burden is to prove that this path creates better risk-adjusted shareholder value than a strategic sale at a premium. With Facebook accelerating audience fragmentation and with headcount rising from 11,400 to 14,300 without clear operating leverage, the board lacks enough evidence for that conclusion.

### Board Action
Reopen negotiations immediately. Anchor at $37 per share or better, using Yahoo's audience scale, display leadership, and strategic stakes as justification for a higher bid. At the same time, prepare a fallback plan only as leverage and downside protection: stricter cost discipline, explicit milestones for search-share stabilization, and a time-boxed review of alternatives if Microsoft will not return.

## Decision Frame
Decision: Reopen negotiations with Microsoft and pursue a sale, targeting at least $37 per share.

Why now: Yahoo has real asset value, but too much of the independent case depends on uncertain operating improvement and a fragile Google partnership rather than on clearly demonstrated momentum.

What would change the decision: Evidence that Yahoo can preserve display leadership, offset or stabilize search decline without relying on Google, and unlock non-operating asset value for shareholders on a credible 12-18 month timetable.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- The fully burdened sum-of-the-parts valuation after taxes, minority discounts, and separation costs.
- Microsoft's actual maximum price and willingness to return with a friendly versus hostile structure.
- Whether Panama can stabilize search share, not just improve monetization per query.
- How much independent value Yahoo can unlock from Yahoo Japan and Alibaba without a broader strategic transaction.
- The DOJ's likely posture on the Google partnership and how much value survives under a narrowed remedy.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if Yahoo can either secure a renewed Microsoft proposal at or above its minimum acceptable value or produce a standalone plan with quantified milestones for search, display, costs, and asset-value realization.

Fail if independence continues to rest mainly on broad audience strength, asset-value rhetoric, or the Google partnership without a credible operating path.

Current status: Pass for reopening negotiations. Not yet pass for independence.
