# Recommendation: Reopen negotiations and sell to Microsoft if Yahoo can secure at least $35-37 per share

Yahoo should not continue as a standalone company on the current path. The board should reopen negotiations with Microsoft immediately and seek a price improvement into the $35-37 range, but it should prioritize consummating a transaction over defending nominal independence. The core reason is strategic, not merely financial: Yahoo still has valuable audience assets and minority stakes, but its control over the most important profit pool in internet advertising, search, is weakening, while the fallback independence plan depends on a Google partnership that faces serious antitrust risk and would further hollow out Yahoo's own strategic position.

The Microsoft bid already values Yahoo at $47.5B, or $33 per share, a 62% premium to the roughly $20 unaffected trading price. That premium is not being offered for a business with improving competitive momentum; it is being offered because Microsoft urgently needs scale in search and Yahoo still has it. Yahoo's board therefore has leverage to negotiate modestly upward, but not to assume that independence will create more value than a near-term sale. After rejection, Yahoo's stock trading around $24 indicates the market does not believe Yahoo can credibly realize standalone value equal to the rejected bid in any reasonable time frame.

## Why selling is the right decision

First, Yahoo's asset quality is better than its operating trajectory. Yahoo remains the #1 US display advertising platform, with 15.6% of total digital ad spend, and it owns high-engagement properties including Mail, Finance, Sports, and Flickr. Yahoo Mail alone has 260M+ users. It also holds strategic stakes in Yahoo Japan and Alibaba worth roughly $11-14B combined on a standalone basis. These are real assets, but they strengthen the argument for selling rather than remaining independent: Microsoft is offering a full-company price that recognizes these assets while also assigning value to a turnaround that Yahoo has not yet demonstrated it can deliver.

Second, the economic gap versus Google is too large and sits in the wrong part of the market. Yahoo generated $6.97B of revenue in 2007 but only $695M of operating income, roughly a 10% margin. Google generated $16.6B of revenue and $5.1B of operating income, roughly a 31% margin. That difference reflects not just scale but business mix and competitive advantage in search, which is the highest-growth and highest-margin segment. Yahoo's search share is around 20% and declining, versus Google's roughly 63%. Panama improved revenue per search by 15-20%, but better monetization has not stopped share losses. A company losing share in the best segment while relying on leadership in a maturing segment is not in a strong standalone strategic position.

Third, the independence plan is not actually an independence plan. The Google search partnership would outsource Yahoo search ads to Google for at least three years. Even if approved, that would mean Yahoo is effectively conceding that it cannot compete directly in search monetization. Worse, the DOJ is already reviewing the deal and is likely to block it or severely narrow it. That makes the board's main defensive alternative both strategically weakening and executionally uncertain. A strategy that depends on regulators blessing a partnership with the category leader, while that leader already has 60%+ search share, is not a sound board-level fallback.

Fourth, time is working against Yahoo. Employee count has risen to roughly 14,300 from 11,400 in 2006, yet financial performance and strategic clarity have not improved commensurately. At the same time, Facebook's rapid rise from 100M to 200M users in 2008 is fragmenting user attention and threatens the durability of Yahoo's display advantage. Yahoo is strongest in a part of digital advertising that is becoming structurally more contested, while weakest in the part with the best economics.

Finally, Microsoft's motivation is unlikely to improve indefinitely. Microsoft's need for search scale is real and gives Yahoo bargaining power now. But this urgency cuts both ways: it creates a window for a higher bid, not a permanent option value. If Yahoo appears intransigent, Microsoft may shift to a partial transaction, a hostile path, or prolonged disengagement, leaving Yahoo with a lower public valuation and a damaged negotiating position amid activist pressure from Carl Icahn.

## Strongest counterarguments, and why they do not change the answer

The strongest argument for staying independent is that the Microsoft offer undervalues Yahoo's sum-of-the-parts. Yahoo Japan and Alibaba alone may be worth $11-14B, and the core media properties, user base, and display franchise still have substantial value. If Microsoft needs Yahoo to compete with Google, then Yahoo should demand more than $33.

That critique is valid up to a point. The board should not accept $33 immediately if there is a credible path to $35-37. But it does not justify remaining independent. Sum-of-the-parts value is only relevant if the board has a realistic way to unlock it faster and more reliably than a sale. In May 2008, Yahoo does not. Its operating business is strategically drifting, its search turnaround has not materialized, its Google partnership faces antitrust challenge, and its public market price after rejection remains far below the bid. The market is effectively signaling that the asset base is valuable, but management's ability to convert that into superior standalone shareholder value is not credible.

A second counterargument is that Panama shows Yahoo can still narrow the gap in search and that more time could let management stabilize share and improve profitability. The problem is that monetization improvement without share stabilization is not enough. Search is a scale business. If Yahoo's share keeps declining, it cannot win merely by extracting somewhat more revenue from a shrinking base. Microsoft, by combining its own roughly 10% search share with Yahoo's roughly 20%, at least creates a plausible scaled challenger. Yahoo on its own does not.

A third counterargument is that selling sacrifices upside from Alibaba and Yahoo Japan. But shareholders can capture that value inside a sale negotiation. The correct response is to use those stakes to justify a higher clearing price, not to anchor the company to an inferior standalone path.

## Recommendation to the board

Yahoo should authorize immediate reengagement with Microsoft with a mandate to:

1. Target a negotiated range of $35-37 per share.
2. Preserve deal certainty over holding out for a price that cannot be defended by standalone alternatives.
3. Use Yahoo's audience assets, display leadership, and Asian stakes as bargaining support for a higher bid.
4. Avoid relying on the Google partnership as the core alternative, given both antitrust and strategic dependency risks.
5. Prepare a contingency value-unlock plan only as negotiating leverage, not as the primary strategy.

If Microsoft returns below $35, the board can press for improvements in price and structure. But unless a materially better standalone plan emerges quickly and credibly, the board should be willing to transact below its stated $37 minimum rather than let the opportunity disappear. The key board mistake now would be confusing negotiating leverage with strategic strength.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Reopen negotiations with Microsoft and pursue a sale, with a target outcome of $35-37 per share and willingness to close a transaction if that range proves slightly unattainable but deal certainty remains high.

Why this is the best choice: Yahoo's assets are valuable, but its standalone strategy is weakening in the highest-value segment, dependent on a likely-blocked Google partnership, and exposed to worsening competitive dynamics in display. The Microsoft offer captures strategic value today that Yahoo has not shown it can realize independently.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- The exact latest internal trend on Yahoo search query share, retention, and monetization post-Panama.
- The board's confidence interval on standalone EBITDA or free-cash-flow expansion over the next 24 months.
- Microsoft's true walk-away price and willingness to separate asset value from operating value in negotiations.
- The DOJ's likely timing and specific remedies on the Google-Yahoo partnership.
- The degree to which Alibaba and Yahoo Japan value can be monetized or surfaced without a full-company sale.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass conditions:
- Microsoft signals credible willingness to reengage above $33.
- Board aligns on a realistic acceptable range rather than a symbolic floor.
- Yahoo can present a coherent BATNA, but not one dependent on the Google deal being approved in full.

Fail conditions:
- Board insists on a price unsupported by standalone evidence.
- Negotiations drag long enough for Microsoft's urgency to fade.
- Management continues presenting the Google partnership as a durable independent strategy despite antitrust and strategic dependency risks.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A board negotiation brief with three components: a walk-away price framework, a rapid standalone downside case if no deal occurs, and a sum-of-the-parts support table showing how Yahoo's Asian stakes and core properties justify a modest bid increase but not continued independence.
