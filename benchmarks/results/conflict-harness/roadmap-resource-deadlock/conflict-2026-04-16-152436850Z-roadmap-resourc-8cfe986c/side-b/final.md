# Revised artifact

## Decision Frame
Do not split the shared engineering team across both initiatives this quarter. Assign a single staffed priority for the quarter, but make the decision binding through the executive who owns engineering capacity allocation for that team, not through an informal sponsor negotiation or an unspecified operating committee.

The practical rule is: the owner of the constrained resource must own the arbitration. In most organizations, that is the engineering executive responsible for quarterly capacity commitments for the team. Both SVP sponsors should submit the same short decision brief within 48 hours, scored on a common rubric: 12-month revenue or retention impact, hard external deadlines, strategic dependency value, delivery confidence, and quantified cost of a 90-day delay. The capacity owner makes the call, records it, and converts the non-selected initiative into a holding plan with explicit restart conditions.

If no one can credibly exercise that authority this week, do not pretend a portfolio decision has been made. Freeze net-new work for both competing asks beyond already committed obligations, require a temporary no-poaching rule on the shared team, and force designation of a decision owner in the next portfolio cadence. A weak or ownerless arbitration process is worse than a hard single-thread decision because it invites covert resource diversion and recurring political relitigation.

This recommendation is stronger than the first-pass version because it makes the enforcement mechanism explicit: one accountable capacity owner, one written decision, one deferred-work holding plan, and one dated review checkpoint.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- It remains unclear which executive formally owns allocation authority for the contested engineering team; without that, the deadlock is procedural before it is strategic.
- The largest missing business input is quantified cost of delay for each initiative over the next quarter and next 12 months.
- It is unknown whether either initiative has non-deferrable external commitments such as contracts, compliance deadlines, or board-visible promises.
- Delivery confidence is unspecified; if one initiative is materially less executable, its nominal business priority should be discounted.
- The risk of political end-runs is unknown; the recommendation assumes the team manager and engineering leadership can enforce a no-side-deals rule once the allocation is recorded.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if:
- a named executive owns capacity allocation for the shared team;
- both sponsors submit comparable decision briefs against the same criteria;
- one initiative shows materially higher cost of delay, timing criticality, or dependency value;
- the deferred initiative has a written holding plan and explicit restart trigger;
- the engineering chain of management agrees to enforce the decision and block unofficial work intake.

Fail if:
- no decision owner is named;
- the decision is framed as a consensus exercise between the two sponsors rather than a capacity-owner call;
- the shared team receives dual commitments anyway;
- the deferred sponsor can still pull engineers through informal channels;
- hidden obligations surface after the allocation is locked.

## Recommended Next Artifact
Produce a one-page capacity arbitration memo within 48 hours containing:
- the named capacity decision owner for the shared team;
- the two initiatives and executive sponsors;
- the scoring criteria and weights;
- a side-by-side cost-of-delay and timing-criticality table;
- engineering capacity assumptions for the quarter;
- the selected staffed priority;
- the deferred initiative's holding-plan milestones;
- enforcement rules, including no unofficial staffing changes;
- the reallocation checkpoint date.

That memo should function as the quarter's binding operating record. If the organization cannot name a capacity owner for the team, the next artifact should instead be a governance-gap memo documenting that resource arbitration authority is undefined and must be assigned before either initiative can claim priority.
