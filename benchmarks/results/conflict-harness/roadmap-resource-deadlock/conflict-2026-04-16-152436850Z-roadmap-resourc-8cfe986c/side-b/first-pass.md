# Resource Allocation Recommendation

## Decision Frame
Do not split the shared engineering team across both initiatives this quarter. Assign one initiative as the single staffed priority for the quarter using a forced ranking based on near-term enterprise value, timing criticality, and reversibility, and move the other initiative to a constrained holding plan with explicit milestones, dependency cleanup, and a dated reallocation checkpoint.

When executive escalation is unavailable, the practical decision rule is not "which sponsor argues harder" but "which option creates the larger cost of delay if deferred by one quarter." The operating committee should require both sponsors and the engineering leader to score each initiative on the same criteria: revenue or retention impact in the next 12 months, contractual or regulatory deadlines, strategic dependency for later roadmap items, delivery confidence, and damage created by a 90-day slip. The higher-scoring initiative gets the team. The deferred initiative gets a written preservation plan rather than partial staffing.

This is the recommended path because shared ownership of one team usually produces context switching, political churn, and two underdelivered programs instead of one finished outcome. The immediate management goal is to convert a status conflict into a transparent portfolio decision with a review date.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- The largest missing input is quantified cost of delay for each initiative over the next quarter and next 12 months.
- It is unclear whether either initiative has hard external dates such as signed customer commitments, compliance deadlines, or board-visible launch promises.
- Current delivery confidence is unknown; if one initiative is materially less executable, equal business importance does not justify equal staffing.
- The degree of reusable prep work on the deferred initiative is unknown; some architecture, design, or vendor work may proceed without the shared team.
- Capacity substitutes are unspecified; the recommendation assumes no realistic short-term backfill through contractors, internal transfers, or scope reduction.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if:
- both sponsors accept a single decision framework and scoring criteria;
- the engineering leader provides a credible capacity view for the quarter;
- one initiative shows clearly higher cost of delay or lower deferral tolerance;
- the deferred initiative can be protected through non-core work, scope shaping, or a dated restart trigger.

Fail if:
- leaders insist on equal priority without quantified tradeoffs;
- the team is forced into dual commitments anyway;
- no review checkpoint is set for reallocation;
- the deferred initiative carries hidden external commitments that were not surfaced in the decision.

## Recommended Next Artifact
Produce a one-page portfolio arbitration memo within 48 hours containing:
- the two initiatives and named executive owners;
- agreed scoring criteria and weights;
- a side-by-side cost-of-delay table;
- quarter capacity assumptions from engineering;
- the selected staffed priority;
- the holding-plan milestones for the deferred work;
- a reallocation checkpoint date and decision owner.

That memo should become the operating record for the quarter so the conflict is managed as a portfolio choice rather than a recurring sponsorship dispute.
