# Revised artifact

## Recommendation
Based on the case packet, Nissan should still favor rejecting the Honda-led parent-subsidiary structure, but only with medium confidence and only as a conditional judgment. The critique is correct that the standalone downside is severe and not fully bounded by the evidence provided. Nissan had already announced major job cuts and capacity reductions, its operating profit and net income had fallen sharply, and it was struggling in the U.S. and China while Honda was financially stronger. Those facts make the argument for accepting subordination materially stronger than the first-pass draft allowed.

Even so, the packet still points to walking away as the better board decision on the evidence available. The proposed structure had shifted from a joint holding company to one with Honda as parent and Nissan as subsidiary; Nissan opposed that on autonomy and control grounds; the talks collapsed over balance-of-power disputes, Nissan's resistance to deeper cuts, and Honda's demand for more control; and analysts viewed the tie-up as a scale and survival play rather than a merger of equals. That combination matters. The industrial logic for scale, purchasing leverage, and technology sharing was real, but the packet does not show a concrete integration design or governance model that would justify surrendering control from a position of weakness.

The board therefore should treat independence not as a win, but as a narrower risk choice: preserve control now, execute a sharper turnaround immediately, continue collaboration on EVs, software, and intelligent vehicle technologies where possible, and stay open to future partnership or capital options if performance does not improve. If there were undisclosed facts showing that Nissan lacked a viable standalone path in the near term, that could change the recommendation. On the packet alone, that threshold is not established.

## Decision Frame
The choice is whether Nissan should have accepted a Honda-led combination as a junior partner to secure scale and stability, or rejected it to preserve independence and control. The best recommendation from the provided evidence is to reject the subsidiary structure, not because independence is attractive on its own, but because the proposed deal appears to have become a control-heavy rescue without enough evidence of an executable governance and integration model.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- The packet does not show whether Nissan faced such acute standalone pressure that accepting subordination was effectively the only viable option.
- The packet does not provide a concrete post-deal governance or integration blueprint, so the likely operating reality of the subsidiary structure is unclear.
- The packet does not show how much EV, software, and intelligent vehicle collaboration could still be captured without a full corporate combination.
- The packet does not establish what additional turnaround actions Nissan was prepared to take after the collapse.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if the board is prepared to pair independence with immediate and credible restructuring, explicit performance checkpoints, and active pursuit of targeted partnerships or investors.

Fail if independence is treated as preserving pride rather than buying time for measurable operational recovery. In that case, rejecting the deal would only defer a weaker future bargaining position.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A board decision memo that pressure-tests standalone viability versus subordinated combination under three scenarios: successful self-help, partial recovery with targeted partnerships, and failed recovery requiring renewed strategic combination talks.
