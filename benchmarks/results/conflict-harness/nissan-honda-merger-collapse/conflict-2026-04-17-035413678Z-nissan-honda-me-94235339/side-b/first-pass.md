# Strategic Recommendation Memo

## Recommendation
Nissan was right to walk away rather than accept a structure that made it a subsidiary. The company clearly needs a sharper turnaround and likely still needs a partner, but conceding control under these conditions would have solved the immediate balance-sheet asymmetry by giving away strategic autonomy before proving that the combination could operate as an aligned enterprise.

The core issue is not whether scale matters. It does. The issue is whether this particular structure, at this moment, would have produced durable strategic improvement for Nissan. The available evidence suggests the answer is no. The talks collapsed over control, deeper cuts, and power balance, and analysts already viewed the transaction as a survival-driven combination rather than a merger of equals. That is a weak foundation for integrating two major automakers in an industry already under pressure from Chinese EV competition and rising software and battery investment demands.

The strongest case for accepting subordination is straightforward: Nissan is under severe pressure, Honda is financially stronger, and a combination could have improved scale, purchasing leverage, and technology sharing. Those are real advantages. But they do not outweigh the governance risk of entering a parent-subsidiary structure from a position of weakness after Nissan had already resisted the terms and while the parties were openly misaligned on control and restructuring intensity. A deal that begins with unresolved distrust about who decides, who cuts, and who controls the future is likely to destroy value even if the industrial logic is directionally sound.

Nissan should therefore preserve independence, but only as a conditional strategy. Independence is not success by itself. It is defensible only if the board now uses it to drive a more credible turnaround, pursue narrower partnerships in EVs, software, and intelligent vehicle technologies, and remain open to future capital or alliance options that do not lock in unfavorable control terms before operational recovery is underway.

## Decision Frame
The decision is whether Nissan should have accepted a Honda-led parent-subsidiary combination to gain scale and stability, or rejected it to retain control. The better choice was to reject the proposed structure and keep independence, because the transaction had shifted from a strategic combination into an imbalance-of-power solution that asked Nissan to surrender control before demonstrating operational or governance fit.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- The packet does not establish whether a subsidiary structure would have preserved meaningful operational autonomy in product, regional, or technology decisions.
- The packet does not show a concrete post-merger integration plan, so there is no evidence that control concessions would actually have translated into a successful turnaround.
- The exact severity and timing of Nissan's standalone liquidity, capital, or covenant pressure is not provided, which limits confidence on how close the company was to having no viable alternative.
- The scope of any continued collaboration outside a merger is unclear, which matters because some technology benefits may still be achievable without giving up control.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if the board is willing to treat independence as a disciplined transition state rather than a symbolic victory. That means executing deeper turnaround actions, setting explicit performance checkpoints, and pursuing targeted partnerships from a position of realism.

Fail if independence becomes a justification for avoiding harder restructuring or for assuming another partner will emerge on better terms without evidence of improved performance. In that case, rejecting the deal would only delay a weaker future negotiation.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A board-level turnaround and partnership options memo comparing three paths over the next 12 months: standalone restructuring, targeted technology collaboration, and future strategic partnership structures with explicit governance red lines and trigger conditions for reopening combination talks.
