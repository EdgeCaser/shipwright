# Revised artifact

## Strategic Recommendation Memo

## Recommendation
Walking away from the deal as it was actually described in February 2025 was the right move. Nissan should not have accepted a Honda-parent / Nissan-subsidiary structure without evidence of binding commitments on capital allocation, product roadmaps, brand autonomy, and restructuring guardrails. Nissan's weakness made a partnership strategically necessary, but that does not mean any subordinating structure was value-maximizing.

The key revision is this: the case packet supports the conclusion that Nissan likely needed a stronger partner, but it does not support the stronger claim that the specific Honda-led structure on the table would have delivered enough protected value to justify surrendering control. Honda pushed for parent-level control precisely because Nissan was the weaker party, and the talks collapsed amid balance-of-power disputes, resistance to deeper cuts, and demands for more control. On that record, the board should assume that governance asymmetry was real and that protections were uncertain at best.

## Why This Position Is Correct
Nissan's condition was severe. Its first-half fiscal 2024 operating profit fell about 90%, net income fell about 94%, and it had already announced 9,000 job cuts plus a 20% reduction in global production capacity. It was also struggling in the U.S. hybrid market and in China. Those facts show Nissan needed a turnaround lifeline and probably external support.

But need for support is not the same as proof that the proposed transaction was attractive. The evidence shows Honda sought to change the structure from a joint holding company to a parent-subsidiary model with Honda in control, while Nissan resisted on autonomy grounds. Analysts viewed the deal as a scale-and-survival play rather than a merger of equals. That strengthens the inference that Honda was unlikely to concede extensive protections to Nissan, not the opposite.

The industrial logic for combination was still real. A roughly $60 billion combination would have created the world's third-largest automaker by sales, and the sector faces heavy battery, software, and EV investment burdens plus pressure from Chinese competitors. Scale, purchasing leverage, and shared technology programs mattered. However, those benefits only translate into board-level value if Nissan could rely on fair capital allocation, credible investment follow-through, and a restructuring logic that preserved the assets worth combining. The case packet does not establish that.

## Strongest Counterargument
The strongest counterargument is that Nissan's situation was bad enough that rejecting subordination was an unaffordable luxury. Honda was financially stronger, remained profitable, and may have been the last credible large-scale partner available. If Nissan still needed a partner after the collapse, rejecting Honda could mean facing the same strategic problem later from an even weaker position.

## Why That Counterargument Still Falls Short
That argument is powerful, but it overreaches from "Nissan needed a partner" to "Nissan should have accepted this structure." Boards do not approve transactions because the alternative is uncomfortable; they approve transactions when the likely value of the actual deal exceeds the likely value of the alternatives. Here, the observable deal drifted away from a merger-of-equals framing toward one-sided control, and the record does not show offsetting protections. In that setting, preserving independence was not a statement of pride; it was a refusal to lock in subordination without a verified value-protection mechanism.

The correct lesson is not that Nissan should prioritize independence indefinitely. It is that Nissan should immediately pursue either a sharper standalone turnaround or a new partner/investor process, because the collapse did not solve its strategic problem. But on the evidence available, walking away from Honda's apparent terms was more defensible than accepting a control transfer on faith.

## Decision Frame
Do not accept the Honda-led subsidiary structure unless enforceable value protections are explicitly on the table. Based on the evidence available in February 2025, walking away from the actual deal trajectory was the better decision, while treating a new partner search or far more severe self-help plan as urgent.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- The exact final governance, capital-allocation, and restructuring terms Honda would have offered are not specified.
- There is no quantified evidence here on synergies, integration costs, or timing.
- Nissan's true standalone liquidity resilience and turnaround credibility over the next 24 months are unclear.
- The availability of alternative partners, investors, or state-backed support is not established.
- It is unclear how much product, platform, and regional autonomy Nissan could realistically have preserved under Honda control.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if Nissan can either secure explicit transaction terms that protect investment, product authority, and restructuring economics, or present a funded standalone turnaround with near-term product and market recovery milestones.

Fail if Nissan remains unable to fund competitiveness alone and still cannot secure a partner on terms that preserve the core value of its brands, platforms, and regional franchises.
