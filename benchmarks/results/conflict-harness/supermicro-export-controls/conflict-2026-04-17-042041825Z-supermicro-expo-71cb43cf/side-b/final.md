# Revised artifact

## Recommendation
Supermicro should pause and narrow selected high-risk activity while the independent investigation and compliance reset proceed, rather than continue aggressive execution at full speed. This should not be a company-wide freeze. The board should keep lower-risk execution moving, but stop new expansion and tighten approval for business that is most exposed to export-control, channel, and partner-risk escalation until the company can show stronger control.

## What counts as high-risk activity for board action now
For this decision, high-risk activity should mean the parts of the business where the current fact pattern creates the greatest risk of repeating or compounding the problem:

- transactions, channels, or routing structures where export-control exposure is highest
- business routed through intermediaries or channel structures where end destination, end customer, or compliance accountability is less direct
- expansion in areas that would increase exposure before the Global Trade Compliance Program review is complete
- deals or operating paths that could further strain credibility with critical suppliers, investors, or regulators if additional facts emerge

The board does not need a perfect final taxonomy before acting. It needs an interim rule: if an activity materially increases export-control or channel-governance exposure while the investigation is unresolved, it should be paused, narrowed, or elevated to board-supervised approval.

## Why this is the better path
First, this is not a routine personnel issue. The company disclosed indictments tied to alleged export-control violations and then announced an independent investigation overseen by independent board leadership with outside counsel. Even if the company is not named as a defendant and says it is not accused of wrongdoing, the existence of a board-led investigation means the facts are unsettled enough that maximum-speed expansion in the most exposed parts of the business is not a defensible default.

Second, the board should optimize for institutional credibility, not just near-term momentum. The case packet indicates concern not only about legal exposure but also about reputational and supplier risk, including possible strain on the Nvidia relationship. In a business dependent on trust, supply continuity, and governance credibility, a narrow defense centered only on individual misconduct is too fragile to be the operating stance before the investigation is complete.

Third, the company is already in remediation mode. It is reviewing its Global Trade Compliance Program, and the acting Chief Compliance Officer now reports to the General Counsel during the review. Those steps imply live control questions. If controls are being re-examined, the prudent move is to narrow the activity most likely to test those controls before remediation is complete.

Fourth, the worst outcome is not a temporary slowdown. The worse outcome is pressing ahead, having further adverse facts emerge, and converting a serious compliance episode into a broader board-level credibility crisis. Once the board chooses speed over visible control in the middle of an active investigation, it becomes harder to argue later that governance was appropriately prioritized.

## Strongest counterarguments, and why they do not change the recommendation
The strongest case for staying aggressive is that Supermicro is a prominent AI infrastructure supplier in a period of intense demand, and slowing down could hand share to rivals. A pause may also cause major customers to redirect orders that do not quickly return.

That risk is real, which is exactly why the recommendation should be selective rather than broad. The answer is not an undefined slowdown across the company. It is an interim operating split: preserve ordinary lower-risk execution and customer support, while pausing new growth pushes in the transactions and channels most exposed to export-control and governance risk. That makes the recommendation actionable without assuming the matter is already contained.

## Decision Frame
The board is choosing between short-term commercial speed and preserving strategic credibility under active investigation. The better choice is a targeted pause in high-risk activity, defined as activity that materially increases export-control or channel-governance exposure while facts are still unsettled.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
The board still does not know whether the alleged misconduct was isolated to the named individuals or indicative of deeper control weaknesses.

It also does not yet know which specific channels, approval points, and transaction patterns were affected, or how counterparties and critical suppliers will react as additional facts emerge.

The most decision-relevant missing evidence is the scope of control failure, the business pathways implicated by the allegations, and whether current remediation is sufficient to prevent recurrence.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if the board can immediately impose an interim risk taxonomy that separates lower-risk ongoing execution from higher-risk transactions, channels, and expansion moves that require pause or board-supervised approval.

Fail if management cannot distinguish those categories quickly, because an undifferentiated pause would risk becoming a broad commercial freeze without producing a clear compliance benefit.

## Recommended Next Artifact
Produce a board action memo that defines the interim high-risk taxonomy, names which activities are paused versus allowed with enhanced approval, assigns oversight ownership during the investigation, and states the findings or remediation milestones required before full-speed expansion resumes.
