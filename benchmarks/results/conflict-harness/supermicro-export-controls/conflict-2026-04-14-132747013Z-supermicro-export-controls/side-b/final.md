# Strategic Recommendation Memo: Narrow High-Risk Activity While Preserving Low-Risk AI Execution

Supermicro should not run a full-speed, business-as-usual growth strategy while the independent investigation is underway. The board should adopt a targeted operating posture instead: continue serving clearly compliant, lower-risk AI demand, but temporarily narrow or gate higher-risk channels, counterparties, geographies, and deal structures until the investigation and compliance review establish that controls are credible.

This recommendation does not depend on claiming that a pause is certainly cheaper than continued expansion. The case packet does not provide enough evidence to quantify that tradeoff. It does show, however, that the board is facing a live export-controls issue serious enough to trigger indictments tied to former personnel, a public March 19, 2026 disclosure, and then a formal April 7, 2026 independent investigation led by independent directors with Munger, Tolles & Olson LLP. That combination means the board is operating under material uncertainty about whether the matter was isolated misconduct or evidence of broader control weakness.

In that setting, the most defensible course is not a full stop and not full-speed expansion. It is a selective reset that preserves compliant revenue while reducing exposure in the areas most likely to deepen regulatory, governance, customer, or supplier consequences if additional adverse facts emerge. This is especially important because Fortune reported that the allegations involve roughly $2.5B in Nvidia-GPU servers allegedly routed to China and that investors are concerned the matter could strain Supermicro's Nvidia relationship. Even if the company is not named as a defendant and is not accused of wrongdoing, the board still has to manage counterparty confidence while facts are incomplete.

The strongest counterargument is real: AI infrastructure demand is time-sensitive, and narrowing activity could cause customers to shift orders to rivals. That risk is concrete and should not be minimized. But the board does not need to choose between surrendering growth and ignoring the control question. A targeted reset is superior because it keeps low-risk business moving while avoiding the harder-to-reverse mistake of expanding aggressively in channels that may later prove problematic.

The board should therefore authorize a 60-90 day targeted compliance reset focused on high-risk activity. Management should keep shipping to low-risk customers and jurisdictions, impose enhanced approval and end-use review for sensitive transactions, intensify channel-partner screening, and elevate compliance visibility to the board for the duration of the investigation. The objective is to protect the AI franchise by proving control integrity before resuming full-speed expansion.

## Decision Frame

Recommendation: pause and narrow high-risk activity, not overall company operations, until the investigation and compliance reset produce enough evidence to support broader expansion.

Why this is the better choice:
- The company has already escalated the issue to a board-led independent investigation with outside counsel, which indicates that business-as-usual assumptions are not yet justified.
- The board does not yet know whether the alleged misconduct was isolated or symptomatic of broader channel or control failures.
- A selective reset preserves low-risk revenue while reducing exposure to the areas most likely to create additional fallout if new facts emerge.
- The company can credibly defend a narrow, evidence-based operating posture more easily than a full-speed stance built on facts not yet established.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- Whether the alleged misconduct was isolated to the indicted individuals or reflected broader process failures.
- Which specific channels, counterparties, and geographies create the highest export-controls risk.
- Whether Nvidia or major customers have already imposed informal constraints, added oversight, or altered demand plans.
- How much near-term revenue is actually at risk under a targeted pause versus a full-speed strategy.
- Whether the acting Chief Compliance Officer's current reporting line to the General Counsel is sufficient during the review period.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if:
- The board can ringfence high-risk transactions without materially disrupting compliant core demand.
- Interim investigation findings support a differentiated risk posture by channel, geography, and deal type.
- Management can demonstrate tighter screening, escalation, and audit controls within 60-90 days.
- Key suppliers and major customers remain engaged during the reset.

Fail if:
- Management continues business as usual in areas where destination, end-use, or intermediary risk remains unresolved.
- The company relies mainly on the fact that it is not named as a defendant rather than proving control effectiveness.
- The reset is symbolic and does not change approval rights, monitoring, or enforcement.
- New facts emerge showing broader awareness or systemic weakness after the board chose an aggressive expansion posture.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A board compliance-reset plan covering:
- channel and geography risk segmentation
- interim approval matrix for sensitive transactions
- supplier and customer communications
- 30/60/90-day remediation milestones
- explicit triggers for resuming broader growth activity
