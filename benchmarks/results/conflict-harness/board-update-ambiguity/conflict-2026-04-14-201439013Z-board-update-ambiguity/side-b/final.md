# Revised artifact

## Board Strategy Update

### Current Assessment
The central fact pattern is not weak performance or strong performance; it is decision-relevant ambiguity. Management does not yet have enough evidence in the packet to prove durable demand, efficient repeatable growth, or scalable operating readiness. Under those conditions, the board should treat this as a governance decision about what evidence must exist before authorizing irreversible scale, not as a confidence-based bet.

### Strategic Recommendation
Adopt a time-bounded staged execution posture for the next review window rather than approve broad scaling immediately. This is not a recommendation to wait passively. It is a recommendation to keep investing in learning, delivery, and the highest-signal commercial motions while withholding structurally hard-to-reverse commitments until management can show predefined evidence against explicit gates.

The recommendation is conditional, not absolute. If management can clear the gates below inside the next operating window, the board should be prepared to accelerate. If not, the board should narrow scope or hold investment. The strategy value comes from converting ambiguity into a governed decision, not from default caution.

## Decision Frame
The board decision is whether to:
1. authorize immediate aggressive scaling with incomplete evidence, or
2. authorize a staged plan with explicit triggers for accelerate, continue, or retrench.

Recommendation: choose staged execution for one defined review period, because the case packet contains no operating evidence strong enough to justify irreversible scale today, and because delay also has a cost that should be measured rather than ignored.

Board instruction to management for the next review period:
1. quantify the cost of waiting versus the cost of premature scale,
2. report baseline values for the core gates below,
3. return with a scale/no-scale recommendation tied to those gates.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
The missing evidence that most affects the decision is:
1. demand durability: whether recent demand is repeatable or episodic,
2. retention or repeat usage quality: whether customers are sustaining value after initial adoption,
3. unit economics trajectory: whether acquisition, expansion, and servicing economics improve with repetition rather than deteriorate,
4. execution capacity: whether operations can absorb growth without margin damage or service degradation,
5. cost of delay: whether waiting one operating window materially increases competitive risk, customer loss, or team attrition.

These gaps are decisive because either error is expensive: scaling too early can lock in bad economics, while waiting too long can forfeit timing advantages. The current packet does not let the board compare those risks with confidence.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
For the next board meeting, management should present explicit thresholds, current baseline, trend line, and owner for each gate. Minimum structure:
1. demand: stable or improving results across a defined multi-period window,
2. retention: cohort behavior that shows sustained value beyond initial adoption,
3. economics: unit economics at or above the board-approved floor, with trend improvement rather than one-off performance,
4. capacity: service levels and delivery performance remaining within acceptable bounds under growth,
5. cost of waiting: quantified estimate of lost pipeline, competitive exposure, or execution risk from deferral.

Pass: management shows baselines, thresholds, and trend evidence across the review window, plus a quantified case that accelerating now has higher expected value than continued staging.

Fail: management cannot establish baselines, cannot quantify the cost of waiting, or shows mixed signals that rely on narrative rather than trend evidence.

## Recommended Next Artifact
Produce a board decision memo for the next meeting with four tables:
1. baseline metrics and target thresholds for each gate,
2. trend data for the defined review window,
3. quantified downside of premature scale versus quantified downside of waiting,
4. recommendation across three paths: accelerate, continue staged execution, or retrench.

That memo should force the next decision to be evidence-backed and falsifiable rather than rhetorical.
