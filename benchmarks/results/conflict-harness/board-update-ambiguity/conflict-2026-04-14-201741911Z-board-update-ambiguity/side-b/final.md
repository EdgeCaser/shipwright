# Revised artifact

## Current Position
The board does not yet have enough evidence to support a clean accelerate-or-retrench decision. That does not make delay automatically prudent. Under ambiguity, both premature scaling and passive waiting can destroy value. The decision is therefore conditional: authorize a tightly bounded validation phase only if the company has enough runway, strategic relevance, and market time to learn faster than conditions are deteriorating.

## What Has Changed
The central update is not a clearer market signal but a clearer decision standard. The prior framing overstated the case for validation as if it were inherently conservative. It is not. A validation window is justified only when the cost of delay is lower than the cost of committing capital against unresolved assumptions. If runway is short, competitive compression is rising, or the market window is closing quickly, the board should force a sharper choice sooner.

## Board-Level Recommendation
Authorize a 60-90 day bounded validation phase only under the following preconditions:
1. The company has sufficient liquidity and organizational stability to complete the window without creating an avoidable financing or execution crisis.
2. Management can instrument the core assumptions immediately and report against explicit thresholds.
3. There is no evidence that the market window will close faster than the learning cycle.

If any of those conditions are false, the board should not default to more validation; it should instead choose an earlier reset, narrower wedge, or selective acceleration based on the strongest available signal.

## Decision Frame
The decision is not simply whether to wait. It is whether a short validation period is more value-maximizing than immediate commitment or immediate retrenchment. Recommendation: approve a bounded validation phase as the default path only because the present record supports ambiguity, not because validation is universally safer. This recommendation should be reversed if runway is constrained, opportunity cost of delay is high, or management cannot define falsifiable gates now.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
The board is missing the specific facts required to determine whether delay is prudent or dangerous:
- Runway and burn tolerance: how many months of decision latitude actually exist.
- Competitive timing: whether rivals are gaining share, distribution, or category position during the proposed window.
- Opportunity cost of delay: what revenue, partnerships, hiring, or category credibility may be lost by waiting.
- Demand quality: whether observed interest converts repeatably enough to justify scaling.
- Economic viability: whether unit economics improve or worsen with added activity.
- Bottleneck diagnosis: whether the core constraint is product, positioning, channel execution, or market timing.

Without those facts, the board should treat any recommendation as conditional rather than definitive.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
A validation phase is board-ready only if management commits now to explicit gates. Minimum pass/fail standard:
- Define 3-5 critical assumptions, each with a numeric metric, baseline, target threshold, and decision owner.
- Set a fixed review date within 60-90 days and prohibit extending the window without board approval.
- Specify in advance what results trigger `accelerate`, `hold/narrow`, or `reset`.
- Include at least one liquidity guardrail and one market-timing guardrail.

The board should fail the validation plan immediately if thresholds remain qualitative, if management cannot show how learning will occur inside one operating cycle, or if delay risk cannot be bounded credibly.

## Recommended Next Artifact
The next artifact should be a board validation memo with:
- current runway, burn, and minimum cash guardrail,
- 3-5 core assumptions with numeric thresholds,
- explicit competitor or market-window watchpoints,
- experiments and owners for each assumption,
- a pre-committed decision table for `accelerate`, `hold/narrow`, and `reset`,
- the exact board date when the company must make the next irreversible decision.

That memo should make the recommendation falsifiable. If management cannot produce it, the board should assume the organization is not ready for a validation phase and should force a narrower or more defensive plan instead.
