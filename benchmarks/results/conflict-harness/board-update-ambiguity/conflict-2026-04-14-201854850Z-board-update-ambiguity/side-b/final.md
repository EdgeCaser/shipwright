# Revised artifact

## Board Strategy Update Under Ambiguity

### Current View
Management does not yet have evidence sufficient to justify either a full acceleration decision or a full defensive pause. The board-relevant judgment is therefore not which narrative feels more confident, but which interim posture creates the best next decision with bounded downside.

The recommended posture is a controlled advance for the next 60-90 days: continue only the work that materially reduces decision uncertainty, cap irreversible commitments, and require explicit triggers for accelerate/hold/reduce at the next review. This recommendation is not based on a proven claim that over-scaling is always riskier than delay. It is based on a narrower governance judgment: in the absence of evidence on both scaling risk and cost of delay, the most decision-useful posture is the one that preserves option value while forcing rapid evidence generation.

### Strategic Assessment
The present ambiguity has two implications.

First, the missing information is decision-critical, not merely informational. Until management identifies which assumptions would actually change the capital allocation decision, board discussion will stay too abstract to be useful.

Second, the current record does not support a confident risk asymmetry claim in either direction. We do not have evidence here showing that premature scaling is definitely more dangerous than waiting, and we do not have evidence showing that delay would definitely close a competitive window. That means the board should avoid treating either posture as self-evidently correct.

Under those conditions, the most defensible interim strategy is to make the next 60-90 days answerable. Management should be required to convert ambiguity into a short list of explicit tests, owners, thresholds, and exposure limits.

### Recommended Near-Term Posture
Approve a controlled advance only if it is run as a bounded experiment, not as a soft continuation of business as usual.

Management should do four things in the next 60-90 days:

1. Name the top three to five assumptions that would change the strategy decision.
2. Define the evidence that would support accelerate, hold, or reduce for each assumption.
3. Cap commitments that are hard to unwind before those thresholds are met.
4. Surface any cost-of-delay indicators explicitly, including lost pipeline, competitive displacement, hiring risk, or partner slippage.

This makes the recommendation falsifiable. If cost-of-delay indicators rise faster than learning, the case for acceleration strengthens. If evidence remains weak and exposure rises, the case for retrenchment strengthens.

## Decision Frame
The board is not deciding whether the strategy is correct in full. The board is deciding what interim posture is most likely to produce a higher-quality next decision without taking hidden exposure.

Recommendation: authorize a 60-90 day controlled advance with explicit evidence gates, capped irreversible spend, and a mandatory readout on both validation progress and cost-of-delay signals.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
The critical gap is not general uncertainty; it is the absence of explicit evidence on the few assumptions that would actually change the decision. The board also lacks a quantified view of cost of delay versus cost of premature commitment. Until both are made explicit, neither aggressive scaling nor a full pause is well-supported.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass: management returns with a ranked assumption set, measurable thresholds, exposure limits, and a clear read on whether cost-of-delay is increasing or remaining tolerable.

Fail: management uses the period to continue broad activity without isolating decisive assumptions, cannot show what evidence changed, or cannot explain whether delay itself is now becoming the dominant risk.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A board decision memo with four sections: the top decision-critical assumptions, evidence collected against each, quantified cost-of-delay indicators, and the trigger table for accelerate/hold/reduce at the next board review.
