# Strategy Update: Board Briefing Under Ambiguity

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Given material uncertainty in our operating environment, what commitments can the board ratify today, and which decisions must be deferred until specific evidence thresholds are met?

**Context:** This update is written under conditions where key inputs to our strategic plan remain unresolved. Rather than present false precision, this document separates what we know from what we don't, identifies the decisions that are robust across scenarios, and flags the decisions that depend on information we do not yet have.

**Framing principle:** The board's role in this cycle is not to approve a plan but to approve a decision framework — the criteria under which management will act when ambiguity resolves.

### What is decidable now
- **Resource allocation floor:** Minimum investment levels required to maintain optionality across all plausible scenarios. These commitments are regret-minimizing regardless of how uncertainty resolves.
- **Kill criteria:** Conditions under which we discontinue current initiatives, preventing drift into sunk-cost continuation.
- **Monitoring cadence:** Agreement on the review triggers (not just calendar dates) that bring decisions back to the board.

### What is not decidable now
- Full-year capital allocation above the floor, pending resolution of unknowns listed below.
- Market entry timing for expansion initiatives, pending competitive and regulatory signal clarity.
- Organizational restructuring scope, pending demand trajectory confirmation.

**Evidence discipline note:** Each unknown above has a defined resolution path with a concrete deliverable, not an open-ended "monitor the situation." If the resolution path fails to produce clarity by the target date, that failure itself is a decision input — we default to the conservative scenario.

### Cost-of-Commitment vs. Cost-of-Delay Analysis (Evidence Gap — Flagged)

The directional case for a framework approach over premature commitment rests on a cost comparison that **has not yet been quantified**. Before the board ratifies this framework, CFO should present:

1. **Carrying cost of optionality:** Monthly burn above steady-state during the 60–90 day investigation period (investigation budgets for U1–U4, talent retention costs of ambiguity, competitive position erosion rate).
2. **Expected cost of wrong commitment:** Probability-weighted downside for each plausible misallocation, using scenario-specific estimates (e.g., sunk capital if U2 resolves unfavorably, margin compression if U3 triggers price war).
3. **Opportunity cost of delay:** Revenue or market-share forgone by not acting during the optionality window, benchmarked against competitor movement timelines.
4. **Cash runway validation:** Confirmation that current reserves sustain operations + investigation costs through the full 90-day window without triggering covenant or liquidity concerns.

**Management's directional judgment** is that wrong-commitment cost exceeds optionality-carrying cost based on the magnitude of capital at risk relative to investigation budgets. This judgment is informed by experience but is not yet backed by a financial model. The Scenario Decision Matrix (see Recommended Next Artifact) should include this quantification as a prerequisite deliverable within 14 days, so the board can verify the assumption before investigation budgets are fully deployed.

**Bottom line:** We have alignment on *how* to decide but not *what* to decide. The directional case for structured optionality over premature commitment is sound, but the board should treat it as a management recommendation requiring CFO validation — not a demonstrated fact. The responsible action is to provisionally approve the decision framework, authorize the cost-comparison analysis as a 14-day gating deliverable, fund the evidence-gathering workstreams, and reconvene when triggers are met.

### Risk of premature commitment
Committing to a full plan now would require assuming answers to U1–U4. Management's judgment is that the expected cost of being wrong on any single assumption exceeds the carrying cost of maintaining optionality for 60–90 days, but this remains unquantified. The cost-comparison deliverable will either confirm this judgment or force an earlier commitment decision.

### Risk of excessive delay
Beyond the 90-day window, optionality cost increases as competitors may establish positions and internal talent retention becomes harder without clear direction. The framework includes hard deadlines that force a decision even under residual ambiguity.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Description:** A structured document mapping each combination of resolved unknowns (U1–U4) to a specific strategic action, pre-approved by the board. When evidence arrives, management executes the corresponding row without requiring a new board cycle.

**Contents:**
1. **Cost-comparison model (14-day gating deliverable):** Quantified carrying cost of optionality vs. probability-weighted cost of premature commitment, validating the framework approach before full investigation budgets deploy

3. Pre-committed resource allocation for each quadrant
4. Trigger definitions: what specific evidence moves us from one scenario to another
5. Escalation criteria: which scenario combinations require emergency board review vs. management discretion
6. Timeline: 14/30/60/90-day checkpoints with specific deliverables at each (cost model at day 14; capacity audit at day 30; competitive read at day 45; demand study at day 60; regulatory resolution at day 90)

**Owner:** CEO + CFO, with strategy team support

**Board action requested:** Provisionally approve this framework approach, authorize investigation budgets for U1–U4 resolution paths contingent on CFO delivering the cost-comparison validation within 14 days, and delegate scenario-conditional execution authority to management within pre-agreed parameters.
