# Strategy Update: Board Decision Under Ambiguity

## Situation

We are operating with insufficient evidence to make a high-confidence strategic commitment today. The appropriate board posture is to avoid a false binary between full approval and delay. The recommended path is a gated strategy decision: authorize a short evidence-building phase, preserve strategic optionality, and define explicit pass/fail thresholds before committing meaningful capital, headcount, or reputational exposure.

## Decision Frame

The board decision is not whether the strategy is definitively correct. The decision is whether the current uncertainty profile justifies continued investment to reach a sharper decision point.

Recommended decision: approve a bounded validation phase rather than a full-scale strategic commitment.

Decision to make now:

- Authorize a focused validation window with a named executive owner.
- Limit investment to learning activities that reduce the largest decision risks.
- Require a return-to-board checkpoint before material scale-up.
- Define in advance what evidence would cause the company to proceed, pivot, pause, or kill the initiative.

Rationale:

- The current case packet provides the artifact request and validator requirements, but no market, customer, financial, operational, or competitive evidence.
- A board-ready strategy under these conditions should be explicit about uncertainty rather than imply confidence the evidence does not support.
- The highest-value board contribution is to clarify decision thresholds, risk appetite, and governance cadence.

## Strategic Posture

The right posture is disciplined exploration.

This means:

- Do not overcommit resources before the core assumptions are tested.
- Do not defer indefinitely while waiting for perfect information.
- Convert ambiguity into a sequence of falsifiable assumptions.
- Treat speed as useful only if paired with clear stop conditions.

The strategy should advance only if the validation phase produces evidence on three fronts: customer demand, economic attractiveness, and execution feasibility.

## Key Assumptions To Validate

1. Customer need exists and is urgent enough to change behavior.
2. The target segment is identifiable, reachable, and large enough to justify investment.
3. The company has or can build a credible right to win.
4. Unit economics can support the desired business model.
5. Execution risk is manageable within existing organizational capacity.
6. Competitive response will not erase the advantage before the company can scale.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

Current evidence gaps are material.

- Customer evidence: no stated customer interviews, usage data, churn signals, willingness-to-pay data, or demand indicators are available in the packet.
- Market evidence: no TAM/SAM/SOM, growth rate, segment definition, or market timing evidence is provided.
- Competitive evidence: no competitor set, substitution behavior, differentiation claim, or right-to-win analysis is provided.
- Financial evidence: no revenue model, margin profile, CAC, payback period, investment envelope, or downside exposure is provided.
- Execution evidence: no resourcing plan, dependency map, delivery timeline, technical feasibility assessment, or operating risk view is provided.
- Governance evidence: no current owner, milestone cadence, or escalation mechanism is provided.

Implication for the board: any confident recommendation to scale would exceed the available evidence. The board can still make a useful decision by approving a limited validation path with explicit criteria.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

The initiative is not ready for full-scale approval. It can be ready for a bounded validation phase if leadership agrees to the following gates.

Pass criteria for continued investment:

- Customer signal: direct evidence from the target segment shows a painful, frequent, and budget-relevant problem.
- Demand signal: prospects or current customers demonstrate behavior stronger than stated interest, such as pilots, paid commitments, expansion intent, or measurable usage.
- Economic signal: preliminary unit economics show a plausible path to attractive gross margin, acquisition efficiency, and payback.
- Strategic signal: the company can articulate a credible advantage that is not easily neutralized by incumbents or substitutes.
- Execution signal: leadership can identify the team, timeline, dependencies, and trade-offs required to pursue the opportunity.

Fail criteria requiring pause or kill:

- Customer urgency is weak or diffuse.
- The target segment is too broad to support focused go-to-market execution.
- Willingness to pay does not support the required investment level.
- Competitive differentiation depends on claims the company cannot substantiate.
- Delivery requires resources that would materially impair higher-priority commitments.
- The validation phase produces mostly anecdotal interest without behavioral evidence.

Current readiness assessment: proceed only to validation, not scale.

## Recommended Next Artifact

Recommended next artifact: a board-ready Validation Plan.

It should include:

- The strategic question the validation phase will answer.
- The top five assumptions ranked by decision risk.
- The evidence required to pass, pivot, pause, or kill.
- A customer research plan with target segment criteria.
- A lightweight market and competitive scan.
- A preliminary financial model with sensitivity ranges.
- Resourcing requirements and opportunity costs.
- A return-to-board date with a decision memo format.

Suggested board ask:

Approve a time-boxed validation phase and require management to return with a decision memo that either supports investment, recommends a narrower wedge, or terminates the initiative based on predefined evidence thresholds.
