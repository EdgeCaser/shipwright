# Revised artifact

## Decision Frame

The board should not treat ambiguity itself as a reason to delay. The decision is whether the available company evidence is strong enough to justify a scale-up commitment now, or whether the next step should be a bounded validation phase.

Because the materials provided for this exercise include no company-specific financials, customer evidence, pipeline data, competitive intelligence, operating constraints, or regulatory timing, this update cannot conclude that the company lacks evidence. It can only conclude that the record available here is insufficient to support an unconditional scale-up recommendation.

Recommended board posture: authorize decisive action only if management can show that the opportunity meets a clear evidence threshold and that delay carries a material cost. Otherwise, approve a bounded validation phase with explicit gates.

Decisive action is justified despite remaining uncertainty if at least three conditions are true:

1. Customer or partner demand is time-sensitive and tied to near-term commitments, not general interest.
2. The company has an identifiable advantage that would erode materially if action is delayed.
3. The downside of waiting exceeds the cost of a controlled execution bet.
4. The financial model remains acceptable under conservative assumptions.
5. The operating plan names the tradeoffs required to fund the initiative without silently weakening higher-priority commitments.

If those conditions are not met, the board should approve a validation plan rather than a scaled commitment.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

The evidence gap is not necessarily that the company lacks evidence. The gap is that the current record does not expose enough evidence to distinguish productive caution from costly delay.

Highest-priority unknowns:

1. Customer urgency: whether target customers or partners have near-term reasons to act and credible willingness to commit.
2. Timing pressure: whether there is a closing competitive, regulatory, distribution, or capital-market window.
3. Economic quality: whether margin, payback, retention, and expansion assumptions remain attractive in the downside case.
4. Competitive durability: whether the company has a specific advantage that matters in the buying decision and can survive response from incumbents or fast followers.
5. Execution tradeoffs: what must be deprioritized, delayed, or funded to pursue the initiative at meaningful scale.

Minimum evidence needed before a scale-up decision:

1. Named customer, partner, or pipeline evidence showing urgency and intent.
2. A quantified opportunity model with base, downside, and upside cases.
3. A timing analysis showing what is lost by waiting 30, 60, or 90 days.
4. A competitive analysis tied to buying criteria and defensibility.
5. A resourcing plan with explicit tradeoffs against the current operating plan.
6. A downside case showing cost, distraction, and reversibility if the initiative fails.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

This strategy is not ready for unconditional scale-up approval based on the current record. It may be ready for approval if management can present decision-grade evidence against the thresholds below.

Pass criteria for immediate scale-up:

1. At least one priority customer segment shows repeated high-urgency demand or signed/near-signed commitments.
2. Delay would create a concrete loss, such as forfeiting a partner window, allowing a competitor to lock distribution, missing a regulatory transition, or losing an economically advantaged capital opportunity.
3. The downside financial case remains within a board-approved loss limit.
4. Differentiation is specific, buyer-relevant, and operationally defensible.
5. Required resources and roadmap tradeoffs are explicit and acceptable.

Pass criteria for bounded validation instead:

1. The opportunity is strategically plausible but urgency is not yet proven.
2. The economic model is sensitive to assumptions that can be tested quickly.
3. Competitive advantage is credible but not yet validated in buying conversations.
4. The next 30 to 90 days can materially reduce uncertainty at contained cost.

Fail criteria:

1. Customer interest is broad but shallow, with no evidence of urgency or willingness to pay.
2. The business case only works under best-case adoption, pricing, retention, or timing assumptions.
3. Differentiation depends on claims the company cannot defend or operationalize.
4. The initiative would materially distract from higher-confidence priorities without a compensating timing advantage.
5. Management cannot specify what evidence would change the decision.

Board readiness assessment: approve immediate scale-up only if the pass criteria for decisive action are met. Otherwise, approve a bounded validation phase and require management to return with evidence against the agreed thresholds before requesting additional capital, headcount, or strategic commitment.

## Recommended Next Artifact

The next artifact should be a board-ready Strategy Decision Memo.

It should include:

1. The specific board ask: scale now, validate, pivot, or stop.
2. The top five assumptions that must be true for the strategy to work.
3. A 30-60-90 day validation or execution plan with owners, cost, and evidence outputs.
4. A financial model with base, downside, and upside cases.
5. A timing-pressure analysis that names the cost of delay.
6. A decision gate defining continue, accelerate, pivot, or stop conditions.

Recommended board ask: do not default to delay. Require management to show whether the evidence supports decisive action now. If it does, approve the smallest irreversible commitment needed to preserve the opportunity. If it does not, approve a bounded validation plan with measurable gates.
