## Strategy Update: Board Decision Under Ambiguity

### Decision Frame
Board action requested: approve a limited learning phase, not a scale commitment.

The constraint is not that the opportunity looks weak; it is that management has not yet translated uncertainty into decision-grade specifics. On the current record, the board should authorize only the minimum spend and operating scope required to answer the few questions that would actually change posture at the next review.

Recommended board stance:
- continue, but only in a gated mode
- prohibit irreversible expansion in headcount, capital, or external commitments before the next review
- require management to return with a threshold-based memo that names the specific assumptions, owners, measures, and decision triggers

The practical decision is therefore not "whether to act" but "whether to scale before the governing assumptions are made explicit." The recommended answer is no.

### What Is Actually Missing
The prior update overstated confidence in a generic uncertainty taxonomy. The board does not yet have evidence of the concrete unknowns that govern this case. What is missing is a ranked list of decision-critical assumptions in three forms:
- demand assumption: which customer, use case, or revenue source must prove true for the strategy to justify scaling
- economics assumption: which cost, margin, or payback variable must stay within bounds for the strategy to remain attractive
- execution assumption: which delivery constraint, dependency, or operating bottleneck could break the plan even if demand is real

Timing should not be treated as a standalone bucket unless management can show a specific window that creates or destroys value. Without that specificity, "timing risk" is only a hypothesis, not a board-level claim.

### Near-Term Direction
Management should spend the next cycle converting ambiguity into a decision set, not broadening the plan. That means:
- identify the top 3 assumptions most likely to flip the recommendation
- define one measurable test for each assumption
- state the scale, hold, and stop trigger attached to each test
- cap exposure until those triggers are observed

This preserves optionality while forcing clarity. It also prevents a common failure mode in ambiguous programs: accumulating cost and narrative commitment faster than evidence.

### Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
The evidence gaps are not "all possible uncertainties." They are the missing specifics the board needs to judge scale readiness:
- the single most important demand assumption has not been named or measured
- the economic threshold for acceptable scaling has not been stated
- the main execution bottleneck has not been identified with an owner and mitigation path
- no ranked view exists of which unknown would most likely change the board's posture

Until those four items are explicit, the board cannot distinguish disciplined staging from passive drift.

### Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass for staged continuation if, by next review, management can show:
- the top 3 decision-governing assumptions, ranked by importance
- one measurable result for each assumption
- explicit thresholds for scale, hold, and stop
- a bounded exposure plan showing what is being risked before proof arrives

Fail for scale readiness if, by next review, management still presents:
- generic uncertainty labels instead of named assumptions
- confidence statements without thresholds
- requests for expanded spend or scope without evidence tied to a trigger

Current readiness: pass for a bounded learning phase; fail for full-scale commitment.

### Recommended Next Artifact
A board decision memo.

It should contain:
- the 3 ranked assumptions that govern the strategy
- the evidence collected so far against each one
- the test plan and owner for each remaining gap
- explicit scale, hold, and stop triggers
- the capital and organizational exposure under each path

That is the minimum artifact required to convert ambiguity into a board-actionable decision.
