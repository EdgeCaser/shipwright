Under partial market data, the objective is not to find the theoretically optimal price now; it is to make the next pricing move safely, learn quickly, and avoid irreversible demand damage.\n\n## Decision Frame\n- Decision: pursue a measured pricing increase through a controlled experiment, not a portfolio-wide reset.\n- Why now: current information is sufficient to justify learning-oriented action, but insufficient to support a one-way strategic pricing commit.\n- Guardrails: protect renewal risk, isolate effects by segment, and require predefined rollback thresholds.\n- Operating principle: optimize for decision quality under uncertainty, not for apparent confidence.\n\n## Approach\n1. Apply the pricing change first to the lowest-risk segment: new customers or a clearly bounded cohort.\n2. Keep packaging stable unless there is direct evidence that packaging, not price level, is the primary problem.\n3. Test one core variable at a time: headline price, discount policy, or feature gate.\n4. Define success on a contribution basis, not just top-line ARPU: conversion quality, gross retention, and payback all matter.\n5. Preserve comparability by running the test long enough to observe early retention and sales-cycle effects.\n\n## Rationale\n- Partial market data supports directional movement, not precision pricing.\n- A staged test creates real willingness-to-pay evidence faster than waiting for perfect external benchmarks.\n- Pricing errors are asymmetric: overpricing can damage pipeline and retention than an aggressive step-change.\n- Exempt current contracted customers unless retention risk is already understood.\n- Track funnel progression, win rate, discounting behavior, sales objections,
