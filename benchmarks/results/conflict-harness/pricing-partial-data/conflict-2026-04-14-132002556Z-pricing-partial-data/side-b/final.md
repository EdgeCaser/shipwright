# Revised artifact

## Recommendation
Use a time-boxed, two-track pricing move rather than either a full repricing or indefinite waiting.

Track 1 starts immediately: tighten discounting, clean up packaging, and prepare a premium-tier offer where value differentiation is already credible. Track 2 runs in parallel for 30 days: collect the minimum evidence needed to decide whether to expand to a broader price change. Do not execute an across-the-board list-price increase until that gate is cleared.

This is not a claim that delay is free. It is a claim that, under a case packet with partial market data and no supporting evidence, the least-regret posture is to capture low-regret monetization upside now while explicitly bounding the cost of waiting with a short decision window.

## Decision Frame

### Objective
Increase monetization while controlling for avoidable conversion and churn damage under severe evidence gaps.

### Decision
1. Do immediately: reduce discretionary discount leakage, simplify packaging where confusion is evident, and define a premium offer only if differentiated value already exists.
2. Do not do yet: a universal list-price increase across the installed base or all new business.
3. Commit now to a 30-day evidence sprint with a hard decision gate at the end.
4. At the gate, choose one of three paths: broaden price change, continue targeted testing, or hold price and focus on packaging/retention instead.

### Why This Is Decision-Useful Under Partial Data
- The case provides no direct evidence on willingness to pay, competitive price position, segmentation, or baseline funnel economics, so a broad repricing would be a high-irreversibility move made without support.
- Waiting also has a cost, so the recommendation is not open-ended deferral. It pairs immediate no-regret monetization actions with a short, precommitted timetable for a harder pricing decision.
- This structure preserves upside if the company is underpriced while limiting the downside of discovering too late that demand is more price-sensitive than assumed.

### Cost Of Waiting vs. Cost Of Acting
- Cost of waiting: foregone near-term revenue, slower learning, and possible competitive anchoring.
- Cost of acting broadly now: harder-to-reverse conversion loss, retention damage, sales friction, and credibility loss if pricing has to be rolled back.
- Under the current evidence state, the right comparison is not action versus inaction. It is reversible action now versus irreversible action now. The recommendation favors reversible action.

### Practical Plan
- Week 1: quantify current discount leakage, define premium fences, and confirm whether cohorts can be isolated.
- Weeks 2-4: run limited pricing or packaging tests on new-logo traffic, selected segments, or renewal cohorts.
- Day 30 gate: broaden only if uplift exceeds operational cost and churn/conversion stay inside pre-set guardrails.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- No customer willingness-to-pay evidence is provided.
- No competitor pricing evidence is provided.
- No baseline conversion, churn, expansion, or discount leakage metrics are provided.
- No segment-level view is provided to show where price sensitivity is lowest.
- No evidence is provided that instrumentation and cohort isolation are operationally ready today.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

### Pass Conditions
- The team can measure baseline conversion, churn, expansion, and discount leakage.
- At least one reversible test cohort can be isolated.
- Premium-tier differentiation is real enough to defend in-market.
- A 30-day decision gate, success thresholds, and rollback rules are agreed in advance.

### Fail Conditions
- The business cannot isolate any cohort or measure outcomes reliably.
- Premium differentiation is too weak, making tiering a disguised price hike.
- Leadership needs immediate revenue relief and refuses a bounded test period.
- Operational cost of testing exceeds the likely value of the learning.

### Readiness Verdict
Pass for a bounded two-track strategy now. Fail for an immediate broad repricing now. If minimum measurement capability is absent, the fallback is not indefinite delay; it is an urgent instrumentation and discount-control sprint with a fixed executive review date.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A pricing experiment plan with three explicit outputs: immediate no-regret actions, 30-day evidence requirements, and the gate criteria for expand/hold/stop.
