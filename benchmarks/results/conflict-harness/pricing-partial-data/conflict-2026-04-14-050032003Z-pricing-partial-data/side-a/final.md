# Revised artifact

## Decision Frame
Recommend a staged, packaging-led pricing change rather than a broad repricing: keep the current base plan stable in the near term, introduce a premium tier only if it is clearly differentiated, and use a controlled pricing test to determine the right premium before any wider catalog change. Under partial market data, the decision should optimize for reversibility and learning rate, not false precision.

Decision logic:
- Avoid an across-the-board increase until elasticity, competitive position, and willingness to pay are better evidenced.
- Use packaging before pricing where possible so the monetization event is tied to clearer customer value.
- Treat premium-tier pricing as a test variable, not a fixed recommendation, until segment response is observed.
- Grandfather existing customers for a fixed period to reduce churn risk and preserve trust.
- Define success on upgrade conversion, net revenue retention impact, and win-rate or churn stability.

Suggested first-pass operating plan:
- Current plan: unchanged for existing customers and as the default low-risk entry point.
- New premium tier: launched only with clearly bounded differentiated capabilities.
- Price test: evaluate a small set of premium price points by segment or channel rather than committing to a single uplift assumption.
- Enterprise/custom deals: preserve negotiation room until test data clarifies acceptable pricing bands.
- Review window: 30-45 days for initial conversion signal, 60-90 days for retention and expansion effects.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
The recommendation is constrained by missing market inputs. The key unknowns are:
- Competitive price benchmarks by segment and package level.
- Customer willingness to pay by persona, company size, and problem urgency.
- Historical elasticity from prior pricing, discounting, or packaging changes.
- Margin sensitivity and support or delivery cost by tier.
- Churn risk for legacy customers exposed to new packaging boundaries.

Evidence gaps that most affect the decision:
- Whether current pricing is below market, at market, or already stretched.
- Whether premium features are strong enough to support a higher anchor.
- Whether buyers perceive enough differentiation between tiers.
- Which test price points are plausible enough to learn quickly without distorting demand.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass for immediate execution if:
- The team can launch a premium tier or controlled pricing test without billing-system disruption.
- Existing customers can be grandfathered or contractually insulated.
- Core metrics are instrumented for conversion, churn, expansion, discount rate, and sales win rate.
- Product and GTM teams agree on feature boundaries and positioning.
- The team can define explicit stop/go thresholds for the pricing test.

Fail for broad repricing now if:
- Billing or entitlement systems cannot support segmented rollout.
- There is no reliable way to measure conversion and retention by cohort.
- The proposed premium tier lacks clearly differentiated value.
- The business is currently exposed to churn or pipeline fragility that cannot absorb pricing risk.
- Leadership requires a precise new list price before the missing market evidence is gathered.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A pricing strategy brief with:
- Tier design and packaging logic.
- Test design with candidate price points, segment splits, and decision thresholds.
- Success metrics and stop/go criteria.
- Competitive benchmark table.
- Customer research plan for willingness-to-pay validation.
