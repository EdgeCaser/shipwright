# Pricing Strategy Recommendation Under Partial Market Data

## Recommendation
Adopt a controlled, reversible pricing change rather than a full repricing. Raise list price modestly for new customers only, keep existing customers grandfathered for one renewal cycle, and pair the change with simplified packaging rather than a broad feature rebundle. This is the highest-confidence move available under partial market data because it creates learning while capping downside.

Rationale: the case provides no direct market evidence, competitor pricing, elasticity data, win/loss data, or customer-segment willingness-to-pay inputs. In that condition, the decision should optimize for error containment, observability, and speed of correction rather than theoretical revenue maximization. [ctx-1]

## Recommended Pricing Posture
- Increase headline price in a narrow band: roughly 5-10% for new sales only.
- Avoid changing all plans at once; apply the change to the default plan/package customers most commonly evaluate.
- Hold discount policy steady so list-price impact can be observed cleanly.
- Preserve existing contract terms for current customers until renewal to reduce churn risk.
- Set an explicit rollback trigger before launch.

## Why This Is Decision-Useful Now
A small, new-logo-only increase is preferable to either freezing pricing indefinitely or executing a large reset.

- Freezing pricing leaves potential value uncaptured and teaches nothing.
- A large increase without market evidence creates asymmetric downside through conversion loss, longer sales cycles, and avoidable churn pressure.
- A measured change produces real demand data quickly and can be expanded, held, or reversed with limited damage. [ctx-1]

## Decision Frame
The decision is not "what is the perfect price"; it is "what pricing move best balances revenue opportunity and downside risk when market evidence is incomplete." The recommended choice is a limited price increase with strict monitoring and pre-committed rollback criteria.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- No competitor price benchmark is available, so relative positioning risk is unknown.
- No elasticity or conversion data is available, so acceptable price ceiling is unknown.
- No segment-level willingness-to-pay evidence is available, so uniform pricing may overcharge weak-fit segments or undercharge strong-fit ones.
- No churn or renewal sensitivity data is available, so current-customer repricing risk is unknown.
- No information is provided on product differentiation, sales-led vs self-serve motion, or contract length, all of which materially affect pricing strategy. [ctx-1]

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if:
- The organization can isolate new-customer performance before and after the change.
- Leadership agrees to explicit stop/rollback thresholds.
- Sales and support can consistently explain the new price without adding ad hoc discounting.
- The price change can be limited to one package/cohort first.

Fail if:
- The business cannot measure conversion, ASP, discounting, and objection rates reliably.
- The change must apply immediately to the installed base.
- Revenue pressure is forcing a large increase without operational ability to reverse course.

## Recommended Next Artifact
Create a pricing test memo with:
- target segment and package in scope
- exact old vs new price points
- success and rollback thresholds
- instrumentation plan for conversion, ASP, discount rate, sales-cycle length, churn risk signals, and win/loss reasons
- 30-day and 60-day review checkpoints

That artifact should be the gating document before launch.
