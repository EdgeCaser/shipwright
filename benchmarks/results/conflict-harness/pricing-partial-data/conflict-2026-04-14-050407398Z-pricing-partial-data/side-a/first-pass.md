# Pricing Strategy Recommendation Under Partial Market Data

## Recommendation
Adopt a staged pricing test rather than a permanent price reset now. Use a narrow, reversible change: introduce a modest list-price increase or packaging fence for new customers only, preserve current pricing for existing customers during the test window, and pair the change with explicit guardrails on conversion, win rate, and early retention. Under partial market data, the goal is to learn willingness-to-pay without taking an irreversible revenue or demand risk.

## Rationale
The case provides no direct market benchmarks, customer elasticity data, competitor pricing evidence, or segment-level willingness-to-pay evidence. That makes a full-market repricing weakly supported. A staged test is the highest-confidence move because it creates decision-grade evidence while limiting downside. If the business must act before richer data is available, the safer recommendation is to change exposure and packaging before changing price deeply across the base.

## Recommended Approach
1. Test on new business only for a defined period.
2. Prefer one-variable changes first: either a modest price increase or a packaging change, not both at once unless measurement design is strong.
3. Protect existing customers from immediate repricing to avoid avoidable churn without evidence.
4. Track four primary metrics: conversion rate, average revenue per new account, sales cycle/win rate, and 30- to 60-day retention or refund signal.
5. Define stop conditions in advance: if conversion drops beyond the acceptable threshold without offsetting revenue lift, roll back.
6. If partial data suggests heterogeneous value, test by segment rather than imposing one universal price.

## Decision Frame
Recommend a controlled pricing experiment, not a full rollout. The decision this supports is whether to pursue monetization uplift now while evidence is incomplete. The appropriate bar is not certainty; it is reversibility, bounded downside, and fast learning. A staged test meets that bar better than a broad permanent change.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- No competitor pricing data is provided.
- No customer willingness-to-pay evidence is provided.
- No elasticity, churn sensitivity, or historical pricing-test data is provided.
- No segment-level differentiation is provided, so uniform pricing may misprice high- and low-value cohorts.
- No channel mix is provided, which matters because self-serve and sales-led motions react differently to price changes.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass for a limited experiment if the team can isolate treatment cohorts, measure conversion and retention cleanly, and roll back quickly.
Fail for a full pricing rollout today because the evidence base is insufficient for a durable market-wide recommendation.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A pricing experiment brief with test design, target segments, guardrail metrics, rollback criteria, and a 2x2 scenario model for revenue lift versus demand loss.
