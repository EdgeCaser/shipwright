# Launch Decision Memo

## Recommendation
Do not commit to a full product launch at the conference in 3 weeks. Use the conference for a controlled announcement of the product direction and a dated launch window only if engineering can confirm a tightly scoped, customer-safe release candidate. Re-baseline the commercial commitment around the 8-week delivery estimate, with an explicit executive decision on scope reduction within 72 hours.

## Rationale
The current commitments conflict on time horizon, not just preference. The engineering estimate implies the team does not believe the full launch can be delivered safely in 3 weeks. Sales has already created market expectations for Q1, which makes indefinite delay costly but does not make an infeasible date real. The conference creates a communications deadline, but an announcement deadline is not the same as a launch readiness threshold.

The practical decision is therefore to separate three events that are currently being conflated: announcement, customer availability, and revenue commitment. That preserves executive visibility at the conference while protecting product quality and reducing the odds of a failed launch.

## Decision Frame
The decision is whether to preserve the conference date as the full launch date or decouple announcement from general availability.

Recommendation: decouple them. Hold the conference date for messaging, not for a full launch, unless a sharply reduced scope can be proven launch-ready against explicit pass criteria within 72 hours.

Alternatives considered:
- Force the original conference date as full launch: highest alignment with executive optics, highest delivery and credibility risk.
- Hold the Q1 sales date without scope change: preserves external commitment only on paper if engineering cannot deliver.
- Move all dates without interim narrative: reduces operational risk but wastes the conference moment and creates avoidable commercial confusion.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- The 8-week estimate may refer to full scope, but the memo does not specify whether a materially smaller launch slice could ship in 3 weeks.
- The sales commitment is stated as Q1, but the size and contractual importance of affected deals is unknown.
- The conference announcement requirement is clear, but the reputational downside of announcing without immediate availability is not quantified.
- There is no evidence on customer harm, support burden, or brand damage if an undercooked release ships.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass conditions for any conference-tied availability date:
- Engineering names a reduced launch scope that can be completed in 3 weeks with named owners and dependency clearance.
- Product, engineering, and go-to-market leaders agree on what is excluded from the first release.
- Sales confirms that the reduced scope still supports the most important committed deals, or explicitly resets those deals.
- CEO agrees that conference success can be defined as announcement plus credible roadmap if general availability slips.

Fail conditions:
- The team cannot define a reduced scope that is customer-safe.
- Revenue-critical commitments depend on capabilities that remain in the 8-week path.
- Leadership continues treating all stakeholder constraints as simultaneously non-negotiable.

## Recommended Next Artifact
Create a 1-page launch reset brief within 72 hours containing:
- three date options: conference announcement only, limited availability, full general availability
- scope by option
- revenue impact by option
- delivery risk by option
- final executive owner for the tradeoff decision

That brief should be the decision record used to realign engineering, sales, and executive communications.
