# Launch Decision Memo

## Recommendation
Do not commit to a full product launch at the conference in 3 weeks. Use the conference for a controlled announcement, customer preview, or launch-date commitment only after leadership explicitly separates three decisions: announcement timing, customer availability, and revenue-plan timing. Re-baseline the full launch plan around the current 8-week engineering estimate unless engineering itself proposes a narrower, customer-safe release path.

## Rationale
The case packet gives one concrete delivery signal: engineering estimates 8 weeks. That is the strongest operational evidence in the scenario, and it conflicts directly with both the 3-week conference deadline and the existing Q1 sales commitment. Sales and CEO constraints matter commercially, but they do not by themselves establish delivery feasibility.

The useful decision is therefore not "whose date wins," but whether leadership will keep conflating announcement, availability, and revenue recognition into one milestone. It should not. Preserving the conference as a messaging event protects executive and market objectives, while separating general availability from the conference date reduces the risk of a rushed, low-confidence launch.

## Decision Frame
The decision is whether to force the conference date to serve as full launch, or to decouple the conference from general availability and reset the operating plan around the only explicit delivery estimate.

Recommendation: decouple the conference from full launch. Treat the conference as an announcement moment unless engineering independently confirms a smaller release path it is willing to stand behind.

Alternatives considered:
- Force full launch at the conference: maximizes optics, maximizes execution and credibility risk.
- Preserve the existing Q1 commitment without scope or date reset: preserves commercial posture rhetorically, but not operationally.
- Move everything and say little at the conference: lowers execution risk, but wastes the communications opportunity and leaves the market narrative unmanaged.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- The packet does not say whether the 8-week estimate is for full scope only or whether a materially smaller launch slice exists.
- The revenue and contractual exposure behind the Q1 sales commitment is unknown.
- The CEO's conference objective is not fully specified: announcement, bookings support, or immediate availability may imply different acceptable paths.
- The stakeholder conflict is described as non-negotiable, so the political feasibility of rapid alignment is uncertain.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass conditions for any conference-linked availability commitment:
- Engineering explicitly states that a narrower release path exists and is customer-safe.
- Product, sales, and executive leadership agree on what is in scope, what is excluded, and what will be said publicly.
- Revenue-critical deals are mapped to the reduced scope or proactively reset.
- The company defines conference success in a way that does not require pretending the 3-week date is a full launch if it is not.

Fail conditions:
- Engineering does not stand behind a smaller safe release path.
- Revenue-critical commitments depend on capabilities still inside the 8-week estimate.
- Leadership continues to treat all three stakeholder constraints as simultaneously fixed.
