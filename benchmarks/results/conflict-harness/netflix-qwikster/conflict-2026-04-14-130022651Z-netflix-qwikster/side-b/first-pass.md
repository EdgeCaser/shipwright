# Reverse Course: Keep DVD and Streaming Under the Netflix Brand

Netflix should reverse the Qwikster split immediately and keep both services under one brand, one account system, and one customer relationship. The strategic logic behind separation is directionally right: streaming is the future, DVD is declining, and the two businesses have different operating models. But the announced form of the split destroys customer value faster than it creates managerial clarity. In September 2011, the binding constraint is not internal org design; it is collapsing trust, accelerating subscriber losses, and preserving the cash engine that funds streaming's transition. The company can separate operating accountability internally without forcing customers into two websites, two bills, two queues, and a second brand with no equity. [ctx-1][ctx-2][ctx-3][ctx-4]

The core issue is sequence. Netflix already imposed a 60% effective price increase on the combined plan, then followed with a structurally worse product experience. Customers are reacting not to abstract strategy but to cumulative value destruction: they now pay more for a smaller streaming library than DVD, with more friction across both products. The result is already visible in hard metrics: 800,000 subscriber losses in Q3 2011, ACSI falling from 79 to 52 in one quarter, 27,000+ overwhelmingly negative comments, and a 77% stock collapse from the July peak. Proceeding with Qwikster would convert an execution error into a self-inflicted retention program for competitors. [ctx-2][ctx-5][ctx-6]

Reverse course does not mean abandoning strategic separation. It means changing where separation happens. Netflix should keep a unified front-end brand and billing relationship while creating sharper internal P&L accountability for DVD and streaming. DVD remains a declining but still highly valuable cash-flow business, with about $1B in revenue and high margins, while streaming is loss-making and facing content-cost inflation from $180M in 2010 to a projected $2B in 2012. Breaking the customer relationship apart now risks damaging the very profit pool financing the streaming build-out and international expansion. [ctx-3][ctx-7][ctx-8]

The strongest counterargument is that bundling DVD and streaming creates organizational drag and obscures the economics of the future business. That is true. Streaming product, licensing, and international expansion need focus, and investors need transparency on the transition path. Competitors including Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO make delay costly. But Qwikster is the wrong separation design. It confuses customers, weakens the Netflix brand just as streaming competition intensifies, and removes the convenience advantage that kept hybrid users loyal during the catalog transition. Internal separation with external unity captures most of the managerial benefit without compounding customer harm. [ctx-1][ctx-3][ctx-4][ctx-8]

Management should announce three moves together: cancel Qwikster, preserve one Netflix identity and login, and present a transition plan that explicitly says DVD cash flow will fund streaming content and international growth while DVD declines over time. That narrative is more credible than pretending the businesses are already independent. Customers will tolerate a difficult transition more readily than obvious unnecessary friction. Investors may still question margins, but they can underwrite a disciplined migration; they will not reward brand self-sabotage.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Reverse course on Qwikster and keep DVD-by-mail and streaming under the Netflix brand.

Why this is the right call:
- The immediate strategic priority is stopping trust erosion and subscriber losses, not maximizing organizational purity. [ctx-2][ctx-5][ctx-6]
- DVD is still the cash generator funding streaming losses and rising content costs; preserving the integrated customer relationship protects that subsidy during the transition. [ctx-3][ctx-7]
- Qwikster lowers customer value through duplicate accounts, queues, billing, and a weaker brand architecture at exactly the moment competition is rising. [ctx-1][ctx-4][ctx-8]
- Netflix can achieve most of the strategic benefits of separation through internal operating structure rather than customer-facing fragmentation. [ctx-1][ctx-3]

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- How much of the Q3 subscriber loss is attributable to price versus the split announcement sequence.
- The retention profile of DVD-only, streaming-only, and combo households under alternative packaging designs.
- Whether clearer reporting transparency alone would satisfy investor demands for strategic clarity.
- The exact pace at which DVD decline accelerates if the service is de-emphasized but not spun out.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if:
- Leadership is willing to publicly reverse the customer-facing split now.
- The company can implement internal business-line accountability without breaking the unified product surface.
- Management can explain DVD's role as a transition cash engine rather than a strategic embarrassment.

Fail if:
- Leadership insists that external separation is required immediately for strategic signaling.
- The company cannot stabilize churn after canceling Qwikster and simplifying the customer experience.
- Content-cost inflation overwhelms the transition model before streaming scale improves.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A transition plan memo covering: customer communication, packaging and pricing options, internal reporting separation, and a 12-month migration strategy from DVD-funded hybrid economics to streaming-led economics.
