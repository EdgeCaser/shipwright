# Reverse course on Qwikster and keep both services under the Netflix brand

## Recommendation
Reverse the Qwikster split immediately. Keep streaming and DVD-by-mail under one Netflix brand, one account, one bill, and one customer relationship, while separating the businesses internally for management, capital allocation, and operating accountability.

The strategic logic behind separation is real: streaming is the future, DVD is declining, and the two businesses have different economics. But the announced form of separation is the wrong move at the wrong time. It converts a sound internal strategy question into a customer-facing experience disaster precisely when Netflix most needs trust, cash flow, and execution focus. The company should preserve the integrated customer proposition and perform the operating split behind the scenes instead. [ctx-1][ctx-2][ctx-3]

## Why reverse course
### 1. The current plan destroys customer value faster than it creates strategic clarity
Customers are reacting not to abstract corporate structure but to concrete friction: a 60% price increase for the combined offer, separate websites, separate logins, separate queues, and separate reviews. That means Netflix is asking customers to pay more for a worse experience. The market has already validated that this is not a tolerable transition cost: 800,000 subscribers were lost in Q3 2011, customer satisfaction fell from 79 to 52 in one quarter, and the announcement generated overwhelming negative feedback. A plan that imposes immediate pain with no near-term customer benefit should be stopped before trust erodes further. [ctx-1][ctx-4][ctx-5]

### 2. Netflix still needs DVD cash flow to fund the streaming transition
DVD may be declining about 5% per quarter organically, but it remains a large, high-margin cash generator at roughly $1B in revenue. Streaming content costs are exploding, from $180M in 2010 to a projected $2B in 2012, while the streaming business is still loss-making. That makes DVD not a distraction but a financing engine for the future business. A visible public split increases the chance that DVD deteriorates faster than necessary, weakening the very cash flows required to win in streaming and international expansion. Netflix should harvest DVD, not stigmatize it. [ctx-2][ctx-6][ctx-7]

### 3. Management attention should be on content, product quality, and competition, not on self-inflicted migration chaos
Netflix is entering the most dangerous phase of the streaming land grab. Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO have all entered the market, while Netflix is also expanding internationally. At the same time, streaming still has a materially weaker catalog than DVD, roughly 20,000 titles versus 100,000. This is the moment to invest executive attention in content licensing, streaming UX, recommendation quality, and international execution. Instead, Qwikster creates migration work, PR cleanup, brand confusion, and operational distraction. Strategic clarity is valuable, but not if it consumes the bandwidth needed to compete. ## Strongest counterarguments and why they do not change the recommendation
### Counterargument 1: The businesses need freedom to optimize independently
Correct. Streaming and DVD have different economics, product requirements, and talent needs. But customer-facing separation is not the only way to get managerial separation. Netflix can create separate P&Ls, leadership owners, roadmaps, and operating metrics internally without forcing customers into two brands and two products. The recommendation is not to deny separation in principle; it is to reject this specific customer-facing implementation.

### Counterargument 2: Keeping the bundle masks the economics and slows the pivot to streaming
Partly true. DVD can obscure the economics of streaming. But the answer is disclosure and internal discipline, not brand fragmentation. Netflix can report segment economics more transparently, stop over-subsidizing weak streaming bets, and manage migration deliberately. What it cannot afford is to accelerate churn while its streaming catalog is still meaningfully inferior to DVD.

### Counterargument 3: Reversing course signals weakness to Wall Street
In the short term, reversing course is embarrassing. In the medium term, persisting in a visibly broken rollout is worse. Investors can tolerate a correction if management shows it learned quickly, protects the cash engine, and sharpens the streaming transition plan. They are less likely to reward stubbornness after a 77% stock decline from the July peak and clear evidence of customer revolt. [ctx-4][ctx-5]

## What Netflix should do instead
1. Cancel the Qwikster rollout and keep one Netflix account, one website, one queue ecosystem where possible, and one bill.
2. Publicly acknowledge that the strategic objective was reasonable but the customer experience design was wrong.
3. Internally separate DVD and streaming into distinct business units with clear leaders, P&Ls, operating metrics, and capital allocation rules.
4. Use DVD cash flow explicitly to fund a bounded set of streaming priorities: content depth, exclusives, device reach, and international market entry.
5. Redesign packaging and pricing only after trust stabilizes, with simpler plans and a clear customer value story.
6. Preserve the Netflix master brand as the umbrella for both services until streaming offers enough breadth and reliability to stand alone without provoking mass defections.

## Decision Frame
Decision: Reverse course on Qwikster as a customer-facing split; retain one Netflix brand and integrated customer experience while separating DVD and streaming internally.

Why now: The current rollout is compounding three risks at once: customer churn, cash-flow erosion, and management distraction. The company does not need public separation urgently enough to justify those costs.

What would change my mind: I would support a fuller external split only if Netflix could show that customers would not face major workflow friction, that DVD cash flow would remain durable post-split, and that streaming had reached enough catalog depth and unit-economic stability to stand without heavy cross-subsidy.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- Precise contribution margin by segment over the next 4 to 8 quarters under both scenarios.
- How much of Q3 churn is reversible if the split is canceled quickly.
- Whether a lighter-weight separation, such as distinct plans under one account, could preserve clarity without brand damage.
- The sensitivity of content licensors and international partners to a public reversal.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if leadership is prepared to do three things immediately: cancel the split, communicate the reversal clearly, and impose real internal operating separation so the original strategic problem is still addressed.

Fail if leadership insists on customer-facing fragmentation as proof of strategic discipline, or if it uses reversal as an excuse to avoid fixing streaming economics and accountability.
