# Strategic Recommendation: Reverse the Qwikster Split Immediately

## Decision Frame

**Decision:** Abandon the Qwikster separation and reunify DVD-by-mail and streaming under the Netflix brand.

**Time horizon:** This recommendation addresses the immediate crisis (Q4 2011) while preserving the long-term strategic intent of transitioning to streaming-first.

**Core tension:** The Qwikster split is strategically directionally correct — DVD is declining and streaming is the future — but the execution destroys more value than the organizational clarity creates. The question is not whether to eventually decouple DVD from streaming, but whether a hard public split right now, in the middle of a subscriber revolt triggered by a 60% price increase, is the right mechanism.

**Our position: Reverse course.** The split should be abandoned. The strategic separation can be achieved internally without the customer-facing damage of two brands, two logins, two billing systems, and two queues.

---

## Key Claims and Evidence

### Claim 1: The subscriber and brand damage from the split compounds the price-increase backlash into an existential threat

Netflix lost 800,000 subscribers in Q3 2011 — the first net loss in the company's history. Customer satisfaction (ACSI) cratered from 79 to 52 in a single quarter. The stock has fallen 77% from its July peak. These are not incremental signals; they indicate a crisis of customer trust.

The Qwikster announcement arrived while this wound was still open. Rather than stabilizing the relationship, it told customers: the service you're already angry about paying more for will now also become harder to use. Separate websites, separate queues, separate billing. The 27,000+ overwhelmingly negative blog comments are a leading indicator — this is the kind of unified customer backlash that can permanently impair a brand.

**Evidence:** 800K subscriber loss (Q3 2011); ACSI drop from 79→52; 77% stock decline; 27,000+ negative comments on announcement post.

### Claim 2: The split destroys a critical financial cross-subsidy at the worst possible time

The DVD business generates approximately $1B in revenue at high margins. The streaming business operates at a loss. Streaming content costs are surging from $180M (2010) to a projected $2B (2012). Netflix is simultaneously expanding internationally (Canada, Latin America, UK) — each new market is a cash-burning investment.

Splitting into two companies severs the financial link between the profitable DVD business and the cash-hungry streaming business. As a unified company, DVD cash flow funds streaming content acquisition and international expansion. As two separate entities, the streaming company must either raise external capital (at a time when the stock has lost 77% of its value) or slow its content and expansion investments — ceding ground to Amazon, Hulu, and HBO GO precisely when competitive intensity is highest.

**Evidence:** DVD ~$1B revenue, high margins; streaming operating at a loss; content costs rising from $180M to $2B; three international markets launched 2010-2011; competitors Amazon, Hulu, HBO GO all launched 2010-2011.

### Claim 3: The operational "clarity" of separation can be achieved without a customer-facing split

The strategic argument for Qwikster — that DVD logistics constrain streaming product decisions — is real but does not require two public-facing brands. Internal organizational separation (separate P&Ls, separate product teams, separate engineering orgs) achieves the same decoupling without forcing customers to manage two accounts, two queues, and two bills.

Netflix can run DVD as a "harvest" business unit internally: maintain service quality, let natural attrition reduce the subscriber base (~5% quarterly organic decline), and redirect margin to streaming investment. The customer sees one Netflix. The org chart sees two business units. This is standard conglomerate practice.

**Evidence:** DVD subscribers already declining ~5% per quarter organically; streaming library (20K titles) vs DVD (100K titles) gap ensures natural migration; no customer-facing change required for internal org restructuring.

### Claim 4: The Qwikster brand itself is compromised beyond recovery

The Qwikster Twitter handle is owned by an unaffiliated individual posting inappropriate content. This is not a minor PR inconvenience — it signals to customers and investors that the split was rushed, poorly planned, and not ready for execution. Launching a major brand without securing basic digital assets undermines confidence in leadership competence at a moment when that confidence is already at a nadir.

**Evidence:** Qwikster Twitter handle controlled by unaffiliated party; no evidence of trademark or domain preparation before announcement.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

1. **Subscriber elasticity post-reversal:** Will reversing the split recover lost subscribers, or has trust been permanently impaired? The 800K loss includes both price-sensitive and trust-damaged customers. We do not know the ratio. A reversal addresses trust but does not reverse the price increase — some portion of losses may be permanent.

2. **Content cost trajectory:** Streaming content licensing is surging to $2B. We lack visibility into whether this is a temporary spike (studios testing the market) or a permanent structural shift. If content costs continue accelerating, the DVD cross-subsidy becomes even more critical — but it also means the streaming business may never reach profitability on licensing alone, which would eventually force an original content strategy.

3. **Competitor conversion rates:** Amazon, Hulu, and HBO GO have launched, but we lack data on how many of Netflix's lost 800K subscribers moved to competitors vs. simply canceled. If competitors captured significant share, reversal alone may not be sufficient — Netflix may need aggressive re-acquisition offers.

4. **International market unit economics:** Three new markets launched in 18 months. We lack per-market subscriber acquisition costs and time-to-profitability estimates. If international expansion is burning cash faster than expected, the financial case for preserving the DVD cross-subsidy strengthens.

5. **Internal organizational resistance:** We do not know how far the Qwikster separation has progressed operationally. If engineering teams, billing systems, and customer databases have already been partially separated, reversal has non-trivial technical costs.

## Counterargument: "Reversal signals weakness and delays inevitable separation"

The strongest case for proceeding with Qwikster is that reversal makes Hastings look indecisive and delays a separation that must eventually happen. DVD is dying. Every quarter Netflix delays the split, organizational complexity accumulates.

This argument fails on timing and mechanism:

- **Timing:** The split was announced during the worst customer crisis in Netflix history. Even a correct strategic move executed at the wrong time is a mistake. The price increase already damaged trust; the split amplified it. Reversing now, with a candid acknowledgment that "we moved too fast," is less damaging than doubling down on a plan customers have overwhelmingly rejected.

- **Mechanism:** Separation does not require two brands. Internal restructuring achieves the same organizational clarity. The DVD business will wind down naturally — 5% quarterly organic decline means it halves in roughly three years without any customer-facing action.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Internal Reorganization Plan:** A detailed operational plan for separating DVD and streaming into distinct business units within Netflix (separate P&Ls, product teams, and engineering orgs) without any customer-facing brand split. This plan should include:

1. Organizational structure and reporting lines for the two business units
2. Financial model: DVD harvest trajectory with margin reallocation to streaming content and international expansion
3. Customer communication strategy for the reversal announcement (tone: humility, clarity, no spin)
4. 12-month streaming content investment plan funded by DVD margins
5. Competitive response framework for Amazon/Hulu/HBO GO market entry
6. Trigger conditions for eventual DVD sunset (subscriber threshold, margin floor) to be monitored quarterly
