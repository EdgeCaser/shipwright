# Reverse Course on Qwikster

## Recommendation
Reverse the Qwikster split and keep DVD-by-mail and streaming under the Netflix brand, while operating them as separate business units internally. The strategic logic behind separation is real, but the announced form of separation destroys customer experience, accelerates churn, weakens the cash engine funding streaming, and burns brand trust at the exact moment Netflix needs customers and investors to finance the transition to streaming.

## Why Reverse Course

### 1. The proposed split compounds an already severe pricing shock
Netflix has already imposed a 60% effective price increase on combined subscribers, and the result was immediate backlash: 800,000 subscribers lost in Q3 2011, a collapse in customer satisfaction from 79 to 52, and extreme negative response to the announcement. Adding separate websites, billing, queues, and logins turns an economic insult into an operational hassle. That is not strategic clarity; it is customer-hostile bundling of pain.

### 2. DVD cash flow is still strategically essential
DVD is declining, but it is still a large, high-margin business generating about $1B in revenue and subsidizing a streaming business that is currently loss-making. Streaming content costs are surging from $180M in 2010 toward a projected $2B in 2012, while the streaming catalog remains far smaller than DVD. Splitting too visibly and too abruptly risks accelerating DVD attrition before streaming has enough content breadth or economics to stand alone.

### 3. Brand fragmentation weakens Netflix at the wrong competitive moment
Streaming competition is intensifying with Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO entering the market. International expansion is also underway. This is the moment to concentrate brand equity behind Netflix, not fracture it. Qwikster adds confusion, lowers trust, and hands competitors a simple message: Netflix is making its product worse while others are trying to make theirs better.

### 4. The company can get most of the strategic benefit without the customer-facing split
The real strategic need is internal: let streaming move faster without DVD logistics dictating product choices. That does not require two brands or two customer experiences. Netflix should keep one account, one bill, one identity, and one brand externally, while separating P&L accountability, product roadmaps, and operating teams internally.

## Strongest Counterargument
The best argument for proceeding is that DVD and streaming are fundamentally different businesses with different economics, operating models, and futures. A clean split could force management discipline, improve capital allocation, and prevent the declining DVD business from slowing streaming innovation.

That argument is directionally correct but misapplied. The issue is not whether the businesses differ; they do. The issue is whether a hard customer-facing separation in September 2011 creates value. It does not. The proposed structure imposes maximum customer friction at the same time Netflix has already damaged trust through pricing. Internal decoupling is necessary; external rupture is not.

## Recommended Actions for the Next 90 Days
1. Cancel the Qwikster brand launch and publicly commit to one Netflix account, one website, and one billing relationship.
2. Reframe the message from structural separation to service improvement: customers keep simplicity while Netflix improves streaming investment and preserves DVD choice.
3. Create internal standalone operating teams and P&Ls for streaming and DVD, with explicit transfer-pricing and capital-allocation rules.
4. Slow the rate of customer-facing change until churn stabilizes and satisfaction recovers.
5. Use DVD cash flow deliberately to fund streaming content and international expansion, with transparent internal milestones for when streaming can stand alone economically.
6. Prioritize product work that reduces perceived value gaps in streaming, especially catalog depth and discovery.

## Decision Frame
Reverse course on Qwikster externally; separate the businesses internally. This preserves the funding bridge from DVD to streaming, avoids additional churn from forced complexity, and keeps the Netflix brand intact during a fragile transition.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- How much of the 800,000 subscriber loss is attributable to pricing alone versus the announced split.
- The precise churn sensitivity of DVD-only, streaming-only, and combo households under alternative packaging options.
- The near-term cash-flow durability of DVD if kept under one brand but managed for yield.
- The degree to which content licensors or investors would reward formal separation versus internal operating separation.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if leadership is willing to prioritize customer retention and transition financing over symbolic strategic purity, and if internal governance can create real separation without forcing it onto customers.

Fail if leadership believes only a public split can create discipline, or if internal teams cannot execute distinct strategies without customer-facing brand separation.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A transition operating memo defining the integrated external experience, separate internal business units, capital-allocation rules, churn guardrails, and 12-month milestones for streaming self-sufficiency.
