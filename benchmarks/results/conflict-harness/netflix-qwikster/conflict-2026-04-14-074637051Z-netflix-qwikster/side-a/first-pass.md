# Reverse Course: Keep DVD and Streaming Under the Netflix Brand

## Recommendation
Reverse the Qwikster split immediately. Keep DVD-by-mail and streaming under one Netflix brand, one account, one bill, and one customer relationship. The strategic premise behind separation is directionally valid: streaming is the future and DVD is in secular decline. But the announced form of the split destroys customer experience, accelerates churn at the wrong moment, weakens the cash engine funding streaming, and consumes management attention while competition and content costs are rising.

The core issue is not whether Netflix should transition from DVDs to streaming. It must. The issue is whether forcing customers through a dual-company experience in September 2011 improves the odds of winning that transition. It does not.

## Why Reverse Course
### 1. The split compounds an active trust crisis
Netflix has already triggered severe customer backlash with the effective 60% increase for customers who want both DVDs and streaming. The company then asked those same customers to accept separate websites, separate billing, separate queues, and separate reviews. That is not strategic clarity from the customer perspective; it is cumulative friction layered on top of a price shock.

Evidence of damage is already visible: total subscribers fell from about 24.6M in Q2 2011 to 23.8M in Q3 2011, a loss of 800,000 subscribers, while customer satisfaction collapsed from 79 to 52 in a single quarter. In this context, the right move is to simplify the customer proposition, not make it more cumbersome.

### 2. The DVD business is still strategically valuable
DVD is declining, but it remains a large, high-margin cash flow business with roughly $1B in revenue. That cash is helping subsidize a streaming business that is still operating at a loss while content licensing costs are exploding from $180M in 2010 toward a projected $2B in 2012.

Splitting the businesses may create managerial neatness, but it also increases the odds that leadership underinvests in the very engine currently financing the streaming transition. The company should harvest DVD cash efficiently while using it to fund content, product, and international expansion in streaming.

### 3. Streaming is not ready to stand alone as the full customer promise
Streaming has strategic momentum, but in 2011 it is still materially inferior to DVD on selection: roughly 20,000 streaming titles versus about 100,000 on DVD. Many customers still need both services to get the experience they associate with Netflix. Separating the brands before streaming reaches stronger catalog parity forces customers to feel the gap more sharply.

A cleaner long-term org chart is not worth exposing a weaker near-term product reality.

### 4. Management focus should be on content, competition, and international execution
Netflix is entering the most resource-intensive phase of the streaming war. Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO are in market. International expansion is underway in Canada, Latin America, and the UK. Content costs are surging. Those are the real strategic battles.

Qwikster creates a self-inflicted execution tax: rebranding, migrating accounts, explaining product confusion, handling billing anger, and repairing reputation damage. Leadership bandwidth is scarce. It should be spent on streaming economics and differentiation, not on administratively separating a still-interdependent customer offer.

## Strongest Counterargument
The strongest argument for proceeding is that bundling DVD and streaming traps Netflix between two business models. DVD logistics, merchandising, and queue design can distort decision-making for a streaming-first future. Separation could create sharper accountability, clearer financial visibility, and faster product decisions for each business.

That logic is real, but it fails the timing and market test. A good internal strategy can still be a bad market move if customers experience it as punishment and confusion. Netflix can preserve internal operating separation without imposing a customer-facing corporate split. The company does not need two brands, two websites, and two bills to create management clarity. It needs clearer internal P&L accountability, distinct operating goals, and a transition plan that does not further damage trust.

## What Netflix Should Do Instead
1. Cancel Qwikster publicly and keep one Netflix brand.
2. Preserve one login, one website, one bill, and one account identity.
3. Reorganize internally into separate DVD and streaming operating units with distinct leaders and metrics.
4. Stabilize churn by reframing pricing around simplicity and value, not corporate structure.
5. Use DVD cash flow deliberately to fund streaming content and international ramp, while managing DVD for profitability rather than growth.
6. Build a staged migration path where streaming earns primacy through better catalog depth and product quality, not forced separation.

## Decision Frame
Reverse course on Qwikster. The separation creates immediate customer harm, threatens the cash flow that funds streaming, and distracts from the actual strategic contest. Internal separation is sufficient; external separation is unnecessary and value-destructive.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- The precise churn mix between DVD-only, streaming-only, and combo households is unclear.
- It is not fully clear how much of the subscriber loss is recoverable if Qwikster is canceled quickly.
- The exact standalone contribution margin of DVD under multiple decline scenarios would sharpen the capital-allocation plan.
- We lack direct data on whether a customer-facing split would improve internal execution enough to offset churn and brand damage.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if leadership is prepared to abandon the public split immediately, accept short-term reputational embarrassment, and install internal operating separation with hard accountability.

Fail if leadership still believes customer inconvenience is an acceptable transition cost, or if it cannot protect DVD cash generation while scaling streaming.
