# Revised artifact

## Recommendation
Reverse course on the Qwikster split and keep both services under the Netflix brand, while separating DVD and streaming internally through distinct leadership, metrics, and capital allocation. The strategic logic behind internal separation is sound. The announced customer-facing form is not. In September 2011, Netflix does not need a second brand, second website, second queue, and second bill; it needs to stabilize trust, retain subscribers, and protect the cash flow funding the streaming transition.

## Core Judgment
The strongest evidence does **not** prove that Q3 2011 subscriber losses were caused primarily by Qwikster. The 800,000 subscriber decline and the sharp customer-satisfaction drop occurred in the same period as the July 2011 60% effective price increase, and that price shock plainly did major damage. But that does not rescue the Qwikster plan. It means leadership is confronting an already-fragile customer base. In that context, adding separate logins, separate queues, separate reviews, and separate billing is not a cure for churn; it is an additional customer burden with no offsetting customer benefit. The decision is therefore forward-looking: reverse course because Qwikster increases execution risk and likely worsens retention and trust from an already weakened starting point. ## Why Reverse Course
First, Qwikster asks customers to accept a meaningfully worse experience just after a severe price increase. Even if the July pricing change explains most of the observed Q3 churn, the announced split still removes convenience from the combined offer. Customers would have to manage two services separately while receiving no improvement in catalog depth, discovery, or value. With streaming at roughly 20,000 titles versus about 100,000 on DVD, Netflix is not in a position to impose more friction on households that still rely on both formats. Reversal is justified because the split weakens the product proposition at the exact moment the company needs to simplify and rebuild trust. [ctx-1][ctx-3][ctx-5]

Second, DVD remains economically essential during the transition. DVD subscribers are declining around 5% per quarter, but the business still generates about $1B of high-margin revenue. That cash flow is subsidizing a streaming business that is operating at a loss while content costs are rising from $180M in 2010 toward a projected $2B in 2012. A customer-facing split increases the risk of accelerating DVD attrition beyond its organic decline rate, which would undermine Netflix's ability to finance streaming content and international expansion. Leadership should not destabilize the profit pool that is paying for the future business. [ctx-4][ctx-5][ctx-6]

Third, the external split solves the wrong problem. The real strategic need is internal clarity: separate operating discipline, cleaner economics, and faster streaming decisions. Those benefits can be achieved without fragmenting the customer relationship. Netflix can create distinct internal business lines, separate roadmaps, and more explicit reporting while keeping one account, one brand, and one bill. That captures most of the managerial upside without imposing avoidable market and customer confusion. [ctx-1][ctx-8]

## Strongest Counterargument
The best argument for proceeding is that only a hard split will force organizational discipline. Streaming is the future, DVD is declining, and keeping both under one umbrella risks slow decisions, muddled incentives, and investor confusion about the economics of each business. Given aggressive competition from Amazon, Hulu, and HBO and the demands of international expansion, leadership may conclude that radical separation is necessary now. [ctx-6][ctx-8]

That argument is directionally right about internal structure and wrong about customer design. Netflix does need sharper separation inside the company. But customer-facing fragmentation is not required to achieve that. In fact, because the streaming catalog is still materially weaker than DVD and customer trust has already been damaged, an external split imposes costs before Netflix has earned the right to impose them. Reverse course on Qwikster, keep the integrated customer experience, and pursue internal decoupling instead.

## Immediate Actions
1. Cancel the Qwikster rollout before launch and recommit to one Netflix identity, one login, one queue system, and one bill.
2. Announce a new internal operating model with separate leaders and performance metrics for DVD and streaming.
3. Stop adding customer-facing complexity for the next 6 to 12 months and make retention and service recovery the top operating priority.
4. Use DVD cash flow intentionally to fund streaming content and international expansion, with explicit guardrails.
5. Improve investor communication through clearer segment economics and milestones rather than through a separate consumer brand.

## Decision Frame
Decision: reverse the announced Qwikster split and keep both services under the Netflix brand.

The key judgment is not that Qwikster already caused the full Q3 churn shock. It is that, after the July 2011 pricing backlash, Qwikster would add further friction, confusion, and brand damage to an already stressed subscriber base while threatening the DVD cash flows needed to fund streaming. Netflix should separate the businesses internally, not in the customer experience.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- The exact portion of Q3 2011 churn attributable to the July price increase versus the September Qwikster announcement cannot be cleanly isolated from the available evidence.
- The size of the incremental churn Qwikster would have caused if fully launched remains a modeled judgment rather than an observed fact.
- The durability of DVD cash flow over the next 6 to 8 quarters is uncertain, especially if customer sentiment deteriorates further.
- It is not fully known how much investor confidence could be restored through improved segment disclosure without a formal split.
- The pace at which streaming catalog quality can close the gap with DVD remains a major strategic variable.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if:
- Leadership is willing to reverse the customer-facing split immediately.
- Netflix can implement internal business-line autonomy without introducing new subscriber friction.
- The company treats retention, trust recovery, and execution stability as the near-term priority.
- Investor communication shifts toward transparent economics and milestones rather than symbolic restructuring.

Fail if:
- Leadership believes only a separate public brand can create operating discipline.
- Management cannot build internal accountability without recreating customer-facing fragmentation.
- Netflix continues layering customer pain on top of an already damaged value proposition.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A 12-month transition memo covering: the internal operating model for separate DVD and streaming business lines, a subscriber-retention recovery plan, and a capital-allocation framework showing how DVD cash flow will fund streaming content and international expansion while Netflix preserves a unified customer experience.
