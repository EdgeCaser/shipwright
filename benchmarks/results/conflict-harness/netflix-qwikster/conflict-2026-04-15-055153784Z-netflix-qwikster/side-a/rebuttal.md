# Critique: finding-1

- Target Side: side_b
- Target Claim IDs: side_b-claim-1
- Attack Type: evidence_gap
- Severity: high

## Claim Under Attack
Netflix should reverse the Qwikster split because the customer-facing separation compounds a price shock with a worse product experience, driving avoidable churn and trust loss.

## Evidence Or Reason
Side B conflates two distinct shocks — the July 2011 price increase and the September 2011 Qwikster announcement — and treats their combined damage as evidence against the split specifically. The 800,000 subscriber loss and ACSI drop from 79 to 52 occurred in Q3 2011, a quarter dominated by the 60% price increase backlash, not by Qwikster operational complexity (Qwikster was never actually launched). Side B provides no counterfactual evidence showing that a cleaner, better-executed split announcement — without the simultaneous price shock — would have produced the same churn. The causal chain from 'customer-facing separation' to 'avoidable churn' is asserted, not established. The observable evidence is equally consistent with the interpretation that the price increase caused the churn and that the Qwikster execution was simply poor communication layered on an already-wounded customer base. Without isolating the price effect from the separation effect, the claim that reversal is the correct cure for the churn problem is undersupported.
