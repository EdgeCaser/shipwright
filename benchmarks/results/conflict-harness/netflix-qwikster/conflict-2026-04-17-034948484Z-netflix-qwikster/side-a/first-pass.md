# Strategic Recommendation Memo: Reverse Course on Qwikster

**To:** Netflix Leadership Team  
**From:** Strategy Advisory  
**Date:** September 2011  
**Re:** Qwikster Decision — Recommended Action

---

## Decision Frame

**Recommended decision: Reverse course. Abandon the Qwikster split and retain both services under the Netflix brand.**

The core error in the Qwikster announcement is conflating *organizational strategy* with *customer-facing strategy*. The underlying problem — that DVD logistics create drag on streaming product decisions — is real. But the proposed solution imposes that organizational separation cost directly onto customers, at the worst possible moment and with deficient execution. The strategic logic of separation does not survive contact with the financial and operational reality of September 2011.

The reversal is not a retreat from the streaming-first vision. It is a recognition that Netflix cannot fund its streaming future if the DVD business — which currently subsidizes streaming operations — is destabilized by a branding and UX rupture that accelerates subscriber loss.

**Why this framing matters:** The choice is not "separation vs. no separation." The choice is *who bears the cost of separation*. The Qwikster plan transfers that cost to customers (separate logins, separate queues, separate billing) at a moment when customers have already absorbed a 60% price increase. That sequencing is untenable.

### The Five-Point Case for Reversal

**1. The DVD business is still the financial engine, and it is fragile right now.**  
Streaming is operating at a loss. DVD generates approximately $1B in revenue at high margins and actively subsidizes streaming content acquisition — which is projected to reach $2B in licensing costs by 2012. The company lost 800,000 subscribers in Q3 2011 after the price increase. A Qwikster launch that adds friction to DVD retention will accelerate that churn and reduce the cash flow available to fund streaming content. Separation makes structural sense in a future state where streaming is self-funding. It does not make sense now.

**2. The streaming library gap makes the bundle genuinely valuable to customers.**  
Netflix streaming offers approximately 20,000 titles. The DVD library offers approximately 100,000. The gap is not cosmetic — it represents the majority of catalog depth. Customers who want both catalog breadth and streaming convenience are not edge cases; they are the customers most likely to pay a premium. Forcing them to manage two separate accounts, billing relationships, and queues to access content they can currently access through one interface is a value destruction, not a value clarification.

**3. The execution details disqualify this version of the plan regardless of strategic merit.**  
The Qwikster Twitter handle is owned by a third party posting inappropriate content. This is not a minor operational detail — it is a signal that the plan was announced before basic brand hygiene was established. Launching a consumer brand without controlling its primary social media identity in 2011 is an unrecoverable credibility problem. No amount of strategic logic repairs the association.

**4. Customer trust has already been spent once. It cannot be spent again on this timeline.**  
The American Customer Satisfaction Index score dropped from 79 to 52 in a single quarter — a 34% decline — following the price increase. The Netflix blog post announcing Qwikster received more than 27,000 comments, overwhelmingly negative. This is not market noise. This is a customer base that has already absorbed one shock and is being asked to absorb another before the first wound has healed. The rate of trust recovery is slower than the rate of competitor entry: Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO all launched in 2010–2011. Customers who cancel during a trust rupture do not wait for the brand to stabilize; they switch.

**5. The strategic goal is achievable through internal reorganization, not customer-facing separation.**  
The organizational drag problem — streaming decisions constrained by DVD logistics — can be addressed through internal structure: separate P&Ls, separate leadership accountability, separate engineering roadmaps. None of that requires customers to manage two accounts. The Qwikster announcement treated an internal organizational design problem as if it required an external customer-facing solution. It does not.

### Strongest Counterargument: The Long-Run Strategic Case

The most serious argument for proceeding is that separation signals commitment to the streaming future, creates clean incentive structures, and prevents the DVD tail from wagging the streaming dog indefinitely. Reed Hastings is correct that DVD is a declining business (declining approximately 5% per quarter organically) and that organizational conflation will eventually impede streaming strategy.

This argument fails for two reasons. First, *timing*: a structurally correct move executed at the wrong moment against a weakened balance sheet, with a damaged customer base, and with competitor alternatives newly available, is not bold strategy — it is compounding risk. Second, *reversibility*: an internal reorganization can be executed now and the customer-facing brand can be separated later, once streaming is self-funding and the content library gap has narrowed. The reverse is not true. Once Qwikster launches and the customer base fragments, reunification becomes far more costly.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

1. **Churn velocity under Qwikster friction:** We know 800,000 subscribers left in Q3 under the price increase alone. We do not have a modeled estimate of incremental churn from the account-separation friction. This is the most critical unknown — it determines whether DVD cash flow holds long enough to fund streaming content at the projected $2B level.

2. **Internal reorganization feasibility:** The memo assumes that organizational separation can be achieved without customer-facing separation. The actual degree of technical and operational entanglement between the DVD and streaming systems is not detailed in the available evidence. If the backend is deeply coupled, the cost and timeline of internal-only separation may be higher than assumed.

3. **Competitor conversion rates:** We know Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO launched in 2010–2011. We do not know what fraction of canceling Netflix subscribers are converting to these services versus simply reducing their entertainment spend. High competitor conversion rates would increase urgency on the streaming roadmap but would not change the reversal recommendation.

4. **International expansion funding dependencies:** Netflix launched in Canada (2010) and Latin America and UK (2011). International expansion likely requires capital. The degree to which international growth plans are funded by DVD margin is not specified. This affects how much cash flow disruption the company can absorb.

---

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**This recommendation is ready to act on.** The core decision — reverse the Qwikster announcement — does not require resolving the open questions above. The questions bear on execution sequencing and risk calibration, not on the direction of the decision.

The reversal should include:
- A public statement that acknowledges the customer feedback directly and announces that both services will remain under the Netflix brand and unified account experience
- No simultaneous announcement of new pricing or structural changes — the reversal must be a clean signal, not bundled with additional complexity
- Internal initiation of a P&L separation project that does not surface to customers

The reversal should not include:
- An apology framed as abandoning the streaming-first strategy — the vision is correct, the implementation was wrong
- Any commitment to a future separation timeline — that decision should be made when streaming is self-funding

The communication itself is now the highest-risk execution element.
