# Revised artifact

## Reverse Course And Keep Both Services Under The Netflix Brand

## Recommendation
Reverse the Qwikster split and keep streaming and DVD-by-mail under the Netflix brand.

The strategic logic behind separation is understandable: DVD is declining, streaming is the future, and the two businesses have different economics. But the announced form of the split is the wrong move now. Netflix is already dealing with subscriber losses, a sharp decline in customer satisfaction, and intense backlash after the 60% combined-plan price increase. Adding separate websites, billing, logins, and queues compounds the customer problem at the worst possible time.

## Why Reverse Course

### 1. The split adds friction during an active trust crisis
The current problem is not abstract strategy. It is immediate customer anger and confusion. Netflix has already lost subscribers, customer satisfaction has dropped sharply, and the Qwikster announcement drew overwhelming negative reaction. A second layer of disruption makes recovery harder, not easier.

### 2. DVD still matters financially even if it is declining strategically
DVD-by-mail is shrinking, but it is still a significant high-margin revenue source. Streaming, by contrast, is operating at a loss while content costs are rising sharply. That means Netflix needs the stability and cash contribution of the DVD business while it strengthens streaming.

### 3. The announced separation is an execution failure, not just a strategic repositioning
Even if some form of sharper separation may eventually be warranted, the current rollout is visibly damaging. The separate queues, reviews, billing, and logins create a worse customer experience, and the Qwikster branding mishap reinforces that leadership is not executing this transition cleanly.

### 4. Competitive pressure makes simplicity more valuable, not less
Streaming competition is increasing while Netflix's streaming catalog remains much smaller than its DVD catalog. In that environment, Netflix should reduce reasons for customers to reconsider the service. A unified brand is the simpler proposition.

## Strongest Counterargument
The strongest argument for proceeding is that the two businesses need distinct focus, incentives, and operating discipline. Keeping them together may blur strategic priorities and slow streaming.

That concern is real. But the evidence in this packet supports the need for strategic focus more clearly than it supports this specific customer-facing split at this specific moment. The decision at hand is not whether streaming and DVD differ. It is whether Netflix should proceed with a separation format that is already worsening customer and brand damage. On that question, the evidence favors reversal.

## Decision Frame
Choose between preserving the announced split for strategic clarity or reversing course to stabilize customers, brand trust, and cash generation. The better decision is to reverse the split now and revisit structural separation only after the customer relationship is stabilized.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- How much of recent subscriber loss is attributable to the pricing change versus the Qwikster structure itself.
- Whether any later form of operational separation could be implemented without similar customer backlash.
- How long DVD can continue subsidizing streaming as content costs rise.
- Whether leadership can create enough internal focus for streaming without a public split.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if leadership is willing to prioritize customer trust recovery, preserve a unified customer relationship, and defer further separation until there is evidence that the benefits outweigh the disruption.

Fail if leadership believes immediate structural separation is necessary despite current churn, customer anger, and brand damage.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A 90-day recovery memo covering the reversal announcement, customer-retention messaging, pricing stabilization, and decision criteria for any future separation proposal.
