# Strategic Recommendation: Reverse the Qwikster Split Immediately

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Should Netflix proceed with splitting into two separate companies (Netflix for streaming, Qwikster for DVD-by-mail), or reverse course and reunify under a single brand?

**Recommendation: Reverse course. Kill Qwikster. Reunify immediately.**

The strategic logic behind separation — letting streaming optimize without DVD drag — is directionally correct but catastrophically mistimed. The Qwikster split conflates *organizational separation* (which can be achieved internally) with *brand and customer-experience separation* (which destroys value). Netflix can run two P&Ls under one roof without forcing customers onto two websites with two logins and two queues.

### Why reversal is the right call

**1. The DVD business is a cash engine funding an unprofitable future.** Streaming operates at a loss. DVD-by-mail generates approximately $1B in revenue at high margins. Splitting the brand accelerates DVD churn beyond its organic ~5% quarterly decline by adding friction (new site, new login, no shared queue). Every DVD subscriber lost early is margin that cannot fund the streaming content war. The separation doesn't just fail to help streaming — it actively defunds it.

**2. Customer trust is a non-renewable resource at this moment.** The 60% price increase already spent enormous goodwill. ACSI scores collapsed from 79 to 52 in a single quarter — a drop almost unprecedented for a major consumer brand. Proceeding with Qwikster compounds the damage by telling customers who just absorbed a price hike that they must now manage two accounts for the same content they previously accessed in one place. The 800,000 subscriber loss in Q3 is a leading indicator, not the full impact. Reversal signals that leadership listens; proceeding signals contempt.

**3. The competitive window demands focus, not self-inflicted distraction.** Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO all launched in 2010-2011. Netflix is simultaneously expanding internationally (Canada, Latin America, UK). Streaming content costs are surging from $180M to a projected $2B. This is the worst possible moment to divert organizational energy into a brand split, migrate customer accounts, and manage the PR fallout. Every week spent on Qwikster logistics is a week not spent on content acquisition and international growth.

**4. The operational benefits of separation can be captured without brand bifurcation.** The legitimate concern — that DVD logistics constrain streaming product decisions — is an org-chart problem, not a brand problem. Create separate internal divisions with distinct P&Ls, engineering teams, and roadmaps. Let the streaming team ship without waiting for DVD compatibility. None of this requires customers to create a second account.

**5. The Qwikster execution itself signals organizational dysfunction.** The company announced a major brand split without securing the Twitter handle (currently posting inappropriate content under @Qwikster). This is not a detail — it reveals that the announcement was rushed, poorly coordinated, and driven by strategic conviction rather than operational readiness. If the team cannot execute a Twitter handle acquisition, confidence in executing a clean company split is low.

### Strongest counterarguments and why they don't prevail

**Counter: "Separation forces strategic clarity and prevents the DVD tail from wagging the streaming dog."**
This is the strongest argument for splitting. It's also available without brand separation. Internal divisional separation with distinct leadership, P&Ls, and engineering teams achieves the same strategic clarity without customer-facing disruption. The forcing function doesn't require forcing customers onto two websites.

**Counter: "DVD is dying anyway — rip the bandage off now."**
DVD is declining at ~5% per quarter organically. That's a managed decline generating high-margin cash flow. The Qwikster split accelerates this decline through added friction, converting a 3-5 year cash runway into a 1-2 year one. The streaming business needs every quarter of DVD subsidy it can get while content costs surge.

**Counter: "Reversing looks weak and indecisive to the market."**
Short-term, yes. But the market has already priced in catastrophe (77% stock decline). A reversal paired with a credible streaming investment narrative gives analysts something to model. Proceeding with a plan the market has rejected provides no upside catalyst. The choice is between looking indecisive for a quarter and looking deaf indefinitely.

**Counter: "Some Wall Street analysts support the strategic logic."**
They support the *logic*, not the *execution*. The logic (streaming is the future, DVD is legacy) is obvious. The execution (two brands, two sites, two logins, announced without securing basic assets like a Twitter handle, layered on top of a 60% price increase) is what the market rejected. You can accept the logic while rejecting this specific plan.

## Recommended Next Artifact

1. **Reversal Communication Plan** — Draft the public announcement killing Qwikster. Tone: humble, brief, forward-looking. Acknowledge the mistake without excessive self-flagellation. Pair with a streaming content investment roadmap to redirect the narrative from "Netflix retreats" to "Netflix doubles down on streaming."

2. **Internal Reorganization Plan** — Design the divisional separation that captures the operational benefits of the split without customer-facing disruption. Separate P&Ls, engineering teams, and product roadmaps for streaming and DVD. Appoint a DVD GM with a mandate to maximize cash extraction during managed decline.

3. **Subscriber Recovery Playbook** — Model the 800K lost subscribers by segment (DVD-only, streaming-only, combo). Design targeted win-back campaigns. Establish a 90-day retention dashboard to track whether the bleeding has stopped.

4. **Streaming Content Investment Thesis** — With content costs surging to $2B, Netflix needs a defensible content strategy. Evaluate the pivot from licensed content to original production (lower per-unit cost, exclusive by definition, builds catalog as permanent asset rather than rented library). This is the strategic move that justifies the streaming bet to investors.

5. **Competitive Response Matrix** — Map Amazon, Hulu, and HBO positioning, pricing, and content moves. Identify where Netflix has structural advantages (recommendation algorithm, global infrastructure, brand recognition) and where it's vulnerable (content library size vs. DVD, price sensitivity after the increase).
