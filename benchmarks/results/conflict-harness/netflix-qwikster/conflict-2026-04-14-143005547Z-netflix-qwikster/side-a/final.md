# Revised artifact

## Recommendation
Reverse the Qwikster split immediately and keep DVD-by-mail and streaming under the Netflix brand. Do not proceed with separate customer-facing websites, bills, queues, and logins in September 2011.

Instead, pair the reversal with a hard internal separation model: distinct business-unit leaders, separate operating plans, ring-fenced streaming investment, and board-level review of segment economics. The decision here is not between full organizational blur and a public brand split. It is between preserving customer trust while installing real internal accountability, or compounding an active trust collapse with more friction.

## Why Reverse Course
### 1. The announced split adds friction at the exact moment Netflix has the least permission to ask for it
Netflix has already absorbed the consequences of the July 2011 price increase: combined-plan pricing moved from $9.99 bundled to $7.99 each, customers reacted as if value had been taken away, Q3 subscribers fell from about 24.6M to 23.8M, and customer satisfaction dropped from 79 to 52. Qwikster layers separate logins, separate queues, separate billing, and a second brand onto that same angry customer base. That is not strategic focus from the customer perspective; it is a convenience tax.

### 2. DVD remains the funding bridge for a streaming business that is strategically necessary but not yet self-supporting
The DVD business is declining, but it is still a large, high-margin cash generator at roughly $1B in revenue. Streaming, by contrast, is strategically critical yet economically strained: content licensing is rising from $180M in 2010 toward a projected $2B in 2012, the streaming catalog is only about 20,000 titles versus roughly 100,000 on DVD, and competition from Amazon, Hulu, and HBO is intensifying. Under these conditions, management should harvest DVD cash flows to accelerate streaming, not destabilize the cash engine through an avoidable customer-facing split.

### 3. Strategic separation is necessary, but public separation is the wrong instrument right now
The critique is correct that "internal separation" cannot mean vague intent. If Netflix reverses Qwikster, it should still impose explicit accountability structures so streaming is not trapped behind DVD inertia:

1. Name a dedicated streaming leader with authority over content, product, and international expansion.
2. Run separate segment P&Ls and board reporting for DVD and streaming.
3. Ring-fence a minimum streaming investment envelope so DVD cash generation cannot repeatedly crowd it out.
4. Set separate operating metrics: DVD for cash yield and churn management; streaming for engagement, catalog depth, and contribution trajectory.
5. Revisit structural separation only after predefined triggers are met, rather than as a branding event imposed during a trust crisis.

This does not deliver every theoretical benefit of a clean public split. It does, however, capture the critical near-term benefit that matters most in September 2011: freeing streaming strategy and resource allocation without forcing customers through another perceived downgrade.

## Strongest Counterarguments, and Why They Still Do Not Win
### Counterargument: Only a full public split creates credible external accountability for streaming
A public split would create clearer optics, but the immediate problem is not investor reporting design; it is customer and execution failure. The stock collapse, subscriber losses, and brand damage show that external clarity is not valuable if it is purchased through self-inflicted churn. Netflix can create real accountability through separate leadership, segment reporting, and ring-fenced capital allocation now, then revisit a structural split once trust and economics stabilize.

### Counterargument: Shared brand and leadership will let DVD keep dominating management attention
That risk is real, which is why reversal must be paired with explicit governance changes rather than a simple retreat to the old model. But the answer to an internal-governance problem is not necessarily a customer-experience break. In September 2011, the larger error is to impose visible service fragmentation before streaming has reached clear catalog and economic readiness.

### Counterargument: DVD decline is inevitable, so management should force migration immediately
Forced migration works only when the replacement product is clearly superior. It is not. Streaming has far fewer titles, rising content costs, and stronger competition. Pushing users off the better-stocked service before the substitute is ready raises the odds that they defect, not that they migrate.

## Immediate Actions
1. Publicly cancel the Qwikster launch and restore the message that customers have one Netflix relationship.
2. Announce the internal separation model at the same time so reversal is framed as operationally disciplined, not indecisive.
3. Freeze additional customer-facing complexity for two quarters.
4. Protect DVD cash flow while accelerating streaming catalog investment and product improvement.
5. Define future separation triggers tied to economics and customer readiness, not narrative preference.

## Decision Frame
Reverse the customer-facing Qwikster split. Keep one Netflix brand and account experience, but immediately install hard internal separation with distinct leadership, segment economics, and protected streaming investment. The core judgment is that Netflix should solve for strategic focus without asking customers to absorb more friction during an active trust collapse.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- Exact contribution margins by DVD-only, streaming-only, and combined cohorts.
- How much incremental churn comes specifically from account and queue fragmentation versus the earlier price increase.
- What minimum streaming catalog depth and contribution profile would make a future structural split viable.
- How investors would value separate segment disclosure without a public brand separation.
- Whether ring-fenced streaming investment would be sufficient in practice or whether stronger structural autonomy would still be required later.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass for reversing Qwikster now: evidence is strong that the announced form of separation is damaging customers, brand trust, and near-term economics.

Pass with conditions for internal separation: only if Netflix adopts explicit accountability mechanisms rather than returning to an undifferentiated combined model.

Fail for a full public split now: streaming is not yet strong enough on catalog depth or economics to justify forcing customers through a second major value shock.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A 90-day operating recovery plan specifying the internal separation model, streaming investment guardrails, customer trust repair actions, and milestone-based criteria for any future structural split.
