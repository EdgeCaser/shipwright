# Strategic Recommendation: Reverse the Qwikster Split Immediately

**To:** Netflix Leadership Team
**From:** Strategy Advisory
**Date:** September 2011
**Classification:** Confidential

---

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Should Netflix proceed with spinning DVD-by-mail into a separate company (Qwikster), or reverse course and keep both services under the Netflix brand?

**Recommendation: Reverse the Qwikster split. Keep both services under the Netflix brand. Execute the strategic separation internally, not customer-facing.**

The strategic logic of separating streaming from DVD is sound. The execution — forcing customers to manage two accounts, two queues, two billing relationships, and two brands — is catastrophically wrong. The Qwikster plan conflates *organizational separation* (which is correct) with *brand and customer-experience separation* (which is destructive). These are independent decisions, and Netflix is treating them as one.

### The Asymmetry of This Decision

Reversing now carries a one-time credibility cost: Hastings looks indecisive. Proceeding carries compounding costs: continued subscriber losses, accelerated churn of the DVD cash-flow engine that funds streaming content acquisition, and a brand fracture during the most critical competitive window Netflix has ever faced.

The credibility cost is recoverable. The subscriber and cash-flow costs may not be.

---

## Analysis

### 1. The DVD Business Is a Cash Engine, Not a Liability

DVD-by-mail generates approximately $1B in revenue at high margins. Streaming operates at a loss. The entire streaming content strategy — including the $2B projected licensing spend for 2012 and international expansion into Latin America and the UK — is subsidized by DVD cash flow.

Spinning Qwikster off does not make this subsidy disappear; it makes it harder to execute. Two separate companies with separate P&Ls create transfer-pricing complexity, split investor attention, and remove the financial flexibility to cross-subsidize during the critical 2011-2013 content acquisition window.

**The DVD business should be managed for harvest, not exile.** A declining business that funds your growth engine is an asset until it isn't. At ~5% quarterly organic decline, DVD still has 3-5 years of meaningful cash generation. Separating it now sacrifices optionality for symbolic clarity.

### 2. The Customer Experience Destruction Is Unforced

The Qwikster plan requires customers who want both services to:
- Maintain two separate accounts with separate logins
- Manage two separate queues
- Lose integrated reviews and recommendations across formats
- Pay two separate bills

This is a pure negative for every customer who uses both services. There is no customer benefit. The ~20,000 streaming titles vs. ~100,000 DVD titles means DVD fills critical catalog gaps — customers who stream *and* rent DVDs do so precisely because the integrated experience lets them find content in one place and get it however it's available.

Breaking this integration punishes your most engaged customers — the ones who value Netflix enough to pay for both services. These are the last customers you should be driving away during a period where you've already lost 800,000 subscribers.

### 3. Competitive Timing Is Terrible

Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO all launched in 2010-2011. Netflix's primary moat is its brand, recommendation engine, and unified content experience. Qwikster fragments all three.

Every month of subscriber confusion is a month where competitors can establish habits. A customer who cancels Netflix DVD service because they don't want to manage a separate Qwikster account doesn't come back — they find that the DVD catalog gap matters less than they thought, or they fill it with Amazon or Redbox.

### 4. The Market Signal Is Not Noise

A 77% stock decline and 800,000 lost subscribers in a single quarter is not an overreaction. Customer satisfaction scores dropping from 79 to 52 (ACSI) in one quarter represents a genuine destruction of brand equity.

The 27,000+ negative comments on the Qwikster announcement and the embarrassing Twitter handle situation are symptoms. The disease is that customers perceive Netflix as acting against their interests — first with the 60% price increase, now with forced account separation. Each additional day that Qwikster remains the plan reinforces this narrative.

### 5. Internal Separation Can Happen Without Brand Separation

The valid strategic insight — that DVD logistics constrain streaming product decisions — can be addressed through internal organizational design:
- Separate engineering teams and roadmaps
- Separate P&L tracking for investor transparency
- Dedicated streaming leadership empowered to make decisions without DVD considerations
- Gradual UI deprioritization of DVD features as the subscriber base naturally declines

None of this requires a customer-facing brand split. Amazon runs dozens of distinct businesses under one account and one brand. The organizational separation is an internal architecture decision, not a customer experience decision.

## Counterarguments Addressed

**"The split gives strategic clarity to investors."**
Investors can get strategic clarity through segment reporting without brand separation. The stock dropped 77% — investors are not rewarding strategic clarity; they are punishing subscriber destruction. Reversing restores confidence that management is responsive to market signals, which is itself a form of strategic clarity.

**"Delaying the split just delays the inevitable."**
The DVD business will decline regardless. But a managed decline under the Netflix brand, where DVD subscribers naturally migrate to streaming, preserves more lifetime value than a forced separation that triggers immediate cancellations. Let customers leave on their own timeline, not yours.

**"Hastings will look weak if he reverses."**
Hastings already apologized for the price increase communication. A reversal framed as "we listened to customers" is consistent with that posture. The alternative — stubbornly proceeding while losing subscribers — looks weaker. Markets reward leaders who correct course quickly over those who double down on failing strategies.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Internal Organizational Separation Plan (PRD)**

Once the Qwikster reversal is announced, the leadership team needs a concrete plan for achieving the *valid* goals of the original separation — freeing streaming product decisions from DVD constraints — through internal organizational design rather than brand separation. This PRD should cover:

1. **Org structure:** Separate streaming and DVD engineering teams with independent roadmaps
2. **P&L transparency:** Segment reporting that gives investors the strategic clarity they need
3. **DVD sunset timeline:** Managed decline plan with trigger points for further cost reduction
4. **Customer migration incentives:** Programs to accelerate natural DVD-to-streaming migration
5. **Content acquisition strategy:** How DVD cash flow funds the streaming content buildout through 2013

This is the artifact that turns the reversal from a retreat into a strategic pivot — same destination, better path.
