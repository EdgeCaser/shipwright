# Reverse Course: Keep DVD and Streaming Under the Netflix Brand

Netflix should reverse the Qwikster split immediately and keep both services under the Netflix brand, while still separating the businesses operationally behind the scenes. The strategic logic for decoupling DVD and streaming is directionally right, but the announced form of separation is an execution failure severe enough to damage the core asset the company actually needs to fund the streaming transition: customer trust and the DVD cash engine.

The central mistake is not recognizing that, in September 2011, Netflix is running two businesses with different futures but one customer relationship. Forcing separate websites, logins, billing, and queues imposes extra friction exactly when customers are already angry over a 60% effective price increase. The result is predictable: subscriber losses, collapsing satisfaction, and brand damage that weakens both businesses at once. A split that reduces convenience while delivering no visible customer benefit is strategically self-defeating.

Netflix still needs DVD cash flow. DVD remains a roughly $1B high-margin business and is subsidizing a streaming business that is operating at a loss while content licensing costs surge from $180M in 2010 toward a projected $2B in 2012. That means leadership cannot treat DVD as a legacy nuisance to be discarded cosmetically. The DVD business is the financing bridge for streaming, international expansion, and content acquisition. A public-facing split that accelerates DVD churn or triggers broader cancellations destroys the bridge before streaming is ready to stand alone.

The strongest argument for proceeding is that streaming needs strategic clarity. Competitors including Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO are entering the market, and the streaming catalog is far smaller than DVD. Leadership is right that streaming must move faster and cannot remain culturally anchored to a declining logistics business. But those are arguments for internal operating separation, capital discipline, and clearer reporting, not for making customers manage two brands and two experiences. The company can create independent product, engineering, content, and P&L accountability without asking customers to absorb the org chart.

Reversal is also the better choice because brand repair is now the immediate priority. The stock collapse, the 800,000 subscriber loss in Q3, the drop in customer satisfaction from 79 to 52, and the overwhelmingly negative public response show that management has exhausted its margin for another trust-breaking move. Continuing with Qwikster would signal stubbornness over judgment. Reversing course would be painful, but it would demonstrate that leadership can distinguish strategic conviction from attachment to a bad implementation.

The recommended path is: cancel Qwikster as a separate consumer brand; keep one Netflix account, one login, one bill, and unified discovery where possible; run DVD and streaming as internally distinct business units with separate operating metrics; continue pricing them separately if needed, but justify pricing through value and simplicity rather than fragmentation; and present investors with a clear transition narrative showing DVD as a managed cash generator funding streaming scale.

This recommendation does not deny that DVD is declining. It accepts the decline and manages it deliberately. Netflix should harvest DVD cash, slow unnecessary churn, and use the time gained to improve streaming selection, economics, and international readiness. When streaming can win on its own merits, structural separation may become viable. In September 2011, it is premature.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Reverse the Qwikster split and keep both services under the Netflix brand.

Why this is the better decision:
- Customer friction from separate brands, sites, billing, and queues compounds an already severe trust crisis.
- DVD cash flow is still strategically essential because streaming is loss-making and content costs are rapidly increasing.
- The customer-facing split provides little customer value while threatening churn in both businesses.
- Internal separation can deliver most of the strategic benefits without the brand and experience damage.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- Exact contribution margin by DVD subscriber cohort and how much faster churn would accelerate under Qwikster.
- Whether separate financial disclosure alone would satisfy investors seeking streaming clarity.
- The near-term elasticity of standalone streaming demand after the pricing backlash stabilizes.
- How quickly streaming catalog quality can improve relative to rising licensing costs and competitive entry.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if:
- Leadership commits publicly within days to cancel Qwikster and restore a unified customer experience.
- The company establishes internal business-unit accountability for DVD and streaming without a consumer-brand split.
- A 2-3 quarter recovery plan is built around churn reduction, trust repair, and streaming content improvement.

Fail if:
- Leadership insists on preserving the announced customer-facing split despite evidence of trust collapse.
- DVD is managed as expendable before streaming economics and catalog depth are ready.
- Investor messaging focuses on strategic purity while ignoring customer experience damage.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A 2-quarter transition plan covering brand repair, pricing communication, DVD cash-flow management, streaming content investment priorities, and operating-model separation by business unit.
