# Reverse Course on Qwikster

## Recommendation
Reverse the Qwikster split now. Keep DVD and streaming under the Netflix brand, on one account system, with one queue relationship where possible, one bill, and one customer promise. Preserve internal financial and operating separation behind the scenes, but do not force a consumer-facing corporate split in September 2011.

The strategic logic behind separation is real: streaming is the future, DVD is declining, and the two businesses have different economics. But the announced form of separation is destroying more value than it creates. Netflix is trying to execute a painful pricing reset, a brand split, a product experience downgrade, and an international expansion wave at the same time. That is too much trust extraction from customers in one move.

## Why Reverse Course
First, the immediate constraint is not organizational drag. It is trust collapse. The company already lost 800,000 subscribers in Q3 2011 after the 60% effective increase in the combined plan, customer satisfaction fell from 79 to 52 in one quarter, and the Qwikster announcement generated overwhelming public backlash. Adding separate logins, queues, billing, and a confused second brand compounds the very problem driving churn. The business does not have the customer goodwill needed to impose more friction right now. [ctx-1][ctx-2][ctx-3][ctx-4]

Second, the DVD business is still strategically valuable even if it is declining. It is generating about $1B in high-margin revenue and subsidizing a streaming business that is still loss-making while content licensing costs are surging from $180M in 2010 toward a projected $2B in 2012. That means leadership should harvest DVD cash flow carefully, not destabilize it with a separation that makes the combined proposition worse and accelerates subscriber loss. A declining cash engine should be managed for yield, not disrupted for symbolism. [ctx-5][ctx-6][ctx-7]

Third, Qwikster weakens Netflix precisely where competition is intensifying. Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO are all entering or scaling in streaming. Netflix cannot afford a self-inflicted downgrade in convenience while rivals improve their own offers. Streaming also has a materially smaller catalog than DVD, roughly 20,000 titles versus 100,000 on DVD, so many customers still experience the Netflix value proposition as the combination of both. Splitting the services makes the product feel worse before streaming is strong enough to stand fully on its own merits. [ctx-8][ctx-9]

## Strongest Counterargument
The best argument for proceeding is that bundling DVD and streaming creates strategic confusion. DVD is shrinking about 5% per quarter organically, streaming requires faster product decisions and heavy content investment, and a clean split could allow each business to optimize independently with clearer accountability. That logic is directionally correct. [ctx-6][ctx-10]

But it does not follow that a consumer-facing split is the right move now. Netflix can get most of the benefit through internal separation: distinct P&Ls, dedicated operating leaders, different investment thresholds, and explicit capital allocation rules. Customers do not need two brands, two websites, and two queues for management to run two businesses differently. The right sequence is internal decoupling first, customer disruption later only if the user value is obvious.

## Recommended Operating Plan
1. Cancel the Qwikster brand and preserve one Netflix identity.
2. Keep streaming and DVD available as separate priceable plans, but manage them through one account and one billing surface.
3. Create internal business-unit separation immediately: dedicated leadership, reporting lines, P&Ls, and roadmap ownership.
4. Position DVD as an add-on for breadth and long-tail selection while positioning streaming as the core future product.
5. Slow further customer-facing change until churn, satisfaction, and retention stabilize.
6. Use DVD cash flow to fund streaming content selectively, not indiscriminately, with tighter hurdle rates on licensing.
7. Rebuild trust with a simple customer promise: fewer surprises, one Netflix experience, clearer pricing.

## What Success Looks Like
A reversal does not mean abandoning the streaming future. It means buying time to reach it without blowing up the bridge that funds it. Success over the next 2 to 4 quarters should be judged by three outcomes: subscriber stabilization, partial recovery in customer satisfaction, and continued streaming adoption without a further collapse in the DVD base.

## Decision Frame
Decision: Reverse the announced Qwikster split and keep both services under the Netflix brand.

Why this decision passes the bar:
- It addresses the company’s binding constraint, which is trust and churn, not org-chart purity.
- It preserves the DVD cash flow still needed to fund streaming and absorb rising content costs.
- It avoids handing competitors an opening created by needless customer friction.
- It still allows internal strategic separation without consumer-facing damage.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- How much of Q3 churn is attributable to pricing alone versus the announced split.
- The true elasticity of DVD add-on demand if maintained inside one Netflix account.
- The degree to which internal org friction has measurably slowed streaming execution versus being asserted qualitatively.
- Near-term contractual flexibility on streaming content commitments and whether spending can be staged.
- Segment-level behavior: heavy DVD users, combo users, streaming-only users, and international cohorts may respond differently.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass:
- Clear decision with immediate executive actionability.
- Supported by the strongest facts in the packet: churn, satisfaction collapse, friction increase, DVD cash contribution, and streaming cost pressure.
- Addresses the strongest counterargument and offers a viable alternative implementation path.

Fail conditions to watch:
- If leadership interprets reversal as abandoning internal separation and cost discipline.
- If one-brand retention leads to hidden cross-subsidization without explicit capital allocation rules.
- If management delays streaming investment decisions under the cover of integration.
