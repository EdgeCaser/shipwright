# Revised artifact

## Recommendation
Reverse the Qwikster split now. Keep DVD and streaming under the Netflix brand, on one account, one billing relationship, and one customer promise. Separate the businesses internally as an interim operating model, but do not proceed with a consumer-facing split in September 2011.

The strategic logic behind separation is real: streaming is the future, DVD is declining, and the two businesses have different economics. But the announced form of separation is imposing certain, immediate damage in order to pursue organizational benefits that are plausible but not yet proven to require a second brand, second website, separate queues, and separate billing.

## Why Reverse Course
First, the binding constraint is customer trust collapse, not corporate-form purity. Netflix already lost 800,000 subscribers in Q3 2011 after the 60% effective increase in the combined plan, customer satisfaction fell from 79 to 52 in one quarter, and the Qwikster announcement triggered overwhelming backlash. Requiring separate logins, queues, billing, and a confusing second brand adds more friction to the exact area where the company is already breaking trust. [ctx-1][ctx-2][ctx-3][ctx-4]

Second, the DVD business is still strategically valuable even while declining. It generates about $1B in high-margin revenue and helps subsidize a streaming business that is still loss-making just as content licensing costs surge from $180M in 2010 toward a projected $2B in 2012. That makes preserving DVD cash flow more important than proving strategic boldness through a public split that could accelerate churn. [ctx-5][ctx-6][ctx-7]

Third, the split is badly timed against the market. Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO are entering or scaling in streaming, while Netflix’s streaming catalog remains much smaller than DVD, roughly 20,000 titles versus 100,000. Many customers still experience Netflix’s value as the combined convenience of both services. Splitting them now makes the product feel worse before streaming is strong enough to carry the full proposition alone. [ctx-8][ctx-9]

## Strongest Counterargument
The strongest case for proceeding is that bundling DVD and streaming creates real strategic drag. DVD is shrinking about 5% per quarter organically, streaming requires faster product and content decisions, and leadership believes DVD logistics constrain streaming execution. That is a legitimate concern. [ctx-6][ctx-10]

But the decision standard here should be comparative certainty. The customer harm from Qwikster is already observable; the incremental strategic benefit of a consumer-facing split over an internal operating separation is not. Leadership should not compound a proven trust crisis in order to capture an unquantified organizational upside. The lower-regret move is to reverse the public split, create distinct leadership and P&L accountability internally, and then test over the next 2 to 4 quarters whether deeper separation is actually needed.

## Recommended Operating Plan
1. Cancel the Qwikster brand and preserve one Netflix identity.
2. Keep streaming and DVD as separately priceable plans, but inside one account and one billing surface.
3. Establish internal business-unit separation immediately: dedicated leaders, reporting lines, P&Ls, and explicit capital allocation rules.
4. Treat this as a staged operating experiment: if internal separation does not reduce execution drag, leadership can revisit deeper structural options later.
5. Position DVD as an add-on for breadth and long-tail selection while positioning streaming as the core future product.
6. Pause further customer-facing disruption until churn and satisfaction stabilize.
7. Rebuild trust with a simpler customer promise: clearer pricing, fewer surprises, one Netflix experience.

## What Success Looks Like
Success over the next 2 to 4 quarters is not "proving" the original announcement right. It is stabilizing subscribers, recovering some customer satisfaction, preserving DVD contribution, and continuing streaming adoption without adding further self-inflicted friction.

## Decision Frame
Decision: Reverse the announced Qwikster split and keep both services under the Netflix brand.

Why this decision passes the bar:
- It addresses the company’s immediate binding constraint: churn, trust loss, and product friction.
- It preserves the DVD cash flow still needed to fund streaming under rising content costs.
- It avoids handing competitors an opening created by a self-inflicted downgrade in convenience.
- It keeps strategic flexibility by allowing internal separation now while deferring any consumer-facing split until the benefits are demonstrated more clearly.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- How much of Q3 churn is attributable to pricing alone versus the Qwikster announcement.
- How much operational drag truly comes from shared internal systems and teams versus from the consumer-facing bundle itself.
- Whether internal P&L and leadership separation will materially improve streaming execution speed.
- The retention and contribution margin of DVD users if DVD is reframed as an add-on inside one Netflix account.
- Near-term flexibility in streaming content commitments and whether spending can be staged more tightly.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass:
- Clear decision with immediate executive actionability.
- Anchored in the strongest observed evidence: churn, satisfaction collapse, added friction, DVD cash contribution, and content cost pressure.
- Treats the strategic case for separation seriously without overstating what is proven.

Fail conditions to watch:
- If leadership treats reversal as abandoning internal separation and cost discipline.
- If unified branding masks poor capital allocation between DVD and streaming.
- If internal separation fails to improve execution and leadership does not revisit structure with fresh evidence.
