# Strategic Recommendation: Reverse the Qwikster Split Immediately

## Decision Frame

**Question:** Should Netflix proceed with the Qwikster separation or reverse course and reunify both services under the Netflix brand?

**Recommendation: Reverse course. Kill Qwikster. Reunify immediately.**

The strategic logic of eventual DVD/streaming separation is sound, but the execution vehicle — a hard split into two companies with separate logins, queues, billing, and brands — destroys customer value, increases the risk of accelerated subscriber losses, and jeopardizes the cash flow engine funding streaming's expansion. The correct move is an internal organizational separation with a unified customer-facing brand.

### Core Argument

Three facts make reversal the only viable path:

1. **The DVD business funds the streaming transition — and the split creates unnecessary downside risk to that cash flow.** DVD-by-mail generates ~$1B in revenue at high margins. Streaming operates at a loss and faces content costs surging from $180M to $2B in two years. The Qwikster split introduces structural friction (separate accounts, separate billing, zero brand equity) that creates a clear mechanism for accelerated DVD churn: customers forced to actively manage a second relationship with an unknown brand will disproportionately drop the less-valued service. However, we must be precise about what we know and don't know. The 800K Q3 subscriber losses cannot be cleanly attributed between the July price increase and the September Qwikster announcement — these events are confounded. What we can say is that the split adds incremental churn risk on top of existing organic decline (~5%/quarter) and price-increase churn, at the worst possible time. Even a modest acceleration — say from 5% to 7-8% quarterly DVD decline — compresses the cash flow runway by 1-2 years during a period when streaming cannot yet stand alone. The prudent course is to eliminate the unnecessary risk factor rather than gamble that the split's impact on DVD churn will be negligible.

2. **The split destroys the integrated value proposition without creating new value.** Customers who subscribe to both DVD and streaming would need separate websites, separate queues, separate billing, and separate review histories. The Qwikster brand carries zero equity. The Netflix brand carries enormous equity. Splitting forces customers to actively choose — and behavioral economics consistently shows that adding friction to an existing relationship increases abandonment. The 800K subscriber loss in Q3 and customer satisfaction dropping from 79 to 52 demonstrate that Netflix is already in a crisis of customer trust. The split compounds that crisis by adding operational burden to an already angry customer base.

3. **Competitive timing makes this an asymmetric risk.** Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO all launched in 2010-2011. Netflix's streaming library is ~20,000 titles versus ~100,000 on DVD — a genuine content gap that competitors can exploit. Fragmenting the brand, damaging customer trust, and handing competitors a narrative ("Netflix doesn't care about its customers") creates asymmetric downside. The upside of organizational separation can be captured internally; the downside of brand fragmentation during a competitive inflection cannot be undone.

### Why the Pro-Split Arguments Fail

**"Separation enables each business to optimize independently."** This is an organizational design problem, not a corporate structure problem. Netflix can create separate P&L centers, separate product teams, and separate roadmaps internally without forcing customers to manage two accounts. Amazon runs dozens of distinct businesses under one brand and one login. The argument confuses internal structure with customer-facing architecture.

**"DVD is dying — rip the bandage off."** DVD subscribers are declining ~5% per quarter organically. That is a managed decline, not a crisis requiring emergency amputation. At that rate, DVD generates significant cash flow for 3-5 more years — precisely the window Netflix needs to build streaming content library depth and complete international expansion. Adding churn risk through a hostile brand split is unnecessary when the organic decline is already manageable.

**"Wall Street wants strategic clarity."** Some analysts do see logic in separation. But the stock has dropped 77%. The market is not rewarding strategic clarity — it is punishing execution incompetence and customer hostility. Reversing course will be read as management listening to customers and correcting a mistake, which restores confidence faster than doubling down on an unpopular decision.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

| Unknown | Why It Matters | How to Resolve |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| **What fraction of the 800K lost subscribers left due to price increase vs. Qwikster announcement?** | This is the critical attribution gap. If losses are primarily price-driven, reversing Qwikster alone won't stop the bleeding — but it still eliminates an unnecessary compounding risk factor. If Qwikster-driven, reversal has immediate positive impact on retention. | Segment churn data by subscription type (streaming-only vs. combo vs. DVD-only) and timing (July price increase vs. September Qwikster announcement). Analyze cancel reason codes and customer service contact logs. |
| **What is the marginal churn impact of requiring separate accounts for DVD service?** | Directly tests the core mechanism of claim 1. If combo subscribers show low DVD queue engagement, forced separation may merely formalize existing disuse. If engagement remains high, the friction cost is real. | Analyze DVD queue activity among combo subscribers: last-30-day disc shipments, queue additions, rating activity. High-activity combo subscribers are the at-risk cohort. |
| **How many combo subscribers would downgrade to streaming-only under reunification?** | Determines how much DVD revenue is recoverable. If most combo subscribers already mentally separated, DVD cash flow projections are optimistic regardless of Qwikster. | Survey current combo subscribers on intent. Cross-reference with queue activity data. |
| **What are the actual content licensing costs for 2012-2013?** | $2B projected is a number without confirmed deal structure. If costs are lower or more back-loaded, the urgency of DVD cash flow preservation changes. | Review committed vs. projected licensing obligations. Identify which deals are locked vs. negotiable. |
| **Can Netflix renegotiate content deals from a position of declining subscriber count?** | Content owners price licenses based on reach. Losing subscribers weakens Netflix's negotiating position for the very content it needs to make streaming viable. | Assess current negotiation pipeline and whether recent subscriber losses have already affected deal terms. |

## Recommended Next Artifact

1. **Churn Attribution Analysis:** Before finalizing the communication plan, segment Q3 churn by subscription type, timing (pre/post Qwikster announcement), and cancel reason codes. This resolves the key evidence gap and calibrates how aggressively to pursue subscriber recovery.

2. **Customer Communication Plan:** Draft the reversal announcement. Tone must be: "We heard you. We were wrong about how to execute this, not about the strategic direction. DVD and streaming stay together under Netflix." Avoid re-apologizing for the price increase — that's a separate issue and conflating them weakens both messages.

3. **Internal Reorganization Proposal:** Design the internal separation of DVD and streaming teams — separate P&Ls, separate product roadmaps, separate engineering priorities — without any customer-facing changes. This preserves the valid strategic insight while eliminating customer harm.

4. **Subscriber Recovery Forecast:** Model expected subscriber recovery over Q4 2011 and Q1 2012 under three scenarios: (a) reversal only, (b) reversal + targeted retention offers for churned subscribers, (c) reversal + partial price rollback. Determine which lever has the highest ROI.

5. **Content Strategy Review:** Given the streaming library gap (20K vs 100K DVD titles) and surging licensing costs, evaluate whether the 2012 content spend of $2B is correctly allocated. Should Netflix begin shifting from licensed content toward original content development as a hedge against rising licensing costs and competitor bidding wars?
