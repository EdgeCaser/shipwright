# Strategic Recommendation: Reverse the Qwikster Split Immediately

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Should Netflix proceed with spinning off DVD-by-mail into a separate company (Qwikster), or reverse course and reunify both services under the Netflix brand?

**Recommendation: Reverse course. Kill Qwikster. Reunify immediately.**

The strategic logic of separation is sound in the abstract — DVD is declining, streaming is the future, and organizational focus matters. But the Qwikster execution is a catastrophic misread of timing, sequencing, and customer tolerance. The separation destroys more value than it creates, and the damage compounds with every week it remains the plan.

**The real decision is not "split vs. don't split." It is: "Can Netflix survive long enough to win streaming if it alienates its paying base and torches its brand now?"** The answer is no. The DVD business is the financial engine funding the streaming transition. Burning customer trust to separate them is strategic self-harm.

### Why reversal is correct

1. **The DVD business funds the streaming war.** DVD-by-mail generates ~$1B in revenue at high margins. Streaming operates at a loss and faces exploding content costs ($180M → $2B in two years). Separating the businesses doesn't change the cash flow dependency — it just makes it harder to manage. Netflix needs DVD profits to survive the streaming investment period. Alienating DVD subscribers accelerates the revenue decline that streaming cannot yet replace.

2. **The price increase already imposed the economic separation.** The July 2011 price change ($9.99 bundle → $7.99 + $7.99) effectively unbundled the services financially. Customers who only want streaming can pay $7.99. The economic logic of separation is already achieved. Qwikster adds operational separation on top — separate websites, separate logins, separate queues, separate billing — that delivers zero customer value and significant customer pain. The incremental "strategic clarity" is not worth the incremental friction.

3. **800,000 lost subscribers is a leading indicator, not a final number.** The Q3 loss represents the immediate reaction to the price increase. Qwikster compounds this by telling those same angry customers they must now manage two accounts. Churn will accelerate. At 5% quarterly organic DVD decline already baked in, adding brand damage and UX friction could double or triple the attrition rate through Q1 2012.

4. **Brand equity is the moat, and it's on fire.** Netflix's competitive advantage in streaming is not technology — Amazon, Hulu, and HBO can all build streaming platforms. The advantage is the brand: 24 million households associate "Netflix" with convenient home entertainment. ACSI scores dropping from 79 to 52 in one quarter represents a generational brand wound. Every week Qwikster remains the plan, competitors benefit from Netflix's self-inflicted distraction.

5. **The Qwikster execution signals organizational dysfunction.** The Twitter handle problem is minor but symbolic — the team didn't check if @Qwikster was available. The separate-everything architecture (logins, queues, reviews) suggests the plan was designed from an org chart perspective, not a customer perspective. Proceeding signals to investors, employees, and content partners that leadership has lost the plot.

### Why the pro-split arguments are wrong (or premature)

**"DVD constrains streaming product decisions."** This is real but solvable without a corporate split. Netflix can create internal business units with separate P&Ls, separate product teams, and separate roadmaps — all under one brand, one login, one customer relationship. The organizational benefits of focus do not require customer-facing separation.

**"Investors need to see streaming as a standalone business."** Investors are currently pricing Netflix at $65 (down from $298). The market is not rewarding strategic clarity — it is punishing execution chaos. Reversing course and demonstrating disciplined management will do more for the stock than doubling down on an unpopular plan.

**"The DVD business will die anyway, so rip the bandaid off."** Correct that DVD will decline. Incorrect that accelerating the decline helps. A managed decline over 3-5 years generates billions in cumulative cash flow to fund streaming content acquisition and international expansion. An unmanaged collapse triggered by brand damage leaves Netflix underfunded in a content arms race against Amazon, Apple, and HBO's parent companies — all of which have deeper pockets.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

| Unknown | Why it matters | How to resolve |
|---|---|---|
| **Subscriber intent data post-announcement** | We know 800K left, but we don't know how many remaining subscribers are actively considering cancellation. Churn pipeline matters more than churn actuals. | Survey current subscribers within 72 hours on intent to keep/cancel. Internal data on queue activity decline, login frequency drops. |
| **Content partner reaction** | Studios may view the chaos as leverage to demand higher licensing fees, or as a reason to withhold content and launch their own services sooner. | Direct outreach to top 10 content partners to gauge sentiment shift. |
| **Employee morale and retention** | A public reversal could be read as leadership instability, or as leadership listening. Internal temperature matters for execution capacity. | Anonymous internal pulse survey before announcement. |
| **International subscriber sensitivity** | Canada, Latin America, and UK launches are recent. These markets have no DVD legacy — does the Qwikster controversy bleed into international brand perception? | Market-specific brand tracking data from international launch teams. |
| **Competitive response timeline** | Amazon Prime Video and Hulu Plus positioning during Netflix's distraction period. Are they accelerating content deals or marketing spend to capture defectors? | Competitive intelligence sweep on recent content licensing announcements and ad spend. |
| **DVD-to-streaming conversion rate** | What percentage of DVD subscribers also stream? If high, separation forces them to manage two relationships for what they experience as one service. If low, separation is less painful. | Internal data on overlap — likely 60-75% based on industry estimates, which would make separation acutely painful for the majority. |

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**This recommendation is ready for decision. Here's why:**

| Criterion | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **Reversibility** | PASS — Reversal is low-cost. Qwikster has not launched. No systems have been separated. The only cost is perceived indecisiveness, which is cheaper than perceived incompetence. |
| **Evidence sufficiency** | PASS — The core data points (subscriber loss, stock decline, ACSI drop, cash flow dependency) are hard numbers, not projections. The direction is unambiguous even if magnitudes are uncertain. |
| **Downside risk of waiting** | FAIL if delayed — Every week the Qwikster plan remains active, more subscribers leave and competitors gain ground. The reversal announcement should happen within days, not weeks. |
| **Stakeholder alignment** | CONDITIONAL — Hastings has publicly defended the logic. Reversal requires him to publicly admit error. This is the hardest part but also the most important: a CEO willing to reverse a bad decision is stronger than one who doubles down. The apology template already exists from July. |
| **Execution complexity** | PASS — "We're not doing Qwikster" is operationally simple. The price increase stays (it's economically necessary). The brand stays unified. Communicate three things: (1) we heard you, (2) Qwikster is cancelled, (3) both services stay under Netflix. |

**Key risk of this recommendation:** The reversal may be perceived as reactive and panicked rather than thoughtful. Mitigation: Frame it as "listening to customers" not "admitting failure." Pair the announcement with a forward-looking streaming investment message to redirect the narrative.

## Recommended Next Artifact

1. **Reversal Communication Plan** — Draft the public announcement (blog post + press release) for Hastings. Tone: humble but forward-looking. Core message: "We moved too fast on the wrong thing. DVD and streaming stay together under Netflix. Here's what we're investing in next." Include employee all-hands talking points and investor call prep.

2. **Subscriber Retention Sprint** — 90-day plan to recover trust with existing subscribers. Potential tactics: temporary goodwill credit for combo plan holders, accelerated streaming library additions, improved recommendation engine visibility. Goal: halt churn by Q4 2011, stabilize by Q1 2012.

3. **Internal Separation Roadmap** — Achieve the organizational benefits of focus without customer-facing separation. Create DVD and Streaming business units with separate leadership, separate P&Ls, and a shared technology platform. This gives the operational clarity Hastings wanted without the customer pain Qwikster would have caused.

4. **Competitive Response Assessment** — Immediate intelligence gathering on Amazon, Hulu, and HBO positioning during the distraction window. Inform Q1 2012 content acquisition priorities and marketing spend allocation.
