# Strategic Recommendation Memo: Reverse Course on Qwikster

## Recommendation
Reverse the Qwikster split immediately and keep both DVD-by-mail and streaming under the Netflix brand, while internally separating operating accountability for the two businesses.

The strategic logic behind separation is directionally right: streaming is the future, DVD is a declining but still valuable cash engine, and each business has different economics. But the announced form of separation is the wrong move at the wrong time. It compounds a severe trust crisis created by the 60% price increase, adds customer friction with no offsetting customer benefit, and jeopardizes the cash flow that funds streaming content and international expansion.

## Why Reverse Course
First, the company is in an acute confidence and retention crisis, not a brand-architecture optimization window. Netflix already lost 800,000 subscribers in Q3 2011, customer satisfaction fell from 79 to 52 in one quarter, and the market reaction has been catastrophic, with the stock down 77% from its July peak. In that context, forcing customers into separate websites, separate queues, separate logins, and separate billing is not strategic clarity; it is self-inflicted churn.

Second, the DVD business is still strategically important even if it is declining. DVD subscribers are falling about 5% per quarter organically, but the business still generates roughly $1B in high-margin revenue and subsidizes a streaming business that is still operating at a loss. Streaming content costs are surging from $180M in 2010 to a projected $2B in 2012, while the streaming catalog remains far thinner than DVD, about 20,000 titles versus 100,000. Netflix cannot afford to destabilize the profit pool that funds the transition before streaming economics are mature.

Third, the announced split creates customer pain without solving the core strategic problem. The real issue is internal resource allocation and managerial focus, not whether customers see one brand or two. Netflix can get most of the operating benefits of separation by creating distinct P&L accountability, product roadmaps, and executive ownership inside one customer-facing experience. That preserves strategic focus without making the customer do the organizational restructuring for the company.

Fourth, the competitive environment makes trust and simplicity more valuable, not less. Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO are all entering streaming. International expansion is underway in Canada, Latin America, and the UK. This is the moment to strengthen the Netflix brand and reduce friction, because customers have more alternatives than before. A fragmented experience weakens the one asset competitors cannot easily copy: Netflix's direct customer relationship.

## Strongest Counterarguments
The best argument for proceeding is that DVD and streaming are fundamentally different businesses, and bundling them obscures economics, slows decisions, and prevents streaming from being run like a focused growth company. That argument is real. DVD logistics, fulfillment, and title depth are different from streaming licensing, product UX, and international scaling.

But that does not require a public split into separate brands and customer journeys. The company should separate internally, not externally. The external split is especially damaging because it follows immediately after a large price increase, making customers interpret it as arrogance and neglect rather than strategic discipline. Even a good long-term strategy can fail if executed in a way that destroys trust faster than value is created.

Another counterargument is that reversing course could signal weakness and inconsistency. That is less dangerous than persisting in a visibly failed rollout. Leadership credibility is repaired by showing judgment, not stubbornness. Reed Hastings should frame the reversal as preserving strategic intent while correcting a customer-hostile execution mistake.

## What Netflix Should Do Instead
1. Cancel Qwikster as a separate customer-facing brand and keep one Netflix account, login, queue relationship, and billing surface.
2. Create internal business separation immediately: distinct leaders, operating plans, metrics, and capital allocation for DVD and streaming.
3. Preserve transparent reporting so investors can see streaming economics and DVD cash generation separately without forcing customer separation.
4. Shift near-term messaging from restructuring to service recovery: apologize, simplify, and stabilize churn.
5. Use DVD cash flows deliberately to fund streaming content and international expansion, with explicit thresholds for when streaming can stand on its own.
6. Revisit a deeper structural separation only after streaming has stronger content breadth, healthier unit economics, and customer trust has recovered.

## Decision Frame
Decision: Reverse course on the announced Qwikster split.

Why now: The decision is primarily about preserving customers, brand trust, and the cash engine needed to finance the streaming transition.

What must be true for this decision to work:
- Customer friction must decline immediately.
- DVD cash flow must be protected during the transition.
- Streaming must gain internal focus without sacrificing the integrated customer relationship.
- Management must show that it can correct execution mistakes quickly.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- Exact churn contribution from the price increase versus the Qwikster announcement.
- Whether investors would reward segment reporting alone without a formal separation.
- How long DVD margins can remain strong under ongoing organic decline.
- The pace at which streaming content obligations will pressure liquidity during international expansion.
- Whether a unified queue or cross-service recommendation layer could materially improve retention.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if:
- Netflix can announce reversal quickly, clearly, and credibly.
- Internal operating separation can be implemented without customer-visible fragmentation.
- Finance can ring-fence DVD cash flows to support streaming investment discipline.
- Leadership aligns on churn recovery as the immediate top priority.

Fail if:
- Management insists on external separation despite current trust damage.
- The company continues adding customer friction before restoring satisfaction.
- DVD cash flow erodes faster because of avoidable brand and experience disruption.
- The reversal is communicated ambiguously and prolongs confusion.

A 90-day recovery plan covering customer communications, churn stabilization actions, internal operating-model changes, investor messaging, and decision checkpoints for when to reconsider structural separation.
