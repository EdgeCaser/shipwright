# Strategic Recommendation Memo: Reverse Course on Qwikster

## Recommendation
Reverse the Qwikster split and keep both services under the Netflix brand, while still managing DVD and streaming as separate internal business units with distinct P&Ls, goals, and operating cadences.

The strategic logic behind separation is real: streaming is the future, DVD is declining, and the two businesses have different economics. But the announced form of separation is value-destructive because it adds customer friction precisely when Netflix needs trust, cash flow, and scale to fund the streaming transition.

## Why Reverse Course Is Correct

### 1. The split destroys customer value faster than it creates strategic clarity
Customers are not reacting to internal org design; they are reacting to a worse product. Qwikster would force separate websites, separate billing, separate queues, and separate logins. At the same time, customers already absorbed a 60% effective price increase on the combined plan. That combination converts a strategic transition into an avoidable service degradation.

This is already visible in the numbers: subscribers fell from 24.6M in Q2 2011 to 23.8M in Q3 2011, a loss of 800,000, while customer satisfaction collapsed from 79 to 52 in one quarter. When the market is telling you that the transition is too painful, adding more friction is not discipline; it is self-inflicted churn.

### 2. Netflix still needs DVD cash flow to finance the streaming war
DVD is declining, but it remains a large, high-margin cash generator, with roughly $1B in revenue and economics strong enough to subsidize a streaming business that is still operating at a loss. Meanwhile, streaming content costs are exploding, rising from $180M in 2010 to a projected $2B in 2012, and competition from Amazon, Hulu, and HBO is intensifying.

In that environment, leadership should protect the cash engine, not detach it from the core brand in a way that accelerates attrition. The right move is to harvest DVD cash efficiently while using it to buy time, content, and scale for streaming.

### 3. Brand fragmentation weakens Netflix at the exact moment it needs a single consumer promise
Netflix is trying to become the default global streaming brand while expanding internationally. Splitting the service into Netflix and Qwikster creates confusion about what Netflix even is. The operational details make this worse, not better: two brands, two experiences, and even a compromised social handle for Qwikster. That is not strategic clarity; it is a broken rollout.

A unified brand allows Netflix to communicate one customer promise: entertainment delivered however the subscriber wants it during the transition from discs to streaming. Internally, the company can separate incentives and roadmaps without asking customers to shoulder that complexity.

### 4. The company can get most of the strategic benefits of separation without the customer-facing split
The strongest pro-split argument is that bundling creates organizational drag. That is credible. But a legal and brand split is not the only remedy. Netflix can create separate internal product teams, operating metrics, pricing accountability, and capital-allocation discipline while preserving a single account, single queue migration path where possible, and a single billing relationship.

That approach preserves optionality. If DVD decline accelerates, Netflix can continue winding it down deliberately. If streaming economics improve, the company benefits. If customers still value both, Netflix keeps cross-sell and retention advantages.

## Strongest Counterarguments and Rebuttal

### Counterargument: Separation is necessary to force managerial focus
Rebuttal: Focus is needed, but the announced structure confuses customer experience with management architecture. Separate P&Ls and leadership teams can create focus without imposing two brands and two account systems.

### Counterargument: DVD decline is inevitable, so Netflix should stop protecting it
Rebuttal: Inevitable decline does not mean immediate disposability. A declining asset that throws off cash is strategically valuable when the growth business is capital-hungry and unprofitable.

### Counterargument: Reversing course signals weakness to Wall Street
Rebuttal: Persisting with a visibly broken rollout signals worse judgment. Investors can tolerate a tactical reversal if it protects subscriber economics, preserves brand trust, and clarifies a more credible transition plan.

## Recommended Near-Term Actions
1. Publicly reverse the Qwikster brand and website split immediately.
2. Keep streaming and DVD available under one Netflix account and one consumer identity.
3. Reframe the strategy externally: one brand, two plans, customer choice, gradual transition.
4. Create separate internal operating units with independent financial targets and leadership accountability.
5. Stabilize churn and trust before making any further packaging changes.
6. Use DVD cash flow to fund streaming content and international expansion, with explicit hurdle rates for content spending.

## Decision Frame
Reverse course on the customer-facing Qwikster split. Preserve one Netflix brand and unified account experience while separating DVD and streaming internally for management focus and capital discipline. The core decision is not whether streaming is the future; it is whether Netflix should force customers to absorb organizational complexity during a fragile transition. It should not.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- The exact retention uplift from keeping unified billing and identity versus forcing separation is not quantified.
- The degree to which DVD subscribers would continue cross-subsidizing streaming under alternative packaging needs cohort analysis.
- International implications of a split brand architecture are directionally negative but not precisely modeled here.
- More precise contribution-margin data by segment would improve the pace and sequencing of the transition plan.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if leadership is willing to distinguish strategic intent from rollout form: streaming-first strategy can continue without the Qwikster brand split.

Fail if leadership treats reversal as abandoning separation economics entirely. The needed correction is customer-facing integration with internal operating separation, not a return to strategic complacency.

A transition operating plan covering packaging, org structure, subscriber migration strategy, DVD cash-flow management, and investor communications for the next 4 quarters.
