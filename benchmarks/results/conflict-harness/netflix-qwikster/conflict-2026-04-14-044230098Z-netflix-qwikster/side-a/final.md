# Strategic Recommendation Memo: Reverse Course on Qwikster

## Recommendation
Reverse the Qwikster split and keep both services under the Netflix brand. Manage DVD and streaming as more clearly separated internal businesses, but do not assume that internal separation fully solves the focus problem. The reason to reverse is narrower and stronger: the customer-facing split is causing immediate value destruction that is better evidenced than the organizational upside of pushing ahead.

The strategic logic behind separation remains real. Streaming is the future, DVD is declining, and the two businesses have different economics. But the announced form of separation is still the wrong move in September 2011 because it adds friction, confusion, and brand damage precisely when Netflix needs trust, scale, and cash flow to fund the transition.

## Why Reverse Course Is Correct

### 1. The announced split makes the product worse at the exact moment customers are already signaling rejection
Customers are not objecting to an org chart. They are reacting to a degraded experience: separate websites, separate billing, separate queues, and separate logins, layered on top of a 60% effective price increase for the combined plan. That is not a subtle repositioning; it is a visible reduction in convenience.

The damage is already showing up in hard signals from the case packet. Subscribers fell from 24.6M in Q2 2011 to 23.8M in Q3 2011, a loss of 800,000. Customer satisfaction dropped from 79 to 52 in one quarter. The Qwikster announcement also triggered overwhelming negative customer feedback and an embarrassing brand execution failure around the social handle. When customers are already telling Netflix the transition is too painful, adding more friction is not strategic discipline. It is compounding a live error.

### 2. Netflix still needs DVD cash flow while streaming economics are deteriorating
DVD is declining, but it remains a meaningful cash engine, with roughly $1B in revenue and high margins, while streaming is still operating at a loss. At the same time, streaming content costs are surging from $180M in 2010 to a projected $2B in 2012, and competition from Amazon, Hulu, and HBO is intensifying.

That makes this a bad moment to accelerate attrition in the legacy business. Netflix should continue harvesting DVD cash flow to finance content acquisition, subscriber growth, and international expansion in streaming. A customer-facing split threatens that funding bridge right when it is most needed.

### 3. The evidence for immediate customer harm is stronger than the evidence that a public split is required for strategic focus
The strongest argument for Qwikster is that bundling DVD and streaming creates organizational drag. That concern is credible. But the case packet does not show that a separate consumer brand, separate websites, and separate account structure are necessary to achieve better focus. What it does show is the opposite side of the tradeoff with unusual clarity: the announced split introduces customer friction and brand confusion during a period of acute trust erosion.

That means the burden of proof should be high for proceeding. Leadership does not need to prove that internal separation captures all benefits of a split. It only needs to recognize that the customer costs of the announced model are immediate and evidenced, while the incremental organizational benefits of a full public separation are real but not demonstrated here at sufficient magnitude to justify those costs now.

### 4. Reversal preserves optionality while reducing current execution risk
Keeping one brand and one account relationship does not lock Netflix into indefinite operating integration. Leadership can still sharpen accountability through distinct business leadership, segment-level financial reporting, and explicit capital-allocation rules. But reversal avoids hard-coding a brittle customer experience into the transition.

That matters because Netflix is simultaneously navigating price backlash, rising content costs, new streaming competitors, and international expansion. In that context, optionality has value. A reversible internal management change is preferable to an externally visible split that is already damaging the brand and subscriber base.

## Strongest Counterarguments and Rebuttal

### Counterargument: Separation is necessary to force managerial focus
Rebuttal: Focus may indeed require stronger internal separation than Netflix has today. But the case for customer-facing separation is weaker than the case for internal accountability. The evidence in hand shows that separate brands, sites, billing, and queues are costly now; it does not show those exact features are necessary to create managerial focus.

### Counterargument: DVD decline is inevitable, so Netflix should stop protecting it
Rebuttal: Inevitable decline does not mean immediate disposability. A declining business with meaningful cash flow is strategically valuable when the growth business is capital-intensive and unprofitable.

### Counterargument: Reversing course signals weakness to Wall Street
Rebuttal: Continuing with a rollout that is already generating subscriber losses, collapsing satisfaction, and brand confusion signals worse judgment. A reversal can be framed as execution correction in service of the same streaming-first strategy.

## Recommended Near-Term Actions
1. Publicly reverse the Qwikster brand and website split immediately.
2. Keep DVD and streaming under one Netflix account, login, billing relationship, and brand.
3. State clearly that streaming remains the long-term priority, but customer complexity will not be used as the transition mechanism.
4. Create sharper internal separation through distinct leadership, segment reporting, and capital-allocation rules.
5. Freeze additional customer-facing packaging disruption until churn and satisfaction stabilize.
6. Use DVD cash flow deliberately to fund streaming content and international expansion, with explicit return thresholds for content commitments.

## Decision Frame
Reverse the customer-facing Qwikster split. Keep one Netflix brand and one unified account experience while increasing internal operating separation where useful. The central decision is not whether streaming is the future; it is whether Netflix should impose additional friction and confusion on customers during an already unstable transition. Based on the evidence in the case packet, it should not.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- The exact retention impact of a unified-account model versus the announced Qwikster structure over the next two quarters is not quantified.
- The magnitude of organizational-focus gains from stronger internal separation, with or without a public split, is not directly evidenced in the case packet.
- More precise cohort economics for DVD-only, streaming-only, and dual-plan subscribers would improve the transition plan.
- International brand and packaging implications are directionally negative for a split but not modeled in detail here.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if leadership accepts that the best-supported decision is to remove customer-facing complexity now while preserving the strategic commitment to streaming.

Fail if leadership insists that public separation must proceed despite lacking evidence that its organizational benefits outweigh the clearly visible customer and brand costs.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A 4-quarter transition operating plan covering subscriber packaging, internal accountability design, DVD cash-flow harvesting, streaming investment thresholds, and investor communications.
