# Strategic Recommendation: Reverse the Qwikster Split Immediately

**To:** Netflix Leadership Team
**From:** Strategy Advisory Group
**Date:** September 2011
**Classification:** Confidential

---

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Should Netflix proceed with splitting into two separate companies (Netflix for streaming, Qwikster for DVD-by-mail), or reverse course and maintain a unified brand?

**Recommendation: Reverse the Qwikster split. Keep both services under the Netflix brand. Execute the strategic separation operationally, not organizationally.**

The strategic logic behind separation is sound — streaming and DVD are diverging businesses with different cost structures, logistics, and growth trajectories. But the Qwikster execution destroys more value than the separation creates. The correct move is to internalize the separation (separate P&Ls, separate product teams, separate optimization loops) while preserving a unified customer experience.

### Why reversal is correct

**1. The DVD business is financing the streaming transition, and you cannot afford to sever that lifeline.**

DVD-by-mail generates approximately $1B in revenue at high margins. Streaming is operating at a loss, with content costs surging from $180M (2010) to a projected $2B (2012). The DVD business is not organizational drag — it is the cash engine funding the most expensive and strategically critical phase of the streaming buildout. Separating the entities forces Qwikster to stand alone as a visibly declining business, accelerating subscriber churn and reducing the cash available to fund streaming content acquisition during the exact window when Netflix must outbid Amazon, Hulu, and HBO for licensing deals.

**2. The customer experience cost of separation is asymmetric and punitive.**

Qwikster requires separate logins, separate queues, and separate review histories. For the ~12M subscribers who use both services, this is pure friction with zero customer benefit. The streaming library has ~20,000 titles versus ~100,000 on DVD — many subscribers rely on DVD precisely to fill gaps in the streaming catalog. Forcing them into two separate interfaces to manage what is, from their perspective, a single entertainment need is solving Netflix's internal problem at the customer's expense.

**3. The market has already priced in catastrophic execution risk.**

The stock has dropped 77% from $298 to ~$65. Customer satisfaction collapsed from 79 to 52 ACSI in a single quarter. 800,000 net subscribers were lost in Q3. The Qwikster Twitter handle situation signals operational unpreparedness. Proceeding with the split in this environment does not demonstrate strategic resolve — it demonstrates inability to read market signals. Reversing course is not weakness; it is the evidence-responsive move.

**4. The competitive window demands capital concentration, not organizational fragmentation.**

Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO all launched in 2010-2011. Netflix is simultaneously expanding internationally (Canada, Latin America, UK). This is the moment to consolidate resources behind the streaming bet, not to spend management attention, engineering effort, and brand equity on a corporate split that could be handled through internal restructuring.

### The strongest counterargument — and why it still fails

Proponents of the split argue that bundling creates organizational drag: product decisions for streaming are constrained by DVD logistics, and a clean separation lets each business optimize independently. This is a real problem. DVD and streaming have different supply chains, different unit economics, and different strategic horizons. Keeping them bundled does create tension.

But the solution is **internal separation, not customer-facing separation**. Create separate business units with separate P&Ls, separate engineering teams, and separate product roadmaps — all under one Netflix brand, one login, one billing relationship. This captures 90% of the organizational clarity benefits while avoiding the customer experience damage and brand fragmentation costs. The remaining 10% (truly independent board governance, separate capital markets access for Qwikster) is not worth the destruction currently underway.

Furthermore, the "organizational drag" argument assumes the DVD business will continue long enough to matter as a constraint. At -5% subscribers per quarter organically, DVD is on a 3-5 year glide path to irrelevance. The problem is self-solving. You do not reorganize your entire company around a business that will naturally wind down.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

| Unknown | Why it matters | How to close |
|---|---|---|
| **What percentage of streaming-only subscribers were acquired through the combo plan?** | If DVD was a major funnel to streaming, accelerating DVD decline via separation may reduce streaming growth. | Analyze cohort data: combo-to-streaming-only conversion rates by tenure. |
| **What is the true marginal cost of maintaining a unified product experience?** | The "organizational drag" argument needs quantification. If unified UX costs $50M/year in engineering overhead, that changes the calculus differently than $5M. | Engineering audit of shared infrastructure costs and product velocity impact. |
| **How price-elastic are the remaining combo subscribers?** | The 800K subscriber loss followed a 60% price increase. How many more are at risk of leaving if the Qwikster confusion continues vs. if prices stay elevated but the brand stabilizes? | Churn modeling segmented by plan type and tenure. |
| **What are the content licensing implications of reversal?** | Some content deals may have been structured around the separation. Reversing could trigger renegotiation clauses. | Legal review of all content contracts executed or amended since the Qwikster announcement. |
| **What is the competitor response timeline?** | If Amazon or Hulu are planning major content investments timed to Netflix's moment of weakness, the window for stabilization may be shorter than assumed. | Competitive intelligence on pending content deal announcements. |

**Key risk of this recommendation:** Reversing the Qwikster announcement within weeks of making it will generate a second wave of media coverage framing Netflix leadership as indecisive. Reed Hastings has already publicly apologized once (for the price increase) and defended the split. A second reversal risks the narrative becoming "Netflix doesn't know what it's doing." This is a real cost. However, the alternative — proceeding with a split that the market, customers, and most analysts have rejected — is worse. A brief credibility hit from reversal is recoverable. Sustained subscriber loss and brand fragmentation during a critical competitive window may not be.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**1. Internal Reorganization Plan (immediate, 2-week timeline)**
Design the internal separation: separate DVD and streaming business units with independent P&Ls, product teams, and operational metrics — all under the Netflix brand. This captures the strategic benefits of the split without the customer-facing costs.

**2. Customer Communication Strategy (within 48 hours of decision)**
Draft a concise public statement from Hastings announcing the reversal. Tone: direct acknowledgment that the execution was wrong, reaffirmation that streaming is the future, and commitment to maintaining a simple, unified customer experience. No over-apologizing. No hedging.

**3. DVD Wind-Down Financial Model (30-day timeline)**
Model the DVD business glide path under three scenarios: natural decline (-5%/quarter), accelerated decline (price signals to migrate customers to streaming), and managed harvest (maximize cash extraction). This becomes the internal roadmap that replaces the public Qwikster split.

**4. Competitive Response Assessment (immediate)**
Assess whether Amazon, Hulu, or HBO have made moves during Netflix's period of distraction that require accelerated content investment or strategic response.

---

**Bottom line:** The Qwikster split solves a real internal problem (organizational tension between DVD and streaming) with a solution that creates worse external problems (customer friction, brand damage, cash flow risk, competitive exposure). Reverse the split. Execute the separation internally. Spend the recovered management attention on winning the streaming content war that will determine Netflix's next decade.
