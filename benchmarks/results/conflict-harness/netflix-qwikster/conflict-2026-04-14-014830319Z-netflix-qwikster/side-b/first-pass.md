# Reverse course: keep DVD and streaming under the Netflix brand

## Recommendation
Netflix should reverse the Qwikster split immediately and keep both services under one Netflix brand, one account system, one queue relationship where possible, and one billing relationship. The strategic logic behind separation is directionally correct: streaming is the future and DVD is declining. But the announced form of the split destroys customer trust, adds user friction at the worst possible moment, and risks killing the cash engine that is still funding streaming and international expansion.

The right move is not to abandon strategic separation internally. It is to abandon customer-facing separation externally.

## Why reverse course now

### 1. The core problem is not strategy clarity; it is customer value destruction
The announced Qwikster design asks customers to accept separate websites, separate bills, separate queues, separate reviews, and separate logins after already absorbing a 60% price increase for the combined plan. That is a compounded degradation in the user experience, not a clarification of it. The market is signaling that customers see this as Netflix making its own operating model simpler by making their lives harder.

This is already visible in the numbers. Subscribers fell from about 24.6M in Q2 2011 to about 23.8M in Q3 2011, a loss of 800,000 members. Customer satisfaction collapsed from 79 to 52 in one quarter. The blog response was overwhelmingly negative, with 27,000+ comments. These are not normal transition costs. They are evidence that the plan has crossed a trust threshold.

### 2. DVD is still strategically valuable even if it is structurally declining
DVD-by-mail is declining by roughly 5% per quarter organically, but it still has major value today. It generates about $1B in revenue with high margins and subsidizes a streaming business that is still loss-making. Streaming content costs are exploding, from $180M in 2010 to a projected $2B in 2012. That means Netflix is entering a capital-intensive competitive phase exactly when it most needs stable cash flow.

Splitting off DVD in a way that accelerates churn or weakens retention would not merely tidy the portfolio. It would impair the funding base required to win streaming.

### 3. Netflix cannot afford self-inflicted friction during a category land grab
Competition in streaming is intensifying: Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO all entered or scaled in 2010-2011. Meanwhile Netflix is expanding internationally across Canada, Latin America, and the UK. That combination already stretches management, content economics, and brand trust.

In that environment, the company should be reducing friction and maximizing brand coherence. Instead, Qwikster fragments identity and attention. A separate DVD brand confuses customers, dilutes the Netflix name, and creates switching opportunities for competitors at the exact moment Netflix needs to deepen habit and loyalty.

### 4. Execution failure is now overwhelming the strategic message
Even if the long-run portfolio logic has merit, execution quality matters because it determines whether the strategy survives contact with customers. The Qwikster Twitter handle problem is a small but telling signal: Netflix launched a major brand change without controlling basic brand assets. That makes the company look unprepared and amplifies the perception that leadership is imposing a theory on customers without operational discipline.

At this point, proceeding would convert a correct long-term thesis into an avoidable credibility crisis.

## Best counterargument
The strongest argument for proceeding is that bundling DVD and streaming traps Netflix in the past. DVD logistics and economics distort product decisions, obscure streaming margins, and delay the organizational focus needed to compete in a streaming-first market. Separation could give investors and managers cleaner signals, sharper accountability, and faster execution for each business.

That argument is real. But it still does not justify the announced split.

First, Netflix can create internal separation without forcing customer-facing fragmentation. It can run distinct P&Ls, leadership structures, roadmaps, and capital allocation processes inside one brand. Second, the timing is wrong: after a sharp price shock and public backlash, another disruptive move multiplies churn risk. Third, strategic clarity is only useful if the company retains enough customer trust and cash flow to finance the transition. A theoretically cleaner structure that accelerates subscriber loss is strategically worse than a messier structure that preserves the right to win.

## What Netflix should do instead
1. Cancel the Qwikster rollout publicly and decisively.
2. Reaffirm one Netflix brand and one customer relationship.
3. Preserve internal organizational separation between DVD and streaming businesses, including distinct operating goals and reporting.
4. Reduce visible customer friction by simplifying plans, accounts, and communication.
5. Use DVD cash flow explicitly to fund streaming content acquisition and international ramp while managing DVD for harvest, not growth.
6. Rebuild trust with a product roadmap centered on convenience, selection improvement in streaming, and communication discipline.

## Decision Frame
Reverse course on the customer-facing Qwikster split. Keep Netflix as the single brand and relationship layer while separating the businesses internally for management focus and financial discipline. The decision criterion is not whether streaming is the future; it is whether the announced split increases Netflix's probability of winning streaming from its September 2011 position. It does not.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- We do not have a cohort-level view of churn by DVD-only, streaming-only, and combo subscribers after the July 2011 price change.
- We do not have retention modeling for customers exposed to separate-login and separate-queue friction.
- We do not have full economics on how much incremental content spend is required for streaming to close the gap with DVD selection.
- We do not know whether Wall Street would reward internal reporting separation without a public brand split.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if leadership is willing to publicly reverse the split, accept short-term embarrassment, and implement internal business separation with tighter customer experience discipline.

Fail if leadership remains committed to public structural purity over customer trust, or if it treats reversal as abandoning streaming focus rather than as preserving the balance sheet and brand needed to fund it.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A 90-day recovery plan covering customer communication, pricing stabilization, internal operating model separation, churn-risk monitoring, and milestones for rebuilding trust while funding streaming growth.
