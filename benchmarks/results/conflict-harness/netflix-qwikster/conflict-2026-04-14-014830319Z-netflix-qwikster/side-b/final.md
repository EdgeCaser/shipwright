# Revised artifact

## Reverse course on Qwikster; separate the businesses operationally, not in the customer experience

Netflix should reverse the public Qwikster split and keep one Netflix brand, one account relationship, and one billing experience. The September 2011 question is not whether streaming is the future. It is whether this specific split, launched immediately after a 60% effective price shock and requiring separate websites, queues, reviews, and logins, increases Netflix's odds of winning streaming. The evidence says no.

The immediate damage is already visible. Subscribers fell from roughly 24.6M in Q2 2011 to 23.8M in Q3 2011, a loss of 800,000. Customer satisfaction fell from 79 to 52 in one quarter. The announcement generated 27,000+ overwhelmingly negative comments. This is not normal transition noise. It is evidence that Netflix has broken the customer value equation: people are being asked to pay more and tolerate more friction for a service that feels worse.

That matters because DVD, while declining at roughly 5% per quarter, is still strategically necessary. It remains a high-margin business generating about $1B in revenue and subsidizing a streaming business that is still operating at a loss just as content costs rise from $180M in 2010 toward a projected $2B in 2012. At the same time, Netflix faces intensifying competition from Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO, while also funding international expansion across Canada, Latin America, and the UK. This is the wrong moment to destabilize the cash engine and brand trust that finance the transition.

The strongest counterargument is that DVD and streaming are different businesses, and that keeping them together causes product contamination: the physical-logistics business slows the digital business, blurs accountability, and biases decisions toward the legacy installed base. That concern is valid. But public separation is not required to solve it, and trying to solve it through customer-facing fragmentation is strategically too expensive.

Netflix should instead adopt a hard internal operating split with a unified customer shell. Concretely: separate GM ownership for DVD and streaming; separate P&Ls and operating targets; ring-fenced engineering and product roadmaps for the streaming experience; a DVD team managed for cash and service reliability rather than growth; and a single Netflix identity, account, and billing layer that minimizes friction for customers who still use both. This does not claim internal separation is identical to a public split. It claims something narrower and more decision-useful: internal separation captures enough of the focus benefits while avoiding the severe churn, trust, and brand damage that the announced Qwikster design is already causing.

That is the key strategic tradeoff. Netflix does not need the theoretically pure structure; it needs the highest-probability path through a dangerous transition. In September 2011, preserving trust and cash flow is more valuable than exposing customers to a second disruptive shock in the name of organizational clarity.

## Decision Frame
Reverse the customer-facing Qwikster split immediately. Keep one Netflix brand and relationship layer, but implement strict internal separation so streaming gets dedicated leadership, budget, and roadmap control while DVD is managed as a declining cash-generating business. The decision standard is comparative: which path better preserves the resources and trust needed to win streaming from the current position. Reversal is the higher-probability choice.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- We do not have cohort-level churn data separating the effects of the July 2011 price increase from anticipated Qwikster friction.
- We do not have quantified estimates of how much product velocity streaming would gain under a public split versus a hard internal split.
- We do not know how investors would respond to enhanced segment reporting and explicit internal separation without a public spinoff.
- We do not have customer research on whether any cross-service queue or account-linking features could preserve convenience while still isolating operating complexity behind the scenes.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if leadership is willing to reverse publicly, accept the embarrassment cost, and immediately install a real internal operating model that protects streaming focus instead of reverting to loose shared governance.

Fail if leadership either proceeds with Qwikster despite the trust collapse, or reverses publicly but keeps the old blended decision structure that originally created internal drag.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A 90-day operating recovery plan specifying the post-reversal org design, product and engineering ownership boundaries, customer communication sequence, churn monitoring, and investor narrative for unified-brand external continuity with internally separate business management.
