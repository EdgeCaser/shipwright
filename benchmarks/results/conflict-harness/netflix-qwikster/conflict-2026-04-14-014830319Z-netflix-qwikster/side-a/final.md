# Strategic Recommendation: Reverse the Qwikster Split Immediately

**To:** Netflix Leadership Team
**From:** Strategy Advisory
**Date:** September 2011
**Classification:** Confidential

---

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Should Netflix proceed with splitting into two separate companies (Netflix for streaming, Qwikster for DVD-by-mail), or reverse course and reunify under the Netflix brand?

**Recommendation: Reverse the Qwikster split. Keep both services under the Netflix brand. Execute the strategic separation operationally — not as a customer-facing divorce.**

The strategic insight behind Qwikster is correct: DVD-by-mail is a declining business, and streaming requires fundamentally different organizational muscles. But the execution path of a public brand split destroys more value than it creates, and the timing is catastrophic. The right move is to decouple the businesses internally while maintaining a unified customer experience.

### Why Reverse — The Evidence Case

**1. The split solves an internal problem by imposing external costs on customers.**

The organizational drag between streaming and DVD operations is real. But Qwikster forces customers to maintain two accounts, two queues, two billing relationships, and two review histories — for a problem they neither created nor care about. The 27,000+ negative comments on the announcement blog post are not noise; they are a direct signal that the value proposition has been broken from the customer's perspective. When your customer satisfaction score drops from 79 to 52 in a single quarter, the market is telling you something unambiguous.

**2. The DVD business funds the streaming transition — separation complicates this at a critical moment.**

DVD-by-mail generates approximately $1B in revenue at high margins. Streaming is operating at a loss and faces surging content costs (from $180M in 2010 to a projected $2B in 2012). Under a unified Netflix, the cross-subsidy from DVD to streaming is frictionless and invisible to investors. A formal separation introduces capital-structure complexity: even if intercompany agreements could theoretically maintain cash flow access, the split creates new governance obligations, transfer-pricing scrutiny, and investor pressure for each entity to optimize independently. The proposed capital structure and intercompany funding mechanisms have not been articulated — and absent those details, the split introduces financing risk during the most capital-intensive phase of Netflix's streaming and international buildout. The burden of proof should fall on the split to demonstrate that DVD cash flows remain fully accessible post-separation, not on the unified structure to justify the status quo.

**3. The competitive window demands focus, not self-inflicted wounds.**

Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO all launched in 2010-2011. Netflix's streaming library (~20,000 titles) is already dwarfed by its DVD library (~100,000 titles). International expansion into Latin America and the UK is underway and capital-intensive. Every day spent managing the Qwikster crisis is a day not spent securing content deals, improving the streaming product, or establishing international beachheads. The 77% stock decline from $298 to ~$65 constrains Netflix's ability to raise capital and negotiate from strength.

**4. The subscriber loss signals real churn risk, not just noise.**

Losing 800,000 subscribers in Q3 2011 after the price increase is significant — a net decline from 24.6M to 23.8M. The Qwikster announcement layers brand confusion and operational inconvenience on top of price shock. Proceeding with the split risks converting temporary anger into permanent defection to competitors who are actively courting Netflix's subscriber base.

**5. The Qwikster brand itself is compromised.**

The Qwikster Twitter handle is owned by an unrelated individual posting inappropriate content. This is a detail, but it is symptomatic of rushed execution. The brand carries zero equity, negative associations, and would require significant marketing spend to establish — spend better directed at content acquisition or international growth.

### The Correct Strategic Path

- **Reverse the Qwikster announcement within weeks.** Frame it as listening to customers, not as admitting strategic error. Hastings has already apologized once; a second public acknowledgment that the company moved too fast will read as responsiveness, not weakness.
- **Execute the internal separation quietly.** Create separate P&L tracking, separate engineering teams, and separate product roadmaps for streaming and DVD — but behind a single Netflix login, single queue interface, and single billing system.
- **Manage DVD decline through natural attrition.** DVD subscribers are declining ~5% per quarter organically. Within 3-5 years, the business will be small enough to spin off or wind down without customer disruption. Let the market solve the problem.
- **Redirect all executive attention to the streaming content gap and international expansion.** These are the existential priorities. The DVD separation question is a second-order optimization that can wait.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

| Unknown | Why It Matters | How to Resolve |
|---|---|---|
| **Churn attribution** — How much of the 800K subscriber loss is due to price increase vs. Qwikster announcement vs. competitive switching? | Determines whether reversing Qwikster alone can stabilize the base, or whether a price rollback is also needed. | Cohort analysis of cancellation timing (pre/post Qwikster announcement) and exit survey data. |
| **Content deal pipeline** — What streaming content deals are in negotiation, and how does the stock decline affect Netflix's negotiating leverage? | If content partners are using Netflix's weakness to demand higher licensing fees, the cost trajectory may be worse than projected. | Confidential deal team briefing on active negotiations and counterparty sentiment. |
| **Competitor subscriber trajectories** — Are Amazon, Hulu, and HBO absorbing Netflix churners, or are subscribers simply canceling? | If competitors are capturing share, the window to recover is shorter. If subscribers are just leaving, a goodwill gesture may bring them back. | Third-party market data (Nielsen, comScore) on competitor streaming adoption Q3 2011. |
| **International unit economics** — What is the subscriber acquisition cost and time-to-profitability in Canada, Latin America, and UK? | International expansion is the growth story, but if it's significantly more expensive than projected, the DVD cash flow subsidy becomes even more critical. | Finance team projections with actuals from Canada (12 months of data). |
| **Employee morale and retention** — Has the Qwikster announcement caused talent flight or internal faction formation? | Reversing course after a public commitment could demoralize teams who invested in the separation plan. Needs to be managed. | HR pulse survey and skip-level conversations with key engineering and content leads. |
| **Post-separation capital structure** — If the split were to proceed, what intercompany agreements, transfer pricing, and capital allocation mechanisms would govern DVD-to-streaming cash flow? | Without this detail, the financial risk of separation cannot be precisely quantified — only directionally assessed. | CFO briefing on proposed Qwikster/Netflix financial architecture. |

**Overall readiness: PASS — ready for leadership decision.**

The strongest counterargument is that reversing course damages Hastings' credibility and signals that Netflix leadership can be bullied by public backlash into abandoning strategically sound decisions. This is a real cost. However, the counterargument assumes the *execution* was sound and only the *reception* was poor. In reality, the execution — separate logins, separate queues, separate billing, a compromised brand name — imposed genuine costs on customers that no amount of strategic logic can justify. Reversing a bad execution of a good strategy is not capitulation; it is correction.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Internal Reorganization Plan:** A detailed operating model for how to achieve streaming/DVD organizational separation *within* a unified Netflix brand. This should include:
- Separate P&L and KPI dashboards for each business unit
- Engineering team boundaries and shared infrastructure agreements
- Product roadmap governance (how streaming decisions are insulated from DVD constraints)
- DVD sunset criteria and timeline triggers (e.g., "when DVD subscribers fall below X, initiate formal wind-down")
- Communication plan for the reversal announcement, including talking points for Hastings, investor relations, and customer-facing messaging

This artifact converts the strategic reversal into an operational reality and ensures the correct underlying insight — that these businesses need to be managed differently — is preserved without the customer-facing damage of Qwikster.
