# Strategic Recommendation: Reverse the Qwikster Split Immediately

## Decision Frame

**Question:** Should Netflix proceed with spinning off DVD-by-mail as Qwikster, or reverse course and keep both services under the Netflix brand?

**Recommendation: Reverse course. Kill Qwikster. Reunify under Netflix.**

The Qwikster split is strategically directionally correct — DVD and streaming are diverging businesses — but catastrophically wrong on timing, execution, and sequencing. The split destroys more value than it creates at this moment, and the strategic benefits can be captured through internal organizational separation without forcing customers into a worse experience.

### The Core Error: Optimizing for Org Structure at the Expense of the Customer

The Qwikster logic assumes the primary bottleneck is organizational drag from bundling DVD and streaming product decisions. But the actual bottleneck in September 2011 is **streaming content depth** — Netflix has ~20,000 streaming titles vs. ~100,000 on DVD. Customers still need DVD-by-mail to access 80% of the catalog. Forcing them onto separate websites with separate queues, separate billing, and separate review systems doesn't solve the content gap; it punishes customers for it.

A split makes sense *after* the streaming library reaches rough parity with customer expectations — not while customers are actively subsidizing the streaming buildout through their DVD subscriptions.

### Why Reverse Now, Not Later

1. **The subscriber bleed is accelerating, not stabilizing.** Netflix lost 800,000 subscribers in Q3 2011 — the first net loss in the company's history. The 60% price increase already created churn pressure; Qwikster adds a second churn event on top of the first. Each lost subscriber reduces the revenue base funding the streaming content buildout that is Netflix's entire future.

2. **DVD cash flow funds the streaming transition.** The DVD business generates ~$1B in revenue at high margins. Streaming operates at a loss. Alienating DVD subscribers — the customers literally financing Netflix's future — is self-defeating. Every DVD customer who churns due to Qwikster friction is a dollar not available for streaming content licensing.

3. **The competitive window is narrow but still open.** Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO all launched in 2010-2011. Netflix's primary competitive advantage is its brand, recommendation engine, and unified experience. Splitting the brand in half while competitors are consolidating their offerings hands them an unforced advantage.

4. **Brand damage compounds nonlinearly.** Customer satisfaction dropped from 79 to 52 ACSI in one quarter — a 34% decline. The 27,000+ negative comments, the Qwikster Twitter debacle, and the public apology-that-wasn't have made Netflix a case study in how not to treat customers. Every week the split remains announced, the brand erodes further. Reversal now reads as "we listened"; reversal later reads as "we failed."

5. **The stock price has already priced in disaster.** A 77% decline from $298 to ~$65 means the market has lost confidence in management judgment. Reversing Qwikster won't recover the stock immediately, but it removes the most visible symbol of mismanagement and lets the conversation shift back to streaming growth fundamentals.

## Internal Separation as a Viable Alternative to Customer-Facing Split

The Qwikster rationale identifies a real problem — organizational drag — but prescribes the wrong remedy. The drag stems from three specific tensions: (a) product roadmap conflicts where DVD logistics constraints slow streaming feature releases, (b) capital allocation disputes between investing in warehouse infrastructure vs. content licensing, and (c) engineering prioritization where a single queue system must serve two diverging use cases.

However, none of these tensions require splitting the customer experience to resolve. The internal separation model works as follows:

- **Separate GM-led divisions** with independent P&Ls, headcount, and roadmaps — the DVD GM optimizes for margin harvesting and managed decline, the streaming GM optimizes for subscriber growth and content acquisition. This is standard conglomerate practice (e.g., Amazon's retail vs. AWS divisions share Prime membership and customer identity while operating with full strategic independence).
- **Shared customer-facing infrastructure** (single login, unified queue, one billing system) maintained by a platform team that serves both divisions. The queue and recommendation engine are assets, not drag — they surface DVD titles to customers whose streaming searches come up empty, preserving engagement.
- **Ring-fenced capital allocation** where DVD profits are allocated to streaming content licensing via an explicit internal transfer price, making the cross-subsidy visible and deliberate rather than implicit.

**Evidence gap acknowledged:** We lack internal data on which specific product decisions the unified structure has blocked or delayed. Before implementing the internal separation, the leadership team should conduct a 2-week audit of product roadmap conflicts from the past 6 months to validate that the drag is real and to size the internal separation correctly. If the audit reveals that the drag is minimal — that Qwikster was solving a problem that didn't meaningfully exist — then even the internal separation may be unnecessary, and the focus should shift entirely to content acquisition.

This approach captures the strategic clarity Wall Street wants (visible separate P&Ls, clear streaming growth metrics) without imposing the customer experience degradation that is driving churn.

### Strongest Counterarguments and Why They Don't Hold

**Counter 1: "The split is strategically correct — DVD is dying. Delaying just makes it harder later."**

This is true in direction but wrong in sequencing. DVD *is* declining ~5% per quarter organically. It will sunset itself. The question is whether to force-sunset it now (destroying cash flow and customer goodwill) or let it decline gracefully while streaming matures. The answer is obvious: a managed decline preserves the cash flow bridge. You can organizationally separate the teams internally without splitting the customer experience.

**Counter 2: "Reversing makes Hastings look indecisive and damages credibility further."**

Short-term, yes. But the credibility damage from proceeding with a plan that customers, analysts, and the market have rejected is worse. Leaders who reverse bad decisions quickly are forgiven faster than leaders who double down. The framing should be: "We moved too fast. We heard you. The services stay together." This is a one-news-cycle story. Proceeding with Qwikster is a multi-quarter story.

**Counter 3: "Wall Street wants strategic clarity — the split provides it."**

Wall Street wants subscriber growth and a credible path to streaming profitability. The split provides neither. It accelerates churn and fragments the brand. Strategic clarity comes from separate P&L reporting under a unified brand — which the internal separation model delivers — combined with a compelling streaming content roadmap and evidence of international traction.

## Recommended Next Artifact

1. **Crisis Communication Plan** — Draft the reversal announcement. Tone: humble, decisive, customer-first. No blame-shifting, no "we were right but you didn't understand." Announce within 72 hours to minimize further brand erosion.

2. **Organizational Drag Audit** — Before designing the internal separation, conduct a 2-week review of specific product roadmap conflicts, blocked decisions, and resource contention between DVD and streaming teams over the past 6 months. This validates whether the internal separation is solving a real problem or an assumed one.

3. **Internal Reorganization Memo** — Informed by the drag audit, design the internal organizational separation: separate GM-led divisions with independent P&Ls, ring-fenced capital allocation, and shared customer-facing platform infrastructure.

4. **Streaming Content Acceleration Roadmap** — Redirect the energy spent on Qwikster logistics into accelerating original content development and exclusive licensing. The real strategic question isn't "how do we separate from DVD" — it's "how do we make streaming compelling enough that customers voluntarily leave DVD behind."

5. **Subscriber Recovery Program** — Design a win-back campaign for the 800,000 lost subscribers. Consider a limited-time pricing bridge (e.g., 3-month combo plan at legacy pricing) to reduce re-acquisition friction.

---

**Bottom line:** Qwikster solves an internal problem by creating an external one. The internal problem (org drag) has internal solutions — but those solutions should be validated by data before implementation. The external problem (customer exodus) does not have a second chance. Reverse now, audit the org drag, reorganize internally if warranted, and refocus the company on the only thing that matters: making streaming so good that DVD becomes irrelevant on its own.
