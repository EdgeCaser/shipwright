# Revised artifact

## Reverse Course on Qwikster

### Recommendation
Reverse the Qwikster split immediately. Keep DVD-by-mail and streaming under one Netflix brand, one account, and one billing relationship, while creating a harder internal operating separation behind the scenes. The split's strategic premise is understandable, but the announced form is wrong for September 2011.

The key decision is not whether DVD and streaming are different businesses; they are. The key decision is whether Netflix should impose customer-facing fragmentation now, after a 60% effective price increase, 800,000 lost subscribers in Q3, an ACSI collapse from 79 to 52, and highly visible public backlash. On that question, the answer is no. [ctx-1]

### Why Reverse

#### 1. Customer trust is the immediate bottleneck
Qwikster adds separate websites, billing, queues, reviews, and logins immediately after customers were told the combined plan would rise from $9.99 to $15.98. That turns an already painful price shock into a broader convenience and trust shock. With 27,000+ negative comments and a 77% stock collapse from the July peak, leadership's first duty is to stop compounding the damage. [ctx-1]

#### 2. DVD cash flow still funds the transition
DVD is declining, but it still generates roughly $1B of high-margin revenue while streaming faces exploding content costs, from $180M in 2010 toward a projected $2B in 2012. Streaming also remains materially weaker on catalog depth, about 20,000 titles versus roughly 100,000 on DVD. That means Netflix still depends on DVD economics to finance streaming scale-up and international expansion. A move likely to accelerate DVD churn is strategically self-defeating. [ctx-1]

#### 3. Streaming is not yet strong enough to carry a forced de-bundled experience
Many households still use Netflix as a combined utility: streaming for immediacy, DVD for depth. Since streaming cannot yet replicate the DVD catalog, splitting the experience now removes integrated value before the replacement product is ready. That invites cancellation and makes Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO more attractive as substitutes. [ctx-1]

#### 4. The operating-focus argument is real, but it does not justify this launch design
The critique of the first draft is correct on one point: internal separation should not be treated as a free substitute for a full split. It will not deliver all the benefits of a clean corporate separation. But Netflix does not need all of those benefits right now. It needs enough organizational discipline to protect streaming investment without triggering another customer revolt.

The practical answer is a hybrid model:
- Separate GM-level ownership for DVD and streaming.
- Separate P&Ls and capital allocation rules.
- Ring-fenced streaming content and product investment budgets.
- Separate operating dashboards: DVD on cash generation and churn management, streaming on engagement, content utilization, and subscriber growth.
- A shared customer layer for brand, login, billing, and account history.
- Explicit executive arbitration rules so DVD cannot win roadmap tradeoffs by default just because it is currently more profitable.
- A published set of milestone triggers for any future external separation, tied to churn stabilization, streaming catalog sufficiency, and trust recovery.

This structure is not offered as proven by direct historical evidence in the packet; it is an operational inference from the evidence that the customer-facing split is highly destructive while the underlying strategic need for focus remains valid. [ctx-1]

### Strongest Counterargument
Proceeding with Qwikster would create cleaner accountability. DVD is declining about 5% per quarter, streaming competition is rising, and content costs are exploding. A hard split could stop the legacy business from distorting priorities and could give investors a clearer view of the future growth engine. [ctx-1]

### Why Reverse Is Still Correct
That argument overweights long-run organizational purity and underweights the current trust emergency.

A full external split creates three immediate risks that are more pressing than org-chart clarity:
- It adds friction to a customer base already in active revolt.
- It weakens the very DVD cash flows still financing streaming.
- It further damages management credibility when credibility is already the scarce asset.

The relevant comparison is not "perfect split" versus "perfect integration." It is "damaging external split now" versus "messier but safer hybrid governance now." In September 2011, the hybrid is the better decision because it preserves transition financing and customer goodwill while still forcing more internal discipline than the status quo. [ctx-1]

## Decision Frame
Decision: Reverse the customer-facing Qwikster split and keep both services under Netflix.

Why this is the right decision:
- The immediate constraint is retention and trust, not maximum structural purity.
- DVD cash flow remains strategically necessary during the streaming build-out.
- Streaming's catalog and economics are not yet ready for a forced stand-alone customer experience.
- A hybrid operating model can capture part of the focus benefit while avoiding the largest customer harms.

What would change the recommendation:
- Evidence that a split improves retention, ARPU, or investor confidence enough to offset churn and brand damage.
- Evidence that streaming can satisfy most former combo households without DVD support.
- Evidence that internal governance cannot prevent DVD from distorting streaming priorities even with ring-fenced budgets and separate accountability.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- How much recent churn is price-driven versus trust/UX-driven.
- How much operating benefit a hybrid internal model can capture relative to a full split.
- Whether content partners and investors would respond positively enough to structural separation to outweigh customer losses.
- How quickly trust could recover after a public reversal.
- What minimum streaming catalog threshold is needed before a future external separation becomes viable.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass conditions:
- Leadership reverses the external split within days, not quarters.
- Netflix installs separate business ownership, P&Ls, and budgeting immediately.
- Executive governance explicitly protects streaming investment from DVD short-termism.
- Public messaging centers on simplification, accountability, and better service.

Fail conditions:
- Reversal is purely cosmetic and leaves the old blurred governance intact.
- DVD continues to dominate roadmap decisions because of current profitability.
- Leadership delays the reversal long enough that churn and trust damage become harder to arrest.
