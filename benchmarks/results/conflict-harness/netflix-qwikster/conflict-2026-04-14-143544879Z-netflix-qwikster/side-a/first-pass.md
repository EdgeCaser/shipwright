# Reverse Course on Qwikster

## Recommendation
Reverse the Qwikster split immediately and keep DVD-by-mail and streaming under the Netflix brand, account system, and customer relationship. Preserve internal operating separation where useful, but do not force a customer-facing separation now.

The strategic logic behind separation is directionally sound: streaming is the future, DVD is declining, and each business needs different economics and operating rhythms. But the announced form of the split is destroying trust faster than it is creating strategic clarity. Netflix's immediate constraint is not organizational elegance; it is customer retention, brand credibility, and funding capacity for the streaming transition.

## Why Reverse Now

### 1. The announced split compounds, rather than solves, the current crisis
Netflix has already triggered severe customer backlash through the July 2011 pricing change: the combined plan effectively moved from $9.99 to $15.98, a 60% increase. Q3 subscriber losses reached 800,000, customer satisfaction fell from 79 to 52 in a single quarter, and the Qwikster announcement drew 27,000+ overwhelmingly negative comments. Adding separate websites, billing, queues, reviews, and logins imposes new friction at the exact moment customers are signaling that they feel overcharged and disrespected. This is not a neutral reorganization; it is a second insult layered on top of the first. [ctx-1]

### 2. Netflix still needs the DVD business economically
DVD is declining, but it remains a high-margin, cash-generating business with about $1B in revenue. Streaming, by contrast, is strategically essential but economically immature: content licensing is surging from $180M in 2010 to a projected $2B in 2012, and the streaming library remains far thinner than DVD, roughly 20,000 titles versus 100,000. That means DVD cash flow is still subsidizing the build-out of streaming and international expansion. Splitting the businesses in a way that weakens retention or accelerates DVD churn makes the transition harder to finance, not easier. [ctx-1]

### 3. The customer proposition is weaker if the services are operationally separated
Customers do not experience Netflix as a portfolio strategy; they experience it as a convenience promise. In September 2011, streaming does not yet fully replace DVD because the content gap is too large. For many households, the value of Netflix is the combination: stream what is available instantly, use DVD for catalog depth. Forcing those customers into two brands and two queues removes the integrated value proposition before streaming can stand alone on merit. That invites cancellations and pushes customers toward emerging substitutes such as Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, and HBO GO. [ctx-1]

### 4. The market is punishing execution failure, not just strategy ambiguity
The 77% stock drop from the July 2011 peak reflects a collapse in confidence in management judgment and execution. Proceeding anyway signals rigidity when credibility is already impaired. Reversing course would not erase the strategic challenges, but it would show the board, investors, and customers that leadership can distinguish between a valid long-term thesis and a failed near-term implementation. Restoring trust is a prerequisite to raising capital confidence, negotiating content, and sustaining the multi-year pivot to streaming. [ctx-1]

## Best Counterargument
The strongest argument for proceeding is that DVD and streaming are fundamentally different businesses. DVD is declining about 5% per quarter organically, while streaming faces rapid competitive entry and massive content-cost inflation. Keeping them together may blur accountability, distort investment decisions, and trap streaming inside the economics and product logic of a shrinking legacy business. A clean split could let each team optimize independently and give investors better visibility into the future growth engine. [ctx-1]

## Why That Counterargument Is Still Not Enough
That argument is strategically real but tactically premature.

First, internal separation does not require customer-facing separation. Netflix can create separate P&Ls, leadership accountability, operating cadences, and resource decisions without asking customers to manage two brands and two products.

Second, the company has not earned the right to impose more friction. After a 60% price increase and visible customer outrage, any move that looks like additional extraction or inconvenience will be interpreted through a trust-deficit lens.

Third, the streaming business is not yet strong enough to stand emotionally or economically on its own. Its catalog is materially weaker than DVD, its costs are exploding, and competition is intensifying. This is the wrong point in the transition curve to de-bundle the user experience.

Fourth, the downside of reversing is mostly narrative embarrassment; the downside of proceeding is accelerated churn, brand fragmentation, and reduced transition cash flow. The asymmetry favors reversal.

## Recommended Immediate Actions
1. Cancel the Qwikster launch and retain one Netflix brand, one website, one billing relationship, and one queue/account experience where possible.
2. Reframe publicly: acknowledge that the strategic goal of improving focus was valid, but the customer experience design was wrong.
3. Build internal separation instead of external separation: separate operating metrics, leadership accountability, and capital allocation for DVD and streaming.
4. Protect DVD cash flows while intentionally harvesting them to fund streaming content and international expansion.
5. Reduce customer friction in the core product before attempting any further structural change.
6. Set explicit trigger conditions for any future separation, tied to streaming catalog sufficiency, churn stability, and brand trust recovery.

## Decision Frame
Decision: Reverse course on Qwikster now.

Why this is the right decision:
- Customer trust is the immediate bottleneck, and Qwikster worsens it.
- DVD economics are still needed to fund the streaming transition.
- Streaming's current product depth is too weak to justify a forced separation.
- Internal operating clarity can be achieved without customer-facing fragmentation.

What would change my mind:
- Evidence that the split improves retention or ARPU without increasing churn.
- Evidence that streaming can satisfy most former combo-plan households without DVD support.
- Evidence that investors and content partners reward the split enough to offset customer losses.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- How much of recent churn is attributable to price alone versus brand/UX confusion.
- Whether a softer internal-only reorganization would deliver most of the operating benefits.
- The true elasticity of remaining combo subscribers if additional friction is introduced.
- The board's tolerance for a public reversal versus continued execution under controversy.
- The near-term impact of reversal on content partner confidence and investor narrative.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass conditions:
- Leadership is willing to publicly reverse the external split within days, not months.
- The company can articulate a coherent alternative operating model with separate internal accountability.
- Customer messaging is centered on simplification and service improvement, not defensiveness.

Fail conditions:
- Leadership insists on preserving the announced split to avoid embarrassment.
- No internal model exists to separate DVD and streaming decision rights without a brand split.
- The company treats reversal as communications only, without operational follow-through.
