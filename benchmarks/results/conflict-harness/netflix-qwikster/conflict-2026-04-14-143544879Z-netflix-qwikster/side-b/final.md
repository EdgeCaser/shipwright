# Strategic Recommendation: Reverse the Qwikster Split Immediately

## Decision Frame

**Question:** Should Netflix proceed with splitting into Netflix (streaming) and Qwikster (DVD-by-mail) as two separate companies, or reverse course and reunify under the Netflix brand?

**Recommendation: Reverse course. Kill Qwikster. Reunify immediately.**

The strategic logic of separation is sound in theory — streaming and DVD are diverging businesses with different operational profiles. But the execution vehicle (Qwikster) destroys more value than organizational separation could ever create. The question is not whether DVD will decline; it will. The question is whether *this specific maneuver, at this specific moment* serves Netflix's strategic interests. It does not.

### Why Reversal Is Correct

**1. The DVD business is funding the streaming transition — severing it now is financially premature.**

DVD-by-mail generates approximately $1B in revenue at high margins. Streaming is operating at a loss and faces a content cost explosion: licensing jumped from $180M (2010) to a projected $2B (2012). Netflix needs DVD cash flow to fund streaming content acquisition and international expansion (Canada 2010, Latin America and UK 2011). Splitting into separate companies with separate P&Ls makes the cross-subsidy visible and politically difficult to sustain — the DVD entity's shareholders would resist funding streaming's losses. Keeping them unified lets management allocate capital internally without external pressure to optimize each unit in isolation.

**2. The separation creates concrete customer harm with no customer benefit.**

Qwikster would require separate logins, separate queues, and separate review systems. A customer who wants to check whether a title is available on streaming or DVD — the most common use case for hybrid subscribers — must now navigate two websites. This is a product regression. The entire value proposition of Netflix is convenience; Qwikster is an anti-convenience move. No customer asked for this. The 27,000+ overwhelmingly negative comments on the announcement blog post are not noise — they are signal that the value proposition is being degraded.

**3. Brand equity destruction is asymmetric and irreversible at this velocity.**

Netflix stock has dropped 77% from $298 to ~$65. The company lost 800,000 subscribers in Q3 2011. Customer satisfaction scores collapsed from 79 to 52 on the ACSI in a single quarter. These are not temporary fluctuations — they represent a trust deficit. Every day Qwikster remains the plan, competitors (Amazon Prime Video, Hulu Plus, HBO GO) gain positioning as the stable, customer-friendly alternative. Netflix's brand moat — built over a decade — is being drained in weeks.

**4. The Qwikster brand itself is a liability.**

The @Qwikster Twitter handle is controlled by a third party posting inappropriate content. The name has become a punchline. Launching a new consumer brand with negative associations from day one is a marketing catastrophe that no amount of spend can reverse.

**5. Internal reorganization can address the legitimate governance concerns — but it is not a guaranteed substitute for full separation, and must be designed with specific safeguards.**

The legitimate case for Qwikster rests on governance problems: capital-allocation conflicts between a profitable legacy business and a loss-making growth business, leadership attention split, and the risk that DVD incentives distort streaming decisions. These are real organizational pathologies. However, corporate separation is the most extreme remedy and carries the highest cost (brand destruction, customer friction, financial exposure). Internal separation — distinct BUs with their own engineering leads, product roadmaps, and tracked P&Ls — is the lower-risk intervention to try first.

Critically, internal separation is not automatically effective. It requires specific design choices to prevent the governance problems the split was meant to solve:

- **Capital allocation:** The CEO must retain explicit authority to direct DVD cash flow toward streaming investment, with board-level agreement that DVD margins fund the transition. This must be a documented capital allocation policy, not an informal understanding.
- **Decision speed:** Streaming product and engineering teams must have independent release authority — no shared deployment pipelines, no DVD-team sign-off on streaming features.
- **Accountability:** Each BU needs its own publicly reported metrics (even if under one brand), so that investors and leadership can track the managed decline of DVD and the growth trajectory of streaming independently.
- **Incentive alignment:** DVD leadership compensation should be tied to managed wind-down metrics (margin preservation, orderly subscriber transition to streaming), not growth targets that would create internal competition.

This is harder than a clean corporate split. It demands sustained executive discipline. But it preserves the customer experience, the brand, and the financial flexibility that Netflix needs during the most capital-intensive phase of the streaming transition. If internal separation proves insufficient after 12-18 months — if DVD incentives demonstrably distort streaming decisions despite these safeguards — corporate separation can be revisited from a position of strength rather than crisis. The option is preserved; the damage is not.

### Strongest Counterargument and Why It Fails

The strongest argument *for* proceeding with Qwikster is organizational focus: streaming and DVD have fundamentally different operational requirements, and bundling them forces product compromises. This is true. DVD logistics (warehouse optimization, postal routing, inventory management) have nothing in common with streaming technology (CDN architecture, content recommendation, device compatibility). A unified organization risks the innovator's dilemma — protecting the profitable legacy business at the expense of the growth business. Internal BU separation is a weaker form of organizational discipline than a clean corporate split, and it requires sustained executive attention to prevent governance drift.

This argument fails for three reasons:

1. **Timing.** The right time to separate was before the price increase, when customer goodwill was intact and the move could be framed as evolution rather than punishment. Doing it now, stacked on top of a 60% price hike, reads as contempt for customers.

2. **Cost-benefit asymmetry.** Corporate separation delivers perhaps 20% more organizational clarity than well-designed internal separation, but at 10-50x the customer, brand, and financial cost. The incremental governance benefit does not justify the destruction already underway.

3. **Sequencing.** Streaming must reach content viability (currently 20K titles vs 100K on DVD) before customers will accept losing unified access. Separation should follow streaming maturity, not precede it. Internal separation buys time for this maturation while preserving the option of future corporate separation if governance proves insufficient.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Artifact: 90-Day Recovery Plan and Internal Reorganization Blueprint**

Scope:
1. **Week 1:** Public announcement reversing Qwikster. Hastings takes ownership of the mistake without over-apologizing — frame as "we listened, we learned, one Netflix is better for customers." Brief, direct, no blog post longer than 300 words.
2. **Weeks 2-4:** Internal reorganization into two business units (Streaming BU, DVD BU) under the Netflix brand. Separate engineering leads, separate product roadmaps, shared customer-facing infrastructure (single login, unified queue, unified reviews). Document capital allocation policy: DVD margins fund streaming transition with board-level agreement. Define BU-level accountability metrics for investor reporting.
3. **Weeks 4-8:** Subscriber recovery campaign. Consider limited-time pricing bridge for departed combo subscribers. Accelerate streaming content acquisition to close the title gap.
4. **Weeks 8-12:** Investor communications resetting the narrative. Present internal separation as the mature version of the Qwikster thesis — same strategic logic, customer-friendly execution. Publish BU-level metrics to demonstrate governance rigor.
5. **Ongoing:** Establish tripwire metrics for eventual DVD sunset (subscriber count, revenue contribution, content gap closure rate) and governance health checks (quarterly review of capital allocation decisions, streaming release velocity, DVD-team interference incidents). If governance drift is detected, escalate to corporate separation from a position of strength.
