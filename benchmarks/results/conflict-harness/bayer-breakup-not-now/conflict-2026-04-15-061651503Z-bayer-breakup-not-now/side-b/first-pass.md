# Bayer breakup: recommend fix operations first, preserve a timed split option

Bayer should **not pursue a breakup in March 2024**. The board should adopt a **24 to 36 month fix-first, split-later-if-needed plan** focused on four priorities: reduce leverage, contain litigation exposure, stabilize operating performance, and execute management simplification. The right framing is not whether a breakup is theoretically value-creating; it is whether Bayer can separate major businesses **now** without locking in avoidable value destruction, credit stress, and execution failure. On that question, the answer is no.

A near-term breakup has intuitive appeal because Bayer's conglomerate structure likely contributes to a valuation discount and weakens capital-allocation clarity. But the company is attempting to separate businesses while carrying about **EUR34.5 billion of net debt at year-end 2023**, facing **tens of thousands of unresolved glyphosate / Roundup cases and other Monsanto liabilities**, guiding to **lower 2024 EBITDA than 2023**, and trying to implement a broad simplification program expected to deliver roughly **EUR2 billion of annual cost savings from 2026**. In that condition, a breakup is more likely to be a distressed restructuring than a clean value-unlocking separation.

The core strategic issue is sequencing. Bayer first needs to improve the quality of the assets it would eventually separate. If the company splits now, creditors, counterparties, labor stakeholders, and litigation plaintiffs will all price off today's stressed position rather than a repaired one. That increases the risk of a subscale or overburdened spin, a higher cost of capital, and defensive deal structures that transfer much of the theoretical upside away from shareholders. By contrast, a two-to-three-year operational repair window gives management a chance to de-risk the balance sheet, improve earnings visibility, and clarify which liabilities sit where before revisiting portfolio separation from a position of greater bargaining power.

The best argument for an immediate breakup is that delay may destroy more trust: Bayer's equity value has been badly damaged since the Monsanto acquisition, investors are openly calling for asset sales or separation, and management could use "not now" to mean "never." That concern is real. The board should therefore reject not only a breakup now, but also an **open-ended deferral**. The correct response is a **conditional commitment**: management gets 24 to 36 months to hit explicit milestones on debt, litigation, pharma pipeline credibility, and simplification, after which the board formally re-evaluates separation options. This keeps strategic pressure on management while avoiding a rushed transaction under weak conditions.

The board should define the operating agenda tightly. First, litigation and liability containment must be treated as prerequisites for any major structural move, because unresolved Monsanto-related exposure clouds valuation and complicates liability allocation across entities. Second, debt reduction matters because a more leveraged separation reduces strategic flexibility and can force the stronger businesses to subsidize the weaker or the more legally exposed. Third, pharma needs clearer pipeline evidence; otherwise Bayer risks separating a business before investors can properly value its upside. Fourth, management simplification should proceed now, because some of the benefits investors seek from a breakup, including faster decisions and lower overhead, can be captured without the full cost and risk of a legal separation.

This approach is also more consistent with Germany's labor and governance context. A breakup of this scale is not just a capital-markets exercise; it is an organizational and political process involving works councils, labor considerations, and complex transition arrangements. Attempting that while simultaneously managing litigation, weak crop-science conditions, and operating resets materially raises execution risk.

The recommendation, then, is clear: **do not split Bayer now**. Announce a fix-first program with quarterly disclosure against milestone metrics and a pre-committed strategic review window in 24 to 36 months. If Bayer materially improves leverage, litigation visibility, operating trajectory, and internal simplification, the board can then choose among a fuller separation, targeted asset sales, or retaining the portfolio with stronger proof that conglomerate coordination is creating value.

## Decision Frame

Recommend **delay any breakup for 24 to 36 months** and prioritize litigation management, debt reduction, operating repair, pharma pipeline strengthening, and simplification. Pair that with a **hard review date and objective triggers** so "not now" remains a sequencing choice, not a permanent avoidance mechanism.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- The exact path and timing for glyphosate and other Monsanto-related liabilities remains uncertain and is the single biggest swing factor for separation feasibility.
- The magnitude of any true conglomerate discount versus business-specific underperformance is not fully quantified here.
- The market's standalone valuation of pharma, consumer health, and crop science depends on forward operating data not yet stabilized in March 2024.
- The board needs a clearer view of what debt and contingent liabilities each separated entity could credibly carry without impairing credit quality.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if Bayer can, within 24 to 36 months, show measurable debt reduction, improved litigation visibility, credible pharma pipeline traction, and delivery toward the restructuring program including the expected EUR2 billion annual savings from 2026. In that state, a split becomes a strategic choice executed from strength.

Fail if operations do not improve, litigation remains highly unstable, or management misses simplification milestones. In that case, the board should not automatically preserve the status quo; it should reopen more forceful options including targeted divestitures, liability ring-fencing alternatives, leadership/accountability changes, or a narrower breakup design.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A board decision memo with a **24 to 36 month separation-readiness scorecard** covering leverage, litigation, operating KPIs by division, pipeline milestones, restructuring delivery, and decision triggers for revisiting breakup, divestiture, or hold-co strategies.
