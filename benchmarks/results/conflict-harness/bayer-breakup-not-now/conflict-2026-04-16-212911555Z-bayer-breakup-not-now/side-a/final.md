# Strategic Recommendation: Delay Breakup — Stabilize First
**To:** Bayer Supervisory Board
**Date:** March 2024
**Re:** Separation timing decision

---

## Decision Frame

**Recommendation:** Do not proceed with a structural separation of Bayer's three businesses now. Pursue the 24-to-36-month operating stabilization program first, with a formal separation readiness review at month 24.

**The core argument:** A breakup executed under current conditions is unlikely to maximize shareholder value. Bayer's leverage, litigation exposure, and declining earnings create compounding headwinds that would complicate — and likely discount — any near-term separation. We cannot assert with certainty that creditors would capture the entire value unlock, because that depends on segment-level cash flow, covenant terms, and separation structure that the available evidence does not resolve. What we can say is that elevated leverage materially increases execution risk and negotiating friction: creditors gain leverage over capital structure allocation, and the absence of a quantified litigation tail makes it impossible for buyers or lenders to price assets accurately. These factors do not make a breakup impossible, but they make a value-accretive breakup significantly harder to execute now than after a stabilization period.

**What a breakup requires to be accretive:**
1. Manageable debt load that survives a capital structure split across standalone entities
2. Defined and quantified litigation tail so buyers and lenders can price assets without punitive uncertainty discounts
3. Stable or improving earnings in each segment so standalone businesses can service their own debt
4. Organizational readiness to run three independent governance structures

Bayer currently faces serious challenges on all four dimensions.

**Debt:** Net debt of approximately 34.5 billion euros at end of 2023 — up roughly 8.5% year-over-year — means any separation must allocate this burden across newly independent balance sheets. Without segment-level leverage capacity analysis (which is not available in the current evidence), we cannot specify exactly how creditors and equity holders would share the outcome. But we can say that high and rising leverage removes the board's optionality: it constrains which separation structures are even feasible and increases the probability that debt allocation terms will be dictated by creditors rather than designed by management. The uncertainty itself is a cost.

**Litigation:** Tens of thousands of unresolved Roundup/glyphosate cases represent an open-ended contingent liability. No rational buyer prices a segment carrying residual Monsanto risk without a substantial uncertainty discount. The discount is not speculative — it is the rational response to a liability with no defined floor or ceiling. Executing a split before the litigation tail is quantified means accepting that discount permanently into the separation price.

**Earnings trajectory:** Bayer's 2024 EBITDA guidance is below 2023 levels. The restructuring program is expected to deliver approximately 2 billion euros in annual savings from 2026 — savings that have not yet materialized and therefore cannot be reflected in current segment valuations. A separation completed after those savings are realized can embed them in valuation; a separation today cannot.

**Organizational complexity:** German codetermination rules and labor organization context add execution friction. The management simplification program currently underway is a prerequisite for running clean standalone governance — launching a structural split before internal simplification is complete would introduce avoidable operational risk.

---

## Counterargument Acknowledgment

The case for acting now is not frivolous. Investor trust has been severely damaged since the 2018 Monsanto acquisition. Continued delay risks being read as management entrenchment. Each quarter of conglomerate structure does impose a real discount — capital allocation across three dissimilar businesses is suboptimal, and the conglomerate structure limits each segment's ability to optimize its own strategy and investor base.

The risk of the "fix first" path is genuine: if operations do not improve over 24-36 months, the board will face the same separation question with even less shareholder patience. A credible commitment mechanism is essential — the board should not endorse an open-ended delay but rather a bounded stabilization window with explicit, pre-announced separation readiness criteria.

Nor can we assume that waiting automatically produces a better outcome. If litigation resolution takes longer than expected, or if the pharma pipeline does not recover, the 2026-2027 window may look no better than today. This risk is real, and the board should monitor it explicitly.

However: the question is not whether to break up, but when. Current conditions introduce compounding execution risk that a stabilization period can reduce. The conglomerate discount is a chronic cost; the unquantified litigation liability and peak leverage are acute constraints on deal quality. Addressing the acute constraints first is the correct sequencing — not because it guarantees a better outcome, but because it preserves more optionality and reduces the probability of a forced or distressed separation.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

1. **Litigation quantification:** The case packet references "tens of thousands" of unresolved cases but provides no estimate of the aggregate liability range. This is the single most important missing input. Until a credible floor/ceiling on the Roundup/Monsanto tail is established, no breakup structure can be properly designed or priced.

2. **Segment-level leverage capacity:** Which of the three businesses can independently service debt at current earnings levels is unknown. This gap is significant — it is the primary reason the debt claim must be stated as risk rather than certainty. If segment-level analysis showed that one or more businesses were clearly self-sustaining, the case for delay would weaken; if none are, it would strengthen.

3. **Debt covenant constraints:** Whether existing covenants require creditor consent for a structural separation, and what renegotiation terms creditors would impose, is not established by available evidence. This directly determines how much negotiating leverage management retains in any separation process.

4. **Pipeline inflection timeline:** The pharma pipeline's recovery trajectory is referenced but not quantified. If key assets achieve milestone readiness within 24 months, the pharma segment's standalone valuation improves substantially. If not, later separation still faces the same pipeline discount.

5. **Labor/codetermination constraints:** The specific constraints imposed by German labor law on a separation timeline have not been elaborated. These could extend any realistic execution window beyond the 24-to-36-month target.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

The board should adopt explicit pass/fail criteria for a separation readiness review at month 24:

| Criterion | Pass Threshold | Current Status |
|---|---|---|
| Net debt trajectory | Declining year-over-year; segment leverage capacity assessed | **Fail** — debt rising, segment analysis absent |
| Litigation tail | Quantified reserve with defined settlement framework | **Fail** — tens of thousands of cases unresolved |
| EBITDA trajectory | 2025 EBITDA at or above 2023 baseline | **Watch** — 2024 guidance already below 2023 |
| Restructuring savings | 2 billion euro run-rate achieved or firmly in sight | **Not yet** — expected from 2026 |
| Organizational readiness | Management simplification program complete | **In progress** |

All five criteria currently fail or are incomplete. Proceeding before meaningful progress on these criteria accepts unnecessary execution risk. The board should communicate clearly: this is "not now" with a defined clock and defined conditions — not "never" and not an open-ended delay.

---

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Litigation Scenario Analysis:** Commission a structured analysis quantifying the Roundup/glyphosate liability under base, adverse, and severe scenarios. This is the single highest-value piece of missing evidence — it determines which separation structures are feasible, what entity would carry the liability, and whether a Crop Science separation is viable at any near-term horizon.

**Segment-Level Standalone Credit Analysis:** Model the debt capacity of each business unit independently at current and projected earnings levels. This directly closes the gap in the debt claim — it converts a directional risk argument into a specific, testable constraint on separation structure and timing, and is the necessary input to any creditor negotiation strategy.
