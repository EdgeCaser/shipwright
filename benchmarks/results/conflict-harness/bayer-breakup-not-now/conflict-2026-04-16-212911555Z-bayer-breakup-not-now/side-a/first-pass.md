# Strategic Recommendation: Delay Breakup — Stabilize First
**To:** Bayer Supervisory Board
**Date:** March 2024
**Re:** Separation timing decision

---

## Decision Frame

**Recommendation:** Do not proceed with a structural separation of Bayer's three businesses now. Pursue the 24-to-36-month operating stabilization program first, with a formal separation readiness review at month 24.

**The core argument:** A breakup executed under current conditions would destroy value rather than unlock it. Bayer's leverage, litigation exposure, and declining earnings create a trifecta that would force the company to negotiate from weakness — ceding value to creditors, counterparties, and acquirers rather than capturing it for shareholders. The conglomerate discount is real, but it is not the primary driver of value destruction right now. Executing a clean separation requires financial headroom and legal clarity that Bayer does not currently possess.

**What a breakup requires to be accretive:**
1. Manageable debt load that survives a capital structure split
2. Defined and quantified litigation tail so buyers and lenders can price assets
3. Stable or improving earnings in each segment so standalone businesses can service their own debt
4. Organizational readiness to run three independent governance structures

Bayer currently fails all four tests.

**Debt:** Net debt of approximately 34.5 billion euros at end of 2023 — up roughly 8.5% year-over-year — means any separation must allocate this burden across newly independent balance sheets. Creditors will have significant leverage in determining which entity bears what portion. A breakup negotiated at peak leverage is a creditor-enriching event, not a shareholder-enriching one.

**Litigation:** Tens of thousands of unresolved Roundup/glyphosate cases, plus other Monsanto-related liabilities, represent an open-ended contingent liability. No rational buyer prices an acquisition of Crop Science (or any segment carrying residual Monsanto risk) without a substantial discount for this uncertainty. Executing a split before the litigation tail is quantified guarantees value leakage.

**Earnings trajectory:** Bayer's 2024 EBITDA guidance is below 2023 levels. Standalone entities cannot command premium multiples in a declining earnings environment. The restructuring program is expected to deliver approximately 2 billion euros in annual savings from 2026 — savings that have not yet materialized and therefore cannot be reflected in current valuations. A split today captures none of that improvement; a split in 2026-2027 may be able to sell it.

**Organizational complexity:** German codetermination rules and labor organization context add execution friction to any separation timeline. The management simplification program currently underway is itself a prerequisite for running clean standalone governance — launching a structural split before internal simplification is complete would be operationally reckless.

---

## Counterargument Acknowledgment

The case for acting now is not frivolous. Investor trust has been severely damaged since the 2018 Monsanto acquisition. Continued delay risks being read as management entrenchment. Each quarter of conglomerate structure does impose a real discount — capital allocation across three dissimilar businesses is suboptimal, and pharma R&D investment competes with crop science capex and consumer health marketing for a constrained budget.

The risk of the "fix first" path is also genuine: if operations do not improve over 24-36 months, the board will face the same separation question with even less shareholder patience. A credible commitment mechanism is essential — the board should not endorse an open-ended delay but rather a bounded stabilization window with explicit separation readiness criteria.

However: these arguments favor a *better-timed* breakup, not a breakup now. The conglomerate discount is a chronic wound; the debt and litigation exposure are acute ones. Treating the chronic condition while the acute one is bleeding is the wrong sequencing.

---

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

1. **Litigation quantification:** The case packet references "tens of thousands" of unresolved cases but provides no estimate of the aggregate liability range. The single most important input for separation timing is a credible floor/ceiling on the Roundup/Monsanto tail. Without this, no breakup structure can be properly designed.

2. **Creditor consent requirements:** It is unclear whether existing debt covenants require creditor consent for a structural separation, or what renegotiation terms creditors would impose. This could materially constrain timing and form of any split.

3. **Segment-level debt capacity:** Which of the three businesses can independently service debt at current earnings levels is unknown from available evidence. Pharma pipeline pressure and weak crop-science conditions may mean that none of the three standalone entities would be investment-grade without parental support — a critical feasibility question.

4. **Pipeline inflection timeline:** The pharma pipeline's recovery trajectory is referenced but not quantified. If key assets achieve milestone readiness within 24 months, the pharma segment's standalone valuation improves substantially. If not, a later split still faces the same pipeline discount.

5. **Labor/codetermination constraints:** The specific constraints imposed by German labor law on a separation timeline have not been elaborated. These could extend any realistic execution window beyond the 24-to-36-month target.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

The board should adopt explicit pass/fail criteria for a separation readiness review at month 24:

| Criterion | Pass Threshold | Current Status |
|---|---|---|
| Net debt / EBITDA | Below a level where standalone entities are investment-grade | **Fail** — debt rising, EBITDA declining |
| Litigation tail | Quantified reserve with defined settlement framework | **Fail** — tens of thousands of cases unresolved |
| EBITDA trajectory | 2025 EBITDA at or above 2023 baseline | **Watch** — 2024 guidance already below 2023 |
| Restructuring savings | 2 billion euro run-rate achieved or firmly in sight | **Not yet** — expected from 2026 |
| Organizational readiness | Management simplification program complete | **In progress** |

All five criteria currently fail or are incomplete. Proceeding with a separation before any criteria are met is not a strategic decision — it is capitulation to short-term investor pressure at the cost of long-term value.

The board should communicate clearly: this is "not now" with a defined clock and defined conditions, not "never" and not an open-ended delay.

---

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Litigation Scenario Analysis:** Commission a structured analysis quantifying the Roundup/glyphosate liability under base, adverse, and severe scenarios. This is the single highest-value piece of missing evidence — it determines which separation structures are feasible, what entity would carry the liability, and whether a Crop Science separation is even viable at any near-term horizon. Until this analysis exists, all breakup planning is speculative.

Secondary: a segment-level standalone credit analysis to determine debt capacity for each business unit independently, which would establish whether a separation creates three viable standalone companies or three distressed ones.
