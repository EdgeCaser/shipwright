# Strategic Recommendation: Delay Breakup — Fix Operations First

**To:** Bayer Supervisory Board
**From:** Strategy Advisory
**Date:** March 2024
**Re:** Breakup Timing Decision

---

## Decision Frame

**Recommendation:** Do not proceed with a breakup in the near term. Commit to a 24–36 month stabilization program focused on debt reduction, litigation resolution, pharma pipeline recovery, and operational simplification — then revisit separation from a position of strength.

This is not a decision against breakup in principle. It is a decision about sequencing. A conglomerate discount is real, and investors are right that the three businesses have distinct capital profiles that may deserve independent ownership structures. The question before this board is not whether to separate eventually, but whether doing so *now* would unlock or destroy value.

The honest answer is that a breakup today would occur under conditions that are unfavorable for maximizing separation value. Bayer's current liability profile, debt load, and execution bandwidth create meaningful headwinds to a clean separation — though the precise magnitude of those headwinds requires further diligence than the available evidence supports.

**Decision logic:** Value from a breakup is contingent on each separated entity being creditworthy, litigation-ring-fenced, and independently investable. Current conditions work against all three. Proceeding now risks handing acquirers and creditors negotiating leverage; demonstrably improving operations first would shift that leverage back to the board.

---

## Supporting Claims

### 1. The debt load and earnings trajectory create unfavorable conditions for separation — though the precise impracticality requires further diligence

Net debt at end of 2023 stood at approximately €34.5 billion, up roughly 8.5% year-over-year. A breakup requires allocating this debt across successor entities. With EBITDA guidance for 2024 already below 2023 levels, each spun-off business would enter the market carrying a debt burden against a deteriorating near-term earnings base — a combination that is likely to narrow the pool of creditworthy standalone entities and raise refinancing risk.

The restructuring program is expected to yield approximately €2 billion in annual cost savings from 2026 onward. That savings stream has not yet materialized or been validated in the income statement. Waiting until restructuring savings are visible strengthens the narrative for each successor entity's credit profile. However, the critique is correct that the available evidence does not demonstrate segment-level leverage capacity, pro forma capital structures for each standalone entity, or whether separation dis-synergies and continued litigation drag might offset those savings. The claim that separation is *structurally impractical* overstates what can be derived from aggregate-level debt and EBITDA data alone. The more defensible framing is that current conditions are unfavorable and that the board lacks the segment-level financial analysis needed to execute a value-maximizing separation.

### 2. Unresolved Roundup / glyphosate litigation creates a liability tail that complicates any transaction

Tens of thousands of glyphosate cases remain unresolved, alongside other Monsanto-related liabilities. Any separation transaction — whether spin-off, sale, or IPO — requires a buyer, underwriter, or rating agency to take a view on liability allocation. While that tail is undefined and contested, counterparties face significant uncertainty pricing it. The discount applied to reflect that uncertainty may well exceed the conglomerate discount the breakup was intended to eliminate — though the precise comparison is not quantifiable from the available evidence.

A breakup before litigation resolution also creates structural design risk: concentrating liabilities in one entity may leave it inadequately capitalized, while distributing them across entities creates inter-company indemnification complexity. Neither outcome is obviously better for shareholders than waiting for greater clarity.

### 3. Management bandwidth is already stretched

Bayer is simultaneously running a major internal simplification program, managing pharma pipeline pressure, navigating weak crop-science market conditions, and handling litigation. A major corporate separation requires sustained C-suite and board attention, external advisors, regulatory filings across multiple jurisdictions, and employee communication in a German labor relations context where works councils have significant standing. Layering breakup execution on top of this agenda risks doing none of it well.

The 'fix first' path is not delay for its own sake — it is recognition that operational recovery is a prerequisite for a transaction that maximizes rather than destroys value.

## Acknowledging the Strongest Counterarguments

**Counterargument 1: Delay entrenches the conglomerate discount and destroys shareholder trust further.**

This is the most serious objection. Investors who have watched equity value erode since the 2018 Monsanto acquisition have limited patience for management promises. If operations do not improve during the 24–36 month window, the board will have spent credibility without producing a transaction.

*Why the recommendation still holds:* The board should treat the stabilization program as a conditional commitment with defined milestones, not a blank check for delay. Specific operational conditions — debt reduction targets, litigation settlement progress, evidence of restructuring savings materializing — should be set now and reviewed at 12-month intervals. If those milestones are not met, the board retains the option to accelerate separation. What the board should not do is execute a transaction before the segment-level financial analysis exists to structure it well.

**Counterargument 2: Each business may already be mature enough to stand alone, and the conglomerate structure prevents optimal capital allocation.**

This may be true in steady state. Capital allocation discipline can be improved operationally — through segment-level accountability, separate reporting, and management incentive structures — without requiring legal separation under current conditions. The conglomerate discount is real but is not the binding constraint on value creation right now. The binding constraints are litigation uncertainty, debt, and the absence of the segment-level financial information needed to structure a separation well.

---

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- **Segment-level leverage capacity and pro forma capital structures:** The available evidence is aggregate. Before any separation decision, the board needs segment-level debt allocation analysis and pro forma credit profiles for each standalone entity. This is the most important missing input.
- **Litigation settlement trajectory and total remaining liability:** The case packet does not specify the rate of resolution or management's internal estimate of total exposure. The 24–36 month timeline implicitly assumes meaningful progress. If resolution is slower, the window extends.
- **Separation dis-synergies:** The €2B restructuring savings figure is stated but the packet does not address whether those savings survive separation or are partially offset by dis-synergies from splitting shared functions and procurement.
- **Creditor covenants:** The packet does not specify whether existing debt covenants restrict corporate reorganization options. This could be a binding constraint independent of strategic preferences.
- **Pharma pipeline value:** Pressure is noted but specific assets at risk and probability-weighted pipeline value are not detailed.
- **Crop science market cycle:** Weak conditions are noted but not quantified. Cycle timing affects the standalone attractiveness of that segment.
- **Works council constraints:** German co-determination rules give works councils significant influence over restructuring timelines. Specific constraints are not detailed.
