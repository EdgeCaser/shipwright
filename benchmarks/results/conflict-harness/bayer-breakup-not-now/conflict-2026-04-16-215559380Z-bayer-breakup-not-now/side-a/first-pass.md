# Strategic Recommendation: Delay Breakup — Fix Operations First

**To:** Bayer Supervisory Board
**From:** Strategy Advisory
**Date:** March 2024
**Re:** Breakup Timing Decision

---

## Decision Frame

**Recommendation:** Do not proceed with a breakup in the near term. Commit to a 24–36 month stabilization program focused on debt reduction, litigation resolution, pharma pipeline recovery, and operational simplification — then revisit separation from a position of strength.

This is not a decision against breakup in principle. It is a decision about sequencing. A conglomerate discount is real, and investors are right that the three businesses (pharmaceuticals, consumer health, crop science) have distinct capital profiles that may deserve independent ownership structures. The question before this board is not whether to separate eventually, but whether doing so *now* would unlock or destroy value.

The honest answer is that a breakup today is a forced sale in disguise. Bayer's current liability profile, debt load, and execution bandwidth make a clean separation impractical and likely value-destructive.

**Decision logic:** Value from a breakup is contingent on each separated entity being creditworthy, litigation-ring-fenced, and independently investable. None of those conditions hold today. Proceeding now hands acquirers and creditors the negotiating leverage; waiting — and demonstrably improving operations — hands it back to the board.

---

## Supporting Claims

### 1. The debt load makes a clean separation structurally difficult

Net debt at end of 2023 stood at approximately €34.5 billion, up roughly 8.5% year-over-year. A breakup requires allocating this debt across successor entities. With EBITDA guidance for 2024 already guided below 2023 levels, each spun-off business would enter the market carrying a debt burden against a deteriorating earnings base. That combination narrows the pool of creditworthy standalone entities and raises refinancing risk at the moment of separation — precisely when new capital structures are being negotiated.

The restructuring program is expected to yield approximately €2 billion in annual cost savings from 2026 onward. That savings stream has not yet materialized. Waiting until it is visible in the income statement strengthens the credit profile of each successor entity and improves separation terms.

### 2. Unresolved Roundup / glyphosate litigation is a poison-pill for any transaction

Tens of thousands of glyphosate cases remain unresolved, alongside other Monsanto-related liabilities. Any separation transaction — whether spin-off, sale, or IPO — requires a buyer, underwriter, or rating agency to take a view on liability allocation. While that tail is undefined and contested, no rational counterparty will price it fairly. The discount applied to reflect uncertainty will exceed the conglomerate discount the breakup was intended to eliminate.

Furthermore, a breakup before litigation resolution creates structural risk: if liabilities are concentrated in one entity post-split, that entity may be inadequately capitalized to absorb them. If they are distributed across entities, the legal complexity and inter-company indemnification arrangements could be litigated for years. Neither outcome serves shareholders.

### 3. Management bandwidth is already stretched — breakup execution would crowd out operational recovery

Bayer is simultaneously running a major internal simplification program, managing pharma pipeline pressure, navigating weak crop-science market conditions, and handling litigation. A major corporate separation requires sustained C-suite and board attention, external advisors, regulatory filings across multiple jurisdictions, and employee communication in a German labor relations context where works councils have significant standing. Layering breakup execution on top of this agenda risks doing none of it well.

The 'fix first' path is not delay for its own sake — it is recognition that the operational recovery *is* the prerequisite for the transaction. A Bayer that has delivered on restructuring savings, reduced debt, and stabilized its pharma pipeline in 2025–2026 commands a materially better negotiating position than Bayer in March 2024.

## Acknowledging the Strongest Counterarguments

**Counterargument 1: Delay entrenches the conglomerate discount and destroys shareholder trust further.**

This is the most serious objection. Investors who have watched equity value erode since the 2018 Monsanto acquisition have limited patience for management promises. If operations do not improve during the 24–36 month window, the board will have spent credibility without producing a transaction — and the breakup, when it eventually comes, will happen under even greater duress.

*Why the recommendation still holds:* The board should treat the stabilization program as a conditional commitment, not a blank check for delay. Specific operational milestones — debt reduction targets, litigation settlement progress, evidence of restructuring savings materializing — should be defined now and reviewed at 12-month intervals. If those milestones are not met, the board retains the option to accelerate separation. What the board should not do is execute a transaction before those conditions are in place, because the downside of a distressed separation is worse than the downside of delay.

**Counterargument 2: Each business is already mature enough to stand alone, and the conglomerate structure prevents optimal capital allocation.**

This may be true in a steady-state world. It is not the world Bayer is in. Capital allocation discipline can be improved operationally — through segment-level accountability, separate reporting, and management incentive structures — without requiring legal separation under current conditions. The conglomerate discount is real but is not the binding constraint on value creation right now. The binding constraints are litigation uncertainty and debt.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- **Litigation settlement trajectory:** The case packet does not specify the rate of resolution of glyphosate cases or management's internal estimate of total remaining liability. The 24–36 month timeline implicitly assumes meaningful progress. If resolution is slower, the window extends.
- **Pharma pipeline specifics:** The recommendation assumes the pipeline has recoverable assets worth protecting through stabilization. The packet notes pressure but does not detail which assets are at risk or what the probability-weighted pipeline value is.
- **Crop science market cycle:** Weak conditions are noted but not quantified. If the cycle turns favorable in 2025, crop science's standalone attractiveness improves; if it worsens, the case for eventual separation of that unit may shift.
- **Labor relations and works council positions:** German co-determination rules give works councils significant influence over restructuring and separation timelines. The packet flags this as a complicating factor but does not detail the specific constraints.
- **Creditor covenants:** The packet does not specify whether existing debt covenants restrict certain forms of corporate reorganization. This could be a binding constraint on separation options independent of strategic preferences.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Milestone framework memo:** Define the specific operational, financial, and litigation milestones that would trigger a go/no-go decision on separation at the 12-month and 24-month marks. This artifact converts the 'not now' position from a delay into a structured conditional commitment — which is what separates a credible strategy from a holding pattern. It should include: debt reduction targets, a litigation resolution threshold (e.g., percentage of cases resolved or liability cap established), restructuring savings confirmation, and pharma pipeline stabilization criteria.

This is the document that will determine whether investors accept 'not now' as a strategy or reject it as evasion.
