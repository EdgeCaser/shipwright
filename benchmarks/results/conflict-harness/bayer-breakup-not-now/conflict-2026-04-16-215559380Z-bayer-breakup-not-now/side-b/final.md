# Recommendation: Do Not Break Up Bayer Now; Use a Time-Bounded Fix-First Window With Explicit Triggers

Bayer should delay any breakup for 24 to 36 months and focus first on litigation management, debt reduction, operating improvement, pharmaceuticals pipeline strengthening, and management simplification. This is not a claim that conditions will automatically improve on their own. It is a sequencing judgment: a breakup attempted now would be launched while debt is elevated, 2024 EBITDA guidance is weaker than 2023, unresolved Monsanto-related liabilities remain large, and separation would face creditor, labor, and execution complications. In that setting, the board would be choosing a complex structural move at a moment when the company has reduced room for error. [ctx-3][ctx-5][ctx-6][ctx-9][ctx-10]

The strongest critique of a fix-first approach is valid: delay by itself does not solve anything. Bayer's debt had already risen by the end of 2023, 2024 guidance was lower, and the litigation path was still unresolved. The board therefore should not treat 24 to 36 months as a passive waiting period or assume that a later split will naturally be easier. Instead, the rationale for delaying is to create a bounded proof period for management's "not now" stance. Management has already framed the period ahead around improving operating performance, reducing debt, strengthening the pharma pipeline, and addressing litigation, while pursuing restructuring expected to generate annual cost savings from 2026. The board should use that agenda as a test: if it produces visible progress, Bayer will revisit separation from a stronger position; if it does not, the case for breakup becomes more urgent rather than less. [ctx-1][ctx-2][ctx-4][ctx-5][ctx-6][ctx-11]

That is why "not now" is stronger than either immediate breakup or open-ended delay. Immediate separation may reduce conglomerate discount and sharpen capital allocation, but the packet itself says those benefits may be hard to realize while debt and litigation remain unresolved and that creditor and execution risk could make a near-term breakup less value-accretive. Open-ended delay would further damage investor trust. A time-bounded fix-first program addresses both concerns: it preserves the option to split later, avoids forcing a transaction under current strain, and imposes accountability so management cannot hide behind simplification rhetoric indefinitely. ## Decision Frame

Delay any breakup for 24 to 36 months, but only as a milestone-based fix-first program rather than a passive deferral. Keep separation as a live option and require a formal board review if progress on debt, litigation, operating performance, pipeline strengthening, and simplification is insufficient.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- The packet does not show whether Bayer can materially reduce litigation exposure within the proposed window.
- It does not establish whether debt reduction and operating improvement will be strong enough to change the economics of a later split.
- It does not quantify how much value loss comes from conglomerate structure versus legal, balance-sheet, and operating pressures.
- It does not specify what creditor or labor constraints would relax, if at all, after a fix-first period.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if the board wants a decision that preserves optionality while imposing explicit accountability on management, and if it is prepared to revisit separation promptly if the fix-first plan does not produce visible improvement.

Fail if the board concludes that the current structure itself is the overriding problem and that the risks of delay exceed the added execution risk of breaking up under today's debt, litigation, and operating conditions.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A supervisory-board scorecard memo defining the specific review triggers for continuing the fix-first plan versus launching a breakup evaluation, tied to debt reduction, litigation progress, operating performance, restructuring delivery, and pharma pipeline strengthening.
