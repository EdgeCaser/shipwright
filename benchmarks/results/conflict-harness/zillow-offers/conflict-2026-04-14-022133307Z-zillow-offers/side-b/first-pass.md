# Exit Zillow Offers and refocus Zillow on the marketplace

Zillow should exit iBuying rather than attempt another reform cycle. The board's decision is not whether iBuying is strategically interesting; it is whether Zillow has demonstrated a repeatable right to win in a business where small pricing errors destroy economics. The evidence says no. Zillow Offers is now consuming management attention, capital, and investor confidence while obscuring a high-margin core business that is still growing.

The immediate trigger is not a temporary operating bottleneck. Zillow bought 9,680 homes in Q3 2021 but sold only 3,032, leaving the company with an inventory overhang of roughly 7,000 homes, many acquired above current market value. Management has already pivoted the pricing algorithm twice in 2021, yet estimated write-down exposure still sits around $300-500M. That is not a tuning problem. It is evidence that Zillow's current forecasting and operating system is structurally unfit for a thin-margin principal-risk business.

The economics are decisive. Home flipping typically requires sub-2% pricing accuracy to earn acceptable returns, while Zestimate median error is about 6.9% nationally. Even if that metric is not a perfect proxy for Zillow Offers underwriting performance, it is directionally fatal: the company's consumer-facing valuation edge is too imprecise for a business where basis-point mistakes compound across holding costs, repairs, financing, and resale timing. In a housing market that rose 19.5% year over year and still produced widening losses, Zillow has effectively failed the friendliest plausible stress test. If the market cools, losses likely worsen.

The strategic cost is equally important. Zillow Offers generated about $1.2B of Q3 2021 revenue, roughly 70% of total company revenue, but that scale is low-quality because it carries negative EBITDA and masks the strength of the IMT segment. By contrast, the core marketplace business produced about $490M of revenue with roughly 30% EBITDA margins and 20%+ year-over-year growth. The board should not allow a low-margin, balance-sheet-intensive activity with deteriorating unit economics to distort capital allocation away from the better business.

The strongest argument for reform is that Zillow has already built consumer awareness, operating infrastructure, and a high-traffic funnel that could support a narrower, lower-volume iBuying model. Opendoor's comparatively better performance suggests disciplined operators can get closer to breakeven. But that is an argument that iBuying may be viable for someone, not proof that Zillow has a differentiated advantage in it. Zillow's original thesis was that Zestimate and audience scale would create superior underwriting. After multiple model pivots, rapid volume growth, and a major backlog, the evidence cuts the other way: Zillow's scale amplified losses faster than its systems learned.

A reform plan would also suffer from credibility problems. Management has publicly cited labor and supply constraints, while the core internal issue is pricing model failure. If the board chooses reform, it is effectively authorizing more capital, more execution risk, and more time to repair a business whose claimed edge has already been falsified in market conditions that should have helped it. That is a poor use of board attention when Zillow's stock has already fallen from about $200 in February 2021 to about $85 in October 2021.

The better path is orderly exit. Zillow should immediately stop new home purchases, aggressively liquidate existing inventory with speed prioritized over margin preservation, resize the cost base tied to Offers, and communicate a clean strategic reset around the high-margin housing marketplace, financing, seller services, and transaction-enablement products that do not require Zillow to hold inventory. The company can preserve pieces of the customer proposition by partnering with third-party buyers rather than acting as principal.

Exiting is not a retreat from innovation. It is recognition that Zillow's durable advantage is demand aggregation, consumer intent, and marketplace monetization, not warehouse-style balance-sheet risk in residential housing. The board should treat this as a strategic unbundling: keep the transaction layer where Zillow can earn fees and data advantages, but abandon the capital-intensive segment where it has not earned the right to scale.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Exit Zillow Offers.

Why this decision clears the threshold:
- The business has failed the core right-to-win test. Zillow's pricing capability is not accurate enough for thin-margin flipping economics.
- The current losses are structural, not merely cyclical or operational, as shown by repeated model pivots and worsening exposure.
- Offers is diluting valuation quality by replacing high-margin marketplace revenue with low-quality, balance-sheet-heavy revenue.
- An exit unlocks a clearer equity story and reallocates capital and talent toward segments where Zillow has demonstrated strong margins and growth.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- The exact geographic distribution of the 7,000-home backlog is not specified; market-by-market loss severity may vary.
- The case gives Zestimate national median error, not the precise underwriting error rate for Zillow Offers acquisitions.
- Severance, contract termination, and liquidation costs are not quantified, so full exit cost is directionally but not precisely bounded.
- The degree to which partner-based alternatives could preserve seller conversion economics is not yet modeled.

These gaps matter for implementation design, but not for the directional decision. None would plausibly reverse the conclusion that Zillow should stop principal-risk home buying.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass for board decision:
- Enough evidence exists to choose exit over reform now.
- The case establishes sustained model failure, poor fit with economics, and clear opportunity cost versus the core business.

Fail for detailed execution plan:
- Additional work is needed on inventory unwind sequencing, workforce restructuring, investor messaging, and partner model replacement.
