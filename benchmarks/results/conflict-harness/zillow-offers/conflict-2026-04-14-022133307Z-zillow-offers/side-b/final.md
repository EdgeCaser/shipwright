# Revised artifact

# Exit Zillow Offers and refocus Zillow on the marketplace

Zillow should exit iBuying rather than authorize another internal reform cycle. The board does not need to prove that iBuying is impossible in general, or that Zillow could never improve its pricing model over a longer time horizon. It needs to decide whether Zillow, in October 2021, should keep allocating capital, management attention, and credibility to a principal-risk business that is currently failing while the core marketplace business remains attractive. On that decision, exit is the stronger choice.

The immediate facts are severe. Zillow bought 9,680 homes in Q3 2021 but sold only 3,032, leaving an inventory backlog of roughly 7,000 homes, many bought above current market value. Estimated write-down exposure is $300-500M. The company has already changed the pricing approach twice in 2021, yet losses widened instead of stabilizing. That does not prove the capability could never be fixed. It does prove that Zillow has not demonstrated a reliable control system today, which is what matters in a thin-margin business carrying large balance-sheet risk.

The economics remain unfavorable. Home flipping typically operates on 2-5% gross margins and requires very tight pricing discipline, while Zestimate median error is about 6.9% nationally. That national error rate is not identical to Zillow Offers underwriting error, so it should not be treated as a precise one-to-one measure. But it is still directionally important: Zillow's public pricing advantage appears far too noisy to justify confidence in scaled principal buying, especially once holding costs, repairs, financing, and resale timing are included. In a housing market that was still up 19.5% year over year, Zillow nevertheless produced worsening performance. If even a favorable market backdrop did not protect margins, a cooler market likely makes the risk worse.

The strongest pro-reform argument is that Zillow may have combined immature models with reckless scaling. Buying 9,680 homes in one quarter while still tuning underwriting likely amplified losses. A narrower pilot, slower acquisition pace, and tighter geography could have produced a cleaner test. Opendoor's relatively better performance also shows that disciplined operators can get closer to breakeven over time. Those points are valid, but they still do not make reform the right board decision now. They imply only that a more cautious experiment might have been worth trying earlier, not that Zillow should continue funding one after this scale of failure, backlog, and investor damage.

That distinction matters. The case for exit should rest less on saying Zillow has permanently disproven any future iBuying capability and more on saying the company has not earned the right to keep betting on it from here. Offers generated about $1.2B of Q3 2021 revenue, roughly 70% of company revenue, but with negative EBITDA and rising capital intensity. By contrast, the IMT segment generated about $490M of revenue with roughly 30% EBITDA margins and 20%+ year-over-year growth. The board's job is capital allocation under uncertainty. When one business is consuming cash and obscuring the performance of a high-margin core, the burden of proof should be on the turnaround case. Zillow cannot meet that burden with the evidence in hand.

A reform plan would require management to ask for more time, more capital, and more credibility after already missing the core thesis that Zestimate and audience scale would create underwriting superiority. Even if a lower-volume redesign could theoretically work, the board should reject it because the expected value is unattractive relative to simply exiting, unwinding inventory, rightsizing the cost base, and preserving the customer proposition through partners rather than through owned inventory.

The better path is orderly exit. Zillow should stop new home purchases, liquidate existing inventory with speed prioritized over margin preservation, restructure the Offers-related cost base, and reposition Zillow around marketplace, financing, seller services, and transaction-enablement products that do not require balance-sheet exposure. That preserves Zillow's real advantage: consumer demand aggregation and monetization at high margins, not speculative housing inventory risk.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Exit Zillow Offers.

Why this decision clears the threshold:
- Zillow has not shown a reliable underwriting and operating system for scaled iBuying at the point the board must decide.
- Current exposure of roughly 7,000 homes and $300-500M of write-down risk makes continued experimentation unusually expensive.
- Offers is degrading valuation quality by replacing attractive marketplace economics with negative-EBITDA, balance-sheet-intensive revenue.
- Even if a slower reform model might have been theoretically possible, the incremental expected value of pursuing it now is worse than exiting and reallocating capital to the core business.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- The exact underwriting error rate for Zillow Offers acquisitions is not provided; Zestimate's national median error is only a directional proxy.
- The geographic mix of the 7,000-home backlog is unspecified, so unwind losses may differ materially by market.
- A true low-volume reform pilot was not tested, so the case does not prove that no narrower version of iBuying could ever work for Zillow.
- One-time exit costs, including severance, contract unwind, and liquidation friction, are not fully quantified.
- The retention rate of seller demand under a partner-led cash-offer model is not yet modeled.

These gaps affect implementation design and the precision of the financial bridge. They do not overturn the board-level conclusion that Zillow should stop principal-risk home buying now.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass for board decision:
- The evidence is sufficient to choose exit over continued reform in October 2021.
- The case shows severe current losses, weak demonstrated control, and clear opportunity cost versus the core marketplace business.

Fail for detailed execution plan:
- More work is needed on market-by-market unwind sequencing, restructuring costs, partner economics, and investor messaging.

A 90-day exit and reallocation plan covering:

- capital reallocation into IMT, mortgage, and transaction services
