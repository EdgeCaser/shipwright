# Strategic Recommendation: Exit Zillow Offers

**To:** Zillow Group Board of Directors
**From:** Strategy Advisor
**Date:** October 2021
**Classification:** Confidential — Board Eyes Only

---

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Should Zillow reform its iBuying operation (Zillow Offers) with tighter controls, or exit the business entirely and return focus to the high-margin marketplace?

**Recommendation: Exit Zillow Offers. Fully. Now.**

The evidence is unambiguous: Zillow Offers has a structural problem, not an execution problem. The Zestimate's 6.9% national median error rate is fundamentally incompatible with an industry that requires sub-2% pricing accuracy to generate 2-5% gross margins. Two algorithm pivots in 2021 failed to close this gap. Continuing to operate means continuing to bet hundreds of millions of dollars per quarter on a pricing engine that is demonstrably not fit for purpose.

This is not a recommendation to abandon ambition. It is a recommendation to stop compounding losses in a business where Zillow lacks a defensible edge, and to redirect capital toward the marketplace business where Zillow's competitive position is dominant and margins are exceptional.

### The Structural Case for Exit

**1. The pricing gap is architectural, not tunable.**

The Zestimate was built to give consumers a rough home value estimate — a demand-generation tool for Zillow's advertising business. It was never engineered for transactional precision. At 6.9% median error nationally, the algorithm overpays or underpays by an average of ~$24,000 on a $350,000 home. The home flipping industry requires accuracy within ~$7,000 on that same home to achieve viable margins. This is not a calibration problem; it is a data architecture problem. The Zestimate lacks granular, real-time inputs (renovation quality, neighborhood micro-trends, property condition) that Opendoor and experienced flippers incorporate through boots-on-the-ground inspection and narrower geographic focus.

Zillow has already attempted two algorithm corrections in 2021. Neither closed the gap. Further iteration faces diminishing returns: each correction tightens some parameters but introduces new blind spots in a housing market with rising volatility.

**2. The inventory position signals compounding risk from multiple failure modes.**

Zillow holds ~7,000 homes, many purchased above current market value. The estimated write-down exposure of $300-500M represents real capital destruction. In Q3 2021, Zillow purchased 9,680 homes but sold only 3,032 — a 3.2:1 buy-to-sell ratio.

This throughput imbalance likely reflects multiple interacting causes: pricing model overpayment reducing resale viability, renovation and labor bottlenecks slowing disposition, and aggressive volume targets outpacing operational capacity. The critical point is not that pricing failure is the sole driver, but that these factors are mutually reinforcing and structurally embedded. Pricing errors create inventory that overwhelms operations, which slows sales, which increases carrying costs, which widens losses. The system is failing as a system — isolating any single cause understates the compounding risk.

What we can observe without ambiguity: Zillow Offers EBITDA is negative and losses are widening quarter-over-quarter, the company has paused purchases (whatever the stated rationale), and the ~7,000-home portfolio represents $300-500M in write-down exposure. Whether the dominant cause is pricing, operations, or their interaction, the outcome is the same — capital destruction at scale.

Every month these homes sit on the books, carrying costs (taxes, maintenance, insurance, financing) compound the loss. If the housing market cools — and Case-Shiller's 19.5% YoY gain is historically unsustainable — the write-down exposure grows.

**A diagnostic gap remains:** We lack per-home geography mix, cohort-level days-to-resale, renovation completion rates, and contribution margin analysis needed to precisely attribute losses between pricing errors and operational bottlenecks. This gap is itself an argument for exit — if the board cannot isolate why the business is losing money after three years and two algorithm pivots, the system is not legible enough to reform with confidence.

**3. Zillow Offers is cannibalizing the core business's story.**

The IMT (Internet, Media & Technology) segment generated $490M in Q3 2021 at ~30% EBITDA margins with 20%+ YoY growth. This is an exceptional software-margin business. Yet Zillow's stock has fallen from $200 to $85 because investors cannot separate marketplace strength from Offers losses. Zillow Offers generates 70% of total revenue but negative EBITDA — it inflates the top line while destroying the margin profile.

Exiting Offers would reveal a $2B+ annual run-rate marketplace business growing 20%+ with 30% EBITDA margins. That is a fundamentally different investment thesis — one the market will reward.

**4. The competitive landscape confirms there is no latecomer advantage.**

Opendoor operates in narrower geographies with lower volume and is consistently closer to breakeven. Their relative performance suggests that iBuying viability depends on operational discipline and geographic focus — exactly what Zillow's scale-first, algorithm-driven approach lacks. Zillow cannot out-execute Opendoor by doing more of what has already failed. Matching Opendoor's discipline would require Zillow to dramatically shrink Offers' footprint, eliminating the scale thesis that justified the business.

### The Reform Alternative — And Why It Fails

The strongest counterargument is: "Tighten pricing controls, reduce volume to manageable levels, and operate Zillow Offers as a smaller, disciplined business that feeds leads into the marketplace."

This argument fails for three reasons:

1. **Reduced volume destroys the economic thesis.** Zillow Offers was predicated on scale: high-volume, algorithm-driven purchasing would achieve unit economics through throughput. A smaller Offers program is just an expensive, capital-intensive lead-gen tool that competes with Zillow's own marketplace for attention and resources.

2. **Organizational distraction is real and measurable.** Zillow grew from 5,400 to 8,000+ employees since 2019, primarily for Offers. A "reformed" Offers program still requires hundreds of operations staff, local market teams, renovation contractors, and risk management infrastructure. Every dollar and hour of leadership attention spent on Offers is not spent on marketplace innovation.

3. **The algorithm problem does not solve itself at lower volume.** Buying fewer homes with the same flawed pricing engine just means smaller losses, not profitability. And the housing market is showing signs of cooling — tighter controls in a softening market means even fewer viable purchases, further undermining the volume economics.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

| Unknown | Why It Matters | How to Resolve |
|---|---|---|
| Per-home loss attribution: pricing error vs. operational bottleneck vs. market timing | Determines whether any subset of the Offers operation could be viable in isolation; without this, reform proposals are speculative | Cohort analysis by geography, purchase date, days-to-resale, renovation status, and contribution margin |
| Exact per-home loss distribution across the 7,000-unit portfolio | Determines whether orderly liquidation or bulk sale minimizes losses | Internal portfolio analysis by home vintage and geography |
| Contractual obligations and pipeline commitments | Homes under contract but not yet closed represent additional exposure | Legal and operations audit of pipeline |
| Employee retention risk during wind-down | Key marketplace talent may exit during restructuring uncertainty | Retention package planning before announcement |
| Housing market trajectory Q4 2021 - Q2 2022 | A continued rise buys time for liquidation; a correction accelerates losses | Scenario modeling at +5%, flat, and -10% price trajectories |
| Tax and accounting treatment of write-down | Timing and structure of write-down affects reported financials and tax benefits | CFO and external auditor guidance |
| Competitor response to Zillow exit | Opendoor may expand aggressively into Zillow's vacated markets, potentially affecting marketplace lead flow | Competitive intelligence assessment |
| Zillow Offers as data flywheel for Zestimate improvement | Transaction data from Offers may improve Zestimate accuracy for marketplace users | Data science team assessment of marginal value vs. cost |

1. Complete portfolio-level loss analysis — including cohort-level attribution across pricing, operations, and market factors — to determine optimal liquidation strategy (orderly sale vs. bulk disposition)
2. Develop workforce transition plan for ~2,600 Offers-related employees
3. Prepare investor communication that reframes Zillow as a pure-play marketplace with best-in-class margins
4. Model tax treatment of write-down to optimize timing

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Artifact:** Zillow Offers Wind-Down Execution Plan

**Scope:** A 90-day operational playbook covering:
1. Portfolio liquidation strategy (segmented by geography, loss severity, and market conditions)
2. Causal diagnostic of inventory losses (pricing vs. operations vs. market timing) to inform liquidation prioritization and any residual iBuying partnerships
3. Workforce reduction and retention plan (identify critical marketplace talent to retain, severance terms for Offers-specific roles)
4. Investor and market communication strategy (earnings call script, analyst Q&A preparation, reframing narrative)
5. Financial modeling of write-down scenarios (immediate recognition vs. phased, tax optimization)
6. Competitive positioning post-exit (how to maintain seller relationships without transactional capability)

**Why this artifact next:** The board decision is binary and the evidence supports exit. The remaining risk is execution — a poorly managed wind-down could damage the marketplace brand, trigger a talent exodus, or result in fire-sale losses on the portfolio. The execution plan converts this strategic recommendation into an actionable sequence with clear ownership and milestones. Embedding the causal diagnostic into the execution plan (rather than delaying the exit decision) ensures that attribution analysis informs liquidation tactics without giving reform advocates a reason to stall.
