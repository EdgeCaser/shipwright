# Recommendation: Exit Zillow Offers and refocus Zillow on asset-light transaction businesses

## Executive Recommendation
Zillow should exit iBuying entirely rather than pursue another reform of Zillow Offers. The core reason is not that the public Zestimate error rate alone proves iBuying is impossible; it is that Zillow's actual operating results already show the company lacks a repeatable underwriting and execution system robust enough for a thin-margin principal-risk business. In Q3 2021 Zillow bought 9,680 homes and sold only 3,032, accumulated an approximately 7,000-home backlog, and faced an estimated $300M-$500M write-down exposure after already revising the pricing approach twice in 2021. That pattern is strong enough to support an exit decision even without metro-level purchase-model error data.

The right board decision in October 2021 is an orderly shutdown of home purchasing, rapid liquidation of inventory, and capital redeployment toward IMT and adjacent transaction-enablement products that deepen Zillow's role in housing transactions without putting the balance sheet at risk.

## Why Exit Is the Correct Decision

### 1. Zillow Offers has failed the practical economics test
Zillow Offers generated $1.2B of Q3 2021 revenue and roughly 70% of total company revenue, but that scale is misleading because the segment remained EBITDA-negative and losses were widening. The decisive facts are operational: Zillow bought far more homes than it sold in the quarter, built a large inventory backlog, and now holds thousands of homes that appear to have been purchased above current market value. In a business with thin gross margins and meaningful holding, repair, financing, and disposition costs, that combination indicates broken unit economics in practice, not just a temporary reporting issue.

A reform case requires credible evidence that underwriting quality can be improved quickly enough to stop capital destruction. The case packet provides the opposite signal: losses widened as volume increased.

### 2. The burden of proof has shifted from theory to demonstrated performance
The strategic bull case for Zillow Offers was that Zillow's data advantage would produce superior pricing. The current evidence does not support that thesis. Whatever the internal offer model includes beyond the public Zestimate, Zillow still overbought into a swelling backlog during one of the strongest housing markets in years. That matters more than debates over proxy metrics. The board does not need proof that every metro is equally bad to conclude that the enterprise, as currently operated, is not investment-worthy.

The 6.9% national Zestimate median error rate is still relevant, but only as a cautionary indicator that consumer-facing valuation strength should not be mistaken for transaction-ready underwriting precision. It should not be the sole pillar of the exit case. The stronger pillar is realized performance: repeated pricing changes, rising exposure, and no evidence of stabilized contribution margins.

### 3. Reform has already been attempted under favorable conditions
Zillow had already pivoted the algorithm twice in 2021 to reduce overpayment. If tighter controls, narrower buy boxes, or better throttling were likely to fix the business quickly, the board would expect to see evidence of stabilization before purchases reached 9,680 homes in a single quarter. Instead, inventory and loss exposure expanded.

The external environment makes this worse. Home prices were up 19.5% year over year as of August 2021, which should have been close to a best-case backdrop for an iBuyer. If the model failed to produce reliable economics in that environment, downside risk is higher in a cooling market.

### 4. Zillow's best business is being obscured by its weakest one
Zillow's IMT segment produced about $490M in revenue at roughly 30% EBITDA margins and was growing more than 20% year over year. The board's concern is correct: Offers losses are masking the quality of the core marketplace business and distorting the company's investment narrative. With the stock down from about $200 in February 2021 to about $85 in October 2021, investors are already applying a penalty for volatility, capital intensity, and uncertainty.

Exiting Offers would clarify that Zillow's durable advantage lies in audience, demand generation, and transaction orchestration, not in taking principal risk on housing inventory.

## Strongest Counterarguments, and Why Exit Still Wins

### Counterargument 1: Zillow can reform by narrowing to the metros where the purchase model works
This is the strongest objection. It is possible that some metros are materially better than others. But the board's decision should be based on demonstrated enterprise viability, not hypothetical pockets of viability that have not been shown in the record. Management has already had time, scale, and a favorable market to prove that a disciplined narrower model works, and the available evidence still shows swelling inventory, widening losses, and repeated model intervention. A metro-by-metro salvage strategy may be worth testing only if the goal were preserving optionality; it is not strong enough to justify continued principal-risk exposure at board level.

### Counterargument 2: Zillow needs to become a transaction company, not just a marketplace
Directionally true. But iBuying is only one route to transaction participation, and it is the most balance-sheet-intensive and precision-sensitive one. Zillow can deepen monetization through mortgages, seller services, closing support, lead conversion, and partner-enabled transactions without owning homes.

### Counterargument 3: Exiting now locks in losses and hurts credibility
The losses are already economic reality. The question is whether Zillow compounds them. Credibility is more likely to recover from a decisive admission of strategic misfit than from extending an operation that management publicly attributes to labor and supply constraints while the deeper issue is underwriting performance.

## Recommended Board Decision
Approve a full exit from Zillow Offers home purchasing and reposition Zillow around asset-light transaction services anchored by its marketplace and IMT businesses.

## Immediate Actions
1. Stop all new home purchases.
2. Liquidate inventory with speed prioritized over maximizing residual margin, using market-specific disposition plans.
3. Provide investors with clean disclosure separating one-time exit costs from ongoing core-business economics.
4. Reallocate capital and talent toward IMT, mortgage, seller services, and transaction software.
5. Reduce Offers-related fixed costs aggressively while retaining only capabilities that support asset-light products.
6. Reset the strategic narrative from balance-sheet market maker to housing transaction platform with explicit capital discipline.

## Decision Frame
Choose `exit` over `reform` because the evidence shows a failed operating model at current scale and no proof that another turnaround would earn back its capital risk.
- `Economics test`: failed, given widening losses, a large buy/sell imbalance in Q3, approximately 7,000 homes in backlog, and $300M-$500M estimated write-down exposure.
- `Execution test`: failed, because two pricing-model pivots in 2021 did not prevent overbuying and inventory accumulation.
- `Opportunity cost test`: failed, because Offers is obscuring a growing, approximately 30% EBITDA-margin IMT business and worsening investor perception.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- We do not have metro-level purchase-model error rates or contribution margins; some markets may be less impaired than the aggregate result suggests.
- We do not have Zillow-specific break-even underwriting tolerances after repairs, carrying costs, and financing.
- We do not know the exact loss envelope under rapid liquidation versus staged liquidation.
- We do not know whether a narrowly scoped pilot in a handful of metros could reach acceptable economics.
- We do not know which Offers capabilities can be retained profitably as software or services.

These gaps leave room for debate about whether a tiny pilot could survive. They do not undermine the board-level conclusion that the current business should be exited rather than reformed in place.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
- `Pass` if the board agrees that deeper transaction participation does not require Zillow to own inventory.
- `Pass` if management is willing to judge Offers on realized economics rather than strategic aspiration.
- `Pass` if inventory can be liquidated within a controlled loss envelope and without restarting purchases.
- `Fail` if leadership treats possible metro-level exceptions as sufficient reason to preserve enterprise-scale principal-risk operations.
- `Fail` if the company frames the decision as a temporary pause rather than a strategic exit.
