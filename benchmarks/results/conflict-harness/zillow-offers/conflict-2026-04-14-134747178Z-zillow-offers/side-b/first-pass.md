# Recommendation: Exit Zillow Offers and refocus Zillow on its marketplace and transaction-enablement businesses

## Executive Recommendation
Zillow should exit iBuying entirely rather than attempt another reform of Zillow Offers. The core issue is not temporary execution strain; it is structural mismatch between Zillow's current pricing capability and the economics of home flipping. A business that requires sub-2% pricing accuracy is being run with an input signal whose median national error is about 6.9%, while scale has amplified losses instead of improving them. Continuing would consume capital, management attention, and investor trust while obscuring the strength of Zillow's higher-margin core business.

The right board decision in October 2021 is an orderly shutdown of home purchasing, rapid liquidation of existing inventory, and redeployment of capital and talent toward the IMT segment and adjacent asset-light transaction products.

## Why Exit Is the Correct Decision

### 1. Zillow Offers has failed the basic economic test
Zillow Offers generated $1.2B of Q3 2021 revenue and about 70% of company revenue, but that scale is misleading because the segment is EBITDA-negative and losses are widening. It purchased 9,680 homes in Q3 while selling only 3,032, leaving inventory to balloon at exactly the moment the pricing model was overpaying. Zillow is now sitting on roughly 7,000 homes, many underwater, with estimated write-down exposure of $300M-$500M. That is not a temporary variance; it is evidence that volume has outrun unit economics.

A reform case requires confidence that margins can be repaired faster than capital is destroyed. The current evidence points the other way.

### 2. The business model demands precision Zillow does not have
Home flipping is a thin-margin business with average gross margins around 2%-5%, which means pricing errors must generally stay below 2% to be reliable after transaction, holding, repair, and financing costs. Zillow's Zestimate median error rate of about 6.9% nationally is far too wide to support that model. Even if local market performance is better than the national average, the current outcome shows the company has not operationalized a sufficiently accurate purchase engine.

This is the central board-level fact: Zillow's consumer traffic advantage does not translate into a durable pricing edge in principal-risk home buying.

### 3. The strongest part of Zillow is being masked by the weakest part
Zillow's IMT segment produced about $490M of revenue at roughly 30% EBITDA margins and was growing 20%+ year over year. The board's concern is correct: Offers losses are hiding the quality of the marketplace business. Investors are valuing Zillow through the noise of an increasingly volatile inventory operation rather than through the economics of its core platform.

With the stock down from about $200 in February 2021 to about $85 in October 2021, the market is already signaling that the conglomeration discount is severe. Exiting Offers would sharpen the investment case around an asset-light, higher-margin, more predictable business.

### 4. Reform has already been attempted and has not worked
Zillow had already pivoted the pricing algorithm twice in 2021 to reduce overpayment. If the answer were simply tighter controls, smaller buy boxes, or better throttling, the board would expect to see stabilization by now. Instead, the company accelerated purchases into a backlog and widened exposure. A third reform attempt would amount to paying tuition for a lesson the company has already learned: this is not a software-only problem that yields quickly to iteration.

### 5. The external environment increases the downside of waiting
The housing market had been unusually favorable, with Case-Shiller up 19.5% year over year as of August 2021. If Zillow still produced large underwriting errors during one of the strongest housing environments in years, the downside in a cooling market is worse, not better. Holding thousands of homes bought above current value into a decelerating market compounds liquidation risk.

The board should not confuse a cyclical tailwind with evidence of strategic viability.

## Strongest Counterarguments, and Why Exit Still Wins

### Counterargument 1: Zillow has strategic logic in becoming a transaction company, not just a lead-gen marketplace
This is directionally true. Zillow should participate more deeply in transactions. But iBuying is only one path to that goal, and it is the most balance-sheet-intensive and precision-sensitive one. Zillow can still deepen transaction participation through mortgages, seller services, closing, touring, lead conversion, and partner-enabled transactions without taking principal risk on homes.

### Counterargument 2: Reforming with lower volume and narrower geographies could preserve upside
A narrower footprint would reduce loss velocity, but it does not solve the board's core decision problem. Zillow would still be committing capital and management time to a business whose competitive advantage remains unproven and whose economics are structurally fragile. Opendoor's relatively better performance reflects narrower geography and tighter operating discipline, but that is not proof Zillow can catch up quickly enough to justify continued exposure.

### Counterargument 3: Exiting now locks in losses and damages credibility
Losses are already real. Refusing to recognize them because realization is painful would be a classic escalation error. Credibility is more likely to recover from a decisive admission of strategic misfit than from a prolonged attempt to disguise a model failure as an operations pause.

## Recommended Board Decision
The board should approve a full exit from Zillow Offers home purchasing and reposition Zillow around asset-light transaction services anchored by its marketplace.

## Immediate Actions
1. Stop all new home purchases.
2. Liquidate existing inventory with speed prioritized over margin optimization, using segmented pricing and market-by-market disposition plans.
3. Present investors with clean segment disclosure that isolates exit costs from ongoing core business performance.
4. Reallocate product and go-to-market investment toward IMT, mortgage, seller solutions, and transaction software/services that do not require Zillow to hold housing inventory.
5. Reduce Offers-related fixed cost aggressively, including headcount and market operations, while retaining selected talent useful for transaction-enablement products.
6. Reset the company narrative from "algorithmic market maker" to "housing super-app and transaction platform" with explicit capital discipline.

## Decision Frame
Choose `exit` over `reform` because the evidence indicates a structural economics problem, not a temporary operating problem. The decision turns on three tests:
- `Unit economics test`: failed, given widening losses, underwater inventory, and $300M-$500M estimated write-down exposure.
- `Capability advantage test`: failed, because required sub-2% pricing precision is inconsistent with roughly 6.9% median Zestimate error and repeated failed model pivots.
- `Opportunity cost test`: failed, because Offers is masking a growing, ~30% EBITDA-margin IMT business and depressing investor confidence.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- Market-by-market contribution margins are not provided; some metros may be materially better than the national picture.
- The exact break-even accuracy threshold after financing, repairs, and holding costs is not quantified for Zillow specifically.
- The pace and discount required to clear the ~7,000-home backlog is not disclosed.
- We do not have a full scenario analysis for a sharply cooling 2022 housing market versus a flat one.
- We do not know whether selected components of the Offers operating stack could be retained profitably as software or services.

These gaps matter for exit execution, but they do not reverse the strategic recommendation.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
- `Pass` if the board accepts that Zillow's strategic goal is deeper transaction participation, not necessarily principal-risk home ownership.
- `Pass` if management can separate one-time exit charges from steady-state core economics and communicate that clearly.
- `Pass` if inventory liquidation can be executed within a controlled loss envelope and without further purchase exposure.
- `Fail` if leadership remains committed to proving iBuying viability for identity reasons rather than capital-return logic.
- `Fail` if the company frames this as a temporary pause and preserves the option structure by continuing to carry the operating platform at scale.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A 90-day exit and repositioning plan covering inventory liquidation, org redesign, investor communications, segment reporting, and the asset-light transaction roadmap.
