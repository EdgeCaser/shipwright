# Strategic Recommendation: Exit Zillow Offers

**To:** Zillow Group Board of Directors
**From:** Strategy Advisory
**Date:** October 2021
**Classification:** Confidential

---

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Should Zillow reform its iBuying operation (Zillow Offers) with tighter controls, or exit entirely and refocus on the high-margin marketplace business?

**Recommendation: Exit Zillow Offers entirely.**

This is not a execution failure that better management can fix. It is a structural mismatch between Zillow's capabilities and the requirements of the iBuying business model. The evidence points to a fundamental conclusion: **algorithmic home pricing at scale requires sub-2% accuracy to generate positive margins, and Zillow's Zestimate operates at ~6.9% median error nationally — more than 3x the threshold required for viability.** Two algorithm pivots in 2021 failed to close this gap. Reform assumes the gap is closable with incremental improvements; the data says otherwise.

### The Structural Case for Exit

**1. The pricing accuracy gap is not closable through iteration.**
Home flipping requires 2-5% gross margins, which demands sub-2% pricing accuracy. The Zestimate's 6.9% national median error is a product of the inherent difficulty of pricing unique, illiquid assets — not a software bug. Zillow has already pivoted the algorithm twice in 2021 with no material improvement. Each home is a bundle of location, condition, neighborhood dynamics, and seller motivation that resists pure algorithmic assessment. Opendoor's closer-to-breakeven performance comes from narrower geography and lower volume — precisely the constraints that would negate Zillow's scale thesis.

**2. The inventory position is an accelerating liability.**
Zillow holds ~7,000 homes, many purchased above current market value. In Q3 2021, Zillow purchased 9,680 homes but sold only 3,032 — a 3.2:1 buy-to-sell ratio that is compounding inventory risk. The housing market rose 19.5% YoY through August 2021 but is showing cooling signs. If the market flattens or declines even modestly, the $300-500M write-down exposure grows substantially. Every month of continued purchasing deepens this hole.

**3. Zillow Offers is destroying value that the core business creates.**
The IMT (Internet, Media & Technology) segment generates $490M in quarterly revenue at ~30% EBITDA margins with 20%+ YoY growth. This is an exceptional business. Zillow Offers generates $1.2B in revenue but at negative and worsening EBITDA. The Offers segment constitutes 70% of consolidated revenue, which means the market cannot properly value the core business — it is buried under Offers losses. The stock decline from $200 to $85 reflects the market's growing recognition that Offers is a value destroyer, not a value creator.

**4. The headcount and organizational distortion is severe.**
Zillow grew from 5,400 to 8,000+ employees primarily to support Offers. This represents ~2,600 employees and their associated costs directed at a loss-making business. These resources — engineering talent, operational capacity, management attention — are unavailable to the profitable core business.

**5. "Reform" is the higher-risk option.**
Reform (tighter pricing controls, reduced volume) sounds like the moderate, de-risked path. It is not. Reform means:
- Continuing to hold capital-intensive physical inventory with asymmetric downside
- Maintaining a large operational workforce for a scaled-down business (dis-economies of scale)
- Keeping management attention split between two fundamentally different business models
- Signaling to the market that Zillow still believes in a thesis the data has falsified
- Exposing the company to ongoing write-down risk in a cooling housing market

Reduced volume with tighter pricing controls yields a subscale iBuying operation that still cannot achieve the accuracy needed for positive margins but now lacks the volume to amortize fixed costs.

### Counterarguments Addressed

**"The Zestimate can be improved with more data and better models."**
Zillow has had the Zestimate for 15 years and has more residential real estate data than any competitor. Two algorithm pivots in 2021 failed to meaningfully close the accuracy gap. The problem is not data scarcity — it is the fundamental difficulty of pricing heterogeneous, illiquid physical assets. Further investment faces sharply diminishing returns.

**"Opendoor is making iBuying work; Zillow just needs to execute better."**
Opendoor is closer to breakeven, not profitable, and operates at lower volume in narrower geographies. Opendoor's relative performance validates the thesis that iBuying requires geographic concentration and volume discipline — the opposite of Zillow's scale-and-speed approach. Matching Opendoor's strategy would require Zillow to abandon its differentiated thesis (Zestimate-powered national scale) and compete as a subscale follower.

**"Exiting means writing off the $300-500M investment and admitting failure."**
The write-down is a sunk cost. The question is whether to add future losses on top of realized losses. Continuing to operate Zillow Offers in reform mode risks converting a $300-500M loss into a $500M-1B+ loss if the housing market cools. The market will reward decisive action — investors are already pricing in Offers failure, as evidenced by the 57% stock decline.

**"Zillow needs a transactional revenue stream to diversify beyond advertising."**
This is a valid strategic concern, but iBuying is the wrong transactional business. Zillow should pursue asset-light transactional models — mortgage origination, title/escrow, agent matching with transaction fees — that leverage its 200M+ monthly visitors without requiring balance sheet exposure to physical homes.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

| Unknown | Impact on Decision | How to Resolve |
|---|---|---|
| Exact loss per home on current inventory | Determines write-down magnitude but not direction of decision | Mark-to-market analysis of full 7,000-home portfolio against current comps |
| Housing market trajectory Q4 2021-Q1 2022 | Cooling market amplifies exit urgency; continued appreciation reduces write-down but doesn't fix structural margin problem | Monitor Case-Shiller, mortgage rate trajectory, inventory levels |
| Contractual/legal obligations on homes under contract but not yet closed | Could affect speed of wind-down | Legal review of pipeline commitments |
| Employee retention risk during wind-down | Key IMT talent may leave during restructuring uncertainty | Retention packages for core business employees, clear communication separating Offers wind-down from IMT investment |
| Opendoor's actual unit economics at maturity | Would validate or invalidate the thesis that iBuying can work at all | Not resolvable in decision timeframe; irrelevant to Zillow's specific position |
| Tax implications of portfolio liquidation | Affects optimal wind-down timing and structure | Tax advisory engagement |

**Critical gap:** We lack granular data on per-market pricing accuracy. It is possible that the Zestimate performs within the required sub-2% range in certain markets while failing badly in others. However, even if true, this would support only a radically reduced geographic footprint — which is the Opendoor model, not the Zillow thesis.

## Recommended Next Artifact

1. **Wind-Down Execution Plan** — 90-day phased exit plan covering: portfolio liquidation strategy (bulk sale vs. individual disposition), employee transition and retention for core business, contractual obligation mapping, and investor/public communication timeline.

2. **Core Business Reinvestment Memo** — Strategic plan for redeploying capital and talent from Offers into asset-light transactional businesses (mortgage, title/escrow, agent transaction fees) that leverage Zillow's audience without balance sheet risk.

3. **Investor Communication Package** — Board-approved narrative for the exit announcement, including forward guidance that re-anchors valuation on the IMT segment's 30% EBITDA margins and 20%+ growth trajectory.
