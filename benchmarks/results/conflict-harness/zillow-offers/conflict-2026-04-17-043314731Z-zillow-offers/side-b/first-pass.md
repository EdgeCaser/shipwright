# Recommendation: Reform Zillow Offers Rather Than Exit

Zillow should keep Zillow Offers, but only as a tightly constrained, lower-volume operating model built around pricing discipline instead of growth. The current version has failed: the business overbought into a cooling market, losses are widening, and the inventory backlog creates immediate financial and credibility pressure. But the right conclusion is not that direct transactions are strategically invalid. It is that Zillow scaled a thin-margin business faster than its pricing accuracy justified.

A full exit would solve the near-term write-down problem, but it would also abandon the company’s attempt to participate directly in the transaction layer after already proving that sellers will use the product at meaningful scale. Zillow Offers generated $1.2B of revenue in Q3 2021 and represented 70% of total company revenue, which shows real customer demand and strategic reach even if the current economics are broken. The board should preserve the option value of transaction participation, but under a much narrower mandate.

The reform plan should be explicit:

1. Freeze expansion and shrink purchase volume materially until pricing performance stabilizes.
2. Limit operations to the geographies and home segments where pricing confidence is strongest.
3. Reset success metrics away from revenue growth and toward inventory turn, realized loss containment, and consistency of pricing outcomes.
4. Treat the current inventory backlog as a separate containment problem with dedicated oversight.
5. Put hard governance gates on any future re-acceleration, since the algorithm was already pivoted twice in 2021 without solving overpayment.

The strongest argument for exit is straightforward: home flipping is a thin-margin business that requires pricing accuracy tighter than Zillow appears able to deliver. The company is carrying about 7,000 homes, faces an estimated $300M to $500M write-down exposure, and its national Zestimate median error rate of about 6.9% is misaligned with a business model where small pricing errors erase margins. Those facts make a credible case that Zillow lacks the operating edge required for scaled iBuying.

That critique is serious, but it still does not make immediate exit the best board decision. First, the evidence proves the current scaled model is broken, not that every narrower version is unworkable. Second, Opendoor’s relatively better performance with narrower geography and lower volume suggests the business can become less fragile when scope is constrained. Third, Zillow’s core marketplace business remains strong, with $490M in IMT revenue, about 30% EBITDA margins, and growth above 20% year over year. That gives the company enough underlying strength to absorb a controlled restructuring and test a smaller operating model without betting the enterprise on a full-scale turnaround.

The real board objective should be separation of strategic option value from reckless execution. Reform does that. Exit destroys the option permanently at the moment when the company has learned, at high cost, exactly where the model breaks.

## Decision Frame

The decision is not whether the current Zillow Offers model should continue. It should not. The decision is whether Zillow should retain a narrowly redesigned transaction business or abandon iBuying altogether. The better choice is to retain the business in a sharply reduced form, because the evidence supports stopping scale-first execution but does not yet prove that a disciplined, geography-constrained model cannot work.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- The case does not show market-level performance by geography, home type, or pricing cohort, which is necessary to judge whether the losses are universal or concentrated.
- The case does not show whether the recent algorithm pivots improved outcomes in any subset of markets.
- The board lacks visibility into whether the current inventory exposure is mostly a one-time unwind from aggressive buying or evidence of a structurally unfixable pricing system.
- The operating implications of shrinking the business are unclear, especially given headcount growth to 8,000+ with many hires tied to Offers.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if the board is willing to impose a hard reset: sharply lower purchase volume, restrict geography, ring-fence inventory liquidation, and require proof of stable pricing before any expansion. Under those conditions, reform is actionable now.

Fail if management insists on defending recent scale, prioritizing revenue share over unit economics, or treating the purchase pause as mainly a labor-and-supply issue. Under those conditions, reform would be cosmetic and exit would become the better answer.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A board operating reset memo for Zillow Offers that defines the reduced-scope model, market-selection criteria, inventory containment plan, governance gates for re-expansion, and the specific conditions under which the board would shut the business down if the redesigned model does not stabilize.
