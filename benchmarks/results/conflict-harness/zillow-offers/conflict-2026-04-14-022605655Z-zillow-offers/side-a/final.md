# Strategic Recommendation: Exit Zillow Offers

**To:** Zillow Group Board of Directors
**From:** Strategy Advisor
**Date:** October 2021
**Classification:** Confidential — Board Eyes Only

---

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Should Zillow reform its iBuying operation (Zillow Offers) with tighter pricing controls, or exit the business entirely and refocus on the high-margin marketplace?

**Recommendation: Exit Zillow Offers. Fully. Now.**

This is not a fixable execution problem. It is a structural mismatch between Zillow's capabilities and the unit economics of algorithmic home buying. The evidence points to a business that cannot reach profitability at scale, is destroying shareholder value, and is obscuring the exceptional performance of Zillow's core marketplace. Every quarter spent attempting reform increases write-down exposure, dilutes management focus, and delays the re-rating of Zillow as a high-margin platform business.

### The Structural Case for Exit

**1. The Zestimate is not a buying algorithm — and the gap is not closable at acceptable cost.**

The Zestimate's 6.9% national median error rate is a consumer-facing engagement tool, not a transactional pricing engine. Home flipping requires sub-2% pricing accuracy to generate the industry-standard 2-5% gross margin. Zillow has already pivoted the algorithm twice in 2021 and overpayment has *worsened*, not improved — evidenced by 7,000 underwater homes on the books. The problem is not calibration; it is that residential real estate pricing at the individual property level involves hyperlocal, condition-specific, and timing-dependent variables that a national dataset cannot capture with sufficient precision. Opendoor's closer-to-breakeven performance comes from narrower geography and lower volume — exactly the opposite of Zillow's scale thesis.

**2. The buy/sell imbalance reveals a liquidity trap, not a timing issue.**

Q3 2021: 9,680 homes purchased vs. 3,032 sold. This 3.2:1 buy/sell ratio is not a temporary backlog — it is the signature of a business acquiring assets faster than it can disposition them. The "labor and supply constraints" narrative is a temporary cover. The real constraint is that homes purchased above market value require either price cuts (crystallizing losses) or holding periods (accumulating carrying costs). Neither path leads to profitability. Estimated write-down exposure of $300-500M on ~7,000 homes represents $43-71K loss per home — a devastating margin on median US home prices.

**3. Offers is cannibalizing the story of a great business.**

Zillow's IMT (Internet, Media & Technology) segment delivered $490M in Q3 revenue at ~30% EBITDA margins, growing 20%+ YoY. This is an exceptional software/marketplace business. Yet Zillow Offers — at $1.2B in revenue with negative and widening EBITDA — constitutes 70% of consolidated revenue and 100% of the value destruction. The stock decline from $200 to $85 reflects the market's inability to value the core business through the Offers noise. Exiting Offers does not shrink Zillow — it reveals a $2B+ run-rate marketplace business with software-like margins.

**4. Headcount growth is misallocated to the wrong business.**

Zillow grew from 5,400 to 8,000+ employees, with the majority of additions supporting Offers operations (renovation, logistics, local market operations). This is a capital- and labor-intensive model grafted onto a capital-light platform. Every incremental hire in Offers operations has negative ROI; every incremental hire in marketplace/platform has high-margin leverage.

### Why Not Reform?

The strongest counterargument is that Zillow has a unique demand-side advantage (200M+ monthly visitors) and should find the right volume/pricing calibration rather than abandon a $1.2B revenue line. Three responses:

1. **Revenue is vanity, margin is sanity.** $1.2B in Offers revenue at negative margins is worse than $0 in Offers revenue. The core business generates more economic value at one-third the revenue.

2. **Reform means slow bleeding, not recovery.** Tighter pricing controls mean lower volume (fewer homes qualify), which means higher per-unit fixed costs, which means margins compress further. Reduced volume also eliminates the scale thesis that justified the investment. You cannot simultaneously tighten pricing and maintain the volume needed for the business model to work.

3. **Two algorithm pivots already failed.** The burden of proof is on reform advocates to explain what *specifically* changes on the third attempt that the first two missed. Without a credible technical path to sub-2% pricing error — which no one inside Zillow has articulated — reform is hope masquerading as strategy.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

| Unknown | Why It Matters | How to Resolve |
|---|---|---|
| **Exact mark-to-market on the 7,000-home portfolio** | Write-down could be $300M or $500M+ — a $200M range that affects exit sequencing and capital needs | Commission independent appraisals on a stratified sample (top 20 markets) within 2 weeks |
| **Monthly carrying cost of unsold inventory** | Financing costs, property taxes, insurance, maintenance, and HOA fees on 7,000 homes represent ongoing cash burn; the precise monthly figure is not available in current reporting but directionally significant — the board should request a detailed holding-cost breakdown from Finance before finalizing disposition sequencing | Finance team to produce per-market carrying cost model within 1 week |
| **Contractual obligations and pipeline commitments** | Homes under contract but not yet closed represent additional exposure; cancellation terms vary by market | Legal/ops audit of pipeline within 1 week |
| **Workforce reduction costs and timeline** | 2,600+ Offers-related employees; severance, retention of key platform talent, and operational wind-down logistics affect total exit cost | HR/Finance model exit scenarios: rapid (90-day) vs. phased (180-day) |
| **Competitive response from Opendoor/Offerpad** | Exit may validate competitors' more conservative approach or may signal iBuying is structurally unviable, affecting Zillow's partnership options | Monitor but do not let competitor positioning delay the decision |
| **Tax treatment of portfolio liquidation** | Bulk sale vs. individual disposition has materially different tax implications | Tax counsel analysis, 1-week turnaround |
| **Housing market trajectory Q4 2021-Q2 2022** | If prices decline further, holding losses deepen; if prices recover, some underwater inventory may surface — but betting on price recovery is speculation, not strategy | Scenario model three paths (flat, -5%, -10%) on current portfolio; do not use optimistic case to justify delay |

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Immediate (Week 1):**
- **Exit execution plan** — 90-day wind-down playbook covering: portfolio disposition strategy (bulk vs. retail), employee transition/severance framework, market-by-market shutdown sequencing, investor/analyst communication plan
- **Portfolio carrying-cost model** — Finance-produced breakdown of monthly holding costs (financing, taxes, insurance, maintenance) across all markets, to quantify the cost of delay and inform disposition urgency and sequencing

**Short-term (Weeks 2-4):**
- **Investor narrative and re-rating thesis** — Position exit as unlocking Zillow's "true" valuation as a pure-play marketplace/platform with 30%+ EBITDA margins. Model forward P&L without Offers to demonstrate margin expansion story.
- **Workforce transition plan** — Identify Offers employees with transferable skills for marketplace/platform roles; design severance packages for remainder; target 25% retention rate to the core business.

**Medium-term (Months 2-3):**
- **Strategic alternatives assessment for housing transaction participation** — Evaluate lighter-touch models (transaction facilitation, closing services, financing partnerships) that leverage Zillow's demand-side scale without balance-sheet risk. Zillow can participate in transactions without *being* the transaction.
- **Core business reinvestment plan** — Redirect freed capital and management attention to IMT segment growth: agent tools, mortgage, rental marketplace expansion.

## Summary

Zillow Offers is a bold thesis that the data has decisively refuted. The Zestimate — brilliant as a consumer engagement tool — cannot achieve the pricing precision that algorithmic home buying demands. Two algorithm pivots failed. Losses are widening. 7,000 homes sit underwater. Meanwhile, the core marketplace business is growing 20%+ at 30% margins and the market cannot see it through the Offers fog.

The board should vote to exit Zillow Offers, announce the decision with the Q3 earnings release, take the write-down cleanly, and begin the re-rating narrative. The direction of delay cost is unambiguously negative — carrying costs and price deterioration risk compound with time — making speed of decision a first-order concern even as precise monthly figures are finalized.

Exit is not retreat. It is the disciplined recognition that Zillow's competitive advantage — massive consumer demand — is best monetized as a platform, not as a balance sheet.
