# Revised artifact

## Recommendation
Zillow should exit iBuying rather than fund another reform cycle. The board does not need proof that iBuying is impossible in every market to make this decision; it needs a credible basis to believe Zillow can earn acceptable risk-adjusted returns from here. The case evidence points the other way. Zillow has already scaled into a large inventory and earnings problem, has already adjusted the pricing system twice in 2021 without restoring control, and is entering a less forgiving housing environment while its core marketplace business remains strong and high margin.

An orderly exit is the better board decision because it contains losses, restores strategic clarity, and redirects capital and management attention toward asset-light transaction businesses that fit Zillow's advantages.

## Why exit is the right decision

### 1. The issue is demonstrated pricing-control failure at scale, not just a bad headline metric
The strongest critique of the exit case is fair: the national 6.9% Zestimate error rate does not by itself prove that the Zillow Offers buy-side model is structurally incapable of working in every metro. Zillow Offers used a more specialized pricing process, and the board lacks the precise Offers-specific error rate by market.

But that uncertainty does not rescue reform. What the board does know is operationally decisive: Zillow pivoted the algorithm twice in 2021 to reduce overpayment, yet still ended up with roughly 7,000 homes bought above current market value and an estimated $300-500M write-down exposure. In a thin-margin business, repeated model intervention followed by a large inventory mispricing event is enough to conclude that Zillow does not currently control pricing risk at the level required for scaled principal investing.

The national Zestimate error still matters, but only as supporting context: it weakens the original thesis that Zillow possessed an exceptional pricing edge. It should not carry the full argument. The core argument is that Zillow's real-world performance after multiple model adjustments has already failed the board's burden of proof.

### 2. Zillow Offers has become a balance-sheet problem, not a contained experiment
In Q3 2021, Zillow Offers purchased 9,680 homes and sold only 3,032. Inventory kept growing even as pricing trouble was becoming visible. That matters more than any abstract debate about model potential. Once a trading business is scaling inventory faster than it can safely recycle it, errors compound into capital risk.

With thousands of homes potentially underwater and a large likely write-down, the question is no longer whether Zillow can learn over time. The question is whether it should continue learning on its own balance sheet while losses widen. The answer should be no.

### 3. Reform would require proving a narrow success case under the worst possible conditions
A credible reform plan would need to show that Zillow can sharply tighten buy boxes, identify only the metros where pricing is reliably strong, slow purchases, work through legacy inventory, and then regrow without repeating the same problem. The case provides no evidence that this narrower success case has been demonstrated anywhere at sufficient scale.

That absence matters because the burden of proof has shifted. Once a business has already produced a large write-down risk, management should need affirmative evidence of controllable unit economics before more capital is committed. Instead, the case offers only the possibility that some subset of markets might eventually work.

That is not enough in October 2021, especially with housing appreciation showing signs of cooling after a 19.5% YoY rise. A cooler market raises the cost of being wrong and reduces the value of continuing an expensive live experiment.

### 4. Zillow's strongest business is being obscured by its weakest one
Zillow Offers generated $1.2B of Q3 2021 revenue and roughly 70% of company revenue, but it carried worsening EBITDA and inventory risk. Meanwhile the IMT segment generated about $490M in revenue, around 30% EBITDA margins, and 20%+ YoY growth. The board concern is correct: Offers is masking the quality of Zillow's core franchise.

That is strategically important because Zillow's durable strengths are audience, demand aggregation, seller and buyer intent, lead flow, and adjacent transaction services. Those strengths support asset-light businesses. They do not require Zillow to own homes.

### 5. Competitor evidence weakens the upside case for reform
The best pro-reform argument is that Opendoor has operated with narrower geography and better discipline, suggesting Zillow could copy that model with a reset. But even that comparison does not create an attractive board case. If a more focused operator only gets closer to breakeven in a difficult business, Zillow should not assume it can rapidly restructure into a superior operator after already suffering a major mispricing event.

The likely upside of reform is not a clearly advantaged business. It is a smaller, narrower, still operationally demanding business with uncertain economics. That is too weak a payoff for the risk.

## Strongest counterarguments and why exit still wins

### Counterargument 1: Zillow has already paid the tuition and should preserve the option
That is a sunk-cost argument unless management can show market-level profitability after fully loaded costs. The case does not provide that evidence. Prior learning has not prevented a major inventory problem.

### Counterargument 2: Zillow should shrink to a few markets rather than abandon transactions
Zillow should stay in transactions, but through mortgages, seller services, touring, closing, lead conversion, and partner-enabled workflows. The company can pursue transaction depth without principal risk.

### Counterargument 3: Exiting now locks in losses and signals failure
The losses are already economically real. Delaying recognition in hopes of a turnaround risks larger realized losses, further investor distrust, and more management distraction. A decisive exit with a clear refocus is more credible than a third attempt to fix pricing under pressure.

## What the board should do now
1. Approve a full exit from Zillow Offers rather than a partial reform pilot.
2. Liquidate existing inventory with disciplined speed, prioritizing loss containment and cash recovery over headline volume.
3. Re-segment external reporting so investors can clearly see the earnings power of IMT and other asset-light businesses.
4. Stop allocating new capital to principal home ownership and selectively redeploy relevant talent into transaction-enablement products.
5. Present a post-Offers strategy centered on asset-light transaction participation instead of inventory ownership.

## Decision Frame
Choose `exit`, not `reform`.

Rationale:
- The evidence shows repeated pricing-control failure after multiple model interventions, even if the precise market-level error rate is unknown.
- Scale has already turned execution issues into material balance-sheet exposure.
- Reform now would require affirmative proof of narrow-market profitability that the case does not provide.
- A cooling housing market raises the cost of continued experimentation.
- Zillow's core IMT business is strong enough to justify a strategic refocus.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- Offers-specific pricing error rates by market, purchase cohort, and home type.
- Whether any small set of metros has positive unit economics after fully loaded holding, resale, and overhead costs.
- The exact liquidation timeline and realized loss distribution for the roughly 7,000-home backlog.
- How much Offers technology and operating talent can be repurposed into asset-light transaction products.
- Investor response to a full exit versus a tightly scoped reform pilot with explicit segment disclosure.

These are real gaps, but they cut against reform more than exit. Without proof of controllable market-level economics, the board should not keep funding principal-risk experimentation.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass for decision.

Why:
- The case provides enough evidence to decide whether Zillow should keep taking principal risk.
- The board does not need to prove iBuying can never work anywhere; it only needs to judge whether Zillow has earned the right to continue. The evidence says it has not.
- Additional analysis would improve wind-down execution, not reverse the direction of the board decision.

Fail conditions that would justify revisiting exit:
- Verified evidence that a defined set of target metros has sustainably positive fully loaded unit economics.
- Demonstrated pricing control after recent model changes, not just planned improvements.
- A credible operating plan showing controlled inventory turns and limited downside in a cooling market.
