# Strategic Recommendation: Exit Zillow Offers

**To:** Zillow Group Board of Directors
**From:** Strategy Advisor
**Date:** October 2021
**Classification:** Confidential — Board Eyes Only

---

## Decision Frame

**The core question:** Should Zillow reform its iBuying operation (Zillow Offers) with tighter controls, or exit entirely and refocus on the high-margin marketplace business?

**Recommendation: Exit Zillow Offers. Immediately. Completely.**

This is not a fixable execution problem. It is a structural mismatch between Zillow's capabilities and the requirements of the iBuying business model. The evidence is unambiguous: Zillow lacks the pricing precision required to operate profitably in home flipping, the losses are accelerating rather than converging toward breakeven, and every quarter Offers continues, it destroys shareholder value and obscures the strength of Zillow's core marketplace business.

### The Structural Problem

Home flipping requires sub-2% pricing accuracy to generate the industry-standard 2-5% gross margin. The Zestimate's national median error rate is ~6.9% — more than three times the maximum tolerable error. This is not a calibration issue that another algorithm pivot will fix. Zillow has already pivoted the pricing algorithm twice in 2021, and losses have widened, not narrowed.

The error rate reflects a fundamental limitation: the Zestimate was designed to give consumers a rough home value estimate for browsing purposes (where ±7% is useful), not to price individual transactions where ±2% is the difference between profit and catastrophic loss. These are different problems requiring different data, different methodologies, and different operational infrastructure.

### The Financial Case for Exit

| Metric | Zillow Offers | Core IMT Business |
|--------|--------------|--------------------|
| Q3 2021 Revenue | $1.2B | $490M |
| EBITDA Margin | Negative (widening) | ~30% |
| YoY Growth | 62% (revenue) | 20%+ |
| Capital Intensity | Extreme (billions on balance sheet) | Asset-light |
| Headcount Drag | ~2,600 incremental hires | Stable |

Zillow Offers generates 70% of consolidated revenue but is a net value destroyer. The core IMT (Internet, Media & Technology) segment generates $490M at ~30% EBITDA margins — a $147M quarterly EBITDA business growing 20%+ year-over-year. This is an exceptional software business being masked by an unprofitable real estate operation.

The math is stark: Zillow is carrying ~7,000 homes on its books, many purchased above current market value. The estimated write-down exposure is $300-500M. But this write-down, while painful, is a one-time event. Continuing Offers means continuing to accumulate losses quarter after quarter with no credible path to profitability.

### Why Reform Will Not Work

The reform thesis assumes that tighter pricing controls and reduced volume can make Zillow Offers viable. This is wrong for three reasons:

**1. Tighter pricing means uncompetitive offers.** If Zillow tightens its algorithm to avoid overpayment, its offers will be lower than competitors'. Sellers choose iBuyers primarily on price. A Zillow that offers less than Opendoor loses deal flow, which eliminates the volume advantage that was supposed to justify the business.

**2. Reduced volume destroys the unit economics theory.** The original Zillow Offers thesis depended on scale: high volume would drive down per-unit renovation, transaction, and holding costs. Reducing volume to manage risk eliminates the scale economics that were supposed to make the model work. You cannot simultaneously de-risk and scale.

**3. The competitive landscape favors specialists.** Opendoor operates in narrower geographies with deeper local expertise and is consistently closer to breakeven. Opendoor's entire company is built around iBuying operations and logistics. Zillow is a technology and media company that bolted on a capital-intensive real estate operation. Opendoor has organizational focus; Zillow has organizational distraction.

### The Inventory Time Bomb

Zillow purchased 9,680 homes in Q3 2021 but sold only 3,032. This 3:1 buy-to-sell ratio means inventory is accumulating at an alarming rate. Each home on the books carries holding costs (taxes, insurance, maintenance, financing) of roughly $2,000-3,000/month. With 7,000 homes, that is $14-21M per month in holding costs alone — before any mark-to-market losses.

The announced "pause" attributed to labor and supply constraints is a fig leaf. The real issue is that the pricing model is broken and inventory is becoming toxic. The longer Zillow holds these homes, the greater the exposure to a housing market correction. With prices up 19.5% YoY but showing signs of cooling, the window to liquidate inventory at tolerable losses is narrowing.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

### Critical Unknowns

1. **Exact mark-to-market loss on current inventory.** The $300-500M range is wide. A home-by-home analysis of current market values versus purchase prices is needed to quantify the actual write-down before announcing exit. This analysis should be completed within 2 weeks.

2. **Housing market trajectory over next 6-12 months.** If the market corrects 5-10%, the write-down could exceed $500M. If the market holds, Zillow may recover some losses on liquidation. We should model three scenarios (continued appreciation, flat, 10% correction) for the liquidation portfolio.

3. **Contractual and legal obligations.** How many homes are under contract but not yet closed? What are the penalties for walking away from pending purchases? What litigation exposure exists from sellers whose transactions are in progress?

4. **Employee retention during wind-down.** Approximately 2,600 employees were hired for Offers-related roles. Mass layoffs will affect morale across the entire company. What is the retention risk for core IMT employees during a turbulent wind-down?

5. **Opendoor and competitor response.** Will competitors bid aggressively on Zillow's liquidation inventory? Could Zillow negotiate a bulk portfolio sale to Opendoor or institutional investors to accelerate exit and reduce holding costs?

### Evidence We Wish We Had

- Per-market profitability data for Zillow Offers (some markets may have been profitable; understanding which ones and why could inform whether any narrow continuation makes sense — though we believe it does not)
- Internal algorithm team's honest assessment of whether sub-2% error is achievable within 12-24 months
- Customer satisfaction data comparing Zillow Offers sellers vs. traditional Zillow lead-gen users (to assess whether exit damages the core brand)
- Competitor unit economics at comparable scale (Opendoor is closer to breakeven at lower volume — does their model actually work at Zillow's volume?)

The board should vote to exit Zillow Offers in this meeting. The announcement should follow in 2-4 weeks once the liquidation strategy and write-down estimate are finalized. Speed matters — every week of delay adds holding costs and market exposure.

## Recommended Next Artifact

### Immediate Next Steps (Board approval required)

1. **Liquidation Options Analysis (1 week):** Evaluate three liquidation paths — (a) bulk portfolio sale to institutional investors at a discount, (b) market-by-market orderly disposition over 6-9 months, (c) hybrid approach. Deliverable: financial model with NPV comparison of each path.

2. **Stakeholder Communication Plan (2 weeks):** Draft investor letter, press release, employee all-hands script, and partner FAQ. Key narrative: Zillow is doubling down on its high-margin marketplace business and returning to asset-light focus. Frame the exit as strategic discipline, not defeat.

3. **Organizational Restructuring Plan (2 weeks):** Identify which Offers employees have skills transferable to core business. Design severance packages for affected employees. Create retention bonuses for critical core-business talent who may be unsettled by the disruption.

4. **"New Zillow" Strategic Plan (4 weeks):** With Offers removed, articulate the forward strategy for the core marketplace business. Key question: how does Zillow redeploy the capital, technology, and data assets from Offers into higher-return opportunities? Potential areas include:
   - Enhanced agent tools and services (higher wallet share of existing transactions)
   - Mortgage and closing services (transaction adjacencies with better unit economics)
   - Zillow-as-platform (connecting buyers, sellers, agents, lenders in an integrated marketplace)

## Addressing the Strongest Counterargument

The strongest case for reform is: *"Zillow has invested billions building iBuying infrastructure, has the largest consumer real estate audience in the country, and exiting now means abandoning that investment just as the team is learning from its mistakes. A reformed, lower-volume Offers program could become profitable in 12-24 months and would give Zillow a transactional revenue stream that diversifies away from advertising dependence."*

This argument fails on three grounds:

**First, sunk cost is not a reason to continue.** The billions already invested are gone regardless of the decision. The only relevant question is whether future investment in Offers will generate positive returns. Given the structural pricing accuracy gap (6.9% vs. sub-2% required), two failed algorithm pivots, and widening losses, there is no credible evidence that continued investment will produce returns.

**Second, the "learning" argument assumes convergence that isn't happening.** If losses were narrowing quarter-over-quarter, the reform case would have merit. They are widening. The team is not converging on a solution; it is discovering that the problem is harder than anticipated. Two algorithm pivots in one year that failed to fix the issue suggest a fundamental capability gap, not a tuning problem.

**Third, the opportunity cost is enormous.** Every dollar and every engineer-hour spent on Offers is unavailable for the core business. The IMT segment is a high-growth, high-margin software business. Zillow's 200M+ monthly visitors represent an extraordinary competitive moat. The strategic question is not "can we make Offers work?" but "what is the highest-return use of Zillow's unique assets?" The answer is clearly the marketplace, not home flipping.

---

**Bottom line:** Exit Zillow Offers. Take the write-down. Refocus every resource on the marketplace business that made Zillow valuable in the first place. The stock market will reward strategic clarity over stubborn persistence in a structurally unprofitable business.
