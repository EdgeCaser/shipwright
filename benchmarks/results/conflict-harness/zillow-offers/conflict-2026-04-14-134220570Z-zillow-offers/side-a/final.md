# Strategic Recommendation: Exit Zillow Offers

**To:** Zillow Group Board of Directors
**From:** Strategy Advisor
**Date:** October 2021
**Classification:** Confidential — Board Eyes Only

---

## Decision Frame

**The core question:** Should Zillow reform its iBuying operation (Zillow Offers) with tighter controls, or exit entirely and refocus on the high-margin marketplace business?

**Recommendation: Exit Zillow Offers. Immediately. Completely.**

This is not a fixable execution problem. The weight of evidence — widening losses, a 3:1 buy-to-sell ratio, ~7,000 homes on the books many purchased above market value, and two failed algorithm corrections in a single year — points to a business whose operational requirements exceed Zillow's demonstrated capabilities. Every quarter Offers continues, it destroys shareholder value and obscures the strength of Zillow's core marketplace business.

### The Pricing Accuracy Problem

Home flipping requires thin margins (industry standard: 2-5% gross), which in turn demands high pricing accuracy. The publicly reported Zestimate median error rate is ~6.9% nationally. We acknowledge an important nuance: the Zillow Offers pricing algorithm is likely not identical to the consumer-facing Zestimate, and national median error may overstate the error in the narrower geographies where Zillow actually buys homes. It is possible that Zillow Offers uses a proprietary model with tighter accuracy in its active markets.

However, this nuance does not rescue the reform case. What we can observe directly is outcomes: Zillow purchased 9,680 homes in Q3 2021 and is sitting on ~7,000 homes bought above current market value, with an estimated write-down of $300-500M. Whatever pricing model Zillow Offers actually uses, it is systematically overpaying — and this is the relevant fact. The company pivoted its pricing algorithm twice in 2021 specifically to address overpayment, and losses widened rather than narrowed after each correction.

Two failed pivots in one year do not constitute mathematical proof that improvement is structurally impossible. But they do constitute strong evidence that the gap between current performance and required performance is large, that the direction of travel is wrong, and that the board should not bet continued hundreds of millions on the hypothesis that a third or fourth pivot will succeed where the first two failed. The burden of proof should rest on those arguing for continued investment, not on those arguing for exit.

### The Financial Case for Exit

| Metric | Zillow Offers | Core IMT Business |
|--------|--------------|--------------------|
| Q3 2021 Revenue | $1.2B | $490M |
| EBITDA Margin | Negative (widening) | ~30% |
| YoY Growth | 62% (revenue) | 20%+ |
| Capital Intensity | Extreme (billions on balance sheet) | Asset-light |
| Headcount Drag | ~2,600 incremental hires | Stable |

Zillow Offers generates 70% of consolidated revenue but is a net value destroyer. The core IMT (Internet, Media & Technology) segment generates $490M at ~30% EBITDA margins — a $147M quarterly EBITDA business growing 20%+ year-over-year. This is an exceptional software business being masked by an unprofitable real estate operation.

The math is stark: Zillow is carrying ~7,000 homes on its books, many purchased above current market value. The estimated write-down exposure is $300-500M. But this write-down, while painful, is a one-time event. Continuing Offers means continuing to accumulate losses quarter after quarter with no demonstrated path to profitability.

### Why Reform Will Not Work

The reform thesis assumes that tighter pricing controls and reduced volume can make Zillow Offers viable. This faces three reinforcing problems:

**1. Tighter pricing means uncompetitive offers.** If Zillow tightens its algorithm to avoid overpayment, its offers will be lower than competitors'. Sellers choose iBuyers primarily on price. A Zillow that offers less than Opendoor loses deal flow, which eliminates the volume advantage that was supposed to justify the business.

**2. Reduced volume undermines the unit economics theory.** The original Zillow Offers thesis depended on scale: high volume would drive down per-unit renovation, transaction, and holding costs. Reducing volume to manage risk weakens the scale economics that were supposed to make the model work. You cannot simultaneously de-risk and scale.

**3. The competitive landscape favors specialists.** Opendoor operates in narrower geographies with deeper local expertise and is consistently closer to breakeven. Opendoor's entire company is built around iBuying operations and logistics. Zillow is a technology and media company that bolted on a capital-intensive real estate operation. Opendoor has organizational focus; Zillow has organizational distraction.

### The Inventory Time Bomb

Zillow purchased 9,680 homes in Q3 2021 but sold only 3,032. This 3:1 buy-to-sell ratio means inventory is accumulating at an alarming rate. Each home on the books carries holding costs (taxes, insurance, maintenance, financing) of roughly $2,000-3,000/month. With 7,000 homes, that is $14-21M per month in holding costs alone — before any mark-to-market losses.

The announced "pause" attributed to labor and supply constraints is telling. Whether it is a fig leaf for pricing model failure or a genuine operational bottleneck, it reveals the same problem: Zillow cannot operate the buy-renovate-sell pipeline at the scale it has been purchasing. The longer Zillow holds these homes, the greater the exposure to a housing market correction. With prices up 19.5% YoY but showing signs of cooling, the window to liquidate inventory at tolerable losses is narrowing.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

### Critical Unknowns

1. **Actual pricing model accuracy in active markets.** The ~6.9% Zestimate error is a national consumer-product figure. We do not know the accuracy of the proprietary Offers pricing model in the specific geographies where Zillow buys. This data exists internally and should be presented to the board. However, the observed outcomes (systematic overpayment, widening losses, $300-500M write-down) strongly suggest the Offers model is not achieving the accuracy required, regardless of how it compares to the consumer Zestimate.

2. **Exact mark-to-market loss on current inventory.** The $300-500M range is wide. A home-by-home analysis of current market values versus purchase prices is needed to quantify the actual write-down before announcing exit. This analysis should be completed within 2 weeks.

3. **Housing market trajectory over next 6-12 months.** If the market corrects 5-10%, the write-down could exceed $500M. If the market holds, Zillow may recover some losses on liquidation. We should model three scenarios (continued appreciation, flat, 10% correction) for the liquidation portfolio.

4. **Contractual and legal obligations.** How many homes are under contract but not yet closed? What are the penalties for walking away from pending purchases? What litigation exposure exists from sellers whose transactions are in progress?

5. **Employee retention during wind-down.** Approximately 2,600 employees were hired for Offers-related roles. Mass layoffs will affect morale across the entire company. What is the retention risk for core IMT employees during a turbulent wind-down?

6. **Zillow Offers unit economics by market.** It is possible that some markets were profitable or near-breakeven. Understanding per-market performance could inform whether any narrow continuation makes sense — though we believe the organizational and capital costs of maintaining even a small iBuying operation outweigh any marginal market-level profitability.

7. **Internal algorithm team assessment.** What is the team's honest estimate of the timeline and probability of achieving pricing accuracy sufficient for profitability? This should be solicited independently from the business leadership that championed Offers.

### What We Cannot Determine from Available Evidence

- Whether the Offers pricing model is literally the Zestimate or a distinct proprietary system (this matters for diagnosing the problem, though not for the exit decision, since observed outcomes are dispositive)
- Whether sub-2% pricing accuracy is a hard threshold or whether Zillow's fee structure and services could sustain profitability at somewhat higher error rates
- Whether Opendoor's closer-to-breakeven performance would survive at Zillow's volume, or whether it benefits primarily from smaller scale and geographic focus

### Go/No-Go Conditions for Announcing Exit

**Must have before announcement:**
- [ ] Home-by-home write-down estimate within ±$50M accuracy
- [ ] Internal Offers pricing model accuracy data reviewed by board (to confirm the gap is as large as outcomes suggest)
- [ ] Legal review of pending contract obligations complete
- [ ] Liquidation strategy selected (bulk sale vs. orderly market disposition)
- [ ] Board-approved severance and restructuring budget
- [ ] Investor communication strategy reviewed by IR and legal
- [ ] CEO prepared to own the narrative (this was a bold bet that didn't work, not a failure of vision)

**Should have but not blocking:**
- [ ] Preliminary conversations with potential bulk buyers (institutional investors, Opendoor)
- [ ] Retention packages designed for key core-business employees
- [ ] 2022 guidance framework for "new Zillow" (marketplace-focused)

### Decision: NOT YET READY to announce, but ready to decide.

The board should vote to exit Zillow Offers in this meeting. The announcement should follow in 2-4 weeks once the liquidation strategy and write-down estimate are finalized. Speed matters — every week of delay adds holding costs and market exposure.

## Recommended Next Artifact

### Immediate Next Steps (Board approval required)

1. **Internal Pricing Model Audit (1 week):** Before public announcement, obtain the actual Offers pricing model accuracy data by market. This serves two purposes: (a) confirming the exit decision with precise data rather than inference from outcomes, and (b) informing the liquidation strategy by identifying which markets have the worst pricing performance and largest exposure.

2. **Liquidation Options Analysis (1 week, parallel):** Evaluate three liquidation paths — (a) bulk portfolio sale to institutional investors at a discount, (b) market-by-market orderly disposition over 6-9 months, (c) hybrid approach. Deliverable: financial model with NPV comparison of each path.

3. **Stakeholder Communication Plan (2 weeks):** Draft investor letter, press release, employee all-hands script, and partner FAQ. Key narrative: Zillow is doubling down on its high-margin marketplace business and returning to asset-light focus. Frame the exit as strategic discipline, not defeat.

4. **Organizational Restructuring Plan (2 weeks):** Identify which Offers employees have skills transferable to core business. Design severance packages for affected employees. Create retention bonuses for critical core-business talent who may be unsettled by the disruption.

5. **"New Zillow" Strategic Plan (4 weeks):** With Offers removed, articulate the forward strategy for the core marketplace business. Key question: how does Zillow redeploy the capital, technology, and data assets from Offers into higher-return opportunities? Potential areas include:
   - Enhanced agent tools and services (higher wallet share of existing transactions)
   - Mortgage and closing services (transaction adjacencies with better unit economics)
   - Zillow-as-platform (connecting buyers, sellers, agents, lenders in an integrated marketplace)

## Addressing the Strongest Counterargument

The strongest case for reform is: *"Zillow has invested billions building iBuying infrastructure, has the largest consumer real estate audience in the country, and exiting now means abandoning that investment just as the team is learning from its mistakes. A reformed, lower-volume Offers program could become profitable in 12-24 months and would give Zillow a transactional revenue stream that diversifies away from advertising dependence."*

This argument fails on three grounds:

**First, sunk cost is not a reason to continue.** The billions already invested are gone regardless of the decision. The only relevant question is whether future investment in Offers will generate positive returns. Given two failed algorithm pivots, widening losses, and 7,000 underwater homes, the evidence available to this board does not support that hypothesis.

**Second, the trend is moving in the wrong direction.** If losses were narrowing quarter-over-quarter, the reform case would have merit. They are widening. Two algorithm pivots in 2021 have not reversed this trend. We cannot rule out that a third pivot would succeed, but the board should require affirmative evidence of convergence — not just hope — before committing additional hundreds of millions. That evidence does not exist today.

**Third, the opportunity cost is enormous.** Every dollar and every engineer-hour spent on Offers is unavailable for the core business. The IMT segment is a high-growth, high-margin software business. Zillow's 200M+ monthly visitors represent an extraordinary competitive moat. The strategic question is not "can we eventually make Offers work?" but "what is the highest-return use of Zillow's unique assets?" The answer is clearly the marketplace, not home flipping.

---

**Bottom line:** Exit Zillow Offers. Take the write-down. Refocus every resource on the marketplace business that made Zillow valuable in the first place. The stock market will reward strategic clarity over stubborn persistence in a business whose outcomes demonstrate it is not working.
