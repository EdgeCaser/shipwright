# Exit Zillow Offers rather than fund another repair cycle

Zillow should exit iBuying in October 2021 rather than pursue another reform attempt. The strongest basis for that recommendation is not that Zillow is mathematically incapable of ever building a workable underwriting model. The critique is correct that the public Zestimate and the internal Offers pricing pipeline are not the same thing, and the 6.9% national Zestimate median error should not be treated as a literal measure of Offers buy-box accuracy. But that distinction does not rescue the business decision facing the board.

The board is not being asked whether a narrowly scoped iBuying model could exist in theory. It is being asked whether Zillow, from its current position, should keep committing capital and management attention to prove that thesis after two 2021 pricing-model pivots, widening EBITDA losses, a purchase pace of 9,680 homes versus only 3,032 sold in Q3, roughly 7,000 homes on the balance sheet that were bought above current market value, and an estimated $300-500M write-down exposure. That operating record shifts the burden of proof. Reform is no longer the default just because iteration is possible; reform now requires affirmative evidence that a smaller, tighter model can earn acceptable unit economics after overhead and capital costs. The case packet does not provide that evidence.

This is why exit remains the better recommendation. Zillow Offers generated $1.2B of Q3 revenue and represented about 70% of company revenue, but it was masking rather than enhancing enterprise value because losses were widening and inventory risk was compounding. By contrast, Zillow's core IMT marketplace business produced about $490M of revenue at roughly 30% EBITDA margins while growing 20%+ year over year. The company already has a high-quality asset-light business with real strategic advantages in consumer traffic, agent demand, and adjacent services. The board does not need to preserve a principal-risk home-flipping operation to stay relevant in transactions.

The best counterargument is that Opendoor's relatively better performance in narrower geographies implies Zillow could shrink the buy box, reduce volume, and salvage a disciplined niche iBuying business. That is directionally true: tighter scope can improve precision. But that supports caution, not continued commitment. Zillow's recent evidence shows that scaling Offers before proving durable local economics created a large inventory and credibility problem. Once the company is already carrying underwater homes and facing a possible market cooling backdrop, the value of optionality falls and the cost of continued experimentation rises. A board should not authorize another reform cycle on a hypothesis that is plausible in principle but unproven in Zillow's own hands under current conditions.

The right move is a clean strategic exit from iBuying and a refocus on Zillow's marketplace, mortgage, and seller-services stack. Operationally, that means stopping new purchases, liquidating inventory with market-by-market discipline, resizing the Offers-related cost base, and retaining only the customer-facing capabilities that strengthen Zillow's asset-light transaction platform. Strategically, the message should be explicit: Zillow is exiting balance-sheet home ownership because it has not demonstrated a sufficiently reliable return profile, not because transactions are unimportant.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Exit Zillow Offers and wind down iBuying rather than attempt another reform cycle.

Why this is the right call:
- The evidence supports governance-level loss of confidence in this business line even if a theoretical narrower model might work.
- Two pricing-model pivots in 2021 did not prevent a large inventory and write-down problem.
- Current inventory exposure and widening losses make further iteration unusually expensive.
- Zillow's stronger asset-light marketplace business gives the company a credible and higher-return alternative path.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- The case packet does not provide market-level unit economics showing whether any sub-geographies are sustainably profitable after full cost allocation.
- The exact relationship between Zestimate accuracy and the internal Offers underwriting model is not specified and should not be conflated.
- The embedded loss distribution across the roughly 7,000-home backlog is uncertain within the estimated $300-500M range.
- One-time shutdown costs, including severance and contractual unwind costs, are not specified.

These gaps matter for execution and for estimating the exact cost of exit. They do not reverse the recommendation because reform now requires positive proof of a viable constrained model, and that proof is absent.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass for board decision:
- There is enough evidence to choose strategic direction now: exit.
- The board has sufficient evidence that continued iteration carries high capital-allocation risk.
- Delay increases exposure because inventory liquidation risk remains live.

Fail for detailed implementation:
- Management still owes the board a market-by-market liquidation plan.
- Management should quantify shutdown costs and identify reusable capabilities from Offers.
- Investor and employee communication plans are still required.

A board action memo covering:

2. A post-Offers operating model focused on marketplace, mortgage, and seller services.
3. A communications plan explaining why exiting principal risk improves returns and strategic focus.
