# Exit Zillow Offers and refocus Zillow on the marketplace

Zillow should exit iBuying entirely rather than attempt another reform cycle. The core issue is not temporary execution noise; it is a structural mismatch between Zillow Offers' required operating precision and Zillow's demonstrated pricing capability. In a business where gross margins are typically 2-5% and sustainable performance requires pricing accuracy inside roughly 2%, Zillow is operating with a Zestimate median error rate around 6.9% nationally, has already pivoted the pricing model twice in 2021, and still accumulated roughly 7,000 homes that were bought above current market value. That combination argues against incremental repair.

The financial evidence points the same way. Zillow Offers generated $1.2B of Q3 2021 revenue and now represents about 70% of company revenue, but it is widening EBITDA losses and carries an estimated $300-500M write-down exposure. In Q3 alone, Zillow Offers bought 9,680 homes and sold just 3,032, which indicates that volume growth is amplifying inventory and balance-sheet risk rather than proving scale economies. The board's concern is correct: the segment is obscuring the strength of Zillow's better business.

That better business is the core IMT marketplace segment, which produced about $490M of revenue at roughly 30% EBITDA margins while growing more than 20% year over year. Zillow's strategic advantage has always been audience, intent, and high-margin lead generation around residential real estate. Zillow does not need to own housing inventory to participate in transactions; it needs to own consumer traffic, seller and buyer workflow, agent demand, mortgage attach, and adjacent services. Exiting iBuying would improve strategic coherence by moving Zillow back toward an asset-light transaction platform instead of a capital-intensive principal-risk model.

The strongest argument for reform is that Zillow has already built substantial operational capability, has large consumer reach, and still could create value by tightening buy-boxes, narrowing geography, cutting volume, and treating Offers as a more disciplined niche business. Competitor evidence suggests that narrower geography and tighter controls can produce results closer to breakeven. If the housing market stabilizes and the company resets underwriting, Zillow might salvage at least part of the original thesis.

That argument is not strong enough. First, Zillow's supposed edge was always its pricing advantage from the Zestimate and consumer data exhaust; the current failure directly invalidates that core thesis. Second, a cooler housing market raises risk exactly when Zillow most needs model reliability. Third, reform would require retaining a large operating footprint, more management attention, and more capital against a segment that has already proven capable of consuming both without producing acceptable economics. Fourth, the market is already signaling a credibility problem: Zillow stock fell from about $200 in February 2021 to roughly $85 in October 2021. Another "pause and repair" message is unlikely to restore confidence if investors suspect the company is preserving a structurally flawed business for narrative reasons.

The board should therefore choose a clean exit, not a partial retreat disguised as reform. The operational priority is orderly wind-down: stop new home purchases, aggressively but rationally clear existing inventory, resize headcount tied to Offers, preserve customer-facing transaction products that do not require balance-sheet risk, and redeploy capital and leadership attention into the marketplace, mortgage, and seller-services stack. The strategic message should be explicit: Zillow is not abandoning transaction participation; it is abandoning principal-risk home ownership as the wrong model for Zillow.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Exit Zillow Offers and wind down iBuying rather than attempt another reform.

Why this is the right call:
- The business requires pricing precision tighter than Zillow has demonstrated.
- Inventory exposure and write-down risk are already large enough to overwhelm the case for incremental repair.
- Zillow's high-margin core marketplace business is strong and is being masked by Offers losses.
- Management credibility and capital allocation discipline both improve with a decisive exit.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- The exact mark-to-market loss embedded in the ~7,000-home backlog is uncertain within the estimated $300-500M range.
- It is unclear whether any sub-geographies or seller cohorts inside Offers are genuinely profitable after full cost allocation.
- The near-term trajectory of home prices matters for liquidation outcomes, especially if market cooling accelerates.
- Detailed severance, contract unwind, and operational shutdown costs are not specified here.

These gaps affect the cost of exit, not the direction of the decision. To justify reform, Zillow would need evidence that a narrowly scoped iBuying business can sustainably earn acceptable unit economics after full overhead and capital costs. The case packet does not provide that evidence.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass for board decision:
- There is enough evidence to decide on strategic direction now: exit.
- The failure mode is diagnosed at the level that matters for governance: business-model mismatch, not just temporary execution error.
- The opportunity cost of delay is high because ongoing ownership of mispriced inventory compounds risk.

Fail for detailed execution planning:
- The board still needs a 90-day wind-down and communication plan.
- Management should provide scenario analysis for inventory liquidation, headcount actions, and investor messaging.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A board-level wind-down plan with three components:
1. Inventory liquidation scenarios by market and expected loss band.
2. Operating model reset for Zillow's post-Offers strategy across marketplace, mortgage, and seller services.
3. External communications package for employees, investors, agents, and customers explaining why exit strengthens Zillow's long-term position.
