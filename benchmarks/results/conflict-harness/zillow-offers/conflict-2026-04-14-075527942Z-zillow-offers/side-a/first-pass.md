# Exit Zillow Offers and refocus Zillow on its high-margin marketplace

## Recommendation
Zillow should exit iBuying entirely rather than attempt another reform of Zillow Offers. The business is failing on the variable that matters most: pricing precision. In home flipping, a few points of pricing error erase all economics; Zillow's national Zestimate median error rate of roughly 6.9% is structurally misaligned with an industry that typically requires sub-2% accuracy and only earns 2-5% gross margins. The result is not a temporary execution miss but a business-model contradiction. Continuing would risk more capital, more write-downs, and more strategic distraction just as the board can still salvage Zillow's stronger marketplace franchise.

## Why Exit Is The Right Call
First, Zillow Offers has already crossed from growth experiment into value destruction. In Q3 2021 Zillow bought 9,680 homes but sold only 3,032, creating a backlog of roughly 7,000 homes, many reportedly underwater. Estimated write-down exposure of $300-500M indicates the business is not suffering from a short-term revenue issue; it is suffering from negative unit economics at scale. A model that loses money faster when volume rises should not be reformed by further iteration unless there is credible evidence the core input assumptions have changed. Here, they have not.

Second, the scale argument in favor of Offers is misleading. Offers generated $1.2B of Q3 2021 revenue and represented about 70% of Zillow's total revenue, but that revenue is low-quality because it carries widening EBITDA losses and material balance-sheet risk. The board should not confuse gross merchandise throughput with enterprise value creation. Zillow's core IMT business generated about $490M of revenue at roughly 30% EBITDA margins while still growing more than 20% year over year. The stronger business is being obscured by the weaker one.

Third, Zillow does not appear to have a durable edge in iBuying despite its traffic and data advantages. The original thesis was that Zestimate and Zillow's consumer reach would provide a superior pricing engine. Instead, Zillow had already pivoted the pricing algorithm twice in 2021 and still systematically overpaid. That matters because iBuying is not won by brand awareness alone; it is won by localized operational discipline, narrow buy-box selection, and exceptionally tight pricing. The case packet explicitly notes that Opendoor operates in narrower geographies and at lower volume while staying consistently closer to breakeven. Zillow's comparative advantage is broad consumer attention and marketplace monetization, not inventory risk management.

Fourth, the timing argues against trying to "ride out" the problem. Housing prices were still up 19.5% year over year as of August 2021, but signs of cooling were emerging. If Zillow is generating losses and sitting on underwater homes during one of the strongest housing markets in recent memory, the downside in a less favorable market is worse, not better. A cooling environment turns an already fragile model into a more dangerous one.

## Strongest Counterarguments And Why They Still Lose
The strongest argument for reform is strategic adjacency: if Zillow exits iBuying, it may surrender the chance to become a true transaction platform and revert to being "just a lead-gen marketplace." That concern is real. But owning homes is not the only path to transaction participation. Zillow can still expand across mortgages, closing, touring, seller services, and transaction software without taking principal risk on inventory. The board should separate the goal of deeper transaction monetization from the specific mechanism of warehousing homes on Zillow's balance sheet.

A second counterargument is that the current failure may be correctable with tighter pricing controls, lower volume, narrower geographies, and human overrides. That sounds prudent, but it underestimates both the evidence already accumulated and the organizational drag of another turnaround attempt. Zillow already changed the algorithm twice in 2021. The problem is not that management forgot to tighten controls; it is that the business has not demonstrated the precision required to make those controls sufficient. A downsized reform would still consume management attention, retain reputational overhang, and preserve the risk of renewed losses, all for a business where Zillow has not shown a defendable edge.

A third argument is that exiting now locks in losses at the bottom and concedes the category to competitors. But the relevant benchmark is not pride or sunk cost; it is forward return on capital. Zillow's stock had already fallen from about $200 in February 2021 to roughly $85 in October 2021, and board concern is specifically that Offers losses are masking the strength of the core business. The market is already penalizing conglomeration of a good business with a bad one. Exiting is the cleaner way to restore valuation clarity.

## What The Board Should Do Next
Zillow should announce an orderly wind-down of Zillow Offers, complete liquidation of existing inventory, and a reallocation of capital and leadership attention toward the core marketplace and adjacent asset-light transaction services. The communication posture should be explicit: this is not a retreat from housing transactions; it is a decision to participate in transactions through higher-margin, lower-risk services where Zillow has genuine structural advantage.

Management should pair the exit with three operating moves. First, isolate and disclose the full financial impact quickly so investors can underwrite the remaining company with confidence. Second, redeploy product, data, and go-to-market talent from Offers into seller demand capture, mortgage attach, touring conversion, and partner-enabled transaction workflows. Third, define a post-Offers strategic narrative built around being the category's best transaction orchestrator rather than a principal home buyer.

## Decision Frame
Decision: Exit iBuying entirely.

Why now: Zillow Offers is failing on unit economics, balance-sheet exposure, and strategic fit at the same time.

Threshold logic:
- If pricing accuracy is materially worse than industry-required tolerance, iBuying is structurally unsound for Zillow.
- If Offers revenue growth masks widening EBITDA losses, revenue scale should not be treated as strategic proof.
- If the core IMT business is high-margin and growing, capital should flow back to that engine and adjacent asset-light extensions.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- The exact mark-to-market loss distribution across the roughly 7,000-home backlog is not broken out.
- The contribution margin by cohort, geography, and hold-time bucket is not provided.
- We do not have evidence that any submarket or seller segment within Offers consistently meets acceptable unit economics.
- We do not have a quantified estimate of how much investor valuation discount is specifically attributable to Offers versus broader market conditions.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass for board decision: Yes.

Why sufficient: The available evidence is enough to choose direction because the case does not hinge on fine optimization. The board is deciding whether to keep funding a capital-intensive business with demonstrated pricing failure and worsening losses versus refocus on a proven high-margin core. The burden of proof should sit with continuation, and that burden has not been met.

What would change the answer: Credible evidence that a tightly scoped iBuying model can sustainably hit pricing accuracy and contribution-margin thresholds in selected markets without significant balance-sheet risk.
