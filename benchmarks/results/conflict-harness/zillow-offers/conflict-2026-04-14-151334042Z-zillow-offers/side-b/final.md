# Strategic Recommendation: Exit Zillow Offers

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Should Zillow reform its iBuying operation with tighter controls, or exit entirely and refocus on its high-margin marketplace business?

**Recommendation: Exit Zillow Offers entirely and begin an orderly wind-down of the home inventory.**

This is not a close call. The evidence points to a severe mismatch between Zillow's demonstrated pricing performance and the requirements of the iBuying business. The case for exit rests on observable outcomes — systematic overpayment, widening losses at scale, and ~7,000 underwater homes — not on theoretical claims about what the algorithm could or could not eventually achieve.

### The structural case for exit

**1. The pricing system has demonstrably failed at operational scale, and reform faces a steep burden of proof.**
The Zestimate's 6.9% national median error rate provides directional context: the consumer-facing algorithm that underpins Zillow's pricing reputation operates at 3-4x the tolerance required for profitable home flipping (sub-2% accuracy). The Offers acquisition model likely uses a more refined variant tuned to target geographies and property types — but the operational results show this refinement has been insufficient. Zillow purchased 9,680 homes in Q3 2021 at systematically inflated prices, accumulating ~7,000 homes now underwater with $300-500M in write-down exposure. The company pivoted the algorithm twice in 2021 and the problem worsened, not improved.

Could further reforms — narrower geography, lower volume, human overrides — eventually close the gap? Possibly. But the burden of proof sits with the reform case, not the exit case. Two failed pivots in a rising market (19.5% YoY appreciation should have been tailwind, not headwind) suggest the problem is deeper than parameter tuning. The board should not bet additional capital on the hypothesis that a third pivot will succeed where the first two failed, especially when the downside of being wrong is hundreds of millions in additional losses.

**2. Volume amplifies the problem rather than solving it.**
The iBuying thesis assumed scale would create a data flywheel: more transactions → better pricing → tighter margins. The opposite has occurred. As Zillow scaled from cautious early purchases to 9,680 homes/quarter, losses widened quarter-over-quarter. Volume in a mispriced system does not produce learning — it produces larger losses faster. The Q3 purchase-to-sale ratio (9,680 bought vs. 3,032 sold) shows inventory is accumulating 3x faster than it can be liquidated.

**3. The core business is being actively damaged.**
Zillow Offers generates 70% of total revenue ($1.2B of $1.7B) but is EBITDA-negative with widening losses. Meanwhile, the IMT segment produces $490M at ~30% EBITDA margins with 20%+ YoY growth. The market has already rendered its verdict: the stock has fallen from $200 to $85 — a ~$20B market cap destruction — primarily because Offers losses are masking the value of the marketplace business. Every quarter Offers continues, it drains capital, management attention, and investor confidence from the profitable core.

**4. The competitive landscape offers no shelter.**
Opendoor operates with narrower geography, lower volume, and tighter pricing discipline — and still struggles to reach breakeven. If the most focused pure-play iBuyer cannot reliably profit, there is no reason to believe Zillow — for whom iBuying is an adjacency, not the core business — will solve the economics that Opendoor cannot.

**5. The housing market is turning against the position.**
Case-Shiller shows 19.5% YoY price increases through August 2021, but the rate of appreciation is showing signs of cooling. Zillow is holding ~7,000 homes purchased at peak-cycle prices. If the market flattens or declines even modestly, the $300-500M write-down estimate becomes a floor, not a ceiling. Every month of delay in liquidation increases exposure to a cooling market.

### The reform counterargument — and why it fails

The strongest case for reform: Zillow has invested heavily in iBuying infrastructure, has brand recognition, and could theoretically tighten pricing — via narrower geography, lower volume, or human pricing overrides — to reduce overpayment while maintaining a smaller, more disciplined operation.

This argument fails for three reasons:

- **Tighter pricing means lower conversion, which destroys the unit economics differently.** If Zillow offers fair-market or below-market prices, sellers will choose competitors or traditional listings. The value proposition of iBuying to sellers is convenience at a reasonable price — not convenience at a below-market price. A reformed Zillow Offers would have dramatically lower volume, which means fixed costs (technology, operations, headcount) are spread over fewer transactions, making per-unit economics worse.

- **Reform preserves the management distraction.** The 2,600+ employees added since 2019 (mostly Offers-related) represent not just cost but organizational complexity. A reform scenario requires continued executive bandwidth on a business that has failed to demonstrate viable economics at any scale, diverting attention from marketplace innovation where Zillow has genuine competitive advantages.

- **The market will not give credit for "trying harder."** Investors have already discounted the stock by ~57%. A reform announcement signals uncertainty and continued risk exposure. A clean exit signals discipline and refocus on the high-margin business, which is the faster path to multiple re-rating.

### Recommended exit plan

1. **Announce full exit from iBuying within 30 days.** Frame as strategic refocus, not failure. Emphasize return to the capital-light marketplace model.
2. **Liquidate the ~7,000 home inventory over 6-9 months.** Accept the $300-500M write-down. Trying to hold for price recovery risks larger losses if the market cools.
3. **Reduce headcount by 2,000-2,500** (the Offers-related hires). Painful but necessary — carrying this cost structure without the revenue is worse.
4. **Redirect engineering talent toward marketplace innovation:** Zillow's data assets, traffic (200M+ monthly visitors), and brand remain extraordinary competitive advantages in the advertising/lead-gen model. Invest in seller tools, agent productivity, and mortgage integration.
5. **Provide clear forward guidance** showing the path back to IMT-level margins across the company.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

| Unknown | Impact on recommendation | How to close |
|---------|------------------------|-------------|
| Offers-specific pricing model accuracy vs. national Zestimate error rate | If the Offers model performs materially better than the 6.9% national Zestimate figure in target markets, the reform case strengthens — but Q3 results (systematic overpayment, ~7,000 underwater homes) suggest it does not perform well enough regardless | Internal analysis of Offers acquisition model error rates by market, property type, and time cohort vs. actual sale prices |
| Exact loss per home in current inventory | Affects write-down magnitude but not direction of recommendation | Internal mark-to-market analysis of all 7,000 homes vs. current local comps |
| Contractual obligations on homes in pipeline | May affect exit timeline | Legal review of purchase commitments not yet closed |
| Zillow Offers employee retention risk to core business | Some Offers engineers may be valuable for marketplace; layoffs risk losing them | Identify top 200 engineers for redeployment to IMT/marketplace roles before announcement |
| Opendoor and Offerpad response to Zillow exit | Competitors may gain share in iBuying but this is a market Zillow should not compete in | Monitor but do not let competitive dynamics drive decision to stay in a losing business |
| Tax treatment of write-down | Could meaningfully affect net financial impact | Engage tax advisors on optimal timing and structuring of inventory liquidation |
| Housing market trajectory Q4 2021-Q2 2022 | If market drops, delay increases losses; if market rises, some losses recover | Immaterial to strategic recommendation — the business has failed to demonstrate viable economics even in a strongly rising market |
