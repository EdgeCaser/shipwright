# Exit Zillow Offers and refocus Zillow on the high-margin marketplace

Zillow should exit iBuying rather than attempt another reform cycle. The board decision should rest on demonstrated operating outcomes, not on the theoretical attractiveness of iBuying as a category. The evidence in hand shows that Zillow Offers scaled into a risk position it could not price or operationally absorb: 9,680 homes purchased in Q3 2021 against only 3,032 sold, roughly 7,000 homes left on the books, and an estimated $300M-$500M write-down exposure. That is not a minor variance inside a healthy system; it is enterprise-level balance-sheet failure.

The most important point is not that algorithmic home buying can never work. Opendoor's narrower footprint and closer-to-breakeven performance show the category is at least conditionally viable. The relevant conclusion is narrower and more useful: Zillow has not shown that its own Offers underwriting and operating model can produce durable profitability at the scale and speed it pursued. Management already pivoted the pricing algorithm twice in 2021, yet losses widened and backlog accumulated during a period of 19.5% year-over-year home price appreciation. If Zillow could not hold unit economics together in an unusually supportive housing market, the burden of proof for another reform attempt should be extremely high.

That burden is even harder to justify because the opportunity cost is large. Zillow Offers generated $1.2B of Q3 2021 revenue, but that revenue mix is misleading because it came with negative EBITDA, capital intensity, inventory risk, and growing operational complexity. Meanwhile, the core IMT business generated $490M of revenue at roughly 30% EBITDA margins while still growing 20%+ year over year. The board should not let a low-margin, balance-sheet-intensive business obscure the economic quality of the core marketplace franchise.

The strongest counterargument for reform is strategic. Zillow wants to participate in the transaction, not just monetize audience attention. Exiting Offers could appear to abandon that ambition after substantial hiring, product investment, and market positioning. A lower-volume restart in fewer metros with tighter human review and stricter buy-box discipline is the best version of that argument.

Even so, exit remains the better decision. First, Zillow has already tested reform in real time through multiple model changes without restoring control. Second, the current inventory overhang means management attention must go to loss containment, not to another experiment. Third, a cooling market raises downside risk exactly when Zillow is least able to absorb additional pricing mistakes. Fourth, Zillow does not need principal risk to stay relevant in transactions; it can pursue asset-light seller convenience, financing, and partner-enabled services without holding homes on its balance sheet.

The board should therefore exit Zillow Offers decisively, unwind inventory with disciplined loss minimization, and reallocate capital and leadership focus toward the marketplace and adjacent transaction services. This is not a retreat from housing transactions. It is a rejection of a specific model that Zillow has failed to make economically reliable.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Exit Zillow Offers rather than pursue another reform cycle.

Why this is the right call:
- Zillow Offers has produced balance-sheet strain, backlog, and widening losses at meaningful scale.
- Zillow has not demonstrated that its own underwriting model can deliver durable profitability, even after two 2021 algorithm pivots.
- The business underperformed during a highly favorable home-price environment, which weakens the case that results would improve after market normalization.
- Offers is masking the strength of Zillow's core IMT marketplace, which is higher-margin and still growing.
- Zillow can remain in the transaction flow through asset-light services and partnerships without taking principal housing risk.

Board instruction for the next 90 days:
- Keep new home purchases stopped.
- Liquidate existing inventory with market-by-market loss minimization discipline.
- Publish cleaner segment reporting that isolates core marketplace performance from Offers unwind effects.
- Reallocate capital and senior attention toward asset-light transaction services, financing, and partner-enabled seller solutions.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- The exact metro-level loss distribution across the roughly 7,000-home inventory pool.
- Whether any small set of geographies produced genuinely acceptable fully burdened unit economics.
- The full one-time cost of exit, including restructuring, vendor unwind, and carrying costs.
- The extent to which Zillow can preserve seller-convenience positioning through partnerships after exit.

These gaps matter for execution design, but they do not reverse the decision logic. Zillow has already failed the proof threshold required to justify continued principal-risk deployment.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass conditions for this recommendation:
- The board prioritizes economic quality over gross revenue contribution.
- Management treats the problem as underwriting and model failure, not as a temporary labor bottleneck.
- Zillow can execute an orderly unwind and communicate a credible asset-light transaction strategy.
- Investor messaging clearly separates durable core earnings power from Offers-related losses.

Fail conditions:
- The board funds a restart without hard evidence of metro-level positive unit economics after fully burdened costs.
- Management reframes the issue as short-term execution friction and resumes purchases prematurely.
- Zillow exits Offers but fails to articulate how it will still participate in transactions without principal risk.
