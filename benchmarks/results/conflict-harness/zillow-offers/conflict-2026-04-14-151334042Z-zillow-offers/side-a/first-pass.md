# Exit Zillow Offers and refocus Zillow on the high-margin housing marketplace

Zillow should exit iBuying rather than attempt another reform. The core issue is not temporary execution noise; it is a structural mismatch between the economics of home flipping and Zillow's current pricing capability, operating model, and risk tolerance.

Zillow Offers has become 70% of company revenue, but that scale is misleading. In Q3 2021 it generated $1.2B of revenue while widening losses, absorbing capital, and obscuring the strength of the core IMT business, which produced $490M of revenue at roughly 30% EBITDA margins and continued 20%+ growth. The board should not preserve a low-margin, balance-sheet-intensive business that is failing precisely when home price appreciation has been unusually favorable.

The evidence points to a broken model, not a fixable short-term stumble. Zillow bought 9,680 homes in Q3 2021 but sold only 3,032, creating a backlog of roughly 7,000 homes, many purchased above current market value. Estimated write-down exposure of $300M-$500M is already large enough to damage confidence, and management had already pivoted the pricing algorithm twice in 2021 without solving the problem. That matters because iBuying economics are unforgiving: industry gross margins are only 2%-5%, which requires pricing accuracy materially tighter than Zillow's roughly 6.9% national Zestimate median error rate. A model that is acceptable for consumer browsing is not adequate for principal risk at scale.

The strongest argument for reform is strategic: Zillow has the traffic, brand, and consumer intent to become a transaction platform rather than remain a media-like marketplace. Exiting Offers could look like surrendering a large future profit pool to Opendoor or others, especially after Zillow invested heavily in headcount, operations, and product. A narrower-footprint relaunch with tighter guardrails, lower volumes, and more human review might reduce risk.

That counterargument is not strong enough. First, the company has already tried reform through multiple algorithm pivots, and the problem persisted during one of the hottest housing markets in decades, when rising prices should have masked underwriting errors rather than expose them. Second, Zillow's scale advantage appears to have become a disadvantage: buying too many homes too quickly amplified model error into enterprise risk. Third, the market is showing signs of cooling. If Zillow cannot produce reliable unit economics in a 19.5% YoY home-price-growth environment, it is poorly positioned for normalization or decline. Finally, competitors with narrower geography and tighter operating discipline are structurally better suited to this business. Zillow does not need to win iBuying to win housing transactions.

The better strategy is to exit Zillow Offers in an orderly but decisive manner, dispose of inventory as quickly as value-preserving execution allows, and redeploy capital and management attention into asset-light transaction services around the core marketplace. Zillow should preserve the consumer promise of convenience by partnering where necessary rather than holding homes on balance sheet. That keeps Zillow in the transaction flow without asking shareholders to fund a business where modest model error destroys profit.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Exit Zillow Offers entirely rather than pursue another reform cycle.

Why this is the right call:
- The pricing edge thesis has not held in practice.
- Thin-margin home flipping cannot tolerate Zillow's current error rate.
- Repeated algorithm changes did not fix the issue.
- Offers is masking a stronger, higher-margin core business.
- Cooling market conditions make downside risk worse from here.

Board instruction for the next 90 days:
- Stop new home purchases.
- Liquidate existing inventory with loss minimization discipline, not growth optics.
- Separate and report core marketplace economics clearly.
- Reallocate capital and talent toward asset-light transaction products, financing, seller services, and partner-enabled convenience offerings.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- Exact loss distribution across the 7,000-home inventory pool by geography and vintage.
- Whether any subset of metros has acceptable unit economics at materially lower volume.
- The one-time restructuring cost of exit, including headcount, contracts, and inventory carrying costs.
- The degree to which exit would affect Zillow's long-term ability to own more of the transaction journey through partnerships instead of principal buying.

These gaps matter for exit execution, not for the go/no-go decision. None plausibly overturn the core conclusion that current iBuying economics are structurally unattractive for Zillow.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass conditions for this recommendation:
- The board accepts that revenue mix is less important than economic quality.
- Management can operationalize a clean shutdown and inventory unwind.
- Investor messaging clearly separates a strong core business from Offers-related losses.
- Zillow commits to an asset-light transactions strategy so exit is seen as refocus, not retreat.

Fail conditions:
- The board insists on preserving iBuying for strategic symbolism despite evidence of structural mismatch.
- Management attempts a partial restart before the inventory and postmortem are complete.
- External messaging frames the problem as temporary labor constraints instead of a failed underwriting model.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A board-level exit execution memo covering:
- inventory liquidation strategy by market

- investor communication narrative
- capital reallocation plan into core marketplace and adjacent transaction services
- partnership strategy for seller convenience without balance-sheet exposure
