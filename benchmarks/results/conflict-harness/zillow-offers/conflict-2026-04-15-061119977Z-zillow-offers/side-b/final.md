# Exit Zillow Offers and refocus on the core marketplace

Zillow's board should exit iBuying rather than authorize another reform cycle. The decisive issue is not that Zillow has mathematically proven iBuying can never work in any narrow market. It is that, as of October 2021, management has not demonstrated a repeatable pricing and operating advantage sufficient to justify keeping principal-risk exposure on the balance sheet when the downside is immediate and large and the alternative use of capital is clearly superior.

The evidence supports that conclusion even without overstating what the national Zestimate error rate proves. Zillow Offers bought 9,680 homes in Q3 2021 and sold only 3,032, leaving a severe inventory build. The company is sitting on roughly 7,000 homes, many reportedly above current market value, with estimated write-down exposure of $300-500M. Management already pivoted the pricing algorithm twice in 2021 and still ended up with a backlog of underwater inventory during a housing market that had risen 19.5% year over year. In other words, this was not a stress case defined by falling prices; it was a favorable tape, and Zillow still overbought. That is enough to conclude the current operating model is not board-worthy.

The critique is correct that a 6.9% national median Zestimate error rate does not by itself prove Zillow could never achieve acceptable accuracy in a smaller set of markets. But that uncertainty cuts against reform, not for it. If the most credible defense of continuing is a hypothetical 3-5 market pilot with a tighter buy box, the board is still being asked to fund another learning cycle after the company has already demonstrated weak controls, widening losses, and poor inventory discipline at scale. The relevant question is not whether a narrower model might someday work; it is whether Zillow should continue risking capital now on an unproven turnaround while its core business is already delivering better economics.

That opportunity cost is material. Zillow Offers generated $1.2B of Zillow's $1.7B total revenue in Q3 2021, but with negative EBITDA and worsening losses. By contrast, the core IMT segment produced about $490M of revenue at roughly 30% EBITDA margins and was growing 20%+ year over year. Offers therefore inflates revenue while obscuring enterprise quality. The board concern is valid: the market is struggling to see the strength of Zillow's core marketplace because the company is choosing to scale a lower-quality, balance-sheet-heavy business with unstable economics.

The strongest case for reform is strategic adjacency. Zillow wanted to move from lead generation into transactions, and competitors such as Opendoor show the category can exist. But competitor existence is not enough. Opendoor's narrower geography and lower volume suggest that disciplined specialization matters; they do not establish that Zillow should persist after failing to show its supposed traffic and pricing-data advantage translated into superior execution. Zillow can still pursue transaction participation through asset-light services, financing, seller tools, and partner-enabled workflows without taking inventory risk.

A second case for reform is to cut volume sharply and treat 2021 as an overexpansion error. That argument would be stronger if losses were mainly operational, or if management could point to clearly profitable markets already performing to standard. The case packet does not provide that evidence. What it does provide is a pattern of overpayment, repeated model adjustment, backlog accumulation, and widening losses. In that evidentiary context, the burden of proof should be on management to show a credible path to attractive returns. They have not met it.

The recommended course is a full exit from iBuying, an orderly wind-down of inventory, explicit investor disclosure on loss range and timing, rapid removal of Offers-related fixed cost, and redeployment of talent and capital toward higher-margin, asset-light transaction products anchored in the marketplace franchise.

## Decision Frame

Exit Zillow Offers. The board does not need proof that no narrower iBuying model could ever work. It only needs to judge whether Zillow has earned the right to continue deploying capital into a thin-margin, principal-risk business after material losses, repeated pricing recalibration, and a large underwater inventory position. The answer is no.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- The exact realized loss range on the roughly 7,000-home backlog remains uncertain and should be sized under base, downside, and accelerated liquidation scenarios.
- The case packet does not show market-level profitability or pricing accuracy by metro, so the board cannot rule out that a few narrow markets might have better economics.
- The case packet also does not show enough evidence that such a narrowed pilot would outperform the risk-adjusted returns available from IMT and adjacent asset-light products.
- Restructuring, severance, and contract unwind costs need tighter quantification before announcement.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if:
- The board accepts a near-term write-down and restructuring hit in order to stop compounding balance-sheet risk.
- Management can present an inventory liquidation plan, cost-removal timeline, and investor communication package with credible ranges.
- Zillow can articulate a post-Offers strategy centered on IMT and transaction enablement without principal home ownership.

Fail if:
- Management can produce robust market-level evidence that a tightly constrained subscale pilot would likely generate superior risk-adjusted returns within a short, board-defined time frame.
- The board cannot quantify wind-down exposure tightly enough to underwrite the exit decision.
- Zillow lacks a credible capital reallocation plan after exit.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A board-ready exit execution memo with three parts: inventory liquidation scenarios and loss sensitivities, headcount and fixed-cost removal plan, and a 12-month capital reallocation roadmap into IMT and asset-light transaction services.
