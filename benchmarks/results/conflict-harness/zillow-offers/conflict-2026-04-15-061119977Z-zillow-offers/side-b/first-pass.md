# Exit Zillow Offers and refocus on the core marketplace

Zillow should exit iBuying rather than attempt another reform cycle. The current loss profile, inventory exposure, and demonstrated pricing-model instability make Zillow Offers structurally misaligned with Zillow's strengths. The board's job is not to preserve a strategic narrative; it is to allocate capital to the business with the highest risk-adjusted return.

The core issue is not temporary execution friction. Zillow Offers bought 9,680 homes in Q3 2021 but sold only 3,032, leaving a large inventory build and roughly 7,000 homes on the books, many reportedly above current market value. Estimated write-down exposure of $300-500M is large enough to overwhelm the thin-margin economics of home flipping, where industry gross margins are typically 2-5% and operational success depends on sub-2% pricing accuracy. Zillow's own Zestimate median error rate of about 6.9% nationally is too wide for this model, and management already pivoted the algorithm twice in 2021 without solving overpayment. That combination indicates a model-risk problem, not a tuning problem.

Offers is also distorting enterprise performance. In Q3 2021, Zillow Offers generated $1.2B of Zillow's $1.7B total revenue, but with negative EBITDA and widening losses. By contrast, the IMT segment produced about $490M of revenue at roughly 30% EBITDA margins and was still growing 20%+ year over year. The board concern is correct: the low-quality revenue from Offers is masking the economics of the core marketplace business. Continuing iBuying would keep management attention, capital, and investor messaging tied to a segment that expands revenue while destroying value.

The strongest counterargument is strategic adjacency: Zillow launched Offers to become a transactional platform, and exiting could look like abandoning the larger vision while competitors such as Opendoor remain in the category. But the evidence suggests Zillow does not currently possess a durable pricing edge. If the foundational advantage thesis were working, Zillow would not be holding thousands of underwater homes after multiple algorithm changes during one of the hottest housing markets in years. Opendoor's narrower geography and lower volume are evidence that discipline and specialization matter more than traffic scale or brand reach. Zillow can still pursue transaction-enablement through higher-margin, lower-balance-sheet products without principal risk.

A second counterargument is that Zillow should shrink volume, tighten buy boxes, and reform the business in a few markets. That would only be compelling if losses were clearly attributable to transient operational bottlenecks. They are not. Management publicly cited labor and supply constraints, but the case packet states insiders know the real issue is pricing model failure. In a cooling housing market, the downside from another experimental reform is asymmetric: even modest model error can erase margins, while success would still likely produce lower returns than Zillow's core marketplace. The board should not fund another learning cycle when the opportunity cost is so high.

The recommended path is a full exit from iBuying, an orderly wind-down of inventory, and a reset of the equity story around the core marketplace and adjacent asset-light transaction services. Zillow should ring-fence the wind-down, give investors explicit disclosure on expected losses and timing, reduce Offers-related headcount and fixed cost, and redeploy product and go-to-market resources toward products that monetize transaction intent without taking housing inventory onto the balance sheet.

## Decision Frame

Exit Zillow Offers. The deciding consideration is not whether iBuying can work in principle, but whether Zillow has demonstrated a repeatable advantage in a business where pricing error destroys economics. The evidence says no. Preserve capital, stop compounding inventory risk, and refocus on the higher-margin marketplace franchise.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- The exact realized loss range on the ~7,000-home backlog is still uncertain; that affects quarter-to-quarter optics, not the direction of the decision.
- It is unclear whether a very small, tightly constrained pilot in a handful of ZIP codes could ever reach acceptable economics, but that is no longer the relevant board question if the enterprise cost of continued experimentation is high.
- The exact severance, restructuring, and wind-down costs are not specified and should be sized before announcement.
- The board should confirm which asset-light transaction products can absorb redeployed talent fastest and with the best margin profile.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if:
- The board is willing to take a near-term write-down and restructuring hit to protect long-term enterprise value.
- Management can present a credible wind-down plan for inventory liquidation, cost removal, and investor communication.
- Zillow can articulate a post-Offers strategy centered on IMT and asset-light transaction services.

Fail if:
- Management cannot quantify wind-down exposure within a narrow enough range for the board to judge downside.
- The company lacks an executable plan to remove Offers fixed costs quickly.
- The board is still implicitly optimizing for revenue scale rather than value creation.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A board-ready exit execution memo covering: inventory liquidation scenarios, write-down sensitivity, headcount and restructuring plan, investor messaging, and a 12-month capital reallocation plan into IMT and asset-light transaction products.
