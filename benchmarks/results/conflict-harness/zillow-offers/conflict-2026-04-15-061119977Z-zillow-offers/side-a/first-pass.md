# Strategic Recommendation: Exit Zillow Offers

**To:** Zillow Board of Directors  
**From:** Strategy Advisor  
**Date:** October 2021  
**Re:** Zillow Offers — Recommended Exit

---

## Decision Frame

**Recommendation: Exit iBuying entirely. Wind down Zillow Offers in an orderly fashion, liquidate the ~7,000-home inventory at managed loss, and redeploy capital and leadership attention to the core IMT marketplace business.**

The central question is not whether iBuying as a category has merit — it does. The question is whether Zillow has a structural advantage in it. The evidence says no, and no operational reform changes that answer.

Zillow's original thesis rested on a single premise: the Zestimate gives Zillow a pricing edge that pure-play iBuyers lack. That premise has been falsified. The Zestimate carries a 6.9% median error rate nationally. Home flipping economics require sub-2% pricing accuracy to generate positive gross margin at scale (industry average gross margins: 2–5%, on a thin spread that 6.9% error consumes entirely). Zillow has already attempted two algorithm pivots in 2021 to address this. Both failed to close the gap. The algorithm isn't broken — it's doing what it was built to do (consumer-grade home valuation, not institutional-grade acquisition pricing). These are different problems.

The reform path asks the board to bet that a third pivot succeeds where two failed, in a housing market that is beginning to cool, against competitors who have been optimizing this problem longer and more narrowly. That is not a bet worth taking when the alternative — the core IMT business — is generating $490M in quarterly revenue at ~30% EBITDA margins and growing 20%+ year-over-year.

**The decision frame: Offers is a capital-intensive, margin-destroying distraction from an exceptional compounding business. Exit is not defeat — it is capital reallocation.**

---

## Core Argument

### 1. The Pricing Edge Thesis Is Falsified, Not Wounded

The bull case for Zillow Offers always depended on Zestimate superiority. Opendoor and Offerpad operate in narrower geographies with tighter local pricing models. Zillow's supposed advantage was breadth — national data at scale generating better predictions.

The evidence refutes this. A 6.9% median national error rate means that in any given transaction, Zillow's model is systematically off by more than the total available gross margin. This is not a model that needs tuning. It is a model built for a different purpose — consumer engagement and lead generation — being asked to perform institutional acquisition pricing. No amount of incremental iteration closes a 3–4× accuracy gap against the margin requirement.

Worse: the two 2021 pivots indicate Zillow already recognized this and attempted correction. The fact that Zillow is still sitting on 7,000 underwater homes after two model revisions is the most damning data point available. The problem is structural, not operational.

### 2. The Core Business Is Being Actively Harmed

The IMT segment — Zillow's marketplace advertising and lead-gen business — earns ~30% EBITDA margins on $490M quarterly revenue, growing 20%+ YoY. This is a compounding high-margin platform business. It is one of the better software-adjacent businesses in the public markets.

Offers is 70% of total revenue but a margin drag. More importantly, it is consuming organizational bandwidth, leadership attention, and board credibility that the core business does not need consumed. Headcount grew from 5,400 to 8,000+ in two years — largely Offers-related. This is not a side experiment. It is a bet that has metastasized through the operating structure.

Every quarter Offers continues, it obscures IMT's strength in earnings reports, depresses the stock (down from $200 to $85), and ties up capital that could fund marketplace growth or return to shareholders.

### 3. Reform Requires Outperforming Opendoor on Their Core Competency

The reform path is not abstractly "tighten pricing and reduce volume." In practice it means: operate a smaller, geographically focused iBuying operation that achieves better unit economics than Opendoor, which has been doing this longer, in fewer markets, with a simpler operational structure.

Opendoor — the most optimized pure-play iBuyer in the market — cannot consistently reach breakeven. In Q3 2021, Opendoor was still generating negative contribution margins in several markets. If the category leader with maximum operational focus cannot consistently break even, the argument that Zillow — running iBuying as a secondary business attached to an ad marketplace — can do better requires extraordinary evidence. None exists.

### 4. Market Timing Amplifies Exit Logic

Housing prices rose 19.5% YoY (Case-Shiller, August 2021) — a pace almost universally recognized as unsustainable. The market is showing early signs of cooling. Zillow already holds ~7,000 homes purchased at peak or near-peak prices. A reform path that continues acquisitions — even at reduced volume — adds inventory exposure into a potentially declining market. The asymmetric downside risk of continuing is severe.

Exiting now allows Zillow to liquidate current inventory in a market that, while cooling, has not yet corrected. Every quarter of continued operation in reform mode risks compounding the write-down from $300–500M to something larger.

---

## Strongest Counterargument: Reform Preserves Option Value

The reform camp argues that iBuying is a real market, consumer demand for certainty-of-sale is genuine, and exiting permanently forfeits Zillow's ability to participate in the transactional real estate market. A tighter, geographically concentrated version of Offers — lower volume, better pricing discipline, regional focus — could achieve breakeven while maintaining the strategic option.

This is the most credible version of the reform case. It is not trivially wrong.

**Why exit is still correct:**

First, option value has real cost. "Maintaining the option" to re-enter iBuying requires continued capital burn, operational distraction, and inventory risk. Zillow can exit now and re-enter iBuying in three years if the category matures and the pricing problem becomes solvable — perhaps via better local ML, better operational partnerships, or market structure changes. The option is not permanently foreclosed by exit.

Second, reform's success condition is outperforming Opendoor — a pure-play that still can't break even. The probability distribution on that outcome is not favorable.

Third, the core business window may be finite. The 20%+ YoY growth in IMT is the real strategic asset. Competitors, consolidation, and market dynamics won't wait. Every dollar and every leadership hour spent trying to fix Offers is a dollar and hour not spent deepening the marketplace moat.

---

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- **Liquidation timeline and carrying costs:** How quickly can 7,000 homes be sold without further depressing prices in concentrated markets? What are monthly carrying costs per unit? This affects the total exit cost estimate, which currently spans a wide $300–500M range.

- **Contractual and workforce obligations:** What severance and contract wind-down costs attach to the 2,600+ Offers-related hires? This affects the true cost of exit vs. the $300–500M inventory write-down estimate.

- **IMT growth sustainability:** The 20% YoY growth rate on IMT — is this structural or pandemic/market-heat driven? If IMT growth decelerates significantly as housing cools, the "exceptional core business" argument weakens.

- **Competitor iBuying trajectory:** If Opendoor achieves consistent breakeven within 12–18 months, the exit decision looks worse in hindsight. What is Opendoor's current unit economics trajectory?

- **Regulatory and reputational exposure:** If the pause is perceived as concealment (pausing for 'labor constraints' when the real issue is pricing failure), what is the litigation and SEC risk exposure? Does exit reduce or increase this exposure?

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**Recommendation is decision-ready with the following conditions:**

| Condition | Status |
|-----------|--------|
| Core thesis (pricing gap is structural) | Supported by evidence — two failed pivots, 6.9% error rate vs. sub-2% requirement |
| Exit cost estimate | Partially supported — $300–500M range is wide; requires CFO-validated liquidation model before board vote |
| IMT baseline quality | Supported — $490M, 30% EBITDA, 20% growth is verifiable from filings |
| Reform alternative disproven | Supported directionally — Opendoor benchmark is compelling; requires deeper Opendoor unit economics review |
| Workforce/contract wind-down costs | **Gap** — requires HR and legal input before final board resolution |

**Pass/Fail verdict:** Ready to proceed to board resolution pending CFO liquidation model and HR wind-down cost estimate. Core strategic logic is sound and defensible without those inputs; they affect execution sequencing, not the directional decision.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Exit Sequencing and Communication Plan**

The strategic decision to exit is separable from the execution problem of how to exit without triggering a market panic that further depresses the value of 7,000 homes Zillow needs to sell.

Next artifact should address:
1. **Inventory liquidation strategy** — pace of sell-down, geographic prioritization, wholesale vs. retail channel mix
2. **Public disclosure framing** — how to characterize the exit as strategic portfolio focus rather than a failed experiment (true, but sequencing of narrative matters)
3. **Workforce transition plan** — retention of key IMT talent, severance structure for Offers-specific hires
4. **IMT reinvestment thesis** — specific articulation of where exit capital and headcount redeploy (mortgage origination? rentals? agent tools?)
5. **Board resolution language** — formal motion language for the October vote

---

## Conclusion

Zillow Offers was a bold, coherent thesis that the evidence has refuted. The Zestimate does not provide a pricing edge sufficient for iBuying economics. Two algorithm pivots confirmed this. The core IMT business — which Offers was meant to enhance — is growing faster and at far higher margins without it.

Exit is not a retreat. It is the correct capital allocation decision: concentrate resources on the 30%-EBITDA-margin, 20%-growth platform that Zillow actually has a durable advantage in, and stop burning capital trying to win a category where the best-positioned pure-play operator cannot break even.

**Recommended action:** Authorize exit. Proceed to liquidation planning. Communicate proactively before Q4 earnings.
