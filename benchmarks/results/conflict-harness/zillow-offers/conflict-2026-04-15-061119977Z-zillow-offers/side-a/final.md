# Strategic Recommendation: Exit Zillow Offers

**To:** Zillow Board of Directors  
**From:** Strategy Advisor  
**Date:** October 2021  
**Re:** Zillow Offers — Recommended Exit

---

## Decision Frame

**Recommendation: Exit iBuying entirely. Wind down Zillow Offers in an orderly fashion, liquidate the ~7,000-home inventory at managed loss, and redeploy capital and leadership attention to the core IMT marketplace business.**

The central question is not whether iBuying as a category has merit — it does. The question is whether Zillow has demonstrated a structural advantage in it, and whether a plausible reform path changes that answer within a tolerable risk window. The evidence says no on both counts.

Zillow's original thesis rested on a single premise: the Zestimate gives Zillow a pricing edge that pure-play iBuyers lack. The available evidence does not support that premise at the scale and operating model Zillow actually ran. The national consumer-facing Zestimate carries a 6.9% median error rate. The home flipping industry operates on 2–5% gross margins, which sets a rough ceiling on tolerable acquisition error — and the packet evidence suggests Zillow's acquisition model in practice has been systematically overpaying, generating a ~7,000-home backlog with estimated write-down exposure of $300–500M after two 2021 algorithm pivots. The gap between the pricing accuracy the business requires and what the model has delivered is not a one-quarter anomaly; it has persisted through two attempted corrections under real operating conditions.

The reform path asks the board to bet that a third operational pivot succeeds where two failed, in a housing market that is beginning to cool, against competitors who have been optimizing this problem longer and more narrowly. That is not a bet worth taking when the alternative — the core IMT business — is generating $490M in quarterly revenue at ~30% EBITDA margins and growing 20%+ year-over-year.

**The decision frame: Offers is a capital-intensive, margin-destroying distraction from an exceptional compounding business. Exit is not defeat — it is correct capital reallocation.**

---

## Core Argument

### 1. Acquisition Model Performance Has Failed Under Real Operating Conditions — Twice

The bull case for Zillow Offers depended on Zestimate superiority translating into acquisition pricing accuracy. The available evidence does not confirm that translation occurred. The 6.9% figure is the national consumer-facing Zestimate error rate; we do not have direct visibility into the acquisition model's accuracy in the specific markets and buy-boxes Zillow ran. What we do have is outcome evidence: 9,680 homes purchased in Q3 2021, 3,032 sold, ~7,000 on the books at estimated write-down exposure of $300–500M. This is the output of a pricing model that has failed to clear its economic hurdle under actual operating conditions.

More critically: Zillow already recognized this and pivoted the algorithm twice in 2021. Both pivots were attempted under real market conditions, in the actual geographies and price bands Zillow was operating. The backlog persisted and widened through both corrections. That is not proof that every conceivable reform is impossible — but it is strong operational evidence that the problem is not a simple calibration issue resolvable on a reasonable timeline.

A reform advocate must now explain not just that reform is theoretically possible (it is), but that Zillow can succeed on its third attempt after two failures, in a deteriorating market environment, faster than the inventory exposure compounds. The burden of proof on that argument is high, and the available evidence does not meet it.

### 2. The Core Business Is Being Actively Harmed

The IMT segment — Zillow's marketplace advertising and lead-gen business — earns ~30% EBITDA margins on $490M quarterly revenue, growing 20%+ YoY. This is a compounding high-margin platform business.

Offers is 70% of total revenue but a margin drag. More importantly, it is consuming organizational bandwidth, leadership attention, and board credibility that the core business does not need consumed. Headcount grew from 5,400 to 8,000+ in two years — largely Offers-related. Every quarter Offers continues, it obscures IMT's strength in earnings reports, depresses the stock (down from $200 to $85), and ties up capital that could fund marketplace growth or return to shareholders.

### 3. Reform Requires Outperforming Opendoor on Their Core Competency

The reform path — tighter geography, lower volume, tighter buy-boxes — is not abstractly novel. It describes something close to what Opendoor actually does. Opendoor operates in narrower geographies with a simpler operational structure, and it still cannot consistently reach breakeven. If the most optimized pure-play iBuyer cannot consistently break even operating iBuying as its primary mission, the argument that Zillow can outperform it while running iBuying as a secondary business attached to an ad marketplace requires strong positive evidence. None exists in the available data.

### 4. Market Timing Amplifies Exit Logic

Housing prices rose 19.5% YoY (Case-Shiller, August 2021) — a pace almost universally recognized as unsustainable. The market is showing early signs of cooling. Zillow already holds ~7,000 homes purchased at peak or near-peak prices. A reform path that continues acquisitions — even at reduced volume — adds inventory exposure into a potentially declining market. The asymmetric downside risk of continuing is severe. Exiting now allows Zillow to liquidate current inventory in a market that, while cooling, has not yet corrected.

---

## Strongest Counterargument: Reform Preserves Option Value and the Evidence Bar Is Not Met for 'Structural Impossibility'

The reform camp makes two valid points.

First, the critique of claim-1 is partly correct: the visible evidence does not prove that iBuying with tighter geography, narrower buy-boxes, and a purpose-built acquisition model (distinct from the consumer Zestimate) is structurally impossible for Zillow. Two pivots under prior operating choices failing does not prove all possible reform configurations fail.

Second, exiting permanently forfeits Zillow's ability to participate in the transactional real estate market. A smaller, geographically concentrated version of Offers could — in principle — achieve breakeven while maintaining the strategic option.

**Why exit is still correct despite these valid points:**

The reform argument is theoretically coherent but operationally underspecified. "Narrower geography, tighter buy-boxes" is a description of Opendoor's model — and Opendoor can't consistently break even. To claim Zillow can succeed at this, the reform advocate needs to specify what Zillow does differently than Opendoor and why that difference closes the economics gap. The available evidence contains no such specification.

Meanwhile, the cost of maintaining the option — continued capital burn, inventory risk in a cooling market, organizational distraction — is real and immediate. Zillow can exit now and re-enter iBuying in three years if the category matures or the pricing problem becomes solvable via better local ML or market structure changes. The option is not permanently foreclosed by exit; it is paused at a lower carrying cost.

The exit recommendation does not require proving reform is structurally impossible. It requires only that the probability-weighted expected value of reform is lower than the probability-weighted expected value of exit and IMT reinvestment — which it is, given two failed pivots, a cooling market, a widening backlog, and no specified mechanism by which Zillow outperforms Opendoor.

---

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- **Acquisition model error rate (not consumer Zestimate):** The 6.9% figure is the national consumer-facing Zestimate. The actual acquisition model's error rate in Zillow's operating markets and buy-boxes is the more precise figure for this analysis. This gap weakens the precision of the pricing-accuracy argument but does not change the directional conclusion — outcome evidence (7,000 underwater homes after two pivots) is more reliable than model error rate estimates anyway.

- **Liquidation timeline and carrying costs:** How quickly can 7,000 homes be sold without further depressing prices in concentrated markets? What are monthly carrying costs per unit? This affects the total exit cost estimate, which currently spans a wide $300–500M range.

- **Contractual and workforce obligations:** What severance and contract wind-down costs attach to the 2,600+ Offers-related hires? This affects the true cost of exit vs. the inventory write-down estimate.

- **IMT growth sustainability:** The 20% YoY growth rate on IMT — is this structural or pandemic/market-heat driven? If IMT growth decelerates significantly as housing cools, the "exceptional core business" argument weakens.

- **Opendoor unit economics trajectory:** If Opendoor achieves consistent breakeven within 12–18 months, the exit decision looks worse in hindsight.

- **Regulatory and reputational exposure:** If the pause is perceived as concealment, what is the litigation and SEC risk exposure? Does exit reduce or increase this?

**Pass/Fail verdict:** Ready to proceed to board resolution pending CFO liquidation model and HR wind-down cost estimate. The precision of the pricing-accuracy claim is narrowed by the critique, but the exit recommendation rests on converging lines of evidence — outcome evidence from two failed pivots, the Opendoor benchmark, market timing — that collectively support the decision without requiring the strong 'structural impossibility' framing.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Exit Sequencing and Communication Plan**

The strategic decision to exit is separable from the execution problem of how to exit without triggering a market panic that further depresses the value of 7,000 homes Zillow needs to sell.

Next artifact should address:
1. **Inventory liquidation strategy** — pace of sell-down, geographic prioritization, wholesale vs. retail channel mix
2. **Public disclosure framing** — how to characterize the exit as strategic portfolio focus rather than failed experiment
3. **Workforce transition plan** — retention of key IMT talent, severance structure for Offers-specific hires
4. **IMT reinvestment thesis** — specific articulation of where exit capital and headcount redeploy (mortgage origination? rentals? agent tools?)
5. **Board resolution language** — formal motion language for the October vote

---

## Conclusion

Zillow Offers was a bold, coherent thesis that the evidence has refuted in practice. The acquisition model — whatever its relationship to the consumer-facing Zestimate — has produced 7,000 underwater homes after two correction attempts, in a market that is beginning to cool. The core IMT business is growing faster and at far higher margins without it.

Exit does not require proving reform is structurally impossible. It requires only that the expected value of continued operation — with its capital burn, inventory risk, and organizational cost — is lower than the expected value of exit and IMT reinvestment. That condition is met.

**Recommended action:** Authorize exit. Proceed to liquidation planning. Communicate proactively before Q4 earnings.
