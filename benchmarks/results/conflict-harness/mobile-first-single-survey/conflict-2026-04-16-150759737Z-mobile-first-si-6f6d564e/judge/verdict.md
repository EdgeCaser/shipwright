## Dimension Rationales
- Claim Quality: Both sides made sound core claims that the current evidence does not justify a 6-month redesign. Side B was slightly stronger because it paired that claim with a clearer operating alternative and explicit decision criteria.
- Evidence Discipline: Both sides were appropriately skeptical of the single-survey evidence base. Side A showed stronger evidence caution overall, but its high stated confidence still slightly overreaches given the sparse record; Side B’s medium confidence is better calibrated.
- Responsiveness To Critique: Side A fixed the criticized overstatement by reframing power-user bias as a hypothesis. Side B responded more decisively by converting a vague gate into quantified thresholds and a bounded timeline, materially improving the artifact.
- Internal Consistency: Both artifacts are coherent and mostly contradiction-free. Side A is consistent but mixes strong rejection language with high confidence on a sparse record; Side B is more tightly aligned between its uncertainty, plan, and stated confidence.
- Decision Usefulness: Side B better supports an actual decision because it defines a time-bounded program and measurable go/no-go thresholds. Side A is strong diagnostically, but its gate is less operationalized and more audit-like than decision-driving.

## Side Summaries
### Side A Strengths
- Strong evidence discipline and a clear articulation of why the current survey signal is insufficient for a major redesign commitment.

### Side A Weaknesses
- Less decision-operational than Side B, and its high conclusion confidence is somewhat too strong for the thin evidence base.

### Side B Strengths
- Most decision-useful artifact because it turns the recommendation into a bounded program with explicit redesign thresholds.

### Side B Weaknesses
- Some quantitative thresholds appear reasoned but not grounded in case-specific evidence, so parts of the gate still rest on judgment.

- Decisive Dimension: decision_usefulness

## Decisive Findings
- Side B converted critique into a quantified redesign gate with explicit reach, severity, persistence, and business-case thresholds.
- Side B added an 8-10 week decision window, reducing the risk of indefinite discovery.
- Side A remained strong on evidence caution but offered a less fully operational decision rule.

## Rationale
Both sides correctly refused to authorize a full mobile-first redesign on the basis of a single NPS survey of 50 power users. Side A was more rigorous in surfacing evidence gaps and in avoiding unsupported factual claims after critique, but Side B produced the more decision-ready final artifact by translating the same skepticism into a bounded validation plan with explicit thresholds and a clearer go/no-go mechanism. The margin is moderate rather than large because Side B’s thresholds are still partly judgmental rather than empirically anchored, and Side A remained very strong on evidence discipline.
