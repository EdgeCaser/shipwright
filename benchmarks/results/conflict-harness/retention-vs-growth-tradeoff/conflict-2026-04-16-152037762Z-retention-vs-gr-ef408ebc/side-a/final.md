# Roadmap Prioritization Memo: Retention vs. Growth Investment
**To:** Leadership / Roadmap Committee  
**Re:** Q3–Q4 Investment Allocation Under Series B Positioning  
**Status:** Decision-Ready Draft

---

## Decision Frame

**The core tension:** Internal performance data supports retention investment as the higher-ROI path. The upcoming Series B raise creates organizational pressure toward growth-metric optics. These two imperatives are in direct conflict on the surface — but whether the conflict is fundamental or a framing problem depends on a variable this packet cannot resolve: investor archetype.

**What must be decided:**
1. Whether to allocate the next investment cycle toward retention infrastructure, growth acquisition, or a split
2. Whether to present the Series B narrative around top-line growth velocity or unit economics durability
3. Whether to accept short-term growth metric softness in exchange for a stronger underlying business

**What the data says:**
- Retention investment generates higher ROI vs. new acquisition spend (internal modeling, ctx-1)
- CAC payback period is materially shorter when churn is suppressed — meaning each retained customer compounds rather than requiring replacement (ctx-1)
- Growth-first investment without solving retention is a leaky-bucket dynamic: headcount and spend scale without proportional LTV improvement

**What leadership wants:**
- Headline metrics that satisfy Series B investor pattern-matching: MoM growth rate, new logo count, top-line ARR expansion
- A narrative of momentum, not consolidation

**The conditional reframe:** NRR >100% is a recognized Series B signal in SaaS — it tells investors the installed base grows without incremental CAC. Whether an NRR-led narrative is *sufficient or preferred* in this specific raise depends entirely on the target investor mix. This packet does not include investor profiling data, so the reframe is a hypothesis that requires validation, not an established path. If target investors are growth-at-all-costs funds, the conflict is real and cannot be framed away. If target investors are durability-focused, the conflict may be resolvable through narrative.

**Decision options:**

| Option | Investment Split | Series B Story | Risk |
|--------|----------------|----------------|------|
| A: Retention-first | 70% retention / 30% growth | NRR + LTV/CAC ratio, unit economics durability | Growth velocity looks soft; only viable if investor archetype confirmed |
| B: Growth-first | 30% retention / 70% growth | New logos, ARR expansion, top-line velocity | Churn accelerates; NRR deteriorates; burn increases |
| C: Parallel track (default) | 50/50 | Mixed metrics, no clear story | Neither story is compelling; execution spread thin |
| D: Sequenced narrative | Retention Q3, growth Q4 | "We fixed the foundation, now we're scaling" | Requires investor appetite for a two-act story |

**Recommended option: Determine investor archetype first, then commit to A or B.** The internal data favors retention ROI, but the fundraising calculus cannot be completed without investor profiling. Proceeding to roadmap lock before this is resolved defaults to Option C by inertia — the worst outcome.

---

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

**What we don't know that could change this recommendation:**

1. **Investor composition of the Series B.** Whether the NRR-led narrative is viable depends on whether target investors weight unit economics over velocity. This is the highest-priority unknown — it determines whether the data conflict is resolvable through framing or must be resolved through actual investment allocation. *Gap: no investor profiling data in this packet.*

2. **Retention ROI model assumptions.** The claim that retention has higher ROI depends on the discount rate applied, the churn cohort being targeted, and whether the intervention cost is fully loaded. If the model uses optimistic retention lift estimates, the ROI case weakens. *Gap: model inputs not surfaced for review.*

3. **Competitive window on growth.** If a competitor is actively taking market share in a land-grab window, the cost of delaying acquisition spend is higher than the model captures. *Gap: no competitive velocity data cited.*

4. **Board composition and existing investor signals.** If existing investors have already signaled expectations for a growth story, pivoting to NRR framing without pre-socializing is a relationship risk. *Gap: no board alignment data.*

5. **Product retention levers available.** "Invest in retention" is not a single intervention — it could mean onboarding, feature depth, pricing, success headcount, or product fixes. Without knowing which lever is being pulled, ROI estimates are fragile. *Gap: retention investment has not been scoped.*

**Confidence calibration:** Medium-low. The directional preference for retention (Option A) holds on operating data alone, but whether it is the right *fundraising* decision is unresolvable without investor archetype data — which is currently absent from the packet.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**This memo is decision-ready IF:**
- ✅ Target investor archetypes have been profiled — specifically whether they weight NRR/unit economics or top-line growth velocity at Series B
- ✅ The retention investment has been scoped to specific interventions with defensible lift estimates
- ✅ Board pre-alignment on narrative shift has been completed or scheduled
- ✅ Leadership has explicitly committed to a Series B narrative before roadmap lock

**This memo is NOT decision-ready IF:**
- ❌ No investor profiling has been done — without this, Option A's viability as a fundraising strategy is unestablished regardless of the operating data
- ❌ Retention ROI model has not been stress-tested against pessimistic assumptions
- ❌ Leadership has not explicitly committed to a Series B narrative — without this, Option C becomes the default by inertia, which is the worst outcome
- ❌ No timeline constraint on the raise has been established — urgency calibrates how much narrative risk is tolerable

**Blocking question that must be answered before roadmap lock:**  
*"What is the investor archetype of our Series B targets, and will they accept NRR/unit economics framing in place of top-line growth velocity?"*  
This is a capital-market question, not a data question. Until it is answered, the memo's recommendation cannot be finalized. If the answer is that target investors require growth velocity, Option B is the honest choice — but its downstream operating risks should be documented and owned explicitly by leadership.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Immediate (before roadmap lock):**  
Produce an **Investor Archetype Profile** — identify the 5–8 specific funds being targeted for Series B, document their portfolio composition, their stated investment theses at growth stage, and their historical pattern on NRR vs. velocity weighting. This artifact unlocks the framing decision and determines whether Option A or Option B is the correct recommendation. Without it, no strategy memo can close.

**Secondary (to validate retention ROI claim):**  
Commission a **Retention ROI Sensitivity Model** — stress-test the retention investment case against three scenarios (base, pessimistic lift, accelerated competitive entry) to establish the confidence interval on the ROI advantage. Without this, the data claim that "retention has higher ROI" is a point estimate being used to justify a major allocation decision.

**If investor profiling confirms NRR-led narrative is viable:**  
Produce a **Series B Narrative Options Brief** — a one-page document presenting two investor-facing stories (NRR-led vs. growth-led) with the specific metrics each story leads with, the assumptions each story requires the business to hit, and the investor archetype each story targets.

**If Option A is chosen following investor profiling:**  
Produce a **Retention Investment Scope Document** — enumerate the specific interventions (onboarding, in-product, CS coverage, pricing), their costs, their expected lift mechanisms, and their measurement plan. This converts the high-level allocation decision into an executable roadmap.
