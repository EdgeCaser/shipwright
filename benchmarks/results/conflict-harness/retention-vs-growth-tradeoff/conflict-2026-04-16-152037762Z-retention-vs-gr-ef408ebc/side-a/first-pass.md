# Roadmap Prioritization Memo: Retention vs. Growth Investment
**To:** Leadership / Roadmap Committee  
**Re:** Q3–Q4 Investment Allocation Under Series B Positioning  
**Status:** Decision-Ready Draft

---

## Decision Frame

**The core tension:** Internal performance data supports retention investment as the higher-ROI path. The upcoming Series B raise creates organizational pressure toward growth-metric optics. These two imperatives are in direct conflict on the surface — but the conflict is partly strategic framing, not purely data.

**What must be decided:**
1. Whether to allocate the next investment cycle toward retention infrastructure, growth acquisition, or a split
2. Whether to present the Series B narrative around top-line growth velocity or unit economics durability
3. Whether to accept short-term growth metric softness in exchange for a stronger underlying business

**What the data says:**
- Retention investment generates higher ROI vs. new acquisition spend (internal modeling, ctx-1)
- CAC payback period is materially shorter when churn is suppressed — meaning each retained customer compounds rather than requiring replacement (ctx-1)
- Growth-first investment without solving retention is a leaky-bucket dynamic: headcount and spend scale without proportional LTV improvement

**What leadership wants:**
- Headline metrics that satisfy Series B investor pattern-matching: MoM growth rate, new logo count, top-line ARR expansion
- A narrative of momentum, not consolidation

**The reframe available:** Strong retention is a growth story if presented correctly. Net Revenue Retention (NRR) >100% is one of the most powerful Series B signals in SaaS — it tells investors the installed base grows without incremental CAC. Retention investment *produces* growth metrics; it doesn't compete with them. The question is whether leadership is willing to shift the narrative frame, not just the spend.

**Decision options:**

| Option | Investment Split | Series B Story | Risk |
|--------|----------------|----------------|------|
| A: Retention-first | 70% retention / 30% growth | NRR + LTV/CAC ratio, unit economics durability | Growth velocity looks soft in short term |
| B: Growth-first | 30% retention / 70% growth | New logos, ARR expansion, top-line velocity | Churn accelerates; NRR deteriorates; burn increases |
| C: Parallel track (default) | 50/50 | Mixed metrics, no clear story | Neither story is compelling; execution spread thin |
| D: Sequenced narrative | Retention Q3, growth Q4 | "We fixed the foundation, now we're scaling" | Requires investor appetite for a two-act story |

**Recommended option: A with reframe to NRR-led Series B narrative.** The data supports it; the risk is a leadership alignment problem, not an analytical one.

---

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

**What we don't know that could change this recommendation:**

1. **Investor composition of the Series B.** If target investors are growth-at-all-costs funds (Tiger-era pattern), NRR framing may underperform. If target investors are durability-focused (Bessemer, Benchmark, Sequoia growth-stage), retention story is directly preferred. *Gap: no investor profiling data in this packet.*

2. **Retention ROI model assumptions.** The claim that retention has higher ROI depends on the discount rate applied, the churn cohort being targeted, and whether the intervention cost is fully loaded. If the model uses optimistic retention lift estimates, the ROI case weakens. *Gap: model inputs not surfaced for review.*

3. **Competitive window on growth.** If a competitor is actively taking market share in a land-grab window, the cost of delaying acquisition spend is higher than the model captures. *Gap: no competitive velocity data cited.*

4. **Board composition and existing investor signals.** If existing investors have already signaled expectations for a growth story, pivoting to NRR framing without pre-socializing is a relationship risk. *Gap: no board alignment data.*

5. **Product retention levers available.** "Invest in retention" is not a single intervention — it could mean onboarding, feature depth, pricing, success headcount, or product fixes. Without knowing which lever is being pulled, ROI estimates are fragile. *Gap: retention investment has not been scoped.*

**Confidence calibration:** Medium. The directional recommendation (Option A) holds under most plausible scenarios, but the magnitude of advantage over Option B is sensitive to unknowns 1 and 3 above.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**This memo is decision-ready IF:**
- ✅ Leadership accepts that the data conflict is a framing problem, not a fundamental strategic contradiction
- ✅ The retention investment has been scoped to specific interventions with defensible lift estimates
- ✅ Investor targets have been profiled for NRR vs. growth preference
- ✅ Board pre-alignment on narrative shift has been completed or scheduled

**This memo is NOT decision-ready IF:**
- ❌ Retention ROI model has not been stress-tested against pessimistic assumptions
- ❌ Leadership has not explicitly committed to a Series B narrative (NRR-led vs. growth-led) — without this, Option C becomes the default by inertia, which is the worst outcome
- ❌ No timeline constraint on the raise has been established — urgency calibrates how much narrative risk is tolerable

**Blocking question that must be answered before roadmap lock:**  
*"Is leadership willing to present a Series B narrative centered on NRR and unit economics durability, even if top-line growth rate is softer than benchmark?"*  
This is a leadership alignment decision, not a data decision. The memo cannot resolve it. If the answer is no, Option B is the honest choice — but its downstream risks should be documented and owned explicitly.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Immediate (before roadmap lock):**  
Produce a **Series B Narrative Options Brief** — a one-page document presenting two investor-facing stories (NRR-led vs. growth-led) with the specific metrics each story leads with, the assumptions each story requires the business to hit, and the investor archetype each story targets. This artifact forces the framing decision out of the abstract and into concrete investor language, making leadership alignment faster and less ambiguous.

**Secondary (to validate retention ROI claim):**  
Commission a **Retention ROI Sensitivity Model** — stress-test the retention investment case against three scenarios (base, pessimistic lift, accelerated competitive entry) to establish the confidence interval on the ROI advantage. Without this, the data claim that "retention has higher ROI" is a point estimate being used to justify a major allocation decision.

**If Option A is chosen:**  
Produce a **Retention Investment Scope Document** — enumerate the specific interventions (onboarding, in-product, CS coverage, pricing), their costs, their expected lift mechanisms, and their measurement plan. This converts the high-level allocation decision into an executable roadmap.
