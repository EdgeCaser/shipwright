# Roadmap Prioritization Memo

## Recommendation
Prioritize retention work as the core roadmap investment for the next planning window, and support the financing narrative with a narrow set of growth initiatives that are explicitly framed as proof points rather than the main spend. When operating evidence and fundraising optics conflict, the operating plan should follow the stronger unit-economics signal and the external narrative should be built around disciplined capital allocation, improving efficiency, and a credible path to durable growth.

## Why This Is The Right Call
The available scenario facts state that retention investment has higher ROI and lower CAC payback than growth investment. That means retention is the better use of scarce roadmap capacity if the goal is enterprise value creation rather than cosmetic metric management. Reversing that prioritization to optimize for a near-term fundraising story creates two risks: weaker real performance if the growth push is inefficient, and lower credibility if investors discover the underlying economics favor a different plan.

The correct compromise is not to ignore fundraising pressure. It is to treat fundraising as a packaging problem, not as permission to override the better operating decision. Leadership can still show growth momentum by selecting a small number of high-visibility growth bets with short measurement cycles, while preserving most capacity for retention programs that improve revenue durability, expansion, and payback efficiency.

## Proposed Allocation
Allocate the majority of product and go-to-market investment to retention and expansion drivers.
Allocate a smaller, tightly bounded tranche to growth experiments that can produce credible investor-facing signals within the raise window.
Define in advance that growth work must clear explicit efficiency thresholds to earn more capacity.
Use board and investor messaging that connects retention gains to stronger LTV, better payback, more efficient growth, and lower financing risk.

## Decision Frame
The decision is whether roadmap priority should follow the stronger operating economics or the preferred fundraising narrative. The recommendation is to let operating evidence determine the roadmap, while shaping the raise narrative around efficiency-led growth rather than top-line optics alone.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
The scenario does not specify the exact fundraising timeline, magnitude of retention ROI advantage, or how quickly retention work can convert into metrics visible to investors.
The scenario also does not define which growth metrics leadership believes matter most in the Series B process, or whether there are specific investor expectations that cannot be met through an efficiency narrative.
The highest-value missing evidence is a side-by-side 2-quarter forecast comparing a retention-led plan versus a growth-led plan on ARR, net revenue retention, burn multiple, CAC payback, and fundraising story strength.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if the company can show that retention-led investment improves near-term business quality and still leaves enough room for a few measurable growth proof points before the raise.
Pass if leadership agrees to stage-gate growth work instead of funding it on narrative preference alone.
Fail if the raise depends on a specific top-line growth threshold that cannot plausibly be supported by retention gains plus limited growth experiments.
Fail if the team cannot translate retention impact into investor-legible metrics and messaging.

## Recommended Next Artifact
Build a board-ready decision memo with a quantified scenario model: retention-led, growth-led, and blended-plan cases; expected KPI outcomes over the next two quarters; investment levels by function; and the investor narrative for each option. That artifact should end with a recommended allocation and explicit kill/scale criteria for the growth experiments.
