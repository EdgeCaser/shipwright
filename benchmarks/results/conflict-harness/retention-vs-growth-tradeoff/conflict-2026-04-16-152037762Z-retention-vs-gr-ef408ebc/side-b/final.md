# Revised artifact

## Recommendation
Prioritize a time-boxed blended roadmap, not a blanket retention-first plan. Put the majority of capacity behind retention only if the selected retention initiatives can produce investor-legible KPI movement inside the Series B window; otherwise shift near-term capacity toward growth proofs while preserving the highest-confidence retention foundations already in flight. The operating plan should still follow economics, but only after normalizing those economics to the actual 90-to-180-day decision window relevant to the raise.

## Why This Is The Right Call
The scenario establishes that retention investment has higher ROI and lower CAC payback than growth investment, which is strong evidence that retention is the better default use of capital. But that evidence does not fully settle roadmap priority for a company under active Series B pressure, because fundraising is sensitive to what metrics can move visibly within the next one to two quarters.

That creates a temporal test: if retention work can credibly improve investor-visible indicators such as gross retention, net revenue retention, expansion, logo churn, or payback efficiency during the raise window, then retention should remain the core roadmap priority and the financing narrative should emphasize efficient growth. If those gains are likely to land after the raise window, leadership should not ignore that lag; it should run a deliberately bounded growth layer to generate near-term proof points while avoiding a full override of the stronger long-run economics.

The practical recommendation is therefore a staged allocation rather than a doctrinaire answer. Use retention as the default strategic posture, but require every major retention initiative to clear a raise-window visibility test. Fund only the growth work most likely to create credible near-term signals, and pre-commit to efficiency thresholds before expanding that spend.

## Proposed Allocation
Allocate roadmap capacity in two buckets: raise-window-visible retention work and tightly bounded growth experiments.
Prioritize retention initiatives only when they can plausibly move KPI evidence within the fundraising period.
Use growth initiatives as proof points, not as the governing logic for the roadmap.
Require explicit scale-or-stop thresholds for growth bets based on payback, conversion efficiency, or pipeline quality.
Translate retention progress into investor-legible metrics and narrative, rather than assuming investors will infer the value automatically.

## Decision Frame
The decision is not simply retention versus growth. It is whether roadmap priority should follow the higher-efficiency investment when leadership also faces a fixed fundraising clock. The recommendation is to prioritize initiatives by economics within the actual Series B measurement window: retain a retention-led strategy when retention gains will surface in time, and use a constrained blended plan when they will not.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
The scenario does not specify the exact Series B timeline or the investor meetings that define the relevant measurement window.
It does not quantify whether retention ROI superiority appears inside 90 to 180 days or only over a longer compounding horizon.
It does not identify which KPIs leadership believes investors must see move before the raise.
The highest-value missing evidence is a two-quarter scenario model showing when retention gains become visible versus when growth experiments could move top-line or pipeline metrics.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if the company can show that its top retention initiatives will generate investor-legible KPI movement within the raise window.
Pass if growth work is limited to a small set of experiments with explicit efficiency gates.
Fail if retention benefits are real but mostly back-loaded beyond the Series B process and leadership still treats them as near-term proof.
Fail if the team cannot define which metrics matter for the raise or cannot map roadmap work to those metrics on a dated timeline.

Build a board-ready timed impact memo comparing three cases over the next two quarters: retention-led, growth-led, and blended. For each case, show dated KPI movement, expected visibility to investors, resource allocation, burn and payback implications, and the specific narrative each plan supports. End with a recommendation tied to the actual fundraising calendar and explicit scale-or-stop rules for growth experiments.
