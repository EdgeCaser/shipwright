# Roadmap Prioritization Memo: Retention vs. Growth Investment

**To:** Leadership / Series B Prep Working Group  
**From:** Product & Strategy  
**Status:** Draft for alignment — not cleared for investor distribution

---

## Decision Frame

We face a structural conflict between what the internal data supports and what the fundraising narrative currently demands. These are not the same decision, and conflating them is the primary risk.

**What the data says:** Retention investment yields higher ROI and shorter CAC payback than equivalent growth spend. On a capital-efficiency basis, retention is the better allocation.

**What the Series B narrative demands:** Investors evaluating growth-stage companies at Series B typically weight top-line expansion, new logo velocity, and TAM penetration — metrics that retention investment does not directly move in the short term.

**The conflict stated plainly:** If we optimize the roadmap for the data, we may underperform on the metrics investors are watching. If we optimize for investor optics, we may allocate capital in ways our own analysis says are less efficient.

This memo does not resolve that conflict by choosing a side. It resolves it by forcing an explicit decision: **we must choose which master we are optimizing for, and be honest about the cost of that choice.**

Three paths exist:

1. **Prioritize retention (data-aligned).** Accept that Series B narrative will require reframing — arguing that retention-driven efficiency is the more durable growth model, and that CAC payback compression is a leading indicator of scalable growth.

2. **Prioritize growth spend (narrative-aligned).** Accept that we are temporarily allocating against our own ROI analysis to hit the metrics that close the round. This is a deliberate, time-bounded bet that the capital raised outweighs the efficiency cost.

3. **Split the roadmap (hybrid).** Dedicate a defined portion of engineering and marketing capacity to growth-metric movers, while protecting the highest-ROI retention investments. This avoids the binary but introduces execution risk from divided focus.

Path 1 is intellectually honest but requires leadership to commit to a harder investor conversation. Path 2 is tactically coherent but should be documented as a deliberate deviation from internal analysis — not rationalized away. Path 3 is the most common choice and the most likely to produce a muddled result if the split is not enforced with real resource commitments.

---

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

The following questions are unresolved and materially affect which path is correct:

**1. What do the target Series B investors actually weight?**  
The assumption that investors want growth metrics over efficiency is a prior, not a confirmed investor preference. Different funds at Series B have meaningfully different frameworks. Without term sheets or direct investor feedback, the premise that retention investment will hurt the raise is unverified.

**2. What is the retention ROI advantage, and over what time horizon?**  
The memo asserts retention has higher ROI and lower CAC payback, but the magnitude and time horizon of that advantage determine whether the hybrid path is viable. A small advantage argues for holding the data-aligned path. A large advantage argues that any deviation is a significant capital misallocation.

**3. What is the round timeline?**  
If the Series B close is 6–9 months out, there is time for retention investment to produce metrics that are investor-legible (net revenue retention, expansion ARR, logo retention rate). If the timeline is 90 days, retention investment produces no new signal before the raise closes.

**4. Is the current growth metric underperformance a retention problem in disguise?**  
Churn and expansion suppression can mask new logo growth in net metrics. If growth numbers are weak partly because retention is weak, then retention investment may also be the correct path to move the growth metrics leadership wants. This causal question is not answered.

**5. Who owns the investor narrative, and are they aligned?**  
If the finance or CEO function has already committed to a specific growth-metrics story in early investor conversations, the roadmap decision is partially constrained regardless of what the data shows.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

This memo is **ready to circulate for internal alignment** but is **not ready to drive roadmap execution** until the following gates are cleared:

| Gate | Status | Owner |
|---|---|---|
| Investor preference confirmed (target fund conversations) | Not done | CEO / BD |
| ROI delta quantified with time horizon specified | Not done | Finance / Analytics |
| Round timeline confirmed | Not done | CEO / Finance |
| Causal analysis: does churn suppress growth metrics? | Not done | Analytics |
| Narrative owner aligned on which path we are taking | Not done | CEO + Product |

**Failure mode if we skip these gates:** We pick Path 3 (hybrid) by default, underfund both priorities, and produce neither the efficiency gains the data supports nor the growth metrics the raise needs. This is the most common failure pattern in this scenario and should be named explicitly.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**A two-page investor narrative test document.**

Before finalizing the roadmap, the team should draft the retention-forward narrative (Path 1) and pressure-test it with one or two friendly investor contacts or advisors who have closed Series B rounds in comparable businesses. The goal is not to get permission — it is to get signal on whether the data-aligned story is actually a harder sell, or whether that assumption is doing work it has not earned.

If the friendly-investor test confirms that retention metrics are legible and valued at Series B, Path 1 is both data-aligned and raise-compatible, and the conflict dissolves. If the test confirms investor preference for growth metrics regardless of efficiency, leadership can make Path 2 as an informed, documented choice rather than an unexamined default.

The roadmap should not be locked until that document exists and has been reviewed.
