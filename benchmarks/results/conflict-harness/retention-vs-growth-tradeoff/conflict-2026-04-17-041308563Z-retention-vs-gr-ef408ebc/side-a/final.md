# Roadmap Prioritization Memo: Retention vs. Growth Investment

**To:** Leadership / Series B Prep Working Group  
**From:** Product & Strategy  
**Status:** Draft for alignment — not cleared for investor distribution

---

## Decision Frame

We face a stated conflict between what the internal data supports and what the fundraising narrative currently demands. Whether that conflict is real and irreconcilable — or whether it dissolves once investor preferences and causal relationships are better understood — is itself an open question that this memo surfaces rather than answers prematurely.

**What the data says:** Retention investment yields higher ROI and shorter CAC payback than equivalent growth spend. On a capital-efficiency basis, retention is the better allocation.

**What the Series B narrative is assumed to demand:** Investors evaluating growth-stage companies at Series B are often assumed to weight top-line expansion, new logo velocity, and TAM penetration — metrics that retention investment does not directly move in the short term. This assumption is a prior, not a confirmed investor position.

**The conflict, stated carefully:** If the assumption holds — that target investors require growth metrics and will not credit retention efficiency — then optimizing the roadmap for the data may produce a harder fundraising conversation. If the assumption does not hold, or if retention investment also moves the growth metrics that matter, the conflict may be smaller than it appears.

This memo does not resolve the conflict by choosing a side before the open questions below are answered. It names the decision structure so that leadership can make an informed, documented choice rather than drifting into a default allocation.

Three paths exist:

1. **Prioritize retention (data-aligned).** Accept that the Series B narrative will require reframing — arguing that retention-driven efficiency is a more durable growth model and that CAC payback compression is a leading indicator of scalable growth. This path is viable if investor preferences are confirmed to credit these metrics.

2. **Prioritize growth spend (narrative-aligned).** Accept a temporary deviation from the internal ROI analysis to hit metrics that close the round. This is a deliberate, time-bounded bet that should be documented as such — not rationalized as consistent with the data.

3. **Split the roadmap (hybrid).** Dedicate a defined portion of capacity to growth-metric movers while protecting the highest-ROI retention investments. This avoids the binary but introduces execution risk from divided focus, and is the most likely path to a muddled outcome if the split is not enforced with real resource commitments.

The correct path depends on answers to the questions in the next section. Locking a path before those answers exist is the primary risk to avoid.

---

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

The following questions are unresolved and materially affect whether the conflict is real and which path is correct:

**1. What do the target Series B investors actually weight?**  
The assumption that investors want growth metrics over efficiency is a prior, not a confirmed investor preference. Different funds at Series B have meaningfully different frameworks. Without direct investor feedback, the premise that retention investment will hurt the raise is unverified — and if it is wrong, Path 1 may be both data-aligned and raise-compatible simultaneously.

**2. What is the retention ROI advantage, and over what time horizon?**  
The magnitude and time horizon of the advantage determine whether any hybrid split is viable. A small advantage argues for holding the data-aligned path with minor concessions. A large advantage argues that any deviation is a significant capital misallocation that must be explicitly authorized.

**3. What is the round timeline?**  
If the Series B close is 6–9 months out, retention investment has time to produce metrics that are investor-legible (net revenue retention, expansion ARR, logo retention rate). If the timeline is 90 days, retention investment produces no new signal before the raise closes, and the calculus changes substantially.

**4. Is the current growth metric underperformance a retention problem in disguise?**  
Churn and expansion suppression can mask new logo growth in net metrics. If growth numbers are weak partly because retention is weak, then retention investment may also be the correct lever to move the growth metrics leadership wants — dissolving the conflict rather than forcing a choice between them.

**5. Who owns the investor narrative, and are they aligned?**  
If the CEO or finance function has already committed to a specific growth-metrics story in early investor conversations, the roadmap decision is partially constrained regardless of what the data shows.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

This memo is **ready to circulate for internal alignment** but is **not ready to drive roadmap execution** until the following gates are cleared:

| Gate | Status | Owner |
|---|---|---|
| Investor preference confirmed (target fund conversations) | Not done | CEO / BD |
| ROI delta quantified with time horizon specified | Not done | Finance / Analytics |
| Round timeline confirmed | Not done | CEO / Finance |
| Causal analysis: does churn suppress growth metrics? | Not done | Analytics |
| Narrative owner aligned on which path we are taking | Not done | CEO + Product |

**Failure mode if we skip these gates:** We default to Path 3 (hybrid), underfund both priorities, and produce neither the efficiency gains the data supports nor the growth metrics the raise needs. This is the most common failure pattern in this scenario and should be named explicitly before the roadmap is set.

---

## Recommended Next Artifact

**A two-page investor narrative test document.**

Before finalizing the roadmap, the team should draft the retention-forward narrative (Path 1) and pressure-test it with one or two friendly investor contacts or advisors who have closed Series B rounds in comparable businesses. The goal is not to get permission — it is to get signal on whether the conflict is real.

If the test confirms that retention metrics are legible and valued at Series B, Path 1 is both data-aligned and raise-compatible, and the conflict dissolves. If the test confirms investor preference for growth metrics regardless of efficiency, leadership can choose Path 2 as an informed, documented decision rather than an unexamined default.

The roadmap should not be locked until that document exists and has been reviewed.
