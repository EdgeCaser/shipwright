# Roadmap Prioritization Memo

## Recommendation
Prioritize growth-visible roadmap work for the upcoming Series B window, but constrain it with explicit retention guardrails and a time-bounded review point. The core issue is not that retention appears unimportant; it is that the financing event creates a near-term narrative requirement that leadership is explicitly optimizing for. In this scenario, roadmap choice should reflect the immediate company objective while limiting the downside of deprioritizing the higher-ROI path.

## Rationale
The case states that internal data favors retention investment, while leadership wants growth metrics for the raise and the data and narrative are in direct conflict. When a company faces that kind of conflict, the roadmap decision is not purely an efficiency decision; it is a sequencing decision tied to the company’s current constraint. If the raise depends on demonstrating growth momentum, then roadmap work that sharpens that story can be justified for a defined period, provided leadership acknowledges that this is a narrative-driven allocation rather than the highest-proven ROI allocation.

That means the right move is not to deny the retention evidence, but to make the tradeoff explicit: choose growth-oriented work now because it serves the financing objective, and set clear conditions for when the roadmap reverts to the stronger retention case if the growth push does not materially improve fundraising readiness.

## Decision Frame
The decision is whether roadmap prioritization should follow the stronger internal ROI signal or the near-term fundraising narrative need. Recommendation: follow the fundraising need for the next planning window, with explicit executive acknowledgment that this is a temporary override of the internally favored retention path.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
The packet provides no underlying evidence beyond the scenario statement, so the key unresolved questions are operational rather than analytical. It is unclear which specific growth metrics matter most for the raise, how quickly roadmap changes could affect those metrics, what minimum retention protection is required to avoid damaging the business during the fundraising period, and what decision checkpoint leadership will use to end the temporary growth-first posture.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if leadership agrees to all of the following: the growth-first choice is temporary, the retention tradeoff is explicitly documented, a review date is set, and retention deterioration triggers re-prioritization. Fail if leadership wants a growth-first roadmap while refusing to define guardrails, review timing, or conditions for returning to the retention-led plan.
