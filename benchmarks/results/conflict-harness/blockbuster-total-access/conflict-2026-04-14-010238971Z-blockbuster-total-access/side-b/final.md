# Strategic Recommendation Memo: Restructure Total Access, Do Not Fund It Unchanged

## Recommendation

Blockbuster should not continue Total Access at current subsidy levels. The board should keep the hybrid online-plus-store strategy, but immediately restructure it around capped, priced, or tiered in-store exchange economics.

This is a scale-back recommendation, not a retreat to stores. Total Access is Blockbuster's only proven competitive lever against Netflix: it added roughly 2M subscribers in under 12 months, pushed Blockbuster online subscribers to about 3M, stalled Netflix growth for the first time, and reportedly made Netflix leadership deeply concerned. Killing the program outright would likely hand Netflix back its growth narrative, subscriber momentum, and strategic freedom.

But continuing the current design unchanged would be financially reckless. The program is burning roughly $400M per year while Blockbuster has $1.1B in long-term debt and just reported an $85M Q2 2007 net loss. The board should preserve the competitive weapon while ending the unlimited subsidy structure that turns customer usage into escalating losses.

## Strategic Logic

The case for continuing Total Access is stronger than a simple subscriber-count argument. A stalled Netflix is not just a bruised rival. It is a rival with a weakened investor story, less pricing freedom, and potentially less leverage as it funds streaming, acquires content rights, and defends DVD-by-mail growth. Blockbuster should not casually give that pressure up.

The problem is that the current Total Access model appears to create negative operating leverage. Each in-store return costs roughly $2 in handling and lost rental revenue from the free replacement rental. The program's most differentiated feature is therefore also the feature that worsens economics as engaged customers use it more. If the $400M annual burn includes heavy acquisition spend that could decline as cohorts mature, the board should verify that before making permanent cuts. But even with that caveat, the current evidence does not justify open-ended funding at the same level.

The right counterfactual is not status quo versus abandonment. The right counterfactual is status quo versus an economically gated version of Total Access that preserves competitive pressure on Netflix while reducing cash burn. Blockbuster has approximately 5,700 U.S. locations and 47M store-visit customers. Those stores are a real asset if used for returns, pickup, local marketing, selective paid convenience, and customer acquisition. They become a liability when franchisees bear cannibalization while the company subsidizes free replacement rentals at scale.

The timing also argues against unlimited funding. DVD-by-mail is projected to peak around 2010-2012 and then decline. Netflix launched Watch Instantly in January 2007 with 1,000 titles, and U.S. broadband penetration is around 50% of households in 2007 with a path above 70% by 2010. Redbox is expanding with $1/night kiosks in grocery and convenience stores. Blockbuster cannot spend the next several years defending the most expensive version of a transitional physical-rental model while underinvesting in the next distribution model.

## What To Do Instead

Blockbuster should restructure Total Access in the next 90 days, using economic gates rather than a blunt shutdown:

1. End unlimited free in-store exchanges, or cap them by plan tier.
2. Move high-frequency exchange users into higher-priced tiers that reflect the $2-per-return burden.
3. Preserve a lower-cost online subscription tier for customers who value mail rental but do not require frequent store exchanges.
4. Use stores for returns, paid convenience, pickup, and acquisition offers rather than universal free replacement rentals.
5. Create a franchisee compensation model tied to measurable incremental traffic and paid transactions.
6. Ring-fence a portion of the savings for streaming rights, technology, or partnership work so Blockbuster is not trapped in DVD-by-mail as the market peaks.
7. Monitor Netflix's response weekly, including pricing, subscriber growth, content licensing moves, and Watch Instantly investment signals.

This keeps pressure on Netflix while reducing the probability that Blockbuster wins a short-term subscriber battle and loses strategic flexibility through cash exhaustion.

## Strongest Counterarguments

The strongest counterargument is that scaling back Total Access could release Netflix from the first competitive pressure it has meaningfully felt. That risk is real. Netflix has 7.5M subscribers versus Blockbuster's roughly 3M online subscribers, and Netflix is already investing in streaming. If Blockbuster removes the hybrid threat too aggressively, Netflix could regain subscriber momentum, cut prices, accelerate streaming investment, and rebuild its investor narrative.

That is why the board should not kill Total Access outright. But this counterargument supports restructuring, not unlimited funding. A competitive lever that costs roughly $400M per year must be redesigned unless the company can prove cohort-level lifetime value exceeds fully loaded costs and that the burn is materially temporary.

A second counterargument is that the $400M burn may include one-time acquisition costs that fall as the subscriber base matures. The board should test that immediately. However, the known $2 cost per in-store return suggests at least part of the problem is usage-driven, not merely launch-driven. If the most loyal customers create the highest exchange cost, maturity alone will not solve the economics.

A third counterargument is that Blockbuster could bridge the funding gap through debt restructuring, asset sales, or board-level commitment from investors. That should be evaluated, but it is not a reason to fund the existing design unchanged. New capital should buy a transition plan, not subsidize an uncapped physical-rental mechanic in a market approaching peak.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Restructure and scale back Total Access at current subsidy levels; do not abandon the online subscriber base or the hybrid strategic position.

Primary reason: Total Access has real competitive value against Netflix, but the current uncapped exchange subsidy imposes a cash burden Blockbuster has not shown it can support.

What must be preserved: Subscriber relationships, Netflix pressure, store-enabled convenience, and a credible online rental proposition.

What must be killed: Unlimited or underpriced free in-store exchanges that convert store usage into escalating losses and franchisee conflict.

What the board should reject: Both extremes: continuing the $400M-burn status quo without proof of cohort profitability, and a full retreat to store profitability that hands Netflix back strategic momentum.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- How much of the $400M annual burn is one-time acquisition spend versus recurring exchange-driven cost.
- Subscriber cohort economics by acquisition month, plan type, exchange frequency, churn, gross margin, and lifetime value.
- The revenue and subscriber loss expected under capped exchange, paid exchange, and store-return-only variants.
- Netflix's likely response if Blockbuster reduces subsidies: pricing, marketing, streaming acceleration, and content licensing behavior.
- Whether debt restructuring, asset sales, or investor support could fund a redesigned hybrid program through the DVD-by-mail peak.
- Franchisee-level economics and the minimum compensation structure required to keep operators aligned.
- Streaming partnership feasibility, rights cost, and the capital required to create a credible digital transition path.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass: The board has enough evidence to reject continued funding of Total Access unchanged. The combination of roughly $400M in annual burn, $1.1B in long-term debt, an $85M Q2 2007 loss, $2-per-return economics, franchisee resistance, streaming momentum, and Redbox pressure makes the current subsidy structure too risky.

Conditional: The board has enough evidence to preserve Total Access as a strategic platform, because it has added subscribers quickly and disrupted Netflix's growth. The appropriate action is redesign, not abandonment.

Fail: The board does not yet have enough evidence to choose the exact replacement design or to claim the entire $400M burn is structurally permanent. That requires cohort economics, burn decomposition, churn sensitivity, and competitive-response modeling.
