# Strategic Recommendation Memo: Scale Back Total Access

## Recommendation

Blockbuster should scale back Total Access now, not continue funding it at current levels. The board should preserve the online rental subscriber base where it can be served economically, but end the unlimited in-store return/free-rental subsidy that is driving the $400M annual burn.

Total Access is a competitively effective tactic inside a structurally declining format. It has slowed Netflix, added roughly 2M subscribers in under 12 months, and made use of Blockbuster's store footprint. But the question for the board is not whether the program hurts Netflix. It is whether Blockbuster can afford to keep buying DVD-by-mail share with cash it does not have, in a market likely to peak around 2010-2012 while streaming, kiosks, and broadband adoption improve. On that test, Total Access fails.

## Strategic Logic

The case for continuing is real: Total Access gave Blockbuster its first credible answer to Netflix. Netflix growth stalled for the first time during the program's peak, and Netflix's own leadership reportedly feared the model. Blockbuster also has assets Netflix lacks: approximately 5,700 U.S. locations and 47M store-visit customers. If the board had a clean balance sheet and a five-year physical-rental growth runway, pressing this advantage might be rational.

But Blockbuster does not have that balance sheet or that runway. Total Access costs roughly $400M per year while Blockbuster carries $1.1B in long-term debt and just reported an $85M Q2 2007 net loss. Each in-store return costs roughly $2 through handling and lost rental revenue from the free re-rental. That means the most differentiated part of the product is also the part that worsens unit economics as customers use it more.

This is not a scale problem that will obviously fix itself. Total Access has already reached about 3M online subscribers, but Netflix has 7.5M total subscribers and is investing in Watch Instantly, launched in January 2007 with 1,000 titles. Broadband is already near 50% of U.S. households and projected to exceed 70% by 2010. At the same time, Redbox is expanding with $1/night kiosks in grocery and convenience locations. Blockbuster is therefore funding a costly hybrid strategy in a physical rental market that is being attacked from both above by streaming and below by kiosks.

The store footprint should be treated as a transitional asset, not the center of the strategy. Stores can still provide customer acquisition, inventory liquidity, local marketing, and selective convenience. But using them to subsidize free rentals creates conflict with franchisees and converts a fixed-cost retail network into an open-ended loss amplifier. Franchisee hostility is not a side issue; it makes execution harder precisely when the company needs cash discipline and alignment.

## What To Do Instead

Blockbuster should immediately redesign Total Access around economic gates:

1. End or sharply limit free in-store exchanges that create the $2-per-return burden.
2. Move heavy users into higher-priced tiers or capped exchange tiers.
3. Retain online subscribers through price/value segmentation rather than universal subsidy.
4. Use stores for paid convenience, returns, local pickup, and targeted acquisition, not free replacement rentals.
5. Redirect a defined portion of savings into a streaming option, partner strategy, or digital rights capability so Blockbuster is not overexposed to DVD-by-mail's peak.
6. Negotiate a franchisee model that compensates stores only where the traffic and economics are measurable.

This is not a recommendation to abandon online. It is a recommendation to stop using store-funded economics to acquire subscribers at a pace the balance sheet cannot sustain.

## Strongest Counterarguments

The strongest argument for continuing is that Total Access is working. It added 2M subscribers in under a year, pushed Blockbuster online subscribers to about 3M, and stalled Netflix's growth. Pulling back may let Netflix resume momentum and could demoralize customers who joined specifically for store exchange convenience.

That argument is directionally correct but incomplete. A strategy can be competitively effective and still be financially wrong. If Blockbuster spends $400M per year to slow Netflix in a market projected to peak in 2010-2012, it may win a temporary DVD-rental battle while losing the company. The board should not confuse Netflix's discomfort with Blockbuster's durable advantage.

A second counterargument is that Blockbuster's 5,700 U.S. stores are the company's unique asset. That is also true, but only if the stores are used in ways that create cash contribution. Under the current Total Access design, the store base intensifies cannibalization and franchisee conflict. A differentiated asset that is monetized through uneconomic subsidies becomes a liability.

A third counterargument is that streaming is too early: Watch Instantly has only 1,000 titles and broadband penetration is only around 50% of households in 2007. The issue is not that streaming has already won. The issue is that the investment clock is moving against physical rental. By 2010, broadband penetration is projected above 70%, and DVD-by-mail is expected to peak around 2010-2012. Blockbuster cannot afford to spend the next several years defending the bridge while underfunding the destination.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Scale back Total Access at current subsidy levels and redesign it into an economically gated hybrid program.

Primary reason: The current program buys competitive share at a cash burn rate the company cannot support.

Success condition: Retain enough online subscribers to keep Blockbuster relevant in rental subscriptions while materially reducing annual losses and freeing capital for digital transition.

What the board should reject: A false binary between unlimited Total Access and a full retreat to stores. The correct move is to kill the uneconomic subsidy, not to abandon online customer relationships.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- Cohort economics by subscriber type: acquisition cost, churn, usage frequency, exchange frequency, gross margin, and lifetime value.
- Distribution of in-store exchange behavior: whether a small heavy-user segment is driving most of the $2-per-return cost.
- Franchisee-level impact: lost revenue, traffic change, and potential compensation structures.
- Customer elasticity: likely churn if free exchanges become capped, priced, or tiered.
- Streaming feasibility: rights costs, technology cost, partnership options, and realistic launch timing.
- Debt covenant and liquidity constraints: how long Blockbuster can fund losses before strategic flexibility collapses.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass: The board has enough evidence to reject continued funding at current levels. The $400M annual burn, $1.1B debt load, $85M Q2 loss, franchisee resistance, Redbox pressure, and streaming transition risk make the status quo unjustifiable.

Conditional: The board does not yet have enough evidence to choose the final replacement design. That requires cohort-level economics and customer elasticity analysis.

Fail: Continuing Total Access unchanged would require proof that subscriber lifetime value exceeds fully loaded exchange costs and that the program creates a path into digital distribution before DVD-by-mail peaks. The current case packet does not provide that proof.
