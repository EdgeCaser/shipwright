# Strategic Recommendation Memo: Kill Total Access at current funding levels and replace it with a redesigned, capped hybrid offer

Blockbuster should **kill Total Access as currently funded and structured**. The program has proved strategic value: it added roughly **2 million subscribers in under a year**, lifted Blockbuster online to about **3 million subscribers**, and **stalled Netflix growth for the first time**. That matters. But the board's actual decision is narrower and harsher: whether to keep funding the current version of Total Access despite about **$400 million in annual burn**, **$1.1 billion in long-term debt**, and an **$85 million Q2 2007 net loss**. On that question, the answer is no.

The strongest critique of a kill decision is that Side B should not assume every in-store return is pure loss. That critique is correct in part. In-store returns may create attach purchases, incremental traffic, and customer-retention benefits; the case packet's **47 million store-visit customers** means that variable could be material. The problem is not that these benefits are impossible. The problem is that the board does not have evidence here that they offset the known burden of the current design. What is evidenced is that each in-store return costs about **$2** in handling and foregone rental economics, and that the total program is burning roughly **$400 million per year**. What is not evidenced is a quantified attach-rate or margin uplift large enough to close that gap. In a leveraged situation, the board should not continue a large subsidy on the basis of an unproven offset.

The same logic applies to redesign options. It is plausible that throttling heavy users, tiering exchanges, limiting free exchanges, or narrowing eligible titles could preserve much of the competitive punch while cutting the burn. But that is an argument for **ending the current version now and replacing it with a redesigned version**, not for continuing at current funding levels. The choice presented is continue or kill. The correct board answer is to kill the present form because its economics are not yet demonstrated to work, then relaunch only under explicit loss caps and revised mechanics.

The competitive argument for continuation is real. Total Access did not just help Blockbuster acquire customers; it appears to have imposed real pressure on Netflix, which was forced onto defense while also beginning its **January 2007 Watch Instantly** buildout. But this cuts both ways. If Netflix is already shifting toward streaming while **broadband penetration is around 50% of U.S. households and projected above 70% by 2010**, then Blockbuster is being asked to fund a very expensive battle in a physical-rental format projected to peak around **2010-2012**. A company with Blockbuster's balance sheet should not run a war of attrition in a format with a narrowing window unless it has high confidence the economics are durable. The case packet does not provide that confidence.

Blockbuster's **5,700 U.S. stores** are still strategically important, but the current hybrid offer turns that footprint into an organizational conflict rather than a clean moat. Franchisees are hostile because the program cannibalizes store economics, and **Carl Icahn's three board seats** make it unlikely that management can sustain several more years of large online losses without destabilizing governance. Even if Total Access is damaging Netflix, it is also damaging Blockbuster's internal coalition at the moment the company needs alignment for the next transition.

## Counterarguments and why the recommendation still stands

The best case for continuation is that Total Access is the first thing that made Netflix vulnerable. True. But winning tactical share while losing financial flexibility is not enough for a leveraged incumbent facing a technology transition.

The second-best case is that store traffic generated by returns may produce profitable attach purchases. Also plausible. But the board has no quantified evidence here that attach economics offset the documented program burn. In a board decision, unmeasured upside should not outweigh measured cash drain.

The third-best case is that operational changes could save the model. Agreed. That is precisely why the recommendation is to kill the current design, not to abandon hybrid entirely. The board should terminate the open-ended subsidy and authorize a redesigned, lower-burn hybrid test instead.

## Recommended board action

Terminate Total Access **in its current form and at its current funding level** within two quarters.

Authorize a replacement plan with four constraints:

1. Eliminate or sharply cap the free in-store exchange benefit that drives the largest known economic leakage.
2. Keep an online offer, but put it under explicit quarterly loss ceilings and redesign it around measured cohort economics.
3. Use stores selectively for pickup, returns, and cross-sell where local economics justify it, rather than treating the entire network as a blanket subsidy layer.
4. Reserve capital for digital partnerships or streaming capability so Blockbuster does not preserve physical share at the cost of future relevance.

## Decision Frame

Kill Total Access as currently designed and funded. The evidence supports that the program is strategically effective but financially unproven and too expensive for Blockbuster's capital structure. The board should not continue a $400 million annual burn without quantified evidence that store attachment, retention, and redesign levers can close the gap.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

The biggest missing number is the net contribution of an in-store return after attach purchases, retention effects, and foregone rental revenue. The board also lacks a redesign model showing whether exchange caps, tiering, or title restrictions can preserve most of the competitive pressure at materially lower burn. Finally, it lacks a quantified view of how much franchisee resistance and governance pressure would constrain any multi-year continuation.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if the board is willing to stop the open-ended subsidy now, require measured unit economics before any relaunch, and accept slower subscriber growth in exchange for liquidity and strategic optionality. Fail if management insists on current-scale continuation without proving that attach revenue and redesigned program rules can bring losses within an acceptable range over the next 12-18 months.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A 90-day redesign memo that quantifies per-return economics, tests capped-exchange plan designs, models franchise/store impact, and compares the NPV of a reduced-burn hybrid strategy versus accelerated digital partnership investment.
