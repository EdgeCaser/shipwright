# Strategic Recommendation: Restructure Total Access as a Bridge to Digital

**To:** Blockbuster Board of Directors
**From:** Strategy Advisory Group
**Date:** Q3 2007
**Re:** Total Access Program — Recommendation to Continue with Structural Modifications

---

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Should Blockbuster continue funding Total Access at current burn rates (~$400M/year), kill the program to shore up near-term financials, or restructure it as a transitional competitive weapon?

**Our position: Continue Total Access, but restructure it immediately to cut burn by 40-50% while preserving subscriber momentum — and redirect savings into streaming capability.**

Killing Total Access outright would be a strategic catastrophe. The program is the only initiative in Blockbuster's history that has demonstrably wounded Netflix. Abandoning it hands Netflix an uncontested runway to scale streaming while Blockbuster retreats to a declining store-only model with $1.1B in debt and no digital future.

However, continuing at current burn rates is also untenable. At ~$400M/year incremental cost against an $85M quarterly loss, the program will exhaust Blockbuster's financial flexibility within 18-24 months. The answer is neither full throttle nor full stop — it is disciplined restructuring.

### The Three Options

| Option | Subscriber Impact | Annual Burn | Strategic Position |
|--------|------------------|-------------|-------------------|
| A. Continue as-is | Maintain ~3M, grow to 4-5M | ~$400M | Competitive but financially fatal |
| B. Kill Total Access | Lose 1.5-2M subscribers within 6 months | Save ~$400M | Concede online to Netflix permanently |
| **C. Restructure (Recommended)** | **Retain 2-2.5M, stabilize** | **~$200-240M** | **Sustain pressure while building digital bridge** |

### Why Continue (Restructured)

**1. Total Access is working — killing it validates Netflix's strategy at the worst possible moment.**

The evidence is unambiguous: Total Access added 2 million online subscribers in under 12 months. Netflix's growth stalled for the first time in company history. Reed Hastings himself identified Total Access as an existential threat. Blockbuster has demonstrated it possesses a genuine competitive advantage — the 5,700-store footprint as a physical return network — that Netflix cannot replicate. Walking away from a working strategy because it is expensive is not discipline; it is capitulation.

**2. The DVD-by-mail market has 3-5 years of runway — enough to build a bridge.**

DVD-by-mail is projected to peak between 2010-2012. Streaming launched in January 2007 with only 1,000 titles. Broadband penetration is at 50% of US households. The content licensing ecosystem for streaming does not yet exist at scale. Blockbuster has a window — narrow but real — to use Total Access subscriber revenue as a foundation for building streaming capability before the physical market declines.

**3. Blockbuster's store network is a transitional asset, not a permanent one.**

The 5,700 stores are simultaneously Blockbuster's greatest asset and greatest liability. As a Total Access return network, they create a hybrid convenience that pure-play competitors cannot match. As standalone retail, they are a depreciating asset facing Redbox ($1/night kiosks in grocery stores) from below and streaming from above. Total Access is the strategy that makes the stores relevant in the online era. Without it, the stores become pure cost centers within 3-4 years.

### Restructuring Specifics

**Reduce per-return cost from ~$2 to ~$0.75-1.00:**
- Cap free in-store exchanges at 3-5 per month (currently unlimited)
- Implement tiered plans: basic online-only ($14.99), hybrid with limited returns ($17.99), premium unlimited ($21.99)
- Negotiate franchisee revenue-sharing on in-store exchanges to align incentives
- Convert in-store returns from "free rental" to "discounted rental" ($1-2 credit vs. full free rental)

**Redirect $150-200M in savings toward streaming:**
- License streaming content aggressively (Netflix has only 1,000 titles — the window is open)
- Leverage existing content relationships with studios (Blockbuster has stronger studio ties than Netflix)
- Explore in-store streaming kiosks as a bridge experience

**Address franchisee conflict directly:**
- Offer franchisees a per-return handling fee ($0.50-0.75) funded from subscriber revenue
- Give franchisees access to online subscriber data for local marketing
- Establish a franchisee advisory council with quarterly business reviews

## Pass/Fail Readiness

### This recommendation is CONDITIONALLY READY for board decision.

**Pass criteria met:**
- ✅ Clear strategic logic: Total Access is the only competitive weapon that has damaged Netflix; abandoning it concedes the online market permanently
- ✅ Financial restructuring path identified: 40-50% burn reduction through plan tiering and return caps
- ✅ Streaming bridge strategy articulated with specific investment redirection
- ✅ Franchisee conflict addressed with economic alignment mechanism

**Conditions that must be validated before full commitment:**
- ⚠️ **Subscriber elasticity testing required** — The restructuring plan assumes subscribers will tolerate capped returns. If A/B testing shows >40% churn from plan changes, the board must revisit whether a more aggressive burn rate is justified or whether the subscriber base is inherently subsidy-dependent.
- ⚠️ **Debt refinancing feasibility** — At $1.1B in long-term debt and quarterly losses, Blockbuster's ability to sustain even a $200M annual burn depends on capital market access. If refinancing is not achievable within 6 months, the timeline compresses dangerously.
- ⚠️ **Streaming content deals** — Without at least 2-3 major studio streaming agreements within 12 months, the bridge-to-digital thesis collapses and the recommendation reverts to an orderly wind-down of online operations.

**Kill triggers (if any of these materialize, reverse course):**
- Subscriber churn exceeds 50% after restructuring despite 90-day testing period
- Debt refinancing fails and cash runway falls below 12 months
- Netflix secures exclusive streaming deals with 3+ major studios, foreclosing Blockbuster's content access

## Strongest Counterarguments and Responses

**Counterargument 1: "Total Access is just buying subscribers with subsidies. They'll leave the moment you cut the subsidy."**

This is the most serious objection and it may be partially correct. However, it argues for testing, not killing. If subscribers stay after restructuring, the program is viable at lower cost. If they leave, we've learned the answer and can wind down deliberately rather than preemptively. Killing without testing leaves the question permanently unanswered and concedes the market based on assumption rather than evidence.

**Counterargument 2: "Streaming will make DVD-by-mail obsolete. Why invest in a declining business?"**

Streaming will eventually dominate, but 'eventually' matters enormously. In Q3 2007, Netflix's streaming library is 1,000 titles versus 90,000+ on DVD. Broadband reaches only 50% of households. Content owners have not yet built streaming licensing models. The transition will take 5-7 years. Total Access buys time and subscribers that can be migrated to streaming — but only if Blockbuster simultaneously invests in streaming capability. Our recommendation explicitly funds this.

**Counterargument 3: "Icahn and the board won't tolerate continued losses. The political reality is that Total Access dies regardless of strategy."**

This is a governance constraint, not a strategic one. If the board kills a working competitive program because of short-term loss aversion, that is a choice to prioritize 2-3 years of improved earnings reports over long-term survival. The restructured plan cuts the burn rate roughly in half, which should satisfy reasonable financial discipline. If the board cannot accept even $200M/year in transitional investment to build a digital future, then the honest conclusion is that Blockbuster's governance structure is incompatible with the strategic transformation required — and the board should explore sale or partnership alternatives rather than pretend that store-only operations represent a viable standalone future.
