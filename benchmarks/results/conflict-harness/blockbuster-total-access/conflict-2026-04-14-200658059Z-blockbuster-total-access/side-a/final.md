# Strategic Recommendation: Restructure Total Access as a Bridge to Digital

**To:** Blockbuster Board of Directors
**From:** Strategy Advisory Group
**Date:** Q3 2007
**Re:** Total Access Program — Recommendation to Continue with Structural Modifications

---

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Should Blockbuster continue funding Total Access at current burn rates (~$400M/year), kill the program to shore up near-term financials, or restructure it as a transitional competitive weapon?

**Our position: Continue Total Access, but restructure it immediately to reduce burn significantly while preserving subscriber momentum — and redirect savings into streaming capability.**

Killing Total Access outright would be a strategic catastrophe. The program is the only initiative in Blockbuster's history that has demonstrably wounded Netflix. Abandoning it hands Netflix an uncontested runway to scale streaming while Blockbuster retreats to a declining store-only model with $1.1B in debt and no digital future.

However, continuing at current burn rates is also untenable. At ~$400M/year incremental cost against an $85M quarterly loss, the program will exhaust Blockbuster's financial flexibility within 18-24 months. The answer is neither full throttle nor full stop — it is disciplined restructuring.

### The Three Options

| Option | Subscriber Impact | Annual Burn | Strategic Position |
|--------|------------------|-------------|-------------------|
| A. Continue as-is | Maintain ~3M, grow to 4-5M | ~$400M | Competitive but financially fatal |
| B. Kill Total Access | Lose 1.5-2M subscribers within 6 months | Save ~$400M | Concede online to Netflix permanently |
| **C. Restructure (Recommended)** | **Retain 2-2.5M, stabilize** | **Target range TBD pending operating model validation** | **Sustain pressure while building digital bridge** |

### Why Continue (Restructured)

**1. Total Access is working — killing it validates Netflix's strategy at the worst possible moment.**

The evidence is unambiguous: Total Access added 2 million online subscribers in under 12 months. Netflix's growth stalled for the first time in company history. Reed Hastings himself identified Total Access as an existential threat. Blockbuster has demonstrated it possesses a genuine competitive advantage — the 5,700-store footprint as a physical return network — that Netflix cannot replicate. Walking away from a working strategy because it is expensive is not discipline; it is capitulation.

**2. The DVD-by-mail market has 3-5 years of runway — enough to build a bridge.**

DVD-by-mail is projected to peak between 2010-2012. Streaming launched in January 2007 with only 1,000 titles. Broadband penetration is at 50% of US households. The content licensing ecosystem for streaming does not yet exist at scale. Blockbuster has a window — narrow but real — to use Total Access subscriber revenue as a foundation for building streaming capability before the physical market declines.

**3. Blockbuster's store network is a transitional asset, not a permanent one.**

The 5,700 stores are simultaneously Blockbuster's greatest asset and greatest liability. As a Total Access return network, they create a hybrid convenience that pure-play competitors cannot match. As standalone retail, they are a depreciating asset facing Redbox ($1/night kiosks in grocery stores) from below and streaming from above. Total Access is the strategy that makes the stores relevant in the online era. Without it, the stores become pure cost centers within 3-4 years.

### Restructuring Levers — Operating Assumptions and Evidence Gaps

The critique is correct that precise savings projections require operating data we do not yet have. We identify the levers and the assumptions that must be validated, rather than claiming a specific savings figure as settled:

**Lever 1: Cap free in-store exchanges (currently unlimited)**
- *Assumption:* The $400M burn is driven by per-return handling costs of ~$2 each. If 3M subscribers average 4-6 in-store returns/month, total annual return volume is 144M-216M returns, costing $288M-$432M. Capping at 3-5/month reduces volume at the margin, but the savings magnitude depends entirely on the actual return frequency distribution — data the operations team must provide.
- *Evidence gap:* We do not have the return frequency distribution. If most subscribers already use fewer than 3-5 returns/month, caps save little. If usage is concentrated among heavy users, caps could save substantially but risk losing precisely the most engaged subscribers. **This is the single most important data point for the restructuring case and must be obtained within 14 days.**

**Lever 2: Implement tiered pricing**
- *Assumption:* Offering basic online-only ($14.99), hybrid with limited returns ($17.99), and premium unlimited ($21.99) creates price discrimination that captures willingness-to-pay differences. Netflix charges $17.99 for its 3-out plan, establishing a market reference.
- *Evidence gap:* We have no subscriber willingness-to-pay data for hybrid tiers. Subscribers may have joined specifically for unlimited free returns; tiering may trigger churn indistinguishable from killing the program. A/B testing in 3-5 DMAs over 60-90 days is required before national rollout.

**Lever 3: Franchisee revenue-sharing**
- *Assumption:* Offering franchisees $0.50-0.75 per handled return converts them from opponents to participants, partially funded by subscriber revenue.
- *Evidence gap:* Franchisees are already hostile. The case packet confirms resistance but provides no data on franchisee unit economics or their minimum acceptable compensation. Pilot negotiations with 10-15 willing franchisees within 60 days will reveal whether cooperation is achievable at any price point.

**Lever 4: Convert free returns to discounted returns ($1-2 credit vs. full free rental)**
- *Assumption:* Changing the value from a free rental to a discount preserves some convenience advantage while reducing the ~$2 per-return cost.
- *Evidence gap:* The perceived value difference between "free" and "$1" may be psychologically disproportionate. Behavioral economics suggests removing a free option triggers outsized negative response.

**Revised savings estimate:** Rather than claiming 40-50% savings as a given, we frame the restructuring as a hypothesis: **if return frequency data shows a heavy-tail distribution and A/B testing confirms <30% churn from tiered plans, savings in the range of $100-200M annually are plausible.** The exact figure is an output of the validation process, not an input to the decision. The board's decision today is whether to fund and execute the 90-day validation, not whether to commit to a specific restructured cost structure.

**Redirect savings toward streaming:**
- License streaming content aggressively (Netflix has only 1,000 titles — the window is open)
- Leverage existing content relationships with studios (Blockbuster has stronger studio ties than Netflix)
- Explore in-store streaming kiosks as a bridge experience
- Exact streaming investment budget contingent on validated restructuring savings

**Address franchisee conflict directly:**
- Offer franchisees a per-return handling fee ($0.50-0.75) funded from subscriber revenue
- Give franchisees access to online subscriber data for local marketing
- Establish a franchisee advisory council with quarterly business reviews

**Conditions that must be validated before full commitment:**

- ⚠️ **Subscriber elasticity testing required (60-90 days)** — The restructuring plan assumes subscribers will tolerate capped returns. If A/B testing shows >40% churn from plan changes, the board must revisit whether a more aggressive burn rate is justified or whether the subscriber base is inherently subsidy-dependent.
- ⚠️ **Debt refinancing feasibility (30 days)** — At $1.1B in long-term debt and quarterly losses, Blockbuster's ability to sustain even a reduced annual burn depends on capital market access. If refinancing is not achievable within 6 months, the timeline compresses dangerously.

- ⚠️ **Streaming content deals (12 months)** — Without at least 2-3 major studio streaming agreements within 12 months, the bridge-to-digital thesis collapses and the recommendation reverts to an orderly wind-down of online operations.

**Kill triggers (if any of these materialize, reverse course):**
- Subscriber churn exceeds 50% after restructuring despite 90-day testing period
- Debt refinancing fails and cash runway falls below 12 months
- Netflix secures exclusive streaming deals with 3+ major studios, foreclosing Blockbuster's content access
- Return frequency data reveals usage is uniformly distributed (no heavy-tail), meaning caps cannot generate meaningful savings without broadly degrading the product

## Strongest Counterarguments and Responses

**Counterargument 1: "Total Access is just buying subscribers with subsidies. They'll leave the moment you cut the subsidy."**

This is the most serious objection and it may be partially correct. However, it argues for testing, not killing. If subscribers stay after restructuring, the program is viable at lower cost. If they leave, we've learned the answer and can wind down deliberately rather than preemptively. Killing without testing leaves the question permanently unanswered and concedes the market based on assumption rather than evidence.

**Counterargument 2: "Streaming will make DVD-by-mail obsolete. Why invest in a declining business?"**

Streaming will eventually dominate, but 'eventually' matters enormously. In Q3 2007, Netflix's streaming library is 1,000 titles versus 90,000+ on DVD. Broadband reaches only 50% of households. Content owners have not yet built streaming licensing models. The transition will take 5-7 years. Total Access buys time and subscribers that can be migrated to streaming — but only if Blockbuster simultaneously invests in streaming capability. Our recommendation explicitly funds this.

**Counterargument 3: "Icahn and the board won't tolerate continued losses. The political reality is that Total Access dies regardless of strategy."**

This is a governance constraint, not a strategic one. If the board kills a working competitive program because of short-term loss aversion, that is a choice to prioritize 2-3 years of improved earnings reports over long-term survival. The restructured plan reduces the burn rate substantially — the exact amount to be validated — which should satisfy reasonable financial discipline. If the board cannot accept transitional investment to build a digital future, then the honest conclusion is that Blockbuster's governance structure is incompatible with the strategic transformation required — and the board should explore sale or partnership alternatives rather than pretend that store-only operations represent a viable standalone future.

**Counterargument 4 (new): "The restructuring savings are speculative — you don't know these levers will actually work."**

This critique is valid and we have adopted it. The revised recommendation explicitly frames the restructuring as a hypothesis requiring validation, not a settled plan. The board's immediate decision is whether to fund a 90-day validation phase (return frequency analysis, A/B testing, franchisee pilot, refinancing exploration) at relatively low cost, not whether to commit to a specific restructured cost structure. The alternative — killing Total Access without testing restructuring — is a larger bet with more irreversible consequences.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**90-Day Validation Plan** with the following components:

1. **Return Frequency Analysis (14 days)** — Pull per-subscriber monthly return counts, segment by decile, model savings scenarios under various cap levels. This is the prerequisite for all other restructuring analysis.
2. **A/B Test Design for Restructured Plans (design in 14 days, execute over 60-90 days)** — Market selection, control groups, churn measurement methodology, go/no-go thresholds. Test at least 3 plan configurations.
3. **Franchisee Pilot (60 days)** — Revenue-sharing term sheet, 10-15 pilot operators, unit economics tracking, scalability assessment.
4. **Capital Structure Review (30 days)** — Refinancing scenarios under multiple burn rate assumptions (not a single point estimate), covenant analysis, contingency planning for capital market stress.
5. **Streaming Investment Business Case (90 days)** — Content licensing target list, technology platform requirements, build-vs-partner analysis, 3-year financial model contingent on restructuring validation results.
6. **Competitive Intelligence Brief (ongoing)** — Netflix streaming roadmap assessment, Redbox expansion tracking, studio content strategy shifts.

The board should reconvene at Day 90 with validated operating data to make the full-commitment decision on restructured Total Access. The cost of the validation phase itself is minimal compared to either continuing at $400M/year or killing the program permanently.
