# Strategic Recommendation Memo: Scale Back Total Access, Preserve the Hybrid Wedge, and Avoid an Unfunded Streaming Leap

## Recommendation

Blockbuster should scale back Total Access at current funding levels, not kill the customer proposition outright and not continue the existing subsidy unchanged. The board should preserve the parts of Total Access that are demonstrably working: online subscriber acquisition, store-assisted convenience, and competitive pressure on Netflix. But it should end the open-ended in-store return economics that are driving an estimated $400M annual burn against a leveraged balance sheet.

This recommendation is not a simple pivot from Total Access to streaming. That would be too speculative. Blockbuster has no demonstrated 2007 streaming capability in the case evidence, and its prior streaming-related effort, the 2000 Enron Broadband deal, failed. Netflix has already launched Watch Instantly with 1,000 titles and has 7.5M subscribers versus Blockbuster's roughly 3M online subscribers. Therefore, Blockbuster should not assume it can buy streaming parity by redirecting money from Total Access. It should fund a narrow digital-options program through savings from a Total Access reset while keeping the proven hybrid wedge alive under stricter economics.

## Core Rationale

Total Access is competitively effective. It added 2M subscribers in under 12 months, helped Blockbuster reach roughly 3M online subscribers, and coincided with Netflix's first growth stall. The 5,700-store footprint created a differentiated convenience model that Netflix could not immediately copy. That evidence argues against a full shutdown.

But the current design is financially unsafe. The program is burning roughly $400M per year, and each in-store return costs about $2 in handling and lost rental revenue. Blockbuster also has $1.1B in long-term debt and lost $85M in Q2 2007. The issue is not that Total Access is a bad idea. The issue is that unlimited or underpriced in-store exchange converts customer growth into worsening unit economics.

The board should treat the $400M burn as a redesign target, not as a fixed law of the business. The right question is whether Blockbuster can retain enough competitive pressure after changing the rules: capping free exchanges, pricing heavy use, compensating franchisees, segmenting offers by customer value, and reducing operational leakage. If those changes preserve subscriber momentum while materially reducing losses, the hybrid strategy remains useful. If they do not, the board should cut deeper.

The streaming context strengthens the case for conserving capital, but it does not justify a blind streaming leap. DVD-by-mail is projected to peak around 2010-2012, broadband penetration is expected to rise from about 50% of US households in 2007 to more than 70% by 2010, and Netflix launched Watch Instantly in January 2007. These facts mean Blockbuster cannot rely on stores and discs as a permanent destination. They do not prove Blockbuster can build a first-class streaming business quickly. The digital program should therefore be options-based: partnerships, licensing tests, product experiments, and capability assessment, not a large unvalidated infrastructure bet.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Scale back current-level Total Access funding while preserving a redesigned hybrid offer.

The board should authorize a 90-day reset with five moves:

1. Replace unlimited free in-store exchanges with capped, tiered, or paid exchange rights so the highest-cost usage no longer destroys contribution margin.
2. Keep online rental as a strategic acquisition and retention channel, but require cohort-level profitability targets by plan and usage segment.
3. Create a franchisee compensation model for store-enabled online activity so independent operators are not forced to absorb cannibalization without offsetting economics.
4. Fund only a bounded digital-options program from verified burn reduction, focused on partnership diligence, licensing feasibility, and technical capability assessment.
5. Establish two-quarter kill criteria: if the redesigned model cannot materially lower burn while preserving acceptable subscriber retention and acquisition, cut Total Access more aggressively.

This is a continuation of the strategic wedge, not a continuation of the current cost structure. It is also a digital hedge, not a declaration that Blockbuster can immediately match Netflix in streaming.

## Strongest Counterarguments

The strongest argument for continuing Total Access unchanged is that Blockbuster has finally found a weapon that scares Netflix. Total Access added 2M subscribers in under a year, Netflix growth stalled for the first time, and the store network created a hybrid advantage. Scaling back could hand momentum back to Netflix.

That argument is serious, but it does not justify an uncapped subsidy. A competitive tactic that works at any price is not automatically a board-level strategy. If each in-store return creates about $2 of cost and lost revenue, growth can deepen the economic problem unless rules and pricing change. The board should preserve the competitive mechanism while forcing it through a margin test.

A second counterargument is that streaming is the future, so Blockbuster should shift aggressively now. The direction is right, but the implied execution confidence is not supported by the evidence. Netflix has already launched Watch Instantly, while Blockbuster's cited prior streaming attempt was the failed Enron Broadband deal in 2000. Blockbuster should not abandon the one proven lever in favor of an unproven internal streaming build. It should buy options and learn quickly while reducing the Total Access burn.

A third counterargument is that stores remain Blockbuster's core asset because 47M customers still visit stores. That base matters, but it should be used as a migration and convenience asset, not protected as a standalone legacy profit pool. Redbox's $1-per-night kiosk expansion and Netflix's subscription model both pressure store economics. The store footprint is valuable only if it lowers acquisition cost, increases retention, or supports profitable convenience.

## Strategic Path Forward

The board should avoid the false binary of either defending stores or copying Netflix. The better path is disciplined migration:

- Short term: redesign Total Access exchange rules, pricing, and franchise economics to reduce burn.
- Medium term: keep stores as a selective acquisition, return, and service layer where they improve retention or lower cost.
- Long term: develop digital options through partnerships and capability tests, not a large unproven attempt to build streaming parity immediately.

The operating goal is to keep pressure on Netflix without letting that pressure consume the balance sheet before the market shifts again.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

The board should request immediate analysis on:

- Total Access cohort profitability by acquisition month, plan, and exchange frequency.
- Return frequency distribution and the share of losses caused by heavy in-store exchange users.
- Subscriber churn sensitivity under capped, tiered, or paid exchange rules.
- Franchisee economics by store type and feasible compensation mechanisms.
- Operational improvements that could reduce per-return cost while preserving customer convenience.
- Streaming capability gaps: technical talent, platform options, content licensing access, partnership candidates, and expected time to launch.
- Liquidity runway under three cases: current Total Access burn, redesigned Total Access, and aggressive cutback.

These gaps should refine implementation, not delay the decision to stop funding the current open-ended subsidy.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass readiness for continuing current-level Total Access funding is not met. The program has strong competitive evidence, but the roughly $400M annual burn is too large relative to $1.1B in long-term debt and an $85M Q2 2007 net loss.

Pass readiness for a full streaming pivot is also not met. The case evidence shows a failed prior streaming-related deal with Enron Broadband and Netflix's existing Watch Instantly launch. Blockbuster should not assume it can buy parity without proving capability, licensing access, and execution speed.

Pass readiness for a scaled-back hybrid reset is met. Total Access has shown demand and competitive pressure, and the board has enough evidence to require a lower-burn version with explicit economic thresholds.

Decision threshold: within two quarters, the redesigned Total Access model must show materially lower burn, acceptable retention after exchange-rule changes, a workable franchisee model, and credible evidence that any digital investments are option-building rather than unfocused spending. If those tests fail, cut deeper.

## Recommended Next Artifact

Create a 90-day Total Access Reset Plan. It should include revised exchange caps and pricing, expected burn reduction, churn scenarios, franchisee economics, operational cost-reduction opportunities, a digital-options diligence plan, and two-quarter kill criteria.
