# Strategic Recommendation Memo: Scale Back Total Access and Rebuild Around a Lower-Burn Digital Transition

## Recommendation

Blockbuster should scale back Total Access immediately rather than continue funding it at current levels. The program is competitively effective but financially mismatched to Blockbuster's balance sheet and strategically over-indexed on a market likely to peak within a few years. The board should preserve the useful parts of the hybrid model, end the unlimited in-store return subsidy, reduce the cash burn, and redirect investment toward a lower-cost online and streaming transition.

This is not a recommendation to retreat into store profitability as a permanent strategy. The stores are not a durable end state. It is a recommendation to stop using a highly subsidized DVD-by-mail and store-return program as the main weapon against Netflix when Blockbuster lacks the balance sheet flexibility to sustain a $400M annual burn.

## Core Rationale

Total Access has proved that Blockbuster can hurt Netflix. It added 2M subscribers in under 12 months, brought Blockbuster's online subscriber base to roughly 3M, and coincided with Netflix's first growth stall. That competitive signal matters. The question is whether the tactic creates enough enterprise value to justify its cost.

It does not.

The annual incremental burn is roughly $400M, driven in part by about $2 of cost per in-store return through handling and lost rental revenue. Blockbuster also has $1.1B in long-term debt and just lost $85M in Q2 2007. That makes Total Access a balance-sheet strategy, not merely a marketing strategy. Continuing the program at current intensity requires the board to believe Blockbuster can outspend Netflix long enough to permanently change the market while absorbing losses that its debt structure and franchise system are already resisting.

That belief is too aggressive. Netflix has 7.5M total subscribers versus Blockbuster's roughly 3M online subscribers, and Netflix is already investing in streaming after launching Watch Instantly in January 2007 with 1,000 titles. Meanwhile, the DVD-by-mail market is projected to peak around 2010-2012 and then decline as broadband penetration rises from roughly 50% of US households in 2007 to more than 70% by 2010. Funding Total Access at current levels means spending heavily to win a transitional channel just as the market's center of gravity starts moving away from discs.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Scale back Total Access now, rather than continue current-level funding.

The board should approve a modified plan with four moves:

1. End or sharply limit the unlimited free in-store exchange subsidy that creates the approximately $2 per-return economic penalty.
2. Keep online rental as a customer acquisition and retention channel, but shift pricing and limits so the program is contribution-margin disciplined by cohort.
3. Create a dedicated streaming and digital roadmap funded from reduced Total Access losses, not from additional leverage.
4. Negotiate a franchisee-compatible participation model so stores are not asked to absorb cannibalization without an offsetting economic mechanism.

The decision test is not whether Total Access is working competitively. It is. The decision test is whether the current version is the best use of scarce capital given debt, losses, franchisee conflict, Redbox pressure, and the coming streaming shift. On that test, the answer is no.

## Strongest Counterarguments

The strongest argument for continuing Total Access is that Blockbuster has finally found a lever that scares Netflix. The program added 2M subscribers in under a year, Netflix growth stalled during its peak, and Blockbuster's store footprint of roughly 5,700 US locations created a hybrid advantage Netflix could not easily copy. Killing the program risks handing momentum back to Netflix and wasting the one strategic asset Blockbuster still has at national scale.

That argument is directionally right but financially incomplete. A tactic that scares Netflix is not automatically a strategy Blockbuster can afford. If each in-store return destroys economics and the program burns roughly $400M per year, then scale can worsen the problem unless pricing or rules change. Blockbuster's store footprint is only an advantage if it lowers customer acquisition costs, improves retention, or creates profitable convenience. Under the current model, it also creates cannibalization, franchisee resistance, and operational cost.

A second counterargument is that scaling back will signal weakness to Netflix, Icahn, franchisees, and customers. But the worse signal would be continuing an unprofitable subsidy until lenders, activists, or liquidity constraints force an abrupt retreat. A planned reset is more credible than a delayed capitulation.

A third counterargument is that stores remain a major asset because Blockbuster still has 47M store-visit customers. That customer base should be used, but not protected blindly. Store traffic is valuable as a migration funnel into online and digital services. It is not a reason to subsidize free rentals in a way that destroys store-level economics and alienates franchisees.

## Strategic Path Forward

The board should not frame this as Total Access versus stores. That is the wrong binary. The correct path is a staged transition:

- Short term: reduce cash losses by changing exchange limits, pricing, and franchise compensation.
- Medium term: use stores as a lower-cost customer acquisition and service layer while avoiding uneconomic free-rental loops.
- Long term: fund streaming partnerships, licensing, and product infrastructure before broadband growth and Netflix's head start make the gap irreversible.

Redbox's accelerating $1/night kiosk expansion also weakens the case for pouring more capital into disc convenience. Redbox competes on low-cost, convenient physical rentals; Netflix competes on subscription and streaming; Blockbuster cannot afford to fight both by subsidizing the most expensive version of physical distribution.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

The board should request immediate analysis on:

- Cohort profitability for Total Access subscribers acquired in late 2006 and 2007.
- Return frequency distribution and the share of users responsible for the highest in-store exchange losses.
- Churn impact if free exchanges are capped, priced, or converted into a loyalty benefit.
- Franchisee economics by store type and proposed compensation mechanisms.
- Required capital and partnership options for a credible streaming product roadmap.
- Liquidity runway under three scenarios: continue current burn, moderate scale-back, and aggressive scale-back.

These gaps should refine implementation, not delay the decision to reduce current-level funding.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass readiness for continuing current-level Total Access funding is not met. The program has strong competitive evidence but fails the financial sustainability test because the $400M annual burn is too large relative to $1.1B in long-term debt and an $85M Q2 2007 net loss.

Pass readiness for a scaled-back hybrid strategy is met. The program has demonstrated demand and competitive pressure, but its economics need to be redesigned before further scale. The board has enough evidence to authorize a reset now while commissioning the cohort and liquidity analyses needed to tune the new model.

Decision threshold: If the revised Total Access model cannot show a credible path to materially lower burn and positive cohort economics within two quarters, Blockbuster should cut deeper and accelerate a digital partnership strategy.

## Recommended Next Artifact

Create a 90-day Total Access Reset Plan for board approval. It should include revised pricing and exchange rules, franchisee economics, expected burn reduction, customer churn scenarios, streaming investment reallocation, and a two-quarter operating dashboard with explicit kill criteria.
