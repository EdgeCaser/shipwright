# Critique: finding-1

- Target Side: side_b
- Target Claim IDs: side_b-claim-1, side_b-claim-5
- Attack Type: evidence_gap
- Severity: high

## Claim Under Attack
Blockbuster should kill Total Access because the ~$400M annual burn is incompatible with its balance sheet, and a narrower transition plan is superior.

## Evidence Or Reason
Side B treats the $400M burn as a fixed, binary cost — either you fund it all or you kill it — then leaps to a 'narrower transition plan' without providing any evidence that a scaled-back program retains competitive value. The artifact's own Unknowns section admits it lacks 'precise cohort economics,' 'whether any customer segments show positive lifetime value,' and 'liquidity headroom' data. This means the kill recommendation rests on the same evidentiary gap it accuses continuation of having: neither side has proven unit economics. More critically, Side B ignores the asymmetric cost of retreating. Total Access added 2M subscribers in under 12 months and stalled Netflix growth for the first time — a demonstrated competitive effect with no precedent in Blockbuster's history. Killing the program surrenders that pressure permanently; Netflix regains subscriber momentum and redirects the war chest it was spending defensively. Side B's proposed replacement ('keep online subscription only where contribution economics can be positive') is unfalsifiable without the cohort data it admits it doesn't have, making the 'narrower plan' not a strategy but a placeholder. The memo effectively recommends destroying proven competitive leverage based on cost concerns it cannot quantify at the segment level, while offering a replacement it cannot validate either.
