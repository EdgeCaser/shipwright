# Strategic Recommendation: Restructure Total Access, Don't Kill It

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Should Blockbuster continue funding Total Access at $400M/year burn, scale it back, or kill it entirely?

**Recommendation: Restructure Total Access to ~$200M/year burn by repricing the in-store exchange benefit, while redirecting $150M+ annually toward streaming infrastructure and Redbox-competitive kiosk pilots.**

This is neither "continue as-is" nor "kill." Both extremes are wrong. Continuing the current burn rate is financially unsustainable against $1.1B in debt and quarterly losses. But killing Total Access entirely surrenders the only competitive weapon that has demonstrably hurt Netflix — the sole program in the DVD rental industry's history to stall Netflix's subscriber growth.

The binary framing (continue vs. kill) is itself the strategic error. The board should reject it.

---

## Strategic Analysis

### What Total Access Proved

Total Access demonstrated a critical insight: **Blockbuster's store footprint is a distribution network advantage that Netflix cannot replicate.** The hybrid model — order online, return in-store, get an instant swap — created a value proposition Netflix structurally cannot match. The evidence is unambiguous:

- 2M online subscribers added in under 12 months
- Netflix growth stalled for the first time in company history
- Reed Hastings characterized his concern at the highest level ("ichiban scared")
- Blockbuster reached 3M online subscribers against Netflix's 7.5M — a 40% ratio achieved in a fraction of the time

Killing this program tells the market Blockbuster has conceded the online rental space. That concession is irreversible.

### Why the Current Burn Is Unsustainable

The $400M/year incremental cost is not viable given Blockbuster's capital structure:

- $1.1B long-term debt with limited refinancing options in a declining industry
- $85M net loss in Q2 2007 alone — annualized trajectory exceeds $300M
- Each in-store return costs ~$2 in handling + lost rental revenue
- Franchisee revolt threatens the store network that makes Total Access work
- No clear path to subscriber-level profitability at current pricing

Carl Icahn's critique, while motivated by short-term shareholder value, is directionally correct on one point: the current economics don't close. Where Icahn is wrong is in concluding the program should be eliminated rather than restructured.

### The Restructured Model

**Phase 1 (Q4 2007 - Q2 2008): Reprice the in-store exchange**
- Limit free in-store exchanges to 3-5 per month (from unlimited) based on subscription tier
- Introduce a premium tier ($24.99/mo) for unlimited exchanges; standard tier ($17.99) gets 5/month
- Projected cost reduction: 40-50% of current in-store exchange costs
- Franchisee accommodation: revenue-sharing on exchanges that drive incremental store traffic

**Phase 2 (Q2 2008 - Q4 2008): Streaming investment**
- Redirect $150M/year from Total Access savings into streaming content licensing and infrastructure
- Netflix launched Watch Instantly with only 1,000 titles in January 2007 — the window to compete is open but closing
- Broadband at ~50% penetration (2007) heading to 70%+ by 2010 means streaming will be mainstream within 36 months
- Blockbuster's brand awareness and existing customer relationships (47M store visitors) provide a subscriber acquisition advantage Netflix lacks in physical channels

**Phase 3 (2009+): Portfolio transition**
- DVD-by-mail projected to peak 2010-2012 then decline — Total Access is a bridge, not a destination
- Stores become fulfillment/experience hubs with kiosk components to compete with Redbox's $1/night model
- Streaming becomes the primary growth vector

## Counterarguments Addressed

### "Just kill it and focus on store profitability"
Store-only profitability is a melting ice cube. Redbox is expanding aggressively at $1/night in grocery and convenience stores — locations with higher foot traffic than Blockbuster stores. Netflix is growing online subscribers. A store-only strategy produces 2-3 years of improved margins followed by terminal decline. This is the Kodak path: optimizing the legacy business while the disruption plays out.

### "The debt load makes any investment impossible"
The debt load makes *wasteful* investment impossible. It does not make *strategic* investment impossible. Restructuring Total Access from $400M to ~$200M burn while redirecting savings to streaming is a net-neutral cash flow change with dramatically better strategic positioning. The question is not whether Blockbuster can afford to invest — it's whether it can afford not to.

### "Netflix has a head start in streaming"
Netflix launched streaming with 1,000 titles in January 2007. The library is small, the technology is early, and broadband penetration is only 50%. This is not a market that has been won. Blockbuster's advantages — content relationships from being the largest physical retailer, brand recognition, and 47M existing customer touchpoints — are real assets in a streaming land grab. The failed Enron Broadband deal in 2000 shows Blockbuster has recognized this trend before; execution, not vision, was the problem.

### "Icahn and the board won't approve continued losses"
This recommendation reduces losses by $150-200M/year versus the current trajectory. Frame it as cost discipline with strategic reallocation, not continued bleeding. Icahn's concern is valid on the economics; address the economics and the political objection resolves.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

1. **Subscriber price elasticity:** How many of the 3M online subscribers chose Total Access specifically for unlimited in-store exchanges? If the answer is >60%, tiered repricing risks significant churn. *Needed: subscriber survey or A/B test on exchange limits before full rollout.*

2. **Streaming content licensing costs:** Can Blockbuster secure streaming rights at competitive rates given its weaker balance sheet versus Netflix? Netflix's scale advantage in content negotiation is unknown but likely real. *Needed: preliminary licensing conversations with major studios.*

3. **Franchisee cooperation:** Will franchisees accept a revenue-sharing model on exchanges, or will they continue to resist? Franchise agreements may limit Blockbuster's ability to mandate participation. *Needed: legal review of franchise agreements and franchisee council negotiation.*

4. **Technology readiness:** Blockbuster's streaming infrastructure is non-existent. Can a credible streaming product be built in 12-18 months, or does this require acquisition? *Needed: technology assessment and build-vs-buy analysis.*

5. **Redbox trajectory:** How fast is Redbox expanding, and at what point does $1/night kiosk rental commoditize the casual rental market that drives Blockbuster store traffic? *Needed: competitive intelligence on Redbox rollout commitments and grocery chain partnerships.*

6. **Refinancing risk:** Can Blockbuster refinance its $1.1B debt given the restructured burn rate, or does the credit market view the company as uninvestable regardless? *Needed: preliminary lender conversations.*
