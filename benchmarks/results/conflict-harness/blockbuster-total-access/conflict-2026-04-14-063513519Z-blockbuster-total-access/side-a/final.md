# Strategic Recommendation: Restructure Total Access, Don't Kill It

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Should Blockbuster continue funding Total Access at $400M/year burn, scale it back, or kill it entirely?

**Recommendation: Restructure Total Access by repricing the in-store exchange benefit, while redirecting realized savings toward streaming infrastructure and Redbox-competitive kiosk pilots.**

This is neither "continue as-is" nor "kill." Both extremes are wrong. Continuing the current burn rate is financially unsustainable against $1.1B in debt and quarterly losses. But killing Total Access entirely surrenders the only competitive weapon that has demonstrably hurt Netflix — the sole program in the DVD rental industry's history to stall Netflix's subscriber growth.

The binary framing (continue vs. kill) is itself the strategic error. The board should reject it.

**Critical caveat on savings estimates:** The case packet provides the ~$400M/year total burn and ~$2 per in-store return cost, but does not provide the exchange volume distribution, subscriber-tier utilization data, or price elasticity curves needed to precisely model a restructured cost envelope. The savings figures below are directional estimates that require validation through the 90-day sprint outlined in the Recommended Next Artifact section. The strategic logic — restructure rather than binary continue/kill — holds across a wide range of savings outcomes, but the specific dollar figures should be treated as hypotheses to test, not commitments to bank on.

## Strategic Analysis

### What Total Access Proved

Total Access demonstrated a critical insight: **Blockbuster's store footprint is a distribution network advantage that Netflix cannot replicate.** The hybrid model — order online, return in-store, get an instant swap — created a value proposition Netflix structurally cannot match. The evidence is unambiguous:

- 2M online subscribers added in under 12 months
- Netflix growth stalled for the first time in company history
- Reed Hastings characterized his concern at the highest level ("ichiban scared")
- Blockbuster reached 3M online subscribers against Netflix's 7.5M — a 40% ratio achieved in a fraction of the time

Killing this program tells the market Blockbuster has conceded the online rental space. That concession is irreversible.

### Why the Current Burn Is Unsustainable

The $400M/year incremental cost is not viable given Blockbuster's capital structure:

- $1.1B long-term debt with limited refinancing options in a declining industry
- $85M net loss in Q2 2007 alone — annualized trajectory exceeds $300M
- Each in-store return costs ~$2 in handling + lost rental revenue
- Franchisee revolt threatens the store network that makes Total Access work
- No clear path to subscriber-level profitability at current pricing

Carl Icahn's critique, while motivated by short-term shareholder value, is directionally correct on one point: the current economics don't close. Where Icahn is wrong is in concluding the program should be eliminated rather than restructured.

### The Restructured Model

The restructuring logic rests on a simple premise: the ~$2 per-exchange cost is driven by exchange volume, and volume is controllable through tier design. However, the precise savings depend on unknowns the case packet does not resolve — chiefly, the distribution of exchange frequency across the subscriber base and the churn sensitivity to caps. The phases below define the restructuring architecture; the dollar outcomes are hypotheses requiring the validation sprint described in the next artifact recommendation.

**Phase 1 (Q4 2007 - Q2 2008): Reprice the in-store exchange**
- Limit free in-store exchanges to 3-5 per month (from unlimited) based on subscription tier
- Introduce a premium tier ($24.99/mo) for unlimited exchanges; standard tier ($17.99) gets 5/month
- **Estimated cost reduction: significant but unquantified with available data.** If the heaviest-usage quartile of subscribers drives a disproportionate share of exchange volume (a common pattern in subscription services), tiered caps could substantially reduce per-subscriber costs without affecting the majority. This must be validated via exchange-frequency analysis and A/B pricing tests before committing to specific savings targets.
- Franchisee accommodation: revenue-sharing on exchanges that drive incremental store traffic

**Phase 2 (Q2 2008 - Q4 2008): Streaming investment**
- Redirect realized savings from Phase 1 into streaming content licensing and infrastructure
- The scale of streaming investment depends on validated Phase 1 savings — this is a sequential dependency, not a parallel bet
- Netflix launched Watch Instantly with only 1,000 titles in January 2007 — the window to compete is open but closing
- Broadband at ~50% penetration (2007) heading to 70%+ by 2010 means streaming will be mainstream within 36 months
- Blockbuster's brand awareness and existing customer relationships (47M store visitors) provide a subscriber acquisition advantage Netflix lacks in physical channels
- **Open question:** Can Blockbuster build or acquire credible streaming infrastructure with restructured savings alone, or does this require external capital? The failed Enron Broadband deal in 2000 demonstrates both that Blockbuster recognized the streaming trend early and that execution on streaming has been a persistent weakness.

**Phase 3 (2009+): Portfolio transition**
- DVD-by-mail projected to peak 2010-2012 then decline — Total Access is a bridge, not a destination
- Stores become fulfillment/experience hubs with kiosk components to compete with Redbox's $1/night model
- Streaming becomes the primary growth vector

## Counterarguments Addressed

### "Just kill it and focus on store profitability"
Store-only profitability is a melting ice cube. Redbox is expanding aggressively at $1/night in grocery and convenience stores — locations with higher foot traffic than Blockbuster stores. Netflix is growing online subscribers. A store-only strategy produces 2-3 years of improved margins followed by terminal decline. This is the Kodak path: optimizing the legacy business while the disruption plays out.

### "The debt load makes any investment impossible"
The debt load makes *wasteful* investment impossible. It does not make *strategic* investment impossible. Restructuring Total Access reduces the current burn (by how much depends on validation), and even partial savings can be redirected. The question is not whether Blockbuster can afford to invest — it's whether it can afford not to.

### "Netflix has a head start in streaming"
Netflix launched streaming with 1,000 titles in January 2007. The library is small, the technology is early, and broadband penetration is only 50%. This is not a market that has been won. Blockbuster's advantages — content relationships from being the largest physical retailer, brand recognition, and 47M existing customer touchpoints — are real assets in a streaming land grab. The failed Enron Broadband deal in 2000 shows Blockbuster has recognized this trend before; execution, not vision, was the problem.

### "Icahn and the board won't approve continued losses"
This recommendation reduces losses versus the current trajectory — the magnitude depends on validation work. Frame it as cost discipline with strategic reallocation, not continued bleeding. Icahn's concern is valid on the economics; address the economics and the political objection becomes manageable.

### "The savings estimates are unproven"
This is correct, and this memo acknowledges it explicitly. The recommendation is not "bank on $200M in savings." The recommendation is: (1) the restructuring direction is correct regardless of precise savings magnitude, (2) the strategic logic — bridge via hybrid, invest in streaming — holds across a range of outcomes, and (3) the 90-day validation sprint must quantify the savings before the board commits capital. The alternative — killing Total Access without testing whether restructuring works — discards a proven competitive weapon based on an untested assumption that it cannot be made economical.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

1. **Exchange volume distribution and savings magnitude:** The case packet provides per-unit cost (~$2/exchange) and total burn (~$400M/year) but not the exchange frequency distribution across subscribers. Without this, savings from tiered caps cannot be precisely modeled. This is the most critical evidence gap — the entire restructuring hinges on it. *Needed: exchange-frequency analysis by subscriber cohort, ideally within 30 days.*

2. **Subscriber price elasticity:** How many of the 3M online subscribers chose Total Access specifically for unlimited in-store exchanges? If the answer is >60%, tiered repricing risks significant churn. *Needed: subscriber survey or A/B test on exchange limits before full rollout.*

3. **Streaming content licensing costs:** Can Blockbuster secure streaming rights at competitive rates given its weaker balance sheet versus Netflix? Netflix's scale advantage in content negotiation is unknown but likely real. *Needed: preliminary licensing conversations with major studios.*

4. **Franchisee cooperation:** Will franchisees accept a revenue-sharing model on exchanges, or will they continue to resist? Franchise agreements may limit Blockbuster's ability to mandate participation. *Needed: legal review of franchise agreements and franchisee council negotiation.*

5. **Technology readiness:** Blockbuster's streaming infrastructure is non-existent. Can a credible streaming product be built in 12-18 months, or does this require acquisition? *Needed: technology assessment and build-vs-buy analysis.*

6. **Redbox trajectory:** How fast is Redbox expanding, and at what point does $1/night kiosk rental commoditize the casual rental market that drives Blockbuster store traffic? *Needed: competitive intelligence on Redbox rollout commitments and grocery chain partnerships.*

7. **Refinancing risk:** Can Blockbuster refinance its $1.1B debt given the restructured burn rate, or does the credit market view the company as uninvestable regardless? *Needed: preliminary lender conversations.*
