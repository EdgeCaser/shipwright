# Revised artifact

## Blockbuster Total Access: kill the program in its current national form, preserve only a tightly bounded transition option

## Recommendation
Blockbuster should kill Total Access as a national, burn-heavy growth program now. The board should not continue funding the offer at current economics. Instead, it should move immediately to a containment plan: end the default free-exchange structure, stop scaling for share growth, and keep only a short, tightly measured transition window to determine whether any customer segments can survive without heavy subsidy.

This is still a kill decision, not a continuation decision. The issue is not whether Total Access created real competitive pressure. It did. The issue is whether Blockbuster can afford to keep buying that pressure at the current rate. On the evidence available in Q3 2007, it cannot.

## Core case for killing the current program
Total Access has demonstrated market appeal. It added 2 million subscribers in under 12 months, helped stall Netflix growth for the first time, and showed that Blockbuster's store network could be turned into a differentiated customer offer. That is the strongest fact in favor of continuation.

It is still not enough.

The program is burning roughly $400 million per year while Blockbuster carries about $1.1 billion of long-term debt and has already posted an $85 million Q2 2007 loss. That means the board is not choosing between two equally financeable strategic paths. It is choosing between preserving strategic flexibility and continuing a capital-intensive share war from a weaker balance-sheet position.

The burden of proof therefore belongs to continuation, not retreat. To justify ongoing national-scale funding, management would need evidence that the current losses are buying a durable strategic position that Blockbuster can realistically hold long enough to monetize. The case packet does not provide that evidence. What it does show is that the economics worsen as customers use the signature feature more often: in-store returns cost about $2 each in handling and foregone rental value, franchisees are alienated, and the store base that funds the company is being cannibalized by the transition vehicle itself.

That problem is magnified by timing. Netflix already launched Watch Instantly in January 2007. Broadband penetration is around 50% of US households and projected to exceed 70% by 2010. DVD-by-mail is expected to peak around 2010-2012 and then decline. So even if Total Access can keep hurting Netflix in the short run, Blockbuster is spending heavily to strengthen its position in a format whose strategic window is narrowing.

## Why the strongest counterargument still loses
The best argument for continuing is that Total Access is the first move that genuinely scared Netflix. If Blockbuster retreats, it gives up proven leverage, Netflix resumes subscriber momentum, and the one effective counterpunch disappears.

That argument is strategically serious, but financially incomplete. A tactic that hurts Netflix is only worth preserving if Blockbuster can survive the cost of using it. Netflix had 7.5 million subscribers versus Blockbuster's roughly 3 million online subscribers, and it was already investing into streaming. Blockbuster, by contrast, was trying to fund a national subsidy war while managing debt, store cannibalization, franchisee conflict, and an oncoming low-end attack from Redbox. Continuing Total Access at current scale effectively asks a financially weaker company to outspend a stronger rival in a declining format while the basis of competition is shifting.

That is not a defensible board bet.

## What the board should approve instead
The board should kill the current program, but it should not pretend it already knows the exact optimal replacement design. The evidence does not support that level of precision. The disciplined move is to stop the open-ended burn first, then use a short operating window to answer the missing questions.

Approve a 90-day transition with four actions:
1. End Total Access as a national growth offer and remove the free in-store exchange structure that drives the worst losses.
2. Freeze expansion spending whose purpose is subscriber acquisition rather than profitability or learning.
3. Require management to produce cohort economics, exchange-frequency data, and liquidity/covenant sensitivity within 60 days.
4. Preserve only limited pilots where management can prove contribution-positive or strategically necessary behavior under the revised economics.

This keeps the recommendation falsifiable. If management can show a subset of customers or markets works without heavy subsidy, the board can preserve that slice. If it cannot, the program should be wound down further. What the board should not do is keep funding the existing national model while waiting for that answer.

## Decision Frame
Decision: Kill Total Access in its current national, burn-heavy form; replace it with a 90-day containment and evidence-gathering transition, not a continued share-war posture.

Why this is the right board decision:
- Total Access proved demand and competitive punch, but not financeable durability.
- The current burn is large relative to Blockbuster's debt load and recent losses.
- The market is moving toward streaming, reducing the payoff period for subsidizing hybrid physical behavior.
- Store and franchise conflicts mean Blockbuster is damaging the asset base it still depends on.
- A bounded transition preserves upside only if evidence appears, while current-scale continuation keeps downside fully exposed.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- Which subscriber cohorts, if any, remain contribution-positive after exchange costs and store cannibalization.
- How much liquidity and covenant headroom Blockbuster has if elevated losses persist.
- Whether a revised hybrid offer can retain meaningful competitive value after free exchanges are removed.
- How quickly Blockbuster can secure a credible digital distribution path through build, buy, or partnership.
- Whether franchisee economics can be realigned without deepening channel conflict.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass for the immediate board decision to stop current-scale funding.

Why pass:
- The evidence is sufficient to reject continuation at current economics.
- The evidence is not sufficient to approve a fully specified replacement model, which is why the recommendation includes a bounded diagnostic phase rather than a detailed redesign.
- The main decision today is whether to keep underwriting the national program. On that question, the case is strong enough.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A board-ready 90-day transition plan with:
- cohort and market-level economics for Total Access users
- liquidity and covenant downside analysis under three wind-down scenarios
- store/franchise operating plan separating company-owned and franchise actions
- digital options memo covering streaming build, partner, and buy paths under tight capital constraints
