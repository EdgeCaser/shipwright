# Blockbuster Total Access: kill the hybrid strategy in its current form

## Recommendation
Blockbuster should kill Total Access as currently configured and replace it with a sharply narrower transition plan. The board should not keep funding the program at current burn levels. The strategy has proven that hybrid online + in-store returns can damage Netflix's near-term momentum, but it does not follow that Blockbuster can afford to win that fight. The company is trying to buy share in a declining physical format while carrying a leveraged balance sheet, hostile franchisees, and a store base whose economics are being undermined by the very program meant to save it.

The core board question is not whether Total Access is competitively clever. It is whether Blockbuster can survive the cost structure long enough to convert that advantage into a durable position. On the evidence available in Q3 2007, the answer is no.

## Why kill it now
First, the unit economics are structurally bad at exactly the scale needed to matter. Total Access is burning roughly $400M per year, while Blockbuster already has $1.1B in long-term debt and posted an $85M net loss in Q2 2007 alone. A strategy that requires ongoing balance-sheet tolerance at that magnitude is not a competitive moat; it is a financing bet. Blockbuster does not have Netflix's cleaner business model or capital flexibility to sustain a long subsidy war.

Second, the apparent success is tactically real but strategically shallow. Total Access added 2M subscribers in under a year and helped stall Netflix growth for the first time. That proves the offer is attractive. It does not prove the offer creates durable shareholder value. The program depends on expensive store interactions, free exchange behavior, and cannibalization of store rental economics. In other words, the more customers use the feature that differentiates Blockbuster, the more Blockbuster damages the economics of the legacy asset base that still funds the company.

Third, the industry is already shifting under the company. DVD-by-mail is projected to peak around 2010-2012 and then decline. Broadband penetration is around 50% of US households in 2007 and expected to exceed 70% by 2010. Netflix has already launched streaming in January 2007. The initial catalog is small, but the strategic direction is obvious: physical rental is becoming transitional. Total Access doubles down on a hybrid physical model precisely when the winning player is investing to make physical logistics less relevant.

Fourth, Blockbuster's store footprint is not a clean advantage. The 5,700-store network helps make in-store returns convenient, but it also locks the company into a cost structure and political system that conflict with online transition. Franchisees are openly resistant because Total Access cannibalizes their economics. That means the company is not just fighting Netflix and Redbox; it is fighting its own channel. A strategy that requires internal civil war is not a strategy the board should scale.

Fifth, a continued burn-heavy posture increases the chance that Blockbuster loses to multiple formats at once. Netflix is attacking from the future with streaming. Redbox is attacking from the low end with $1-per-night convenience. Total Access sits awkwardly between them: too expensive to beat Redbox on value and too tied to physical media to beat Netflix on trajectory.

## Strongest counterarguments, and why they do not change the decision
The strongest argument for continuing is that Total Access is the first move that genuinely scared Netflix. That matters. If Blockbuster cuts back now, it may surrender the one weapon that has demonstrably slowed the market leader.

That argument still fails because scaring a rival is not the same as building a financeable strategy. Blockbuster has only about 3M online subscribers versus Netflix's 7.5M total subscribers, and it is spending at a rate that is disproportionate to its financial capacity. If the cost to close the gap is persistent nine-figure annual burn, the board is effectively choosing competitive theater over solvency.

A second argument is that stores can be repurposed as a distribution and customer-acquisition advantage. That is directionally plausible, but Total Access in its current form does not repurpose the stores efficiently; it uses them to subsidize behavior that destroys store-level economics. If the board wants to preserve optionality in the footprint, it should do so through a redesigned, limited hybrid model, not by continuing an open-ended subsidy.

A third argument is that withdrawing now will damage brand momentum and signal retreat. That is true in the short run. But the worse signal would be persisting until liquidity pressure forces a more disorderly retreat later, after even more value has been destroyed.

## What Blockbuster should do instead
Kill Total Access at current levels immediately.

Replace it with a controlled transition plan:
- End free in-store exchange economics that create the worst per-transaction losses.
- Keep online subscription only in segments and geographies where contribution economics can be positive without heavy subsidy.
- Reframe stores around cash generation, used-disc monetization, and selective convenience services rather than as the centerpiece of a national anti-Netflix war.
- Allocate scarce capital toward option value in digital distribution partnerships and streaming capability, even if modest at first.
- Develop a direct response to Redbox's convenience/value attack rather than assuming the legacy store base can defend the low end.

This is not a recommendation to abandon transition. It is a recommendation to stop funding the wrong transition vehicle.

## Decision Frame
Decision: Kill Total Access in its current burn-heavy form and replace it with a narrower, economically disciplined transition strategy.

Why this is the right board decision:
- The program demonstrates demand but not sustainable economics.
- Balance-sheet constraints make a prolonged subsidy battle untenable.
- Market evolution toward streaming reduces the payoff period for doubling down on hybrid physical rental.
- Internal channel conflict makes scaling execution unusually fragile.
- Continuing at current levels risks destroying the legacy cash engine before a viable digital future exists.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- Precise cohort economics for Total Access subscribers versus standard online subscribers.
- Whether any customer segments show positive lifetime value after in-store exchange costs.
- Liquidity headroom and covenant sensitivity if the current burn rate persists for 4-8 more quarters.
- The realistic cost, timeline, and content constraints for a streaming pivot from Blockbuster's starting position.
- Whether store footprint can be restructured fast enough to avoid ongoing channel conflict with franchisees.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass for board decision on strategic direction.

Why pass:
- The evidence is sufficient to judge that current-scale continuation is financially disproportionate to likely strategic payoff.
- The major uncertainty is not whether the current model is expensive; it is whether a narrower transition model can preserve option value.
- That uncertainty does not block the immediate decision to stop the current burn.
