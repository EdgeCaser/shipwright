# Revised artifact

## Recommendation
Kill Total Access in its current form. Do not continue funding the hybrid strategy at current subsidy levels. Preserve online as a smaller strategic option, but end the open-ended subsidy attached to free in-store returns and reset the business around liquidity, debt capacity, and a narrower transition path.

## Core Judgment
Total Access proved that Blockbuster found a real competitive wedge. It added about 2 million subscribers in under a year, brought Blockbuster online to roughly 3 million subscribers, and coincided with Netflix's first visible growth stall. That matters. The program is not failing in the market.

The board's question, however, is not whether the offer is attractive. It is whether Blockbuster can rationally finance and operationalize that offer long enough for it to become a durable advantage. On that question, the answer is no.

The decisive issue is not simply that the program burns about $400 million annually. It is that Blockbuster is being asked to fund that burn from a leveraged, already-loss-making base: roughly $1.1 billion of long-term debt and an $85 million Q2 2007 loss. A company in that position cannot treat a shrinking-format land grab as if it were venture-funded optionality. To justify continuation, the board would need evidence that another 12 to 18 months of subsidy produces one of three outcomes: clearly improving unit economics, a believable path to category leadership, or a bridge to a defined digital end-state. The case packet supports none of those.

## Why Retrenchment Is Still Correct Despite The Counterfactual Risk
The strongest critique of killing the program is valid: Blockbuster does not have quantified churn data for a scaled-back offer, so the retreat path may destroy substantial online revenue and weaken the brand's only demonstrated wedge against Netflix. That uncertainty is real.

But that uncertainty cuts against continuation more than against retrenchment. If Blockbuster lacks evidence on retention under a repriced offer, it also lacks evidence that the current subsidy ever converges to acceptable economics. The known facts are stronger on the continuation side's risk profile than on the retreat side's: each in-store return carries roughly $2 in handling and lost rental economics; usage makes the model worse, not better; franchisees are hostile; Icahn-backed board opposition is entrenched; Redbox is pressuring store traffic; and Netflix is already investing in streaming while DVD-by-mail is projected to peak in 2010-2012 and then decline. Continuing therefore requires underwriting a very expensive race through a narrowing physical-media window with no proof that scale fixes the core defect.

The forward projection that Blockbuster could reach 5 to 6 million subscribers in 12 to 18 months is strategically interesting but not sufficient. Even if achieved, Blockbuster would still be spending heavily to gain share in a format heading toward maturity while the category leader is building the next delivery model. Subscriber growth alone is not victory if the acquired subscribers sit on structurally negative unit economics and the organization financing them is balance-sheet constrained.

## Why "Save Cash For Digital" Is Not Hand-Waving
Blockbuster does not currently possess a credible streaming plan. That is precisely why the board should not spend scarce capital pretending Total Access solves the digital transition. Total Access is a physical-retail counterpunch, not a digital migration strategy. The case for killing the current program is not that saved capital automatically creates a winning streaming business. It is that continuing the current burn reduces the odds that Blockbuster has any capital, borrowing capacity, or governance cohesion left to pursue partnerships, acquisitions, or narrower digital experiments at all.

In other words, the choice is not between a proven growth engine and vague optionality. It is between:

1. continuing to finance a successful but structurally loss-making physical hybrid model in a declining format, or
2. preserving enterprise survivability and a smaller online foothold while the board redefines the transition plan.

For this board, with this balance sheet, the second choice is the only defensible one.

## Strongest Counterargument
Total Access is the only move that has visibly scared Netflix. If Blockbuster retreats now, it may surrender the one weapon that actually worked, cede online leadership permanently, and accelerate store decline without a replacement engine.

That argument is strongest if the board believes Blockbuster can survive another multi-quarter subsidy war and that scale will eventually improve economics fast enough to matter. The record here does not support either belief. The economics deteriorate with usage, internal alignment is weak, and the market window for winning a discs-based war is shortening. The company may indeed lose online momentum by scaling back, but preserving momentum at any cost is not the same as preserving enterprise value.

## Board Action
1. End Total Access in its current unlimited-subsidy form.
2. Eliminate or sharply cap the free in-store exchange benefit that drives negative unit economics.
3. Keep online, but reprice and redesign it around profitable or near-profitable customer segments rather than maximum gross subscriber growth.
4. Create an explicit franchise compensation and channel-conflict reset instead of asking stores to absorb online cannibalization invisibly.
5. Ring-fence capital for a 12-month digital options program focused on partnership, acquisition, or tightly scoped streaming tests, not a full-scale build.

## Decision Frame
Decision: Kill the current Total Access strategy at current funding levels.

Rationale: Total Access demonstrated demand and competitive relevance, but the board lacks evidence that continued subsidy produces sustainable economics, durable leadership, or a credible bridge to digital. Given Blockbuster's debt load, operating losses, franchise conflict, and the narrowing life of the DVD-by-mail market, continuing at current levels is an asymmetric downside bet.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- We still lack cohort-level contribution margin data net of in-store return frequency and lost rental revenue.
- We do not know the precise churn effect from eliminating or capping free exchanges.
- We do not have a quantified downside model for a scaled-back online offer versus the current subsidy path.
- We lack a concrete partnership or acquisition map for streaming capability within 12 to 24 months.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if the board's objective is to maximize the odds of enterprise survival while preserving a constrained online option and the capacity to pursue a narrower transition strategy.

Fail if the board is willing to accept prolonged losses, balance-sheet stress, and internal channel conflict in order to keep pressing for online share in a declining physical format.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A 12-month capital-allocation and transition memo comparing three specific post-Total-Access designs: capped-hybrid, online-only repricing, and partner-led digital entry, each with churn, cash burn, franchise impact, and covenant-risk ranges.
