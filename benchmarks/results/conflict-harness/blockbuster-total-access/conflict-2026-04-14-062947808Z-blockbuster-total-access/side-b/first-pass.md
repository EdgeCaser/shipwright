# Strategic Recommendation: Restructure Total Access as a Bridge to Digital — Do Not Kill, Do Not Continue at Current Burn

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Should Blockbuster continue funding Total Access at ~$400M/year, kill it to protect near-term profitability, or pursue a third path?

**Recommendation: Neither continue at current burn nor kill. Restructure Total Access into a tiered, self-funding hybrid program while redirecting $150-200M/year into streaming and digital distribution.**

The binary framing — continue or kill — is itself the trap. Killing Total Access surrenders the only competitive weapon that has demonstrably hurt Netflix. Continuing at $400M/year on a $1.1B debt load with $85M quarterly losses is financial suicide. The correct move is to restructure Total Access to reduce burn by 50-60% while preserving its competitive core, and redirect freed capital toward the platform shift that will determine who survives past 2012.

---

## Strategic Analysis

### What Total Access Proved

Total Access is the most successful competitive response in Blockbuster's history. The evidence is unambiguous:

- **2M online subscribers added in under 12 months** — a growth rate Netflix took years to achieve at equivalent scale
- **Netflix growth stalled for the first time**, forcing Netflix to cut prices and increase marketing spend
- **Reed Hastings privately admitted fear** of the program — a signal that the competitive damage was real, not cosmetic
- **The hybrid model exploits Blockbuster's actual structural advantage**: 5,700 physical locations that Netflix cannot replicate

Killing this program outright tells the market — and Netflix — that Blockbuster has conceded the online transition. Subscriber churn would be immediate. Netflix would reprice upward and reinvest the margin into streaming, accelerating the timeline against which Blockbuster has no answer.

### Why Current Burn Is Unsustainable

The math is disqualifying at current levels:

- **$400M/year incremental burn** against **$1.1B long-term debt** means Total Access alone consumes roughly 36% of total debt capacity annually in cash burn
- **$85M Q2 loss** means Blockbuster is losing money even before the full annualized Total Access cost is absorbed
- **~$2 per in-store return** in handling + cannibalized rental revenue creates a structural cost that scales with success — every new subscriber makes the unit economics worse
- **Franchisee revolt** is not merely political; it threatens the store network that makes the hybrid model possible. If franchisees defect or litigate, the physical advantage evaporates
- **Icahn's board presence** means capital markets patience is already exhausted. Continued $400M burns will trigger a proxy fight or forced restructuring within 12-18 months

### The Real Threat: Streaming, Not Netflix-by-Mail

The most critical evidence in this decision is not about Total Access at all:

- **Netflix launched Watch Instantly in January 2007** with only 1,000 titles — this is a beachhead, not a product
- **Broadband penetration at ~50% heading to 70%+ by 2010** means the addressable market for streaming doubles in 3 years
- **DVD-by-mail projected to peak 2010-2012 then decline** means Total Access is competing for a shrinking prize
- **Redbox kiosk expansion** attacks the impulse-rental segment at $1/night — eroding store traffic from below while streaming erodes it from above

Spending $400M/year to win the DVD-by-mail war is like spending $400M to become the world's best telegraph company in 1920. The war that matters is streaming. Every dollar spent on disc logistics is a dollar not spent on the only business that will exist in 2015.

## Recommended Action Plan

### Phase 1: Restructure Total Access (Q4 2007 — Q1 2008)

1. **Tier the program**: Introduce a premium tier ($24.99/mo) that retains unlimited in-store exchanges, and a standard tier ($17.99/mo) with 5 in-store exchanges per month. This preserves the hybrid value proposition while capping the per-subscriber cost hemorrhage.
2. **Cap in-store exchange costs**: Negotiate with corporate stores to limit free exchange inventory to catalog titles (not new releases), reducing the ~$2/return cost by eliminating the highest-value cannibalization.
3. **Franchisee revenue share**: Offer franchisees a per-exchange fee ($0.50-0.75) funded by the tier pricing, converting them from opponents to participants.
4. **Target: reduce Total Access burn from $400M to $150-175M/year** while retaining 70%+ of current subscribers through the tier structure.

### Phase 2: Streaming Investment (Q1 2008 — Q4 2009)

1. **Redirect $150-200M/year** into streaming infrastructure and content licensing
2. **Leverage store network as streaming onramp**: In-store kiosks for streaming demos, bundle streaming access with in-store membership cards, use stores as customer acquisition at near-zero marginal cost
3. **Pursue content deals aggressively**: Blockbuster's existing studio relationships for physical distribution are a licensing advantage Netflix does not have
4. **Target: launch competitive streaming product by Q3 2008** — 18 months behind Netflix but with a physical distribution advantage for the transition period

### Phase 3: Store Network Rationalization (2009-2010)

1. **Close 20-30% of lowest-performing locations** to reduce fixed costs
2. **Convert remaining stores to hybrid entertainment hubs**: streaming access points, game rentals, merchandise — reducing dependence on disc rental revenue
3. **Sell or franchise marginal locations** to generate cash for debt reduction

## Counterarguments Addressed

**"Just kill Total Access and focus on stores — they're still profitable."**
Store-only profitability is a mirage. Redbox is undercutting store pricing at $1/night with zero real estate cost. Netflix streaming will eliminate the convenience advantage of locations. Store profits are declining on a 3-5 year trajectory regardless. Killing Total Access doesn't save the stores — it just means Blockbuster dies with higher near-term margins.

**"Double down on Total Access — it's working, Netflix is scared."**
Netflix is scared of Total Access *and* investing in streaming simultaneously. They can afford to lose the DVD-by-mail war if they win streaming. Blockbuster cannot afford to win DVD-by-mail at $400M/year and have nothing when the format dies. Winning a war over a dying medium is not victory.

**"Blockbuster can't do streaming — the Enron deal failed in 2000."**
The Enron deal failed because broadband infrastructure didn't exist in 2000. At 50% penetration heading to 70%, the infrastructure constraint has been removed. The question is no longer technical feasibility but execution speed and content licensing.

**"The debt load makes any investment impossible."**
The debt load makes $400M/year impossible. It does not make $150-200M/year impossible, especially if the restructured Total Access is cash-flow neutral at tier pricing. The restructuring *creates* the capital for streaming investment without additional borrowing.
