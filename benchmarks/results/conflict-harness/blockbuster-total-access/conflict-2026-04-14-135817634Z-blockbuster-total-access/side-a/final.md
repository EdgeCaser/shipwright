# Blockbuster Board Memo: Kill Total Access in Its Current Form

Blockbuster should kill Total Access as currently designed. The board should end the free in-store return subsidy, retain a narrower online business, and use the savings first to stabilize liquidity and preserve strategic option value. The case for killing the program does not depend on Blockbuster being able to build a full streaming business immediately. It depends on a simpler fact: a leveraged retailer cannot keep spending roughly $400M per year to win share in a physical-rental format whose strategic window is limited.

Total Access proved that customers value the hybrid offer. Adding about 2M subscribers in under a year, reaching roughly 3M online subscribers, and visibly stalling Netflix growth shows the program was competitively potent. But potency is not the same as sustainability. Blockbuster is carrying about $1.1B in long-term debt and already posted an $85M Q2 2007 loss. In that context, continuing a program with roughly $400M of annual incremental burn is not an aggressive investment posture; it is balance-sheet deterioration. The board should not confuse evidence of customer demand with evidence of a viable business model.

The most important strategic distinction is between preserving an online foothold and preserving the current subsidy. Blockbuster should do the first and stop doing the second. Each in-store return costs about $2 in handling and foregone rental economics, and those costs scale directly with usage. The offer also creates internal conflict because franchisees absorb traffic and margin disruption from a corporate program that does not support store-level economics. A strategy that requires both heavy cash burn at headquarters and economic pain across the franchise base is structurally unstable even before considering external competition.

The strongest counterargument is that killing or shrinking Total Access gives Netflix room to recover DVD-by-mail momentum during the 2007-2012 window, when discs still matter. That risk is real. But continuing unchanged is a worse bet because it asks Blockbuster to fund a long negative-margin war from a weaker capital position than Netflix. Netflix has 7.5M subscribers to Blockbuster's roughly 3M online subscribers, and it has already launched Watch Instantly in January 2007. Streaming is early, but the direction of travel matters: broadband penetration is around 50% of U.S. households in 2007 and projected to exceed 70% by 2010, while DVD-by-mail is expected to peak around 2010-2012 and then decline. Blockbuster does not need to assume it can become a streaming leader immediately to recognize that overfunding a hybrid DVD subsidy reduces its ability to respond to that shift at all.

That point matters because Blockbuster's realistic near-term objective is not to outbuild Netflix in streaming in 2007. There is no evidence in the case that it has a ready technology stack, licensing position, or partner path that would justify a major self-build commitment today. The right conclusion is therefore narrower than a full digital pivot thesis: Blockbuster should stop burning cash in Total Access so it can protect debt capacity, maintain a scaled but disciplined online presence, and explore low-commitment digital options from a position of greater financial control. Preserving optionality is strategically superior to exhausting capital on a subsidized physical advantage with a shortening runway.

The board should therefore take five actions immediately:

1. End the free in-store return and free-rental subsidy embedded in Total Access.
2. Retain the online subscription business, but reprice and redesign it for positive contribution margin.
3. Recast stores as selective acquisition, service, and brand-presence assets rather than the economic engine of the online offer.
4. Use the savings primarily for debt protection, liquidity stabilization, and a limited options portfolio of partnership scouting, licensing discussions, and test-and-learn digital experiments rather than a large assumed streaming build.
5. Create a franchisee alignment plan so the transition does not further damage store-level incentives.

This recommendation gives up some near-term headline growth. Some subscribers acquired mainly for the in-store return benefit will churn, and Netflix may regain momentum. But that is still the superior choice because it trades a visible but uneconomic growth story for a survivable transition posture. The board's job is not to maximize subscriber additions in a vacuum; it is to allocate capital so the company can endure the category shift ahead.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Kill Total Access in its current subsidized form, keep a narrower online business, and redeploy savings first to balance-sheet protection and only second to limited strategic option-building.

Why this is the right call:
- The current program proves demand but fails the capital-structure test.
- Its economics worsen with use because the customer benefit is tied to costly in-store returns.
- Franchisee resistance makes the model harder to scale and govern.
- The physical rental market still matters, but its remaining strategic window is too short to justify open-ended subsidy.
- Blockbuster does not need a fully credible streaming plan today to know it cannot afford to keep weakening itself in order to fight yesterday's format war.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- How many Total Access subscribers remain profitable after removing the in-store return subsidy.
- Elasticity of demand if the program is repriced or stripped of free-return benefits.
- Whether any credible streaming or digital-distribution partner path is available to Blockbuster in 2007 on acceptable terms.
- Which portions of the store base are strategic assets versus fixed-cost liabilities under a narrower hybrid model.
- How much franchisee compensation would be required to maintain network stability during the reset.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if:
- The board prioritizes liquidity and debt protection over subsidized subscriber growth.
- Management can remove the costly return benefit quickly and communicate a narrower offer clearly.
- The company preserves a scaled online presence instead of retreating to pure store defense.
- Any digital investment is staged as option exploration with explicit spending caps and milestones.

Fail if:
- Leadership preserves growth targets that still rely on uneconomic subsidy.
- Savings are redirected into a large speculative streaming build without proof of partner access, content rights, or technical feasibility.
- The company cuts Total Access but does not redesign the online offer to positive contribution margin.
- Franchisee conflict is ignored and the transition fractures execution.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A 12-month transition plan covering three decisions: the redesigned online offer and unit economics, store-network segmentation and franchisee compensation, and a bounded strategic-options memo evaluating partnership-led digital paths with explicit spend ceilings and kill criteria.
