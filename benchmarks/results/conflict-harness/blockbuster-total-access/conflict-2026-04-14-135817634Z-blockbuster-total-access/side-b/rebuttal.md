# Critique: finding-2

- Target Side: side_a
- Target Claim IDs: side_a-claim-1, side_a-claim-3
- Attack Type: evidence_gap
- Severity: high

## Claim Under Attack
Blockbuster should kill Total Access because the $400M annual burn is incompatible with a leveraged balance sheet, and capital should be redirected toward a streaming/digital transition instead.

## Evidence Or Reason
Side A treats killing Total Access and funding a streaming transition as a single coherent move, but provides no evidence that Blockbuster — a leveraged physical retailer with a failed 2000 Enron streaming attempt, no streaming technology stack, no content licensing relationships, and no engineering talent pipeline — could credibly build or buy a streaming capability with the savings. The memo acknowledges this as an unknown ('the real timetable and capital requirement for a credible streaming offer') yet treats the streaming pivot as an achievable destination rather than an equally speculative bet. If the $400M saved from Total Access cannot produce a viable streaming product, then killing the one program demonstrably hurting Netflix hands the DVD-by-mail market to Netflix uncontested during the 2007-2012 period when DVDs still dominate — giving Netflix the cash flow and subscriber base to fund its own streaming transition unopposed. Side A's framing implicitly assumes a streaming alternative exists for Blockbuster to fund; the case evidence suggests no such option was remotely actionable in 2007. The memo recommends 'ring-fencing a smaller investment pool for streaming partnerships' without identifying a single viable partner, technology path, or content licensing strategy, making the proposed reallocation aspirational rather than evidence-based.
