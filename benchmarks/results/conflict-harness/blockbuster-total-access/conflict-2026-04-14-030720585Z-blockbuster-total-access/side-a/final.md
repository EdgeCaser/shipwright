# Revised artifact

## Recommendation
Blockbuster should **kill Total Access in its current broad, subsidized form** and shift to a narrower transition plan that stops the open-ended hybrid burn. The program proved that hybrid convenience can pressure Netflix, but the board's decision in Q3 2007 is not whether the concept has merit; it is whether Blockbuster can afford and execute it at scale. On the evidence provided, the answer is no.

The decisive fact is the mismatch between **strategic promise** and **organizational capacity**. Total Access added roughly **2 million subscribers in under a year** and contributed to the first visible stall in Netflix growth. That is real evidence of customer appeal. But it is being purchased at roughly **$400 million per year** of incremental burn while Blockbuster carries **$1.1 billion of long-term debt** and has already posted an **$85 million Q2 2007 net loss**. At the same time, the company is managing a CEO transition, franchisee hostility, and investor pressure from a board faction that already views the online losses as value-destructive. That is not a platform for a delicate multi-front turnaround. It is a setup for losing both the transition business and the legacy business.

## Why killing the current program is the right decision

### 1. The economics are not a temporary blemish; they are the central board constraint
A strategy that burns about **$400 million annually** against this balance sheet cannot be justified by saying it is directionally correct. Directionally correct strategies still fail when the company funding them runs out of time, cash, or political support. The board is not choosing in abstract between growth and retrenchment. It is choosing whether to keep underwriting a subsidy level that the case itself describes as unsustainable.

### 2. The proposed middle path requires execution capacity the case does not support
A credible "continue but redesign" plan would require Blockbuster to do several hard things at once: reprice the subsidy, identify profitable cohorts, realign franchise incentives, and establish a digital roadmap while under financial and governance stress. The case provides no evidence that Blockbuster had quantified cohort economics, liquidity headroom, franchise compensation mechanics, or viable streaming options on a timetable short enough to rescue the program. In this context, "continue but fix it" is not disciplined strategy; it is an execution bet without evidence.

### 3. Preserving the current hybrid model could destroy the option value it is supposed to buy
The bullish argument for Total Access is that it buys time for the migration to digital. But a bridge that consumes too much capital can collapse before the destination is reachable. **Netflix had already launched Watch Instantly in January 2007**, broadband penetration was around **50% of U.S. households** and rising, and DVD-by-mail was projected to peak within a few years. Blockbuster still needed resources to respond to streaming and to defend against **Redbox's** low-cost physical alternative. Continuing a loss-heavy hybrid at current scale risks exhausting the company's flexibility before those next moves are funded.

### 4. The store footprint is not automatically an advantage
Blockbuster's **5,700 U.S. stores** and **47 million store-visit customers** are meaningful assets only if they can be converted into profitable cross-channel behavior. The case evidence cuts the other way: franchisees are hostile because the program undermines store economics, and each in-store return imposes handling cost and lost rental revenue. That means the footprint is acting less like a flywheel and more like an expensive conflict surface. The board should not keep paying to prove that a contested asset base can be integrated later.

## Strongest counterarguments, and why they do not change the recommendation

### Counterargument: Killing Total Access hands the initiative back to Netflix
This is true in the narrow sense. Total Access was the first move that visibly disrupted Netflix. But the board cannot confuse a successful attack with a sustainable business. If Blockbuster lacks the capital and operating capacity to hold the attack, persistence becomes self-harm. Preserving a weaker balance sheet does not beat Netflix either, but it at least keeps open the possibility of targeted digital moves instead of doubling down on a structurally loss-making subsidy.

### Counterargument: Retreating to stores is a harvest strategy
Also true, if the company simply declares victory for stores and does nothing else. That is not the recommended path. The recommendation is to **kill the current Total Access model**, preserve only selective low-burn online capabilities, and redeploy scarce capital toward options that do not require subsidizing store-mail interactions at scale. The board should treat stores as a cash-management problem and digital as a focused investment problem, not force both into a single expensive hybrid.

### Counterargument: Streaming is early, so Blockbuster still has time
Streaming was early in 2007, which is exactly why scarce capital matters. Early markets reward firms that can survive long enough to iterate. A heavily indebted company burning hundreds of millions to defend a transitional DVD offer is weakening its ability to participate in the next market when timing matters most.

## Board action
The board should:
1. End Total Access in its current broad form immediately rather than continue the open-ended subsidy.
2. Retain only online elements that can stand on materially improved unit economics without free in-store exchange behavior.
3. Ring-fence capital for a smaller digital-option program, including partnership exploration, instead of funding a full hybrid redesign.
4. Stabilize franchise relations by removing the corporate program feature that is directly cannibalizing store economics.

## Decision Frame
**Decision:** Kill Total Access as a scaled hybrid strategy in Q3 2007; do not continue funding it at current levels.

**Why this passes the threshold:**
- The program's customer traction is real, but the cost structure is incompatible with Blockbuster's debt load, quarterly losses, and governance instability.
- The evidence does not show that Blockbuster can execute the required redesign fast enough to convert traction into a sustainable model.
- Preserving capital and organizational focus is more valuable than extending an unproven rescue attempt.

**What would invalidate this decision:**
- Clear evidence of sufficient liquidity and covenant headroom to fund a transition period.
- Demonstrated cohort economics showing a near-term path to sharply lower burn.
- Concrete proof that franchisee alignment and digital execution were operationally ready, not aspirational.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- Detailed liquidity and covenant headroom are not provided.
- Cohort-level retention, churn, and contribution margin for Total Access subscribers are not provided.
- The cost, timing, and feasibility of Blockbuster streaming partnership or build options are not specified.
- The precise magnitude of franchisee cannibalization is not quantified.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
**Pass if:**
- The board prioritizes solvency, execution realism, and strategic optionality over short-term competitive signaling.
- Management can preserve a narrower online presence without recreating the same subsidy loop.
- Capital freed from Total Access is explicitly redirected to focused digital options rather than absorbed by general decline.

**Fail if:**
- The board believes customer traction alone justifies continued large-scale losses.
- Management cannot distinguish between killing the current program and abandoning all digital transition efforts.
- The company continues subsidizing hybrid behavior without proof of a path to viable unit economics.
