# Continue Total Access, but immediately redesign it from an open-ended subsidy into a bridge-to-digital strategy

## Recommendation
Blockbuster should **continue** Total Access in Q3 2007, but **not at current economics**. The board should keep funding the hybrid strategy for 12-18 months while restructuring it around tighter unit economics, franchisee alignment, and a rapid path into digital delivery. Killing it now would protect near-term store P&L at the exact moment the company has finally found a credible way to slow Netflix and reactivate its customer base.

The core judgment is straightforward: Total Access is expensive, but it is the first strategy that has materially changed the competitive trajectory in Blockbuster's favor. A company with 5,700 stores and 47 million store-visit customers cannot win the next era by retreating to a declining retail base. It needs a bridge from stores to subscription to streaming. Total Access is that bridge.

## Why continuing is the right decision

### 1. Total Access is the first move that has produced real competitive impact
The program added roughly **2 million subscribers in under a year**, taking Blockbuster online to about **3 million subscribers**. More importantly, it coincided with the **first visible stall in Netflix's growth**, and Netflix leadership reportedly viewed the offer as a serious threat. That is rare strategic evidence: the program is not merely generating activity, it is changing competitor behavior.

Boards should not kill the one initiative that has demonstrably weakened the category leader unless the economics are unrecoverable. Here, the economics are bad today, but the strategic signal is strong enough to justify redesign rather than abandonment.

### 2. Retreating to stores is not a real strategy; it is a harvest plan
The bear case is financially serious: about **$400 million per year** of incremental burn, **$1.1 billion** of long-term debt, and an **$85 million Q2 2007 loss**. But the alternative is not stability. The alternative is doubling down on a store-centric model while:
- DVD-by-mail is expected to peak in roughly **2010-2012** and then decline.
- **Netflix is already investing in streaming**, having launched Watch Instantly in **January 2007**.
- **Broadband penetration is around 50% of U.S. households in 2007** and projected to exceed **70% by 2010**, making digital substitution more plausible each year.
- **Redbox** is expanding with a radically lower-cost physical rental model.

If Blockbuster kills Total Access to preserve store profitability, it is effectively choosing to defend a business under attack from both above and below: Netflix on subscriptions and streaming, Redbox on low-cost physical convenience.

### 3. The store footprint is only valuable if it is used as a conversion asset
Blockbuster's **5,700 U.S. stores** are either a transition advantage or a stranded-cost burden. Total Access is one of the few models that turns the footprint into an acquisition and service network: faster gratification, in-store returns, local customer touchpoints, and cross-channel convenience.

Without a hybrid offer, the stores revert to a fixed-cost legacy asset. With a redesigned hybrid model, they can still function as a customer acquisition funnel, exchange hub, and migration path into digital subscriptions. The board should evaluate stores not on standalone historical margin, but on whether they help transfer customers into the next consumption model before those customers are lost.

### 4. Blockbuster still has a realistic window to build a migration path that Netflix and Redbox do not fully match
Blockbuster is behind in total subscribers: **Netflix has 7.5 million versus Blockbuster's ~3 million online**. That gap matters. But the fact that Total Access slowed Netflix at all shows Blockbuster still has strategic leverage. The relevant question is not whether Blockbuster has already won online; it has not. The question is whether it has found a differentiated wedge strong enough to buy time for the next transition. The answer is yes.

That wedge should now be used to:
- retain and grow subscribers with the hybrid offer,
- reduce the most destructive subsidy mechanics,
- use stores to migrate households into a digital relationship,
- accelerate streaming capability through partnership, acquisition, or internal build.

## What the board should actually do
Continue Total Access, but change the operating model immediately.

1. **Cap the subsidy**
Replace unlimited or overly generous in-store exchange economics with plan designs that limit free exchange frequency, segment heavy users, and reduce the effective ~$2 per return handling burden.

2. **Redesign around profitable cohorts**
Prioritize customer segments where hybrid convenience drives retention and where store proximity is high. Stop treating every subscriber as equally attractive.

3. **Align franchisees economically**
Create compensation or transfer-payment mechanisms so franchisees are not punished for participating in a corporate strategic shift. Franchise hostility is not a side issue; it is a structural execution risk.

4. **Commit to a digital option now**
The board should require a concrete streaming roadmap within two quarters. Total Access only makes sense if it is explicitly a bridge to digital, not a permanent subsidy for mail-order DVDs.

5. **Set a hard performance gate**
Approve a 12-18 month continuation window with board-level milestones on subscriber growth, churn, exchange-rate economics, and cash burn reduction. Continue the strategy, but under disciplined thresholds.

## Strongest counterarguments, and why they do not change the recommendation

### Counterargument: The balance sheet cannot support this burn
This is the strongest objection. A company with **$1.1 billion in debt** and recent quarterly losses cannot absorb unlimited strategic experimentation. If Total Access remains a $400 million annual burn with no redesign, it should indeed be cut.

But that is an argument against the **current cost structure**, not against the **strategy itself**. The board's task is not to choose between reckless continuation and immediate surrender. It is to preserve the only credible competitive response while imposing financial discipline.

### Counterargument: Stores are being cannibalized and franchisees are revolting
True. But cannibalization is unavoidable when consumer behavior is shifting. The key question is whether Blockbuster would rather cannibalize itself into a viable new model or let competitors do it. Franchise conflict is a governance and incentive-design problem, not proof that the strategy is wrong.

### Counterargument: Netflix is already ahead in streaming, so Blockbuster is too late
Blockbuster is late, but not yet irrelevant. In Q3 2007, streaming remains early: Netflix launched with about **1,000 titles**, broadband adoption is still incomplete, and physical rental still matters. This is precisely why Total Access should continue now: it buys time and customer relevance during the transition window. Killing it would shorten that window further.

## Bottom line
The board should **continue Total Access**, but recast it as a **time-bounded transition strategy** with redesigned economics and an explicit digital destination. Killing the program now would improve optics and possibly near-term cash burn, but it would amount to strategic retreat into a declining store base while stronger competitors define the future.

## Decision Frame
**Decision:** Continue Total Access for 12-18 months, but redesign immediately; do not continue at current economics.

**Why this passes the threshold:**
- It has already shown real competitive effect through rapid subscriber growth and visible pressure on Netflix.
- It is the only credible bridge between Blockbuster's store footprint and the coming digital market.
- The alternative is not a healthy core business; it is managed decline.

**What would invalidate this decision:**
- Inability to materially reduce exchange-related subsidy costs within two quarters.
- No actionable streaming roadmap within two quarters.
- Continued subscriber gains that are low-quality, high-churn, or unprofitable after redesign.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- Cohort-level economics by subscriber type are not provided.
- Churn, retention, and lifetime value of Total Access subscribers are not provided.
- Store-level and franchise-level cannibalization effects are directionally clear but not quantified here.
- Streaming capability options, costs, and time-to-market for Blockbuster are not specified.
- Liquidity headroom and covenant constraints are not specified, which could tighten the feasible continuation window.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
**Pass if:**
- Board accepts a redesigned, gated continuation rather than unconditional continuation.
- Management can present a concrete cost-reduction and franchise-alignment plan within one quarter.
- Management commits to a digital roadmap with named milestones.

**Fail if:**
- The company insists on maintaining the current subsidy model unchanged.
- Balance-sheet constraints are so tight that even a 12-month bridge cannot be funded.
- Leadership treats Total Access as a standalone DVD strategy rather than a migration mechanism.
