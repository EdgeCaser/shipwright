# Strategic Recommendation: Restructure Total Access as a Bridge to Digital — Do Not Kill, Do Not Continue at Current Burn

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Should Blockbuster continue funding Total Access at ~$400M/year, kill it entirely, or restructure it?

**Recommendation: Restructure and reduce burn, while redirecting freed capital toward streaming capability — but gate the restructuring on rapid validation of cost-reduction assumptions before committing to specific savings targets.**

The binary framing — continue or kill — is a false choice that serves neither shareholders nor competitive positioning. The evidence supports a third path: throttle Total Access to a sustainable level while building a streaming hedge. However, the exact achievable savings depend on subscriber behavior data that must be validated before the board can commit to a specific burn-rate target.

**Why not continue at current levels:** The math is terminal. At $400M/year incremental burn against $1.1B in long-term debt and quarterly losses of $85M, Blockbuster faces a solvency crisis within 18-24 months. Winning the DVD-by-mail war is pyrrhic if the company is bankrupt before the war ends. The DVD-by-mail market itself peaks in 2010-2012 then declines — we would be deficit-spending to dominate a shrinking category.

**Why not kill entirely:** Total Access is the only proof point that Blockbuster can compete in digital-era distribution. Killing it signals strategic surrender, accelerates subscriber defection back to Netflix, and destroys the 3M online subscriber base that represents Blockbuster's only non-store asset. Netflix growth *stalled* for the first time because of this program. That competitive leverage has real option value — eliminating it hands Netflix an uncontested runway at precisely the moment they are most vulnerable (pre-streaming scale, 1,000-title library, 50% broadband penetration).

## Strategic Rationale for Restructuring

### 1. Cost reduction levers exist but savings magnitude requires validation

The $2/return in-store cost is the primary burn driver. The restructuring hypothesis rests on three levers:
- **Capping free in-store returns** (e.g., 3-5/month) instead of unlimited. The premise is that heavy users drive disproportionate cost while casual users form the majority — but the actual return-volume distribution across the subscriber base is unknown. If usage is evenly distributed rather than skewed, caps produce less savings and broader churn.
- **Tiering the program:** Premium tier with unlimited returns at a higher price point; Standard tier with capped returns at the current price. This segments price-sensitive subscribers from power users — but willingness-to-pay for the premium tier is untested. If most subscribers downgrade or churn rather than pay more, tiering becomes a revenue loss.
- **Franchisee revenue sharing:** Negotiating a per-return fee to convert hostile operators into program participants. This reduces franchisee complaints but adds a new cost line rather than proven savings, and franchise agreement constraints may block implementation entirely.

**Honest savings estimate:** The levers above could plausibly reduce burn by 30-60%, yielding a restructured cost of $160-280M/year. The range is wide because the critical inputs — return-volume concentration, premium tier uptake, and churn elasticity — are unknown. The board should not commit to a specific savings number until a 90-day validation sprint produces real data.

### 2. Redirect freed capital toward streaming and digital

Netflix launched Watch Instantly with 1,000 titles and limited device support. The library is thin and broadband is at 50%. There is a 2-3 year window before streaming becomes the primary battlefield. Blockbuster must use this window to:
- **License streaming content aggressively.** 5,700 stores give Blockbuster leverage with studios that Netflix lacks — studios fear Netflix disintermediation but view Blockbuster as a known retail partner.
- **Build or acquire streaming technology.** The failed Enron deal (2000) shows Blockbuster recognized this need early but executed poorly. Do not repeat the mistake of being right on thesis, wrong on execution.
- **Leverage the store network as a streaming on-ramp.** In-store kiosks for streaming demos, bundled broadband partnerships, and physical-to-digital conversion paths that Netflix cannot offer.

The streaming investment amount depends on actual Total Access savings. At the pessimistic end of the savings range ($120M freed), streaming gets a modest but meaningful budget. At the optimistic end ($240M freed), Blockbuster can pursue aggressive content licensing. Either way, any dollar redirected from subsidizing DVD-by-mail to building streaming capability improves the company's terminal value.

### 3. The store network is a transitional asset, not a permanent one

5,700 locations are currently both asset and liability. As an asset: they provide physical touchpoints, inventory depth, and impulse-rental revenue that pure-play competitors lack. As a liability: lease obligations, labor costs, and declining foot traffic as Redbox ($1/night) captures convenience-driven rentals.

**The honest assessment:** Blockbuster should plan to reduce to ~3,000-3,500 stores by 2010, closing underperforming locations and converting high-traffic stores into experience/pickup hubs. This is painful but necessary. Denial about store count is the surest path to bankruptcy.

The board should approve the validation phase, not the final restructuring parameters.

**Kill criteria for the restructured program (commit in advance):**
- If return-volume data shows even distribution (no heavy-user concentration), the cap-based savings thesis fails — revert to a full strategic review within 30 days

- If streaming content licensing fails to secure top-3 studio deals within 12 months, accelerate store-focused strategy
- If quarterly losses exceed $100M for two consecutive quarters post-restructure, trigger emergency board review

## Counterarguments Addressed

**"Just press the advantage — Netflix is scared, finish them."**
Netflix has $300M cash, no debt, and 7.5M subscribers generating positive unit economics on their core mail plan. Blockbuster has $1.1B in debt. This is not a fair fight of attrition. Spending $400M/year to compete with a company that has a stronger balance sheet and is simultaneously building a streaming moat is how you win the battle and lose the war. Netflix's fear is *tactical and temporary* — they are scared of Total Access in 2007, not in 2010 when streaming scales.

**"Restructuring is just slow death — either commit or don't."**
Restructuring with validated economics and pre-committed kill criteria is not indecision — it is capital discipline. The alternative (full commitment) requires ~$800M-1.2B over 3 years that Blockbuster cannot finance without dilutive equity raises or additional debt. But the critique is right that restructuring based on *assumed* savings is also risky. That is why the recommendation is to validate first, then commit — not to assume the savings and proceed.

**"The savings estimates are asserted, not proven."**
This critique is valid. The first version of this memo presented $150-180M/year as a target with more confidence than the evidence warrants. The return-volume distribution, churn elasticity, and premium tier uptake are genuinely unknown. The revised recommendation explicitly acknowledges this: the savings range is $120-240M/year, the range is wide, and the board should fund a 90-day validation sprint rather than commit to a specific restructured cost structure. The strategic direction (restructure, don't kill or continue at current burn) is well-supported; the precise economics require data the company has internally but has not yet analyzed.

**"Streaming is years away — focus on physical."**
Broadband is at 50% and rising. Netflix has launched streaming. YouTube is proving consumer appetite for on-demand video. The question is not *whether* streaming displaces physical rental but *when*. Every month of delay in building streaming capability widens Netflix's head start.
