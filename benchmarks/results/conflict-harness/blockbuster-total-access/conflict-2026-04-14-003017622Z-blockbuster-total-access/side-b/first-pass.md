# Recommendation Memo: Kill Total Access as Currently Funded

## Position

Blockbuster should scale back Total Access immediately rather than continue funding the program at current levels. The board should not interpret this as a retreat into a store-only strategy. The right move is to kill the current free in-store return economics, preserve the online subscriber base through a repriced offer, and redirect cash toward streaming capability, debt protection, and a narrower store footprint.

Total Access is a competitively effective tactic, but it is not a viable corporate strategy under Blockbuster's 2007 balance sheet. It has added 2 million online subscribers in less than 12 months and appears to have disrupted Netflix growth [ctx-1]. But it is burning roughly $400 million per year while Blockbuster carries $1.1 billion of long-term debt and just reported an $85 million quarterly loss [ctx-2]. Funding a transitional DVD-by-mail price war with this capital structure is not strategic courage; it is insolvency risk.

## Why Kill the Current Program

The decisive issue is not whether Total Access works. It does. The decisive issue is whether Blockbuster can afford to win this particular fight long enough for the win to matter. The answer is no.

First, Total Access turns Blockbuster's store base into an expensive subsidy machine. Each in-store return costs about $2 through handling and lost rental revenue, and the program shifts economics away from stores and franchisees [ctx-2][ctx-4]. A 5,700-store footprint can be a customer acquisition and fulfillment asset only if the transaction economics are positive. Under the current design, the stores are being used to subsidize online growth while alienating independent operators whose cooperation Blockbuster needs [ctx-4].

Second, the market being subsidized is already time-limited. DVD-by-mail is projected to peak around 2010-2012, while broadband penetration is expected to rise from roughly 50% of U.S. households in 2007 to 70%+ by 2010 [ctx-3]. Netflix has already launched Watch Instantly with 1,000 titles and is investing against the next distribution model [ctx-3]. Blockbuster should not spend $400 million per year to maximize share in a channel that is likely to become a bridge, not the destination.

Third, Blockbuster is still behind online. Netflix has 7.5 million subscribers versus Blockbuster's roughly 3 million online subscribers [ctx-1][ctx-3]. Even after a year of strong Total Access traction, Blockbuster would need sustained heavy spending to close the gap. That may be rational for a company with low leverage and a pure subscription model. It is not rational for Blockbuster given the debt load, operating losses, franchisee conflict, and activist pressure [ctx-2][ctx-4].

Fourth, the store-profitability thesis is also not enough. Redbox is accelerating with $1-per-night grocery and convenience store rentals, attacking the impulse rental use case that stores historically owned [ctx-5]. Therefore, the recommendation is not to protect every store or revert to late-fee-era economics. It is to stop using the stores to fund uneconomic online growth and make them a cash-generating transition asset while Blockbuster builds a credible digital path.

## Strongest Counterarguments

The strongest argument for continuing Total Access is that it is the first Blockbuster strategy in years that clearly hurt Netflix. It added 2 million subscribers quickly, brought Blockbuster online to roughly 3 million subscribers, and coincided with Netflix's first growth stall [ctx-1]. Killing it risks handing momentum back to Netflix.

That argument is real, but incomplete. Competitive pressure is valuable only if the company can sustain it and convert it into a future advantage. Here, the program's $400 million annual burn is too large relative to Blockbuster's debt and loss profile [ctx-2]. If continuing Total Access weakens the company before streaming becomes mainstream, Blockbuster may win a DVD-by-mail skirmish and still lose the business.

A second counterargument is that the store footprint is Blockbuster's unique advantage. Netflix cannot match immediate in-store exchanges across 5,700 U.S. locations [ctx-4]. That is true, but the advantage is structurally flawed if every exchange destroys store economics and triggers franchisee resistance. The board should keep the parts of the footprint that produce profitable customer access and eliminate the free-return subsidy that turns the footprint into a liability.

A third counterargument is that scaling back will signal weakness to investors, franchisees, and customers. The opposite can be true if the board frames the move as discipline: Blockbuster is ending an uneconomic subsidy, not abandoning digital distribution. The company should announce a repriced hybrid subscription, a streaming acceleration plan, and a store rationalization program in one package.

## Recommended Action

Kill Total Access in its current form in Q3 2007. Replace it with a paid hybrid plan that limits or charges for in-store exchanges, preserves the online customer relationship, and stops free re-rental economics from compounding losses. Use the savings to fund three priorities: stabilize liquidity, build or acquire streaming capability, and rationalize the store base around profitable markets.

The board should set a hard rule: no customer acquisition program may consume hundreds of millions of dollars annually unless it produces a clear path to positive unit economics and durable relevance after DVD-by-mail peaks. Total Access currently fails that test.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Scale back Total Access immediately; do not continue current funding levels.

Strategic logic: Blockbuster should transition from a subsidized hybrid DVD program to a disciplined digital transition strategy. Stores should be used where they create profitable access, not as an unlimited subsidy for online subscriber growth.

Primary tradeoff: The company will likely lose some near-term pressure on Netflix, but it preserves cash and strategic flexibility for streaming and store rationalization.

Board test: Would Blockbuster borrow or consume $400 million per year today to gain share in DVD-by-mail if it did not already own 5,700 stores? If the answer is no, the current Total Access design should be killed.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

The board needs tighter cohort economics for Total Access subscribers, including retention, average exchange frequency, contribution margin, and conversion from store-only customers to online subscribers.

The board needs a liquidity runway analysis showing how long Blockbuster can sustain the current burn under the $1.1 billion debt load and current loss profile.

The board needs an assessment of streaming build, buy, or partner options, including licensing cost, platform readiness, and timeline to a consumer launch.

The board needs a franchisee impact model that separates company-owned store economics from independent operator economics.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass: The decision is ready if management can execute a repriced hybrid offer within one quarter, preserve a meaningful share of the 3 million online subscribers, and redirect spending toward streaming and liquidity protection.

Fail: Continuing current funding should be rejected unless management proves that Total Access can materially reduce the $400 million annual burn while sustaining subscriber growth and avoiding franchisee breakdown.

Fail: A pure store-profitability pivot is also insufficient because Redbox and streaming are already attacking the core store model.

## Recommended Next Artifact

Create a 90-day transition plan for replacing Total Access with a paid hybrid subscription, including pricing tests, subscriber retention targets, franchisee compensation rules, store rationalization criteria, and a streaming investment roadmap.
