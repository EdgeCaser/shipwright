# Your artifact

## Recommendation
Kill Total Access in its current hybrid form. Do not keep funding the program at current subsidy levels. Preserve the online business, but immediately remove the free in-store return economics that are driving the losses, and reallocate capital toward a lower-burn transition plan: mail subscription retention, selective digital capability, and store-footprint rationalization.

The core reason is not that Total Access failed competitively. It worked. The reason to kill it is that Blockbuster is buying share in a declining format with a balance sheet that cannot absorb the burn long enough to convert that share into a durable advantage.

## Why this is the right decision
Total Access proved that Blockbuster's store network can blunt Netflix in DVD-by-mail. Adding roughly 2 million subscribers in under a year and stalling Netflix growth is strong market evidence that the offer resonated. But the economics are structurally wrong for Blockbuster's capital position. The program is burning about $400 million annually while the company already carries $1.1 billion in long-term debt and posted an $85 million quarterly loss in Q2 2007. A strategy that requires sustained subsidy only works if the winner can finance the bridge to the next industry state. Blockbuster cannot; Netflix is better positioned to do so.

The board should judge Total Access against the industry's direction, not just today's competitive skirmish. Netflix has already launched streaming in January 2007, however limited the catalog is today. Broadband penetration is around 50% of U.S. households and is projected to exceed 70% by 2010. At the same time, DVD-by-mail is expected to peak around 2010-2012 and then decline. That means Blockbuster is currently overspending to win share in a channel with a short remaining growth window, while its competitor is investing in the next one.

Blockbuster's 5,700-store footprint is not a durable moat under these conditions. In the near term, stores make the hybrid offer more attractive. In the medium term, they become a cost burden, a source of channel conflict with franchisees, and an organizational anchor to legacy economics. Redbox's expansion adds further pressure by attacking the low-end convenience segment at $1 per night. So even if Total Access slows Netflix, it does not create a defensible end-state for Blockbuster.

## Strongest counterargument, and why it still does not change the answer
The best argument for continuing is that Total Access is the first move in years that visibly hurt Netflix. If Blockbuster stops now, it may surrender the one advantage Netflix cannot easily replicate: instant local convenience through stores. There is also a plausible strategic case that management should press the attack while Netflix remains early in streaming and before consumer behavior fully shifts.

That argument would be stronger if Blockbuster had excess capital, aligned franchisees, and a credible path from hybrid DVD leadership to digital leadership. It has none of the three. The balance sheet is leveraged, franchisees are actively hostile because the program damages store economics, and the company has already shown weakness in digital execution through its failed earlier streaming effort. Continuing the current program therefore compounds financial strain without solving the capability gap that matters most.

In other words: Total Access is tactically effective but strategically unfundable.

## What the board should do instead
End the free in-store return subsidy and redesign the online offer around positive or near-breakeven unit economics.

Use the store base selectively rather than universally: treat stores as a retention and liquidation channel, not as the centerpiece of a national subsidy program.

Redirect investment toward a narrower transition plan with three priorities: retain the highest-value online customers, reduce fixed store exposure, and secure a credible digital distribution path through partnership or acquisition rather than trying to outspend Netflix in DVD-by-mail.

Communicate the change as discipline, not retreat. The message is that Blockbuster will compete where it has economic right to win, not continue paying for growth that weakens the enterprise.

## Decision Frame
Decision: kill Total Access as currently structured.

Meaning: stop funding the hybrid program at current levels, remove the free-return subsidy, keep an online subscription business only where economics are defensible, and shift capital toward balance-sheet protection and digital transition options.

Why now: the company has proof of customer demand, but not proof of a sustainable business model. Waiting for more proof mainly increases burn.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
The board still needs cohort-level retention and lifetime value data for Total Access subscribers after benefit reductions.

It also needs a market-tested view of how many customers value in-store return enough to stay at a modified price/benefit level.

Digital options remain underdeveloped. The board lacks a concrete assessment of whether Blockbuster can build, buy, or partner into streaming fast enough to matter.

Franchisee economics and litigation risk from program changes should be quantified before rollout.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if management can present within 60 days a restructuring plan that materially reduces annualized burn, protects liquidity, and shows a viable retention path for the most profitable online segments.

Pass if the redesigned offer can approach breakeven unit economics without relying on broad free in-store returns.

Fail if management proposes only a softer version of the same subsidy logic.

Fail if capital released from Total Access is not explicitly redirected toward store rationalization and digital option creation.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A 90-day board action memo with three components: a Total Access wind-down plan, a customer retention and repricing model by cohort, and a digital strategy options analysis covering partner, acquisition, and internal-build paths.
