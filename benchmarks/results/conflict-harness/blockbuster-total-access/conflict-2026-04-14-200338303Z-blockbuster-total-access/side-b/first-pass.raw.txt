The program has proven that Blockbuster can slow Netflix when it uses stores as a distribution advantage, but the current economics are not survivable. A strategy that wins customers while burning roughly $400M per year on a leveraged balance sheet is not a competitive advantage; it is an expensive proof of concept.\n\nThe board should preserve only the parts of the model that can be made economically disciplined: online subscription relationships, selective store-based convenience, and a faster path into digital delivery. The company should stop funding free in-store return economics at current levels.\n\n## Why This Is The Right Decision\nFirst, the core financial math is unacceptable for Blockbuster's capital structure. Total Access is reportedly burning about $400M annually while the company already carries $1.1B in long-term debt and posted an $85M net loss in Q2 2007. That is too much balance-sheet strain for a business whose legacy store base is itself under pressure. The board does not have the luxury of buying share at any price.\n\nSecond, Total Access appears tactically effective but strategically misaligned with where the market is going. It added roughly 2M subscribers in under a year, pushed Blockbuster online to about 3M, and even contributed to Netflix's first visible growth stall. Those are real signals that the offer resonates. But the offer works by increasing exposure to a format likely to peak around 2010-2012 and then decline, just as broadband penetration is rising from roughly 50% of US households toward 70%+ by 2010. Netflix has already launched Watch Instantly. The industry is beginning to pivot from physical fulfillment advantage to digital delivery advantage.\n\nThird, Blockbuster's store footprint is becoming more ambiguous, not more defensible. The 5,700-store network helps create convenience today, but Total Access converts that footprint into a cost center when returned discs trigger handling costs and lost rental revenue. Franchisee resistance is not a side issue; it is evidence that the program is attacking the economics of the channel Blockbuster still depends on to fund any transition. Meanwhile, Redbox is expanding with a radically cheaper physical-rental model in high-convenience retail locations, which further compresses the value of carrying a large specialty-store footprint into the next phase of the market.\n\n## Strongest Counterargument\nThe strongest case for continuing Total Access is that it is the first move in years that visibly put Netflix on the defensive. The program demonstrates that Blockbuster can combine online convenience with an installed local presence that Netflix cannot match. Killing it risks conceding the subscription customer, restoring Netflix's momentum, and shrinking Blockbuster back into a declining store-only business.\n\nThat argument is directionally right but operationally wrong. Total Access proved a strategic insight, not a sustainable operating model. The right lesson is not to keep spending at current levels until the balance sheet breaks. The right lesson is that Blockbuster should extract what is valuable from the experiment, then redesign the offer around contribution margin, not raw subscriber optics. If management continues the current subsidy war, Netflix is better positioned to survive it and is simultaneously investing in the next platform. Blockbuster would be overfunding yesterday's advantage while underfunding tomorrow's.\n\n## What The Board Should Do Instead\n1. End free in-store return economics as a default feature of the subscription.\n2. Migrate existing online customers to revised plans with tighter store entitlements, price discipline, and fewer free exchanges.\n3. Preserve online subscription capabilities and customer relationships as a bridge asset, not a scale-at-all-costs growth engine.\n4. Ring-fence capital for digital partnerships or internal streaming capability rather than doubling down on mail-plus-store subsidies.\n5. Stabilize franchise and store economics so the legacy base can fund a controlled transition instead of becoming collateral damage.\n\n## Decision Frame\nThe decision is not whether Total Access created competitive traction; it did. The decision is whether Blockbuster should keep funding that traction at a level that its balance sheet, store system, and market timing do not support. The board should kill the current program because the company is winning the wrong war on the wrong economics at the wrong moment in the technology cycle.\n\n## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps\n- Cohort
