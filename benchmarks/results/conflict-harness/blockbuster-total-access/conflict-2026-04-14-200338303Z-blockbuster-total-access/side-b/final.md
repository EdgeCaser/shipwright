# Revised artifact

## Blockbuster Board Memo: Kill Total Access at Current Scale; Retain a Narrow Transitional Hybrid

## Recommendation
Blockbuster should kill Total Access in its current broad, heavily subsidized form. The board should not kill the online business outright, and it should not justify continuation on the hope of an immediate streaming pivot. The correct move in Q3 2007 is to terminate the current economics of free in-store returns at scale, preserve the online subscriber base as a transition asset, and redesign the offer around strict liquidity and contribution constraints.

The core judgment is financial, not technological. Total Access has proven that Blockbuster can slow Netflix by combining mail with store convenience. But a program burning roughly $400M annually inside a company carrying $1.1B of long-term debt and posting an $85M Q2 loss cannot remain the company's default growth engine unless management can prove rapid cost improvement. On the evidence provided, that proof does not exist.

## Why This Is The Right Decision
Total Access is strategically interesting but financially undisciplined. It added roughly 2M subscribers in under a year, took Blockbuster online to about 3M, and coincided with Netflix's first growth stall. That establishes demand and competitive relevance. It does not establish that the current model is fixable fast enough for Blockbuster's balance sheet.

The main issue is not whether the program could be optimized in theory. It is whether Blockbuster can afford to keep funding current-scale losses while waiting to find out. Each in-store return reportedly costs about $2 in handling and foregone rental economics; franchisees are already hostile because the program disrupts store-level incentives; Carl Icahn's board influence raises the likelihood that patience for prolonged losses is low. In that context, the burden of proof should be on continuation, not on retrenchment.

The market outlook strengthens the case for narrowing the program, but not for pretending streaming is ready to replace it immediately. Netflix's January 2007 Watch Instantly launch shows where industry investment is heading, and rising broadband penetration suggests digital delivery will matter more over time. But streaming is still nascent in 2007, and Blockbuster has not demonstrated a credible digital capability yet. That means the board should not continue a $400M subsidy war in physical rental, and it also should not anchor its decision on a near-term digital rescue. It should conserve capital and preserve optionality.

## Strongest Counterargument
The strongest counterargument is that Total Access is Blockbuster's only proven weapon against Netflix, and the DVD-by-mail market may remain relevant through 2010-2012. On that view, killing the program in 2007 would surrender several remaining years of market life just as Blockbuster finally found a winning offer.

That argument would be more persuasive if Blockbuster had evidence that current losses are temporary, reducible, and worth financing. The record provided shows the opposite problem: large current burn, a leveraged balance sheet, channel conflict, and no demonstrated path to acceptable unit economics. A company in Blockbuster's position should not keep a proven customer-acquisition engine if the engine may destroy the financing base needed to operate the rest of the business. The right response is not to abandon hybrid entirely; it is to stop subsidizing it at a level the company cannot defend.

## What The Board Should Do Instead
1. End the current default of generous free in-store return economics across the subscriber base.
2. Move existing subscribers to narrower plans with capped in-store exchanges, higher pricing, or eligibility limits tied to contribution margin.
3. Keep the online platform and customer relationships, but stop managing them as a scale-at-all-costs business.
4. Require management to prove within one operating cycle that a redesigned hybrid offer can materially reduce burn without collapsing retention.
5. Preserve capital for strategic flexibility, including selective digital partnerships, but do not underwrite the decision on the assumption that Blockbuster can quickly become a streaming leader.

## Decision Frame
The board is not choosing between growth and no growth. It is choosing between a financially constrained transition strategy and an open-ended subsidy war. Blockbuster should kill Total Access at its current scale because the present economics are not defensible on the evidence available, even though the underlying hybrid insight has value and should be retained in narrower form.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- Whether per-return costs and free-exchange behavior can be reduced fast enough to make a narrower hybrid plan viable.
- How much subscriber retention survives after tighter pricing and exchange limits.
- Which customer cohorts are genuinely profitable versus promotion-driven and structurally negative.
- Whether Blockbuster can secure any credible digital partner or content path at realistic cost.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if management can present, within 30 days, a revised hybrid model that materially lowers annualized burn, protects liquidity, and shows credible cohort-level improvement under tighter entitlements.

Fail if management argues for maintaining current-scale subsidies without evidence of near-term unit-economic improvement, or if a revised plan cannot preserve enough subscriber value to justify ongoing investment.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A board decision memo with a 12-month transition model: revised plan architecture, cohort-retention scenarios under reduced benefits, franchise impact analysis, and a capital-allocation comparison between narrow hybrid continuation and current-scale Total Access.
