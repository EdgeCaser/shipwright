# Strategic Recommendation: Continue Total Access with Structural Reform

**TO:** Blockbuster Board of Directors  
**FROM:** Strategy Advisory  
**DATE:** Q3 2007  
**RE:** Total Access Program — Continuation Decision

---

## Decision Frame

**Recommended Position: Continue Total Access — with targeted cost reform, not retreat.**

The board faces a choice between two forms of risk: continuing a program with a dangerous burn rate, or killing the one initiative that has demonstrated competitive effectiveness against Netflix. Neither option is safe. The question is which risk is more recoverable.

Total Access is the only program Blockbuster has launched that measurably disrupted Netflix — adding 2 million online subscribers in under twelve months and stalling Netflix growth for the first time. That competitive result is real. Whether those subscribers are economically valuable enough to justify the burn is a separate and currently unanswerable question: the board does not have subscriber lifetime value, retention, or margin data. The recommendation is therefore to continue the program at current levels for one additional quarter while commissioning the economic analysis that should have preceded this decision.

**What we know:** The program works competitively. Netflix CEO Reed Hastings was, by report, genuinely alarmed.

**What we do not know:** Whether the subscribers acquired are worth what they cost.

**The window:** DVD-by-mail is projected to peak 2010–2012. Broadband sits at roughly 50% of US households now, headed toward 70%+ by 2010. Netflix launched Watch Instantly in January 2007 with 1,000 titles — a real streaming bet, but not yet a decisive catalog. Blockbuster has a multi-year window in which physical disc rental remains the dominant consumer behavior. Total Access uses the physical store network — 5,700 US locations — as subscriber acquisition infrastructure during that window. No online-only competitor can replicate that footprint.

---

## Case for Continuation

**1. Subscriber momentum is real and documented.**  
Two million subscribers in under twelve months is a concrete result. Netflix growth stalling for the first time is a concrete result. The program demonstrably shifted competitive momentum. These facts do not by themselves prove the program is economically justified — see the unknowns section — but they establish that the program has strategic effect, which is a necessary (if not sufficient) condition for continuation.

**2. The store footprint is a depreciating but currently valuable asset.**  
Under Total Access, 5,700 US store locations function as subscriber acquisition and retention infrastructure. This advantage is time-limited as streaming scales, which makes it more urgent to extract value during the physical-rental window, not less. Killing Total Access before that window closes abandons the hybrid advantage without replacing it.

**3. Streaming displacement is not yet decisive.**  
Netflix's Watch Instantly launched with 1,000 titles. Fifty percent broadband penetration is not ubiquity. Consumer habit and catalog depth favor physical disc rental for the near-term window identified above. Blockbuster's streaming capability is nonexistent (the Enron Broadband attempt in 2000 failed when infrastructure was inadequate), but the window before streaming becomes dominant may be sufficient to build or license one — if subscriber scale is maintained as negotiating leverage.

---

## Strongest Counterarguments — and Their Actual Weight

**Counterargument 1: The burn rate threatens near-term survival.**  
This critique has full force and we adopt it without qualification. At $400M/year incremental burn against $1.1B long-term debt and an $85M Q2 loss, the financial stress is not theoretical — it is an existential risk on a short time horizon. Terminating Total Access would materially improve near-term cash flow. That is a legitimate and important consideration, not a dismissible one.

Why we still recommend continuation: improving near-term cash flow by eliminating Total Access does not address the structural problem — store-based rental revenue is declining as Redbox kiosk expansion compresses price floors and consumer behavior shifts. The board would be buying survivability time at the cost of the only growth program the company has. That trade may still be correct if subscriber LTV turns out to be deeply negative, but it has not been established.

**Counterargument 2: 'Working' is the wrong standard without economics.**  
We adopt this critique. The first-pass framing — Total Access is "a working competitive weapon" — is an incomplete standard. Subscriber growth and competitive disruption are necessary but not sufficient evidence of program value. A program that acquires subscribers at a cost exceeding their lifetime value is destroying capital even if it disrupts competitors. The board should not treat the competitive results as sufficient justification for continued full funding. They are sufficient justification for one additional quarter to commission the LTV analysis that determines whether the economics are recoverable.

**Counterargument 3: Carl Icahn controls 3 board seats and will force the issue.**  
Icahn's concern that online losses are value-destroying is legitimate given the available data. His governance pressure does not resolve the strategic question — it is possible to agree that the current burn is unsustainable while disagreeing that termination is the correct response. The board should request the same subscriber economics data before yielding to or overriding activist pressure.

**Counterargument 4: Franchisee resistance threatens the store network.**  
Franchisee hostility reflects real economic harm from cannibalization. This is a solvable structural problem — through revised revenue sharing, exclusion of franchise locations from the return program, or other arrangements — but the board has no visibility into franchise contract constraints that might limit modification speed.

---

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

The following material uncertainties limit confidence in this recommendation. The directional conclusion (do not terminate without economic analysis) holds across these gaps; the budget authorization does not.

1. **Subscriber LTV is unknown.** Cost per in-store return (~$2) and aggregate burn (~$400M/year) are established. Average subscriber tenure, retention rates by acquisition channel, and revenue per subscriber are not. Without LTV, the board cannot determine whether the program is capital-destructive or capital-generative per subscriber.

2. **Cost reform feasibility is unknown.** Whether the $400M burn can be reduced 30–40% through fee restructuring, return caps, or franchisee exclusions — without destroying subscriber momentum — has not been modeled.

3. **Streaming options are unquantified.** No data exists on what a streaming capability would cost to build or license, or what partnership terms are available in 2007. The 3-million-subscriber base may constitute meaningful negotiating leverage; it may not.

4. **Franchisee legal constraints are unknown.** Franchise agreements may limit how quickly in-store return economics can be restructured. The board has no visibility into this.

5. **Redbox impact is unquantified.** Redbox kiosk expansion is noted as accelerating but no market share or store revenue impact data is provided. If Redbox is already materially compressing store economics, the case for protecting franchise store revenue weakens further.

---

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**Readiness Assessment: CONDITIONAL PASS — sufficient to recommend against immediate termination; insufficient to approve continued full-scale funding.**

The competitive evidence (2M subscribers, stalled Netflix growth) establishes that Total Access has strategic effect. The financial evidence ($400M burn, $1.1B debt, $85M Q2 loss) establishes that the current cost structure is unsustainable. These two facts are both true and not yet resolved by the available data.

The correct board action is not to continue full funding and not to terminate, but to:

1. Authorize continuation at current subscriber levels for one additional quarter (preserving the competitive position while the economic analysis is completed).
2. Commission a subscriber LTV and cost-reform feasibility study as the immediate next deliverable.
3. Set a decision gate: if the LTV analysis shows the program cannot reach cash-flow sustainability within 18 months through operational reform, termination or radical restructuring becomes the correct answer.

**The termination case becomes correct if:** LTV modeling shows subscribers are acquired at structurally negative value, or cost-reform modeling shows the burn cannot be reduced without destroying subscriber retention. Neither has been established — which is precisely the problem with approving termination now.

---

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Subscriber Economics Model** — a structured financial analysis establishing:
- Average revenue per subscriber per month by segment (online-only vs. hybrid in-store)
- Average subscriber tenure and retention rate by acquisition channel
- Implied LTV vs. current acquisition cost and per-return handling cost
- Cost-reform sensitivity table: what burn reduction is achievable under each scenario, and what subscriber loss rate each scenario implies
- Break-even analysis: what LTV is required at current acquisition costs for the program to be capital-neutral

This model is the decision gate. The board's current position — authorizing one additional quarter of continuation — is conditional on commissioning it immediately. Without it, neither the continuation nor the termination decision has an adequate evidentiary foundation.
