# Recommendation: Kill Total Access in its current form and scale back immediately

## Executive View
Blockbuster should not continue funding Total Access at current levels. The program has proven that a hybrid offer can win customers and slow Netflix, but the board is not deciding whether the idea has competitive merit. It is deciding whether Blockbuster can afford to keep buying that advantage while carrying heavy debt, current losses, franchisee conflict, and a market transition that favors streaming over an extended physical-rental war. On that question, the answer is no.

The right move is to end the current subsidy structure, preserve only the most strategically useful elements of the hybrid model, and redirect management focus toward a narrower transition plan that does not depend on sustained large losses.

## Why this is the right decision

### 1. The current program is financially incompatible with Blockbuster's position
Total Access is burning roughly $400M per year while Blockbuster already carries $1.1B in long-term debt and posted an $85M net loss in Q2 2007. That combination means the company is trying to fund an expensive competitive assault from a weak balance-sheet position. A strategy that requires sustained heavy losses is much more dangerous for Blockbuster than for a less encumbered rival.

### 2. The program's success does not solve the bigger strategic problem
Total Access added subscribers quickly, pushed Blockbuster online to about 3 million, and helped stall Netflix growth. That matters. But Netflix still has 7.5 million subscribers and is already investing in streaming infrastructure after launching Watch Instantly in January 2007. The core issue is that Blockbuster is spending heavily to compete in a DVD-by-mail market that is projected to peak and then decline, while the rival is investing into the next delivery model.

### 3. The hybrid model creates internal conflict Blockbuster cannot easily carry
The company is not only fighting Netflix. It is also straining its own store economics and franchise relationships. In-store returns impose handling costs and cannibalize store rental revenue, while franchisees see the program as directly harmful to their economics. A strategy that depends on the store footprint as an advantage becomes less credible when the same program weakens the incentives of the operators tied to that footprint.

### 4. The board should not mistake tactical pressure on Netflix for strategic control of the future
The strongest argument for continuing is clear: Total Access is the first thing that has visibly scared Netflix and proven Blockbuster can use its store network as a competitive weapon. But that evidence shows tactical effectiveness, not long-term strategic fitness. If maintaining pressure requires ongoing large losses while consumer behavior, broadband penetration, and Netflix's platform investments are shifting the industry toward streaming, then Blockbuster is winning the wrong fight at too high a price.

## Best counterargument, acknowledged
The best case for staying the course is that Blockbuster finally found a differentiated offer: online convenience plus store immediacy. With 5,700 U.S. locations and 47 million store-visit customers, the company may have a distribution asset Netflix cannot match. Killing the program too early could surrender the one move that demonstrably interrupted Netflix's momentum.

That argument is serious, but still insufficient. The problem is not whether the offer attracts customers; the evidence says it does. The problem is whether Blockbuster can sustain the economics and governance strain long enough for that advantage to compound. The case packet suggests the opposite: high burn, leverage, operating losses, franchisee hostility, and activist resistance all make endurance doubtful. A strategy that only works if Blockbuster can outlast the burden is not board-worthy when the burden itself may break the company first.

## Recommendation
Kill Total Access as a full-scale, current-level funding strategy. Retain a reduced hybrid offer only where it supports customer retention or selective market defense, but stop treating it as the company's primary growth engine. The board should prioritize financial survivability and strategic flexibility over an expensive attempt to win the late stage of the disc-rental battle.

## Decision Frame
The decision is whether to keep funding Total Access at current levels to press the competitive attack, or scale it back to reduce losses and protect the business during a format transition. The recommended choice is to scale it back now.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
The board still lacks evidence on which parts of Total Access produce the most retention benefit relative to cost, whether the store footprint can be reconfigured into a true transition asset rather than a subsidy burden, and how quickly streaming adoption will weaken the economics of physical rental. It is also unclear how much franchisee opposition can be contained under a narrower version of the program.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if the board is willing to accept near-term competitive retreat in exchange for lower financial risk, narrower capital commitments, and a transition strategy not built on sustained subsidy. Fail if management believes the company can safely absorb continued heavy losses for multiple years while also funding a credible response to streaming.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A board decision memo with a phased wind-down and transition plan: which Total Access benefits to retain, which subsidies to end first, how to manage franchisee fallout, and what near-term digital priorities can be funded within tighter loss limits.
