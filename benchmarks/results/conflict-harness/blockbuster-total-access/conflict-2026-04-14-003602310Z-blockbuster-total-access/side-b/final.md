# Strategic Recommendation Memo: Continue Total Access, But Stop Treating It as an Unfunded Streaming Bet

## Recommendation
Continue Total Access for a bounded two-quarter operating window, but do not continue it as an open-ended $400M/year subsidy and do not assume Blockbuster can internally build a Netflix-equivalent streaming platform on the same balance sheet.

The board should continue because Total Access is the only current strategy that has demonstrated direct competitive pressure against Netflix: it added 2M online subscribers in under 12 months, lifted Blockbuster online to roughly 3M subscribers, and coincided with Netflix's first growth stall [ctx-1][ctx-2][ctx-3]. Killing or materially shrinking the program now would hand Netflix back momentum and leave Blockbuster defending stores against Redbox, broadband growth, and a DVD-by-mail market expected to peak in 2010-2012 [ctx-6][ctx-7].

But the critique is correct: the first-pass memo overstated the evidence that Total Access can become a digital distribution platform. Netflix's Watch Instantly launch proves the market is moving toward streaming, not that Blockbuster has the technical capability, content rights, or capital access to build streaming while absorbing a roughly $400M/year Total Access burn, $1.1B in long-term debt, and an $85M Q2 2007 loss [ctx-5][ctx-8][ctx-9]. Blockbuster's failed Enron Broadband effort is a warning, not proof of readiness [ctx-11].

The correct decision is therefore not "continue and build streaming." It is: continue Total Access as a short-duration competitive option on the subscriber relationship, while forcing the program to prove it can move toward economic viability and while pursuing streaming only through a capital-light partnership, licensing, or acquisition path with separate board approval.

## Why Continue
Total Access has created competitive leverage that Blockbuster rarely gets. Netflix has 7.5M total subscribers versus Blockbuster's roughly 3M online subscribers, so Blockbuster is still behind [ctx-3]. However, adding 2M online subscribers in less than a year is real evidence of customer demand, and Netflix's growth stall during the same period suggests the program affected the competitive battlefield [ctx-1][ctx-2].

The 5,700-store footprint should be used as a temporary differentiator, not protected as the final destination [ctx-4]. In-store returns and exchanges give Blockbuster a feature Netflix cannot immediately copy, but the same store base is structurally exposed: Redbox is accelerating with $1/night kiosk rentals, broadband penetration is expected to rise from roughly 50% of U.S. households in 2007 to more than 70% by 2010, and DVD-by-mail is projected to peak in 2010-2012 [ctx-6][ctx-7]. Scaling back mainly to restore store economics would conserve cash inside a weakening category.

## What Must Change
The board should stop funding Total Access as a broad subsidy. Each in-store return costs roughly $2 in handling and lost rental revenue, and the program is burning about $400M per year [ctx-8]. Continuing requires immediate controls:

1. Segment heavy exchange users and cap or reprice usage patterns that drive negative contribution.
2. Test price increases or tiering for high-frequency in-store exchange behavior.
3. Require monthly cohort reporting by acquisition source, exchange frequency, retention, gross margin, and contribution margin.
4. Freeze any internal streaming buildout until management presents a funded plan showing rights costs, technology costs, capital source, and a partner-or-build recommendation.
5. Create a franchisee participation model that either compensates stores for subscription-driven workload or limits store obligations where the unit economics are destructive [ctx-10].

The board should release only two more quarters of funding at current intensity. After that, management must earn continuation.

## Counterarguments
The strongest kill argument is that the company cannot afford the burn. That argument is serious: Blockbuster has $1.1B in long-term debt, lost $85M in Q2 2007, and faces an activist investor bloc that views online losses as unsustainable [ctx-9][ctx-10]. If Total Access remains a $400M/year subsidy with no path to improved contribution margin, it should be cut.

The reason not to kill it today is that the evidence does not show failure of demand; it shows failure of economics. Those are different board decisions. Demand is unusually strong, competitive impact is visible, and the store model being protected is itself under pressure. A two-quarter continuation with hard economic gates preserves the only live subscriber-growth engine while limiting further drift.

The strongest critique of the streaming thesis is also valid. Blockbuster should not pretend that Total Access subscribers automatically become streaming subscribers. The revised strategy treats streaming as a strategic requirement to investigate, not as a funded assumption. If management cannot produce a partner-led, capital-light streaming roadmap, the board should still evaluate Total Access on its DVD-by-mail and store-exchange economics rather than funding a second unfunded bet.

## Decision Frame
This is not a binary choice between transformation and discipline. The board should choose disciplined continuation.

Continue Total Access for two quarters because it has demonstrated subscriber growth and competitive pressure against Netflix. Do not approve open-ended funding, do not protect franchise economics at the expense of the only growing subscription channel, and do not fund an internal streaming buildout without separate proof of capability, rights access, and capital.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
The board still needs cohort-level economics: acquisition cost, retention, exchange frequency, contribution margin, and whether heavy exchange users are responsible for most of the burn.

The board needs a quantified franchise exposure model: affected operators, compensation options, store workload, and the cost of turning stores into subscription service points.

The board needs a streaming feasibility memo, not a promise: content rights options, infrastructure approach, partner candidates, capital required, time to launch, and whether Blockbuster can credibly compete with Netflix's Watch Instantly path [ctx-5].

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if, within two quarters, Total Access continues to grow or retain subscribers, reduces per-subscriber exchange losses through pricing or usage controls, produces transparent cohort economics, and presents a capital-light streaming or digital partnership plan for separate approval.

Fail if the program continues burning near $400M/year with no improving contribution trend, no franchise settlement path, no usage controls, and no credible financing or partner path for digital distribution [ctx-8][ctx-10].

If those fail conditions hold by mid-2008, the board should scale the program back aggressively rather than continue current funding.

## Recommended Next Artifact
Create a two-quarter Total Access operating plan for board approval. It should include a cohort economics dashboard, exchange-loss reduction plan, franchisee mitigation model, and a separate streaming feasibility memo with partner-led and build-vs-buy options.
