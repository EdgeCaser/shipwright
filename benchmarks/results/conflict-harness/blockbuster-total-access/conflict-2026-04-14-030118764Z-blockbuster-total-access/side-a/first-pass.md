# Continue Total Access, but narrow it immediately

Blockbuster should continue the hybrid strategy, not kill it. The board should keep funding Total Access for the next 24 months, but only under a redesigned economics model that caps burn, compensates franchisees, and treats the store base as a distribution advantage for the transition from mail to digital.

The core fact is strategic: Total Access is the first move that has materially hurt Netflix. Blockbuster added roughly 2 million online subscribers in under a year and reached about 3 million online subscribers; during the program's peak, Netflix's growth stalled for the first time. That is rare evidence of actual competitive disruption, not theoretical upside. Killing the program would amount to surrendering the only initiative that has shown it can slow the category leader.

The financial objection is real but incomplete. At roughly $400 million of annual incremental burn, against $1.1 billion of long-term debt and an $85 million Q2 2007 loss, the current version of Total Access is not financeable indefinitely. But this does not justify abandoning the strategy; it just proves the current offer design is too generous. A board should kill a failed strategy or a structurally unwinnable one. Total Access is neither. It is commercially effective and strategically aligned with where the market is going.

That market is plainly shifting. DVD-by-mail is likely to peak around 2010-2012 and then decline. Broadband is already near 50% of U.S. households in 2007 and projected to exceed 70% by 2010. Netflix launched Watch Instantly in January 2007, signaling that management sees physical rental as a transition business. If Blockbuster retreats now to defend store profitability, it will be optimizing a declining format while its primary rival builds the next one.

Blockbuster's 5,700-store footprint should be treated as a transition asset, not defended as a legacy P&L in isolation. Stores create a real customer convenience advantage in returns, exchanges, acquisition, and local brand presence. That is what made Total Access powerful. The mistake is not the hybrid concept; the mistake is letting store-level economics absorb costs without redesigning incentives. The board should preserve the customer proposition while changing the cost-sharing model and tightening free in-store exchange rules.

The strongest counterargument is balance-sheet risk. Given leverage, franchisee hostility, activist pressure, and Redbox's low-cost encroachment, Blockbuster may not have the financial capacity to fund a long war. That concern is valid. But killing Total Access does not solve the real problem; it leaves Blockbuster with a weakening store base, a smaller online business than Netflix, and no credible bridge into streaming. It would improve near-term losses while worsening terminal value.

The right decision is therefore to continue, but with hard guardrails:

1. Redesign Total Access economics within one quarter by limiting free in-store exchanges, reducing the effective subsidy per return, and segmenting plans by usage intensity.
2. Create a franchisee compensation pool so store operators are paid for hybrid-service activity rather than punished by it.
3. Ring-fence a streaming transition budget and partnership plan now, using the hybrid subscriber base as the seed audience for digital migration.
4. Set a 24-month milestone framework: subscriber growth, churn, cost per subscriber, store-assisted exchange rate, and path to lower burn.
5. Pre-commit to exit triggers if burn does not fall materially while subscriber and retention advantages persistently deteriorate.

This is not a bet on mail as the end state. It is a bet that the only viable way for Blockbuster to survive the format transition is to use Total Access to convert store customers into hybrid customers, then into digital customers, before Netflix and Redbox split the future market.

## Decision Frame

Recommendation: continue Total Access, but narrow and reprice it immediately rather than funding the current version unchanged.

Why this is the best board-level choice:
- It is the only strategy in evidence that has slowed Netflix.
- It leverages Blockbuster's store footprint as a transition advantage instead of treating it only as stranded overhead.
- It preserves an option on streaming-era relevance.
- It addresses the real problem, which is offer design and capital discipline, not strategic direction.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- Precise cohort economics of Total Access customers by usage frequency, churn, and lifetime value.
- How much of the $400 million burn can be removed without collapsing the value proposition.
- Store-by-store and franchisee-by-franchisee economic impact of in-store exchanges.
- Blockbuster's realistic ability to fund or partner into streaming fast enough to matter.
- Whether customer preference for hybrid convenience is durable or mostly promotional.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if:
- The board is willing to preserve the strategy while forcing a rapid repricing and incentive redesign.
- Management can produce a 90-day economics reset and a 24-month transition scorecard.
- Franchisee compensation can be renegotiated fast enough to stabilize channel conflict.

Fail if:
- The board insists on near-term earnings optics over transition positioning.
- Management cannot lower burn materially without destroying subscriber momentum.
- There is no credible streaming migration plan attached to the hybrid strategy.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A 24-month transition plan with three modules: revised Total Access unit economics, franchisee incentive redesign, and streaming-entry options with capital requirements and trigger-based decision gates.
