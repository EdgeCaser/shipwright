# Revised artifact

Blockbuster should kill Total Access in its current form and immediately scale back the hybrid strategy. The program has proved that customers value the offer and that Netflix can be pressured, but the board should not keep funding a $400M annual burn on a leveraged balance sheet without evidence that the economics can be repaired. In Q3 2007, this is no longer a question of strategic imagination; it is a question of survival and sequencing.

The strongest fact in favor of continuing is real: Total Access added roughly 2 million online subscribers in under a year, Blockbuster reached about 3 million online subscribers, and Netflix's growth stalled during the program's peak. That means the hybrid concept has competitive power. But competitive power is not enough if the mechanism that creates it destroys more value than the company can carry. Each in-store return imposes about $2 of handling and lost rental economics, the program is burning roughly $400M per year, Blockbuster has $1.1B in long-term debt, and it already posted an $85M Q2 2007 loss. Those are not pilot losses. They are balance-sheet-threatening losses.

The critical issue is decision readiness. There is no evidence in the case packet that Blockbuster can materially reduce the subsidy and keep the same customer response. The proposition that a narrower version of Total Access would preserve most of the subscriber gains at far lower cost may be true, but it is unproven. The board should not underwrite a multi-quarter capital commitment on that assumption. When the economics of the winning offer are themselves the source of the distress, the burden of proof shifts: management must prove repairability before the board keeps funding it, not after.

This matters even more because the physical rental market is transitional. DVD-by-mail is projected to peak around 2010-2012 and then decline. Broadband penetration is around 50% of U.S. households in 2007 and expected to exceed 70% by 2010. Netflix launched Watch Instantly in January 2007, signaling where the category leader intends to invest. Redbox is also expanding rapidly with a structurally cheaper physical-rental model. Blockbuster therefore faces two threats at once: a low-cost attack on physical rentals and a digital transition above them. That is exactly why it cannot afford to pour scarce capital into a hybrid program whose current economics are negative at scale.

The board should therefore kill Total Access as a mass-market subsidized offer, not kill digital transition altogether. The correct move is to exit the unlimited hybrid subsidy, protect liquidity, and redeploy a smaller pool of capital into three narrower objectives: retain the highest-value online households, negotiate franchisee-stabilizing economics, and secure a credible streaming path through partnership or selective build. The store base should be managed as a cash-and-customer asset during transition, not as the centerpiece of a loss-making attempt to outspend Netflix in mail.

The strongest counterargument is that killing Total Access hands momentum back to Netflix and abandons the only strategy that has visibly worked. That is partly true. Blockbuster will give up a real tactical advantage. But the alternative is worse: continue financing a program whose success condition depends on an unverified economics fix while debt, losses, franchisee conflict, and market transition all intensify. A board's job is not to preserve momentum at any cost; it is to preserve enterprise value through the transition. On the evidence available, continuing Total Access at current levels is a high-confidence path to deeper financial impairment, not to a durable turnaround.

The recommended actions are immediate:

1. End the current subsidized Total Access offer and stop marketing it as the flagship growth engine.
2. Migrate existing subscribers to narrower, repriced plans with tighter exchange terms and explicit retention targets.
3. Use the store network to harvest cash and customer relationships, not to subsidize free exchanges that worsen unit economics.
4. Ring-fence a smaller strategic budget for streaming partnership or acquisition options, with board approval gates.
5. Return with a 90-day restructuring plan showing online retention, store economics, franchisee stabilization, and digital-option costs.

## Decision Frame

Recommendation: kill Total Access in its current form and scale back the hybrid strategy now.

Why this is the best board-level choice:
- The program's competitive success is proven, but its economic repairability is not.
- $400M annual burn, $1.1B long-term debt, and ongoing quarterly losses leave little room for an evidence-free continuation bet.
- The market is shifting toward both lower-cost physical distribution and streaming, so capital flexibility matters more than defending one expensive offer.
- A controlled retrenchment preserves the option to pursue digital transition without betting the balance sheet on an unproven redesign.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- Whether a repriced hybrid offer could retain enough of the customer benefit to remain strategically useful.
- The exact churn and lifetime value profile of current Total Access cohorts by usage intensity.
- The cost, timing, and feasibility of a credible streaming partnership or acquisition path.
- How much franchisee conflict can be reduced under a scaled-back online model.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if:
- The board accepts near-term subscriber contraction in exchange for liquidity preservation and strategic flexibility.
- Management can execute a rapid migration off the current subsidy without operational disorder.
- A smaller digital-transition budget is protected from being reabsorbed into store-defense tactics.

Fail if:
- The board believes tactical pressure on Netflix justifies funding losses that have no proven path to acceptable unit economics.
- Management treats scale-back as a retreat to legacy-store optimization rather than a bridge to digital repositioning.
- No credible streaming path is developed after the hybrid subsidy is withdrawn.
