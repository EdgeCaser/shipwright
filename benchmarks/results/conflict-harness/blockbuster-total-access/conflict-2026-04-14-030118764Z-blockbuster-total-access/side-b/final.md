# Strategic Recommendation: Restructure Total Access, Don't Kill It

**TO:** Blockbuster Board of Directors
**FROM:** Strategy Advisory Group
**DATE:** Q3 2007
**RE:** Total Access Program — Recommendation for Controlled Restructuring

---

## Decision Frame

The board faces a false binary. The question is not "continue at $400M/year burn or kill the program" — it is whether Blockbuster can restructure Total Access into a sustainable competitive weapon while buying time to build a streaming capability.

**Our position: Restructure Total Access to reduce burn significantly, preserve the competitive pressure on Netflix, and redirect savings into streaming infrastructure. Do not kill the program outright.**

However, the viability of restructuring depends on validating a critical unknown: whether exchange caps and tiered pricing can meaningfully reduce costs without destroying the program's competitive value. We recommend immediate data analysis to resolve this before committing to a restructured design.

The rationale rests on three pillars:

### 1. Total Access Is the Only Leverage Blockbuster Has Against Netflix

Total Access added 2 million online subscribers in under 12 months — a growth rate that caused Netflix's first-ever subscriber growth stall. Reed Hastings called it his top competitive threat. Killing the program entirely signals surrender in the online rental market and hands Netflix an uncontested runway to convert its 7.5M subscribers to streaming at its own pace.

Blockbuster's 5,700 stores are the only structural advantage no competitor can replicate. Netflix cannot offer instant physical gratification. Redbox offers convenience but not selection or online integration. Total Access uniquely converts a retail liability into a competitive moat — but only if the unit economics can be fixed.

### 2. The Current Burn Rate May Be a Pricing Problem — But This Must Be Validated Before Assuming It

The ~$400M/year cost is driven by a specific mechanism: each in-store return triggers a free rental worth ~$2 in handling and lost revenue. The hypothesis is that this is a pricing and policy design flaw amenable to restructuring, not evidence that the hybrid model is fundamentally broken.

However, the case for restructuring rests on assumptions that have not yet been validated with subscriber data:

- **Exchange frequency distribution is unknown.** If most subscribers are heavy exchange users (treating unlimited exchanges as the core value proposition), then capping exchanges would gut the program's appeal and trigger mass churn. If most subscribers are light users who rarely hit a hypothetical cap, restructuring is viable with limited subscriber impact.
- **Willingness-to-pay for tiered plans is untested.** We do not yet know whether subscribers would accept a premium tier for exchanges or defect to Netflix's simpler model.
- **Churn response to policy changes is speculative.** The 50-60% cost reduction target is a modeling exercise, not a demonstrated outcome. The actual reduction depends on behavioral responses we cannot predict without data.

Restructuring options that could preserve the program's value while cutting costs — **contingent on favorable data**:

- **Cap free in-store exchanges** at 3-5 per month (vs. unlimited), potentially reducing per-subscriber cost significantly — but only if the exchange frequency distribution supports it
- **Introduce tiered pricing**: base online plan without in-store exchange; premium tier with exchanges at a higher price point
- **Revenue-share with franchisees** on exchange transactions rather than cannibalizing their economics — each exchange drives foot traffic that can be monetized through impulse purchases, concessions, and game rentals
- **Targeted geographic rollout**: concentrate exchanges in company-owned stores where Blockbuster captures the full margin, reduce franchisee friction

A restructured program at significantly lower burn is defensible against a $1.1B debt load if — and only if — retention modeling confirms that policy changes preserve subscriber momentum and drive store traffic that supports the broader P&L. **The board should not commit to a restructured burn target until exchange frequency and churn sensitivity data are in hand.**

### 3. Streaming Is the Existential Bet, and Total Access Buys Time to Make It

Netflix launched Watch Instantly in January 2007 with only 1,000 titles. Broadband penetration is ~50% of US households. Streaming will become dominant, but not in 2007 or 2008. The DVD-by-mail market is projected to peak between 2010-2012.

This gives Blockbuster a 3-5 year window in which physical media remains the primary delivery mechanism. Total Access — even restructured — keeps Blockbuster competitive during this window. Savings from restructuring must be redirected to:

- Licensing streaming content (the 2000 Enron Broadband deal failed on execution, not concept)
- Building or acquiring a streaming platform
- Converting the store network into a distribution + experience asset (in-store streaming kiosks, exclusive content partnerships)

Killing Total Access does not solve the streaming problem. It merely accelerates the timeline on which Blockbuster becomes irrelevant.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

| Unknown | Why It Matters | How to Resolve |
|---|---|---|
| **Subscriber exchange frequency distribution** (CRITICAL — must resolve before committing to restructuring) | If >60% of subscribers are heavy exchange users, capping exchanges destroys the program's core value proposition and restructuring is not viable. If most are light users, restructuring preserves value at lower cost. This is the single most important data point for the board's decision. | Analyze exchange frequency data from existing subscriber records immediately. Segment by usage quartile. This data exists in Blockbuster's systems today and should be available within 2 weeks. |
| **Churn sensitivity to policy changes** | Even if most subscribers are light users, the perception of losing "unlimited" may drive churn beyond what usage-based modeling predicts. | Design A/B test of capped vs. unlimited plans in 2-3 test markets before national rollout. |
| **Subscriber price elasticity for tiered plans** | Will subscribers pay a premium for exchange privileges, or defect to Netflix's simpler pricing? | Conjoint analysis or test-market pricing experiment before committing to tier structure. |
| **Franchisee breakeven on exchanges** | Can a revenue-share model make exchanges profitable for franchisees, or is the cannibalization irreversible? | Pilot revenue-share model in 50 franchisee locations over 90 days; measure incremental in-store purchases per exchange visit. |
| **Streaming content licensing costs** | What does it cost Blockbuster to build a competitive streaming library? Netflix has first-mover deals. | Engage 3 studios in preliminary licensing conversations to benchmark cost per title vs. Netflix's reported content spend. |
| **Redbox substitution rate** | Are Redbox's $1/night kiosks pulling casual renters out of Blockbuster stores, and does Total Access insulate against this? | Compare same-store traffic trends in markets with high vs. low Redbox kiosk density. |
| **Broadband adoption trajectory** | If broadband reaches 70%+ by 2010, does the physical-to-streaming transition accelerate faster than projected? | Commission updated broadband penetration forecast from telecom analysts; model streaming adoption S-curve scenarios. |
| **Icahn's actual walk-away number** | What level of online investment loss is Icahn willing to tolerate, and is there a restructured burn rate he would accept? | Direct board-level conversation with Icahn's representatives, presenting restructured economics rather than current $400M figure. |
| **Debt covenant constraints** | Does Blockbuster's debt structure permit even a restructured incremental burn without triggering default provisions? | Immediate review of covenant terms by CFO and outside counsel. |

## Pass/Fail Readiness

### This recommendation PASSES if:
- [ ] **Exchange frequency data confirms restructuring viability**: >70% of subscribers use fewer than 5 exchanges/month, indicating a cap would affect a minority of users
- [ ] Churn modeling or test-market results show <20% subscriber loss from policy changes
- [ ] Restructured burn rate — validated by actual usage data, not estimates — is sustainable against current debt load and covenant structure
- [ ] Franchisee pilot shows positive or neutral incremental economics on exchange traffic within 90 days
- [ ] Board secures commitment to redirect meaningful savings into streaming infrastructure within 12 months
- [ ] Icahn bloc agrees to a 12-month evaluation period for the restructured program before revisiting cancellation

### This recommendation FAILS if:
- [ ] Exchange frequency data reveals >60% of subscribers are heavy exchange users (cap would trigger mass churn and the "pricing flaw" hypothesis is wrong — unlimited exchanges are the product, not a bug)
- [ ] Test-market churn from capped plans exceeds 30%, indicating the program's value is inseparable from its cost
- [ ] Franchisee economics are irreparably negative — exchange visits destroy more value than they create and no revenue-share model fixes it
- [ ] Debt covenants or liquidity constraints make even a reduced incremental burn unsustainable over 12 months
- [ ] Streaming licensing costs are prohibitive (>$500M to reach competitive parity with Netflix's library), making the "buy time" thesis moot

**If the exchange frequency data comes back unfavorable (majority heavy users), the board should shift to an orderly wind-down of Total Access over 6 months while redirecting all available capital to streaming. The restructuring thesis is only as strong as the data behind it.**

## Counterarguments Addressed

**"The $400M burn is unsustainable — we should cut losses now."**
Correct about the current burn. Whether restructuring solves this depends on subscriber usage patterns we have not yet analyzed. But cutting the program entirely — before even checking whether a lower-cost version is viable — forfeits Blockbuster's only competitive leverage without data-driven justification. Run the analysis first; the data will arrive within weeks, not quarters.

**"Streaming makes physical rental obsolete — why invest in a dying channel?"**
Streaming will win long-term. But Netflix has 1,000 titles and 50% broadband penetration today. The transition will take years, not months. Blockbuster needs a bridge strategy, and Total Access is that bridge. The mistake would be optimizing for 2012 at the expense of surviving 2008-2010.

**"Franchisees will revolt."**
Franchisee concerns are legitimate and must be addressed — which is why restructuring includes a revenue-share pilot and geographic concentration in company-owned stores. A dead program serves franchisees no better than a restructured one; if Blockbuster loses the online market, store traffic declines regardless.

**"Carl Icahn will force the board's hand."**
Icahn's objection is to $400M in losses, not to the concept of online competition. Presenting a restructured program with validated economics and clear kill-switch criteria gives the activist bloc a rational basis for support. If the restructured numbers don't work after 12 months, kill it then — with data, not ideology.
