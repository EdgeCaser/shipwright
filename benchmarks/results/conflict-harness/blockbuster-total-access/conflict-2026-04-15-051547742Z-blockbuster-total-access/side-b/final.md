# Revised artifact

# Recommendation: Kill Total Access in its current form; do not kill the online business

Blockbuster's board should end Total Access as a broad, subsidy-heavy hybrid offer at current funding levels and replace it with a narrower, explicitly priced transition model. The critique is correct that the first-pass memo treated the roughly $400 million annual burn too much as a gross burden without enough discussion of offsetting subscription revenue or of intermediate redesign options. The right conclusion still remains to kill the current program, but on a more precise basis: Total Access appears to buy growth, yet the board has not been shown that the net economics after subscriber revenue, exchange handling cost, lost in-store rental margin, churn risk, and franchisee disruption produce a financeable path to acceptable returns for a company carrying $1.1 billion of long-term debt and reporting an $85 million Q2 2007 loss. [Citations: ctx-1, ctx-3, ctx-4]

The strongest fact in favor of continuing is real: Total Access added about 2 million subscribers in under a year, brought Blockbuster online to about 3 million subscribers, and coincided with Netflix's first growth stall. If 3 million online subscribers are paying roughly subscription-level monthly fees, the business is plainly generating meaningful revenue rather than being a pure cost center. That matters. But it does not rescue the current board decision, because the issue is not whether the program creates revenue; it is whether the marginal economics of the free in-store exchange benefit create a durable and financeable advantage. On the evidence given, every additional use of the hybrid feature appears to trigger about $2 of handling and lost-rental cost, meaning the differentiator that drives acquisition is also the mechanism that deepens subsidy load. In other words, the burden is not just large; it is attached to the very behavior the offer is designed to maximize. [Citations: ctx-1, ctx-2, ctx-3]

That distinction changes the recommendation from "shut down online" to "stop funding the current hybrid promise." The board should not assume the $400 million gross burn is the whole economic picture, but it also should not assume subscriber revenue solves the problem when no evidence here shows contribution profitability, retention after subsidy reduction, or a credible timetable to break-even. A leveraged incumbent cannot continue underwriting a structurally uncertain loss leader merely because it has demonstrated customer appeal. The burden of proof should be reversed: management should have to prove that capped exchanges, tiered pricing, or other redesigns preserve enough subscriber value to improve net economics quickly. Until then, continuing the program at current scale is a capital-allocation error. [Citations: ctx-3, ctx-4]

The timing risk makes that burden of proof even higher. Netflix has 7.5 million subscribers versus Blockbuster's roughly 3 million online subscribers and has already launched streaming with Watch Instantly in January 2007. Broadband penetration is around 50% of U.S. households in 2007 and expected to exceed 70% by 2010, while DVD-by-mail is projected to peak around 2010-2012. That means Blockbuster is being asked to spend heavily not just on a lossy offer, but on a lossy offer tied to a format whose strategic relevance is likely to decline within the same planning window. The fact that Total Access hurt Netflix tactically does not mean it improves Blockbuster's position in the next basis of competition. [Citations: ctx-5, ctx-6, ctx-7]

Franchisee resistance and the store footprint reinforce the case for ending the current design. Blockbuster's 5,700 U.S. stores are valuable only if they improve economics or create a durable moat. Under Total Access, they instead create channel conflict, cannibalization, and operating friction. Redbox's expansion into low-cost kiosk rentals further weakens the argument that the store network can remain the center of a profitable physical-rental defense. The footprint is still useful, but more as a fulfillment, acquisition, and cash-harvest asset than as the basis for an open-ended free-exchange promise. [Citations: ctx-6, ctx-8, ctx-10]

## Decision Frame

Decision: Kill Total Access as currently structured and funded; keep the online subscriber business, but immediately remove the open-ended free in-store exchange subsidy unless a rapid operating review proves a redesigned version can reach acceptable net economics.

Why: The evidence supports that Total Access is competitively effective, but does not support that it is net-economically sound or financeable for Blockbuster's balance sheet. In a company with $1.1 billion of long-term debt, an $85 million quarterly loss, franchise conflict, and exposure to an approaching format shift, the board should not keep funding the current design without proof that intermediate fixes materially reduce burn while preserving enough revenue and retention. [Citations: ctx-1, ctx-3, ctx-4, ctx-5]

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

The central missing evidence is net unit economics, not customer demand. The board needs contribution margin by subscriber cohort after exchange usage, store handling cost, lost rental revenue, marketing cost, and churn. It also needs scenario analysis for capped exchanges, tiered pricing, and reduced subsidy designs. The most important unresolved question is whether a redesigned hybrid can keep enough of the 3 million online subscribers to approach break-even before streaming shifts the market further. [Citations: ctx-1, ctx-3, ctx-5]

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass: End the current program now if management cannot demonstrate within 60-90 days that a redesigned offer materially lowers burn, preserves a substantial share of subscriber revenue, and reduces franchise conflict.

Fail: Continue only if management produces a board-grade model showing that intermediate options such as capped exchanges or tiered pricing convert Total Access from grossly subsidy-driven growth into a credible net-positive or near-break-even transition platform. On the present evidence, that standard has not been met. [Citations: ctx-3, ctx-4]

## Recommended Next Artifact

A 90-day board decision pack with four exhibits: net cohort economics for current Total Access subscribers; churn and retention sensitivity under capped-exchange and tiered-price designs; franchise/store cash-flow impact; and a digital transition options memo covering partner, build, or acquisition paths in streaming. That artifact should determine whether Blockbuster runs a narrower transition offer or exits the hybrid model more aggressively. [Citations: ctx-3, ctx-5, ctx-8]
