# Recommendation: Kill the current Total Access hybrid strategy in its present form and pivot to a lower-burn transition plan

Blockbuster should not continue funding Total Access at current levels. The program has proven that a hybrid online plus store offer can win customers and pressure Netflix, but the current version is financially incompatible with Blockbuster's capital structure and with the industry's likely shift toward streaming. The right board decision in Q3 2007 is to kill the current subsidy-heavy form of Total Access, preserve the customer insights and selective hybrid elements that work, and reallocate capital toward a controlled digital transition and store cash-flow defense. [Citations: ctx-1, ctx-2, ctx-3, ctx-4, ctx-5, ctx-6]

## Why this is the right decision

First, the program is strategically effective but economically broken. Total Access added roughly 2 million subscribers in under a year and helped stall Netflix's growth for the first time, which proves the offer is competitively potent. But it is burning about $400 million annually while Blockbuster already carries $1.1 billion of long-term debt and just posted an $85 million quarterly loss. A company in Blockbuster's financial condition cannot fund a loss leader of this size without high confidence that the spending buys a durable advantage. That confidence is absent here. [Citations: ctx-1, ctx-2, ctx-3, ctx-4]

Second, the advantage Total Access creates is unlikely to be durable enough to justify the burn. Its core differentiator depends on the store network: customers return discs in-store and receive immediate rental value. But each in-store return costs about $2 in handling and lost rental economics, and the more customers use the feature, the more value leaks out of the system. This is not a scale economy. It is the opposite: growth deepens the subsidy burden. A strategy whose unit economics worsen as adoption rises is dangerous for a leveraged incumbent. [Citations: ctx-1, ctx-3, ctx-4]

Third, the market is already beginning to move beyond the basis of competition on which Total Access wins. Netflix launched Watch Instantly in January 2007 and is investing aggressively in streaming infrastructure. Broadband penetration is around 50% of U.S. households in 2007 and expected to exceed 70% by 2010, while DVD-by-mail is projected to peak around 2010-2012 and then decline. That does not mean discs are irrelevant today; it means the board should be wary of doubling down on an expensive hybrid built to maximize a format that is approaching maturity while a better-capitalized rival invests in the next format. [Citations: ctx-5, ctx-6, ctx-7]

Fourth, the store base is not a clean strategic asset in this fight. Blockbuster's roughly 5,700 U.S. stores create reach and brand presence, but Total Access turns that footprint into an internal conflict: franchisees see cannibalization, store economics are disrupted, and management attention is split between preserving store profitability and subsidizing online growth. At the same time, Redbox is attacking the low-end physical rental occasion with a cheaper, more convenient kiosk model. That means Blockbuster is being squeezed from both directions: Netflix in the digital future and Redbox in commodity physical rental. [Citations: ctx-6, ctx-8, ctx-9, ctx-10]

## Strongest counterargument and why it still loses

The strongest case for continuing Total Access is that it is the first credible move Blockbuster has made that visibly scared Netflix. The program added subscribers quickly, took advantage of a unique physical footprint Netflix does not have, and may represent the company's last chance to convert 47 million store visitors into a multi-channel subscription base. Killing it risks surrendering the field just as the company found a winning offer. [Citations: ctx-1, ctx-2, ctx-6, ctx-11]

That argument is serious, but it still fails on board-level capital allocation logic. A winning customer proposition is not enough if the company cannot finance the path to payoff. Blockbuster is not choosing between a profitable strategy and a timid one. It is choosing between a cash-burning hybrid with structurally weak economics and a controlled retreat that preserves solvency and optionality. Even if Total Access continued slowing Netflix for another 12-24 months, Netflix still has a much larger subscriber base, fewer internal channel conflicts, and a more coherent path into streaming. Blockbuster would be spending heavily to defend a transitional advantage while its rival builds the next platform. [Citations: ctx-2, ctx-3, ctx-4, ctx-5, ctx-6, ctx-7]

## What the board should do instead

Kill the current all-in version of Total Access, specifically the broad free in-store return subsidy that drives the worst economics. Replace it with a narrower transition plan:

1. Retain online DVD service, but remove or sharply limit the free in-store exchange benefit.
2. Use stores primarily as low-cost fulfillment, marketing, and customer-acquisition nodes where economics are positive, not as open-ended subsidy points.
3. Protect near-term liquidity and stabilize franchise relations by stopping the most destructive cannibalization.
4. Redirect a defined portion of saved capital toward digital partnerships, streaming capability exploration, and a smaller set of pilots tied to broadband adoption and content rights feasibility.
5. Treat the store network as a declining cash-flow asset to harvest and selectively repurpose, not as the foundation of a long-term moat.

This is not a recommendation to abandon online or digital. It is a recommendation to abandon an unsound cost structure before it compounds strategic and financial decline. [Citations: ctx-3, ctx-4, ctx-5, ctx-8]

## Decision Frame

Decision: Kill Total Access at current funding levels and end the broad subsidy-heavy hybrid model.

Rationale: The program demonstrates demand but not sustainable economics; Blockbuster's debt load and current losses materially reduce tolerance for a $400 million annual burn; and the likely industry shift toward streaming lowers the probability that continued spending on this version of the hybrid model creates durable shareholder value. [Citations: ctx-1, ctx-3, ctx-4, ctx-5, ctx-7]

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

The board still lacks clean cohort-level evidence on whether Total Access customers would remain profitable after subsidy removal, how many store customers can be converted to lower-cost subscription forms, and whether Blockbuster can secure any credible streaming path fast enough to matter. The most decision-relevant unknown is not whether customers like Total Access; that is already clear. The key unknown is whether any modified version can produce acceptable unit economics before streaming reshapes the category. [Citations: ctx-1, ctx-5, ctx-7, ctx-11]

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if the board's objective is preserving solvency, protecting optionality, and avoiding value destruction from scaling a strategically interesting but financially unsound program.

Fail if management can prove within a near-term operating review that a redesigned Total Access offer materially improves unit economics without collapsing subscriber retention, and that the resulting economics are strong enough to fund a digital transition. On the evidence provided, that proof is not yet available. [Citations: ctx-3, ctx-4]

## Recommended Next Artifact

A 90-day transition memo with three outputs: a revised offer architecture for post-subsidy online membership, a store-network cash-flow and franchise impact model, and a digital options paper covering build-versus-partner paths for streaming and digital delivery. That is the minimum artifact set needed to replace an unsustainable competitive tactic with a credible transition strategy. [Citations: ctx-5, ctx-8]
