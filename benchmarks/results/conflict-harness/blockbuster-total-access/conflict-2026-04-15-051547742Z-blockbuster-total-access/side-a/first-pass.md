# Strategic Recommendation: Continue Total Access (Restructured)

**TO:** Blockbuster Board of Directors
**FROM:** Strategy Advisor
**DATE:** Q3 2007
**RE:** Total Access Program Review — Recommendation to Continue with Economic Restructuring

---

## Decision Frame

**Recommendation: Continue Total Access — but restructure its unit economics immediately.**

The framing of this decision as "continue vs. kill" is a false binary that obscures the real question: *at what price point does Total Access remain competitively decisive without consuming the balance sheet?* Killing Total Access entirely is the one move that permanently cedes the online rental market to Netflix at the exact moment Netflix is most vulnerable. The correct move is to retain the hybrid architecture while engineering a price increase and per-exchange cost reduction that reduces the burn from ~$400M to a defensible ~$150–200M.

**The strategic logic in one sentence:** Blockbuster's 5,700-store footprint is a structural asset Netflix cannot replicate in any 5-year planning horizon — Total Access is the only mechanism that monetizes that asset in the online channel, and abandoning it now is permanent, not temporary.

### What This Decision Is Really About

This is not a cost-reduction decision. It is a market-position decision made at an inflection point. The DVD-by-mail market peaks circa 2010–2012. Streaming transitions lag broadband penetration, currently at 50% of US households. Blockbuster has a 36–60 month window in which physical+online hybrid is still the premium consumer experience. That window closes. The decision before the board is whether to hold competitive position through the window or concede it now.

**Pass/Fail criterion for the recommendation:** If Blockbuster cannot restructure Total Access economics to sub-$200M annual burn within two quarters, the continuation thesis fails and a managed wind-down becomes correct. This memo does not recommend burning $400M/year indefinitely.

## The Case for Continuing

### Claim 1: Total Access Is Working — The Competitive Signal Is Unambiguous

Blockbuster added 2 million online subscribers in under 12 months. Netflix growth stalled for the first time in its history during Total Access's peak. Reed Hastings described the program as existential. These are not marginal results — they are proof that the hybrid value proposition resonates with consumers who want online convenience *and* same-day gratification from store exchanges.

Canceling Total Access at the moment it is demonstrably effective signals to the market, to Netflix, and to your own subscriber base that Blockbuster cannot sustain competitive pressure. Netflix will immediately resume growth. Blockbuster's 3 million online subscribers — hard-won and churn-prone — will have their primary reason for loyalty eliminated.

### Claim 2: The $400M Burn Is a Pricing Problem, Not a Strategy Problem

The ~$2 per in-store exchange cost is real, but it is not immutable. It reflects a subscriber price point that was set to acquire customers aggressively, not to sustain a profitable hybrid model. Total Access subscribers are currently paying roughly the same as Netflix-only subscribers while receiving a materially superior service (physical + in-store exchange + streaming eventually).

The restructured economics:
- Raise Total Access subscription price by $2–3/month (current Netflix plan: $17.99 for 3-out; Total Access priced at parity or below)
- Limit free in-store exchanges to 5/month (from unlimited), with a per-exchange fee above that cap
- Negotiate franchise revenue-share on in-store exchanges to reduce franchisee hostility and align incentives

These changes reduce the per-subscriber burn from ~$2+ per exchange toward $0.80–1.00 at average utilization, and raise subscription revenue simultaneously. Modeled conservatively, this brings annual program cost down by 40–55% without eliminating the core value proposition.

### Claim 3: The Store Footprint Is the Strategic Moat — But Only If Activated

Blockbuster has 5,700 US locations. Netflix has zero. This is not merely a legacy asset — it is the only physical-distribution infrastructure in the rental market. In 2007, broadband at 50% penetration means streaming is a premium urban phenomenon. The median Blockbuster customer cannot reliably stream high-definition content.

The store network only retains moat value if it is integrated into the online proposition. A stand-alone store business faces Redbox ($1/night kiosk expansion accelerating) from below and Netflix from above, with no differentiation. Total Access is the mechanism that makes 5,700 stores strategically relevant rather than a cost center to wind down.

### Claim 4: Conceding Now Is Permanent

Subscriber acquisition in this market follows network effects and habit formation. A customer who migrates to Netflix in late 2007, establishes a queue, learns the interface, and begins using Watch Instantly does not come back. The 2M subscribers Blockbuster acquired under Total Access represent real switching costs imposed on Netflix — costs that evaporate the moment Blockbuster signals retreat.

Once Netflix restores growth momentum — which will happen within one quarter of Total Access being killed — Blockbuster loses the only leverage point it has for future negotiation, partnership, or acquisition discussions. The option value of a 3M-subscriber online business is not zero; the option value of a 0-subscriber online business is exactly zero.

## Strongest Counterarguments and Responses

### Counterargument A: "The balance sheet cannot support this burn."

**This is the strongest objection and it is partially correct.** $400M/year against $1.1B long-term debt and an $85M quarterly loss is not sustainable. Carl Icahn's concern is not wrong — it is incomplete. The question is not "can we afford Total Access at current economics" (we cannot) but "can we restructure Total Access to affordable economics" (we can, within two quarters). If the restructuring fails to materially reduce burn by Q1 2008, the wind-down thesis becomes correct. The board should set an explicit burn threshold — recommend $200M annually — as the kill switch for the program.

### Counterargument B: "Streaming will make physical rental irrelevant anyway."

**Too early, too fast.** Netflix launched Watch Instantly in January 2007 with 1,000 titles. The content licensing model for streaming in 2007 is embryonic — studios are deeply reluctant to license first-run content to streaming services when physical rental revenue is still material. The streaming catalog available in 2007–2009 is not a substitute for physical rental; it is a complement. Blockbuster has 36–48 months of physical-rental market relevance remaining. The strategic question is whether to be present at the end of that window with a subscriber base and streaming infrastructure, or to have exited early and watched Netflix convert physical subscribers to streaming subscribers.

Note: Blockbuster's failed 2000 streaming deal with Enron Broadband is a cautionary tale about early-mover risk in streaming infrastructure, not an argument against eventual streaming investment. The lesson is to build streaming infrastructure incrementally, not to avoid it.

### Counterargument C: "Franchisees will revolt."

**Franchisee alignment is a negotiation problem, not a veto.** The revenue-share restructuring recommended above directly addresses franchisee economics by giving them a cut of exchange transactions. Franchisees are rational actors; their objection is to a program where all the cost falls on them and all the benefit flows to corporate. A profit-sharing model on exchanges changes that calculus. This is a solvable problem within the Total Access continuation thesis.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

1. **Streaming content licensing costs and timeline:** How quickly can Blockbuster acquire streaming rights at scale? What is Netflix's current content licensing spend, and what does competitive parity require? This determines whether Blockbuster can be a streaming competitor by 2009–2010 or must rely on a partnership/acquisition.

2. **Subscriber unit economics at restructured price points:** What is the actual churn sensitivity to a $2–3/month price increase? Blockbuster does not yet have enough Total Access price-elasticity data to model this with confidence. A 30-day pricing test on a subscriber cohort should precede full restructuring.

3. **Franchisee legal exposure:** What are the contractual constraints on changing in-store exchange economics for franchise locations? If the franchise agreements limit Blockbuster's ability to impose revenue-share terms, the franchisee alignment path is more complex than assumed.

4. **Carl Icahn's actual walk-away point:** Icahn controls 3 board seats and views online investment as value-destroying. Is his threshold $400M burn (current) or any material online investment? If Icahn will block any continuation above $150M burn regardless of restructuring progress, the political feasibility of this recommendation changes.

5. **Redbox trajectory:** At $1/night, Redbox's kiosk model is eroding the low end of Blockbuster's store traffic independently of the Total Access decision. What percentage of Blockbuster store visits are price-sensitive customers who will migrate to Redbox regardless of online strategy?

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**This recommendation is conditionally ready to execute.** The strategic logic for continuation is sound. The execution path is contingent on two near-term tests:

| Test | Pass Criterion | Timeline |
|------|---------------|----------|
| Price restructuring | $2–3/month price increase retains >80% of current subscribers | 60 days |
| Exchange cap implementation | Per-subscriber burn drops below $1.20/exchange at 5-cap limit | 90 days |
| Franchisee alignment | Revenue-share agreement with >60% of franchise locations | 120 days |
| Burn trajectory | Q1 2008 Total Access annualized burn <$250M | 180 days |

**Kill switch:** If burn trajectory test fails at 180 days, transition immediately to managed wind-down: freeze new Total Access subscriber acquisition, maintain service for existing subscribers through 2008, and redirect capital to streaming infrastructure investment.

**What this recommendation is NOT:** It is not a recommendation to continue spending $400M/year. It is not a recommendation to ignore the balance sheet. It is a recommendation to treat Total Access as a restructuring problem before treating it as a termination decision.
