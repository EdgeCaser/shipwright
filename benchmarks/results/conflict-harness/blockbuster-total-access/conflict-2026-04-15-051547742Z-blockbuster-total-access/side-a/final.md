# Strategic Recommendation: Continue Total Access (Restructured)

**TO:** Blockbuster Board of Directors
**FROM:** Strategy Advisor
**DATE:** Q3 2007
**RE:** Total Access Program Review — Recommendation to Continue with Economic Restructuring

---

## Decision Frame

**Recommendation: Continue Total Access — but gate continuation on a 90-day restructuring test.**

The framing of this decision as "continue vs. kill" obscures the real question: *can Total Access's unit economics be restructured to a defensible burn rate before the balance sheet forces a decision?* Killing Total Access entirely is the one move that permanently cedes the online rental market to Netflix at the exact moment Netflix is most vulnerable. But continuing at $400M/year against a $1.1B debt load and an $85M quarterly loss is also indefensible.

The correct move is a structured 90-day test: authorize a price increase and exchange-cap pilot on a subscriber cohort, measure churn elasticity and per-subscriber burn, and bring a data-backed continue/kill decision to the board in Q1 2008. The board cannot responsibly kill the program before that test, and cannot responsibly continue it indefinitely without one.

**The strategic logic in one sentence:** Blockbuster's 5,700-store footprint is a structural asset Netflix cannot replicate — Total Access is the only mechanism that monetizes that asset in the online channel, and the question of whether it can be made economically viable requires testing, not assumption.

**Pass/Fail criterion:** If the 90-day pricing test shows subscriber churn above 25% at a $2–3/month increase, or burn trajectory does not reach a clear path to sub-$200M annually, wind-down begins immediately. If those tests pass, full restructuring proceeds.

## The Case for Continuing

### Claim 1: Total Access Is Working — The Competitive Signal Is Unambiguous

Blockbuster added 2 million online subscribers in under 12 months. Netflix growth stalled for the first time in its history during Total Access's peak. Reed Hastings described the program as existential. These are not marginal results — they are proof that the hybrid value proposition resonates with consumers who want online convenience *and* same-day gratification from store exchanges.

Canceling Total Access at this moment signals to the market, to Netflix, and to your own subscriber base that Blockbuster cannot sustain competitive pressure. Netflix will resume growth within one quarter. Blockbuster's 3 million online subscribers — hard-won and churn-prone — will have their primary reason for loyalty eliminated.

### Claim 2: Pricing Levers Exist and Must Be Tested Before the Program Is Killed

**This claim is now stated with appropriate epistemic caution.** The first-pass artifact overstated precision: specific savings ranges (40–55%), post-restructuring per-exchange costs ($0.80–1.00), and subscriber retention projections were modeled assumptions, not evidence-grounded findings. The case packet does not provide utilization distributions, exchange-frequency cohorts, or price-elasticity data. That absence was correctly identified as a gap.

What *is* supportable from available evidence:
- Two structural levers exist: subscription price increases and per-subscriber exchange caps
- The current price point was set for subscriber acquisition, not sustainable unit economics — there is no evidence it represents the consumer's willingness-to-pay ceiling
- Netflix's comparable plan was priced at $17.99/month for 3-out; Total Access was priced at parity or below despite offering materially more (in-store exchange)
- The per-exchange cost (~$2) is a function of both handling cost and the free disc re-rental, both of which are addressable through operational and pricing design

The critical unknown — what happens to subscriber retention when price increases and caps are imposed — cannot be resolved from available data. It requires a 60–90 day pricing test. **The board should authorize that test, not assume its outcome.** The previous memo's error was presenting projected restructuring outcomes as if they were demonstrated. They are not. They are plausible hypotheses that the test will confirm or refute.

**Revised kill-switch logic:** If the pricing test shows the hybrid offer loses material subscriber value when economically rationalized, then Total Access is not a pricing problem — it is a structural problem where the competitive value proposition only exists at an unsustainable subsidy level. That outcome would make the wind-down thesis correct. But we do not know that yet.

### Claim 3: The Store Footprint Is the Strategic Moat — But Only If Activated

Blockbuster has 5,700 US locations. Netflix has zero. In 2007, with broadband at 50% household penetration, streaming is a premium urban phenomenon. The median Blockbuster customer cannot reliably stream high-definition content.

The store network only retains moat value if integrated into the online proposition. A stand-alone store business faces Redbox ($1/night kiosk expansion accelerating) from below and Netflix from above, with no differentiation. Total Access is the mechanism that makes 5,700 stores strategically relevant rather than a cost center awaiting wind-down.

This moat argument is conditional: it holds through the physical-rental peak period (projected 2010–2012), degrades rapidly as streaming catalog expands and broadband penetration reaches 70%+ (projected by 2010), and becomes irrelevant beyond that window. The board should not treat the store footprint as a permanent asset — it is a 36–60 month window.

### Claim 4: Conceding Now Is Permanent

Subscriber acquisition in this market follows habit formation. A customer who migrates to Netflix in late 2007, establishes a queue, and begins using Watch Instantly does not return. The 3M subscribers Blockbuster acquired under Total Access represent real switching costs imposed on Netflix — costs that evaporate the moment Blockbuster signals retreat.

Once Netflix restores growth momentum — which will happen within one quarter of Total Access being killed — Blockbuster loses the only leverage point it has for future negotiation, partnership, or acquisition discussions. The option value of a 3M-subscriber online business is not zero; the option value of a 0-subscriber online business is exactly zero.

## Strongest Counterarguments and Responses

### Counterargument A: "The balance sheet cannot support this burn."

**This is the strongest objection and it is correct on its current terms.** $400M/year against $1.1B long-term debt and an $85M quarterly loss is not sustainable — full stop. The response is not to dispute this but to reframe the timeline: the board is not being asked to approve $400M/year ongoing. It is being asked to approve a 90-day pricing test that costs roughly $100M in incremental burn, at the end of which the board makes a data-backed decision. If Icahn's threshold is any online investment above a de minimis level, the political feasibility of this recommendation collapses regardless of strategy logic — which is why Claim 4 in the Unknowns section below is load-bearing.

### Counterargument B: "Streaming will make physical rental irrelevant anyway."

**Too early, too fast — but the timeline is tight.** Netflix launched Watch Instantly in January 2007 with 1,000 titles. Studio licensing for streaming is embryonic; first-run content licensing to streaming services remains constrained while physical rental revenue is material. Streaming is a complement to physical rental in 2007, not a substitute. However, this window closes faster than Blockbuster's organizational metabolism may allow for response. The 36–48 month physical-rental relevance window is not an argument for complacency — it is an argument for urgency.

### Counterargument C: "Franchisees will revolt."

**Franchisee alignment is a negotiation problem, not a veto — but it has contractual constraints we have not fully assessed.** Revenue-share on exchanges directly addresses franchisee economics. The unknown is whether existing franchise agreements allow Blockbuster to impose revenue-share terms unilaterally. If they do not, alignment requires renegotiation, which takes time and legal cost the balance sheet may not tolerate. This is a genuine gap, not a dismissed objection.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

1. **Subscriber churn elasticity to price increases [CRITICAL]:** The entire restructuring thesis hinges on subscribers accepting a $2–3/month increase without material churn. No price-elasticity data exists in the case record. This is the single most important unknown and must be resolved via a 60-day cohort test before the board can approve full continuation. Until this data exists, the restructuring thesis is a plausible hypothesis, not a demonstrated path.

2. **Exchange utilization distribution [CRITICAL]:** The per-subscriber burn calculation depends on how many exchanges the average subscriber actually uses. If most subscribers use 1–2 exchanges/month, a 5-cap barely changes economics. If a meaningful tail uses 10+, the cap drives most of the savings. The case packet provides only an average cost ($2/exchange) with no distribution data. The financial model for restructuring cannot be completed without this.

3. **Carl Icahn's actual walk-away point:** Is his objection to $400M burn specifically, or to any material online investment? If Icahn will block continuation at any burn above $150M regardless of test results, political feasibility overrides strategic logic. His position needs to be established before the board votes on the 90-day test authorization.

4. **Franchisee contractual constraints:** What are the existing franchise agreements' terms on in-store exchange economics? If revenue-share requires franchise agreement renegotiation rather than unilateral imposition, the franchisee alignment path is significantly more complex and slower than assumed.

5. **Streaming content licensing cost and timeline:** How quickly can Blockbuster acquire streaming rights at competitive scale? This determines whether the physical-rental window can be bridged to a viable streaming position or whether Blockbuster exits the physical-rental window with no digital successor.

6. **Redbox migration rate from Blockbuster stores:** At $1/night, Redbox is eroding the price-sensitive end of Blockbuster store traffic independently of Total Access. The store-footprint moat argument weakens if this segment migrates regardless of online strategy.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**This recommendation is ready to execute at the 90-day test level only.** Full continuation cannot be authorized today without the pricing and utilization data noted above.

| Gate | Pass Criterion | Timeline | Decision if Failed |
|------|---------------|----------|-----------------|
| Price test cohort | Churn <25% at $2–3/month increase on 200K-subscriber cohort | 60 days | Wind-down |
| Exchange cap modeling | Clear path to <$1.50/exchange at 5-cap based on utilization data | 60 days | Wind-down |
| Icahn alignment | Board control agreement on 90-day test authorization | 30 days | Pause and negotiate |
| Burn trajectory | Q1 2008 Total Access annualized burn tracking toward <$250M | 180 days | Wind-down |

**Kill switch:** If any pass criterion above fails, transition immediately to managed wind-down: freeze new Total Access subscriber acquisition, maintain service for existing subscribers through Q3 2008, redirect capital to streaming infrastructure investment or debt reduction.

**What this recommendation is NOT:** It is not a recommendation to continue spending $400M/year. It is not a recommendation to ignore the balance sheet. It is a recommendation to authorize a bounded, time-limited test before making an irreversible decision to exit a market where Blockbuster currently holds genuine competitive advantage.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Artifact: Total Access 90-Day Test Design and Authorization Memo**

A focused operating document covering:
1. Pricing A/B test cohort design (size, price points, measurement criteria, churn thresholds)
2. Exchange utilization data pull and distribution analysis (requires internal operational data)
3. Icahn pre-alignment talking points (what he needs to see to support test authorization)
4. Franchise agreement legal review scope (what can be changed unilaterally vs. requires renegotiation)
5. 18-month financial model under three scenarios: full continuation (post-restructuring), managed wind-down, and immediate kill
6. Streaming infrastructure minimum viable investment estimate (what does "bridge to streaming" actually cost?)

The board's decision today is not Continue vs. Kill — it is **Authorize 90-Day Test vs. Kill Immediately.** This memo argues strongly for the former, with the data gaps above acknowledged as the conditions under which the Kill thesis becomes correct.
