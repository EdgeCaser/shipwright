# Strategic Recommendation: Restructure Total Access as a Bridge to Digital

**TO:** Blockbuster Board of Directors
**FROM:** Strategy Advisory Group
**DATE:** Q3 2007
**RE:** Total Access Program — Continue with Structural Modifications

---

## Decision Frame

**Core Question:** Should Blockbuster continue funding Total Access at current burn rates, or scale it back to protect near-term profitability?

**Recommendation: Continue Total Access, but restructure it as a time-limited bridge strategy toward a hybrid digital-physical model.** Neither full-speed continuation nor outright termination is correct. The program must be preserved as a competitive weapon while its cost structure is reformed and its strategic purpose is redirected from subscriber acquisition toward digital transition.

**The strategic logic is as follows:**

1. **Total Access is the only asset keeping Blockbuster competitively relevant in the subscription market.** Killing it returns all momentum to Netflix and cedes the online rental market permanently. There is no plausible re-entry point once subscribers defect.
2. **The $400M/year burn is unsustainable at current debt levels, but the burn rate is a pricing problem, not a structural one.** The per-return cost of ~$2 can be reduced through throttling, tiered pricing, and franchisee revenue-sharing without destroying the program's value proposition.
3. **The real strategic threat is not Netflix's DVD-by-mail business — it is streaming.** Total Access buys time, but only if that time is used to build digital capabilities. A restructured Total Access funds 18-24 months of runway to develop or acquire streaming infrastructure.

### Why "Continue" Over "Kill"

The case for killing Total Access rests on three premises, all of which are flawed:

| Kill Argument | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| "The losses are unsustainable" | True at current run rate, but the cost structure is modifiable. Throttle in-store returns to 3-5/month, introduce a $2-3 exchange fee, and negotiate franchisee cost-sharing. Estimated savings: $150-200M/year. |
| "Store profitability should be the priority" | Store-only strategy assumes the DVD rental market is stable. It is not. Redbox is compressing store economics from below ($1/night). Netflix streaming will compress from above. Optimizing stores without a digital strategy is optimizing a declining asset. |
| "Icahn and shareholders demand cost discipline" | Icahn's position is rational given current disclosure. The board must reframe Total Access as a transition investment with a defined sunset, not an open-ended subsidy. Present a 24-month restructuring plan with quarterly milestones. |

### Why "Restructure" Over "Full Speed"

Continuing at current burn rates is also wrong:

- **$400M/year against $1.1B in long-term debt leaves no margin for error.** A single bad quarter could trigger covenant violations or credit downgrades.
- **Subscriber acquisition without retention economics is value-destroying.** If subscribers churn when terms tighten, the investment was wasted. Better to tighten terms now and measure true retention.
- **Franchisee revolt is a real operational risk.** Losing franchisee cooperation undermines the in-store return advantage that differentiates Total Access from Netflix.

---

## The Restructured Total Access Plan

### Phase 1: Cost Reform (Q4 2007 - Q1 2008)
- Cap in-store returns at 5 per month per subscriber (currently unlimited)
- Introduce tiered pricing: Basic (online only, $14.99), Plus (5 in-store returns, $19.99), Premium (unlimited returns, $24.99)
- Negotiate franchisee revenue-share: Blockbuster corporate absorbs 60% of return costs, franchisees absorb 40% in exchange for guaranteed foot traffic
- Target: reduce Total Access burn from ~$400M to ~$200M/year

### Phase 2: Digital Investment (Q1 2008 - Q4 2008)
- Allocate $50-75M of savings to streaming/digital download capability
- Evaluate build vs. acquire vs. license for streaming technology
- Leverage store footprint as digital onboarding points (in-store kiosks, broadband demos)
- Explore Redbox-style kiosk deployment in non-store locations to defend against kiosk competition

### Phase 3: Hybrid Transition (2009-2010)
- Migrate Total Access subscribers to hybrid disc+streaming plans
- Begin planned store footprint rationalization (close bottom 15-20% of locations)
- Position Blockbuster as the only provider offering physical + digital + in-store in one subscription

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

1. **Subscriber elasticity to price/term changes is unknown.** We do not know how many of the 3M online subscribers would churn if in-store returns were capped or priced. This is the single most important data point for the restructuring plan. **Recommended action:** A/B test modified terms in 2-3 regional markets before national rollout.

2. **Streaming technology costs and timeline are uncertain.** Blockbuster's failed Enron Broadband deal in 2000 and lack of current digital infrastructure suggest a build-from-scratch approach could take 18-24 months and $100M+. Licensing or acquiring existing technology may be faster. **Recommended action:** Engage 3-4 streaming technology providers for term sheets within 60 days.

3. **Franchisee response to cost-sharing is unpredictable.** Franchisees may refuse terms or accelerate legal challenges. **Recommended action:** Convene franchisee advisory council within 30 days; model worst-case scenario of franchisee defections.

4. **Broadband adoption trajectory affects streaming viability.** At ~50% household penetration in 2007, streaming is not yet a mass-market replacement for physical media. If broadband growth stalls, the physical-to-digital bridge extends. **Recommended action:** Monitor broadband penetration quarterly; adjust Phase 3 timeline accordingly.

5. **Content licensing economics for streaming are opaque.** Netflix's current streaming library of ~1,000 titles suggests content owners are cautious. Blockbuster's studio relationships (from purchasing) may or may not translate to favorable streaming terms. **Recommended action:** Initiate exploratory licensing conversations with top 5 studios.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

### This Recommendation PASSES If:
- [ ] A/B testing confirms >60% subscriber retention under modified Total Access terms (capped returns, tiered pricing)
- [ ] Restructured burn rate falls below $225M/year within two quarters of implementation
- [ ] At least one viable streaming technology path (build, acquire, or license) is identified with <18-month deployment timeline
- [ ] Franchisee advisory council reaches agreement on cost-sharing framework without triggering >10% franchisee defections
- [ ] Icahn bloc agrees to 24-month restructuring runway with quarterly milestone reviews

### This Recommendation FAILS If:
- [ ] Subscriber retention under modified terms falls below 40% (indicates Total Access value proposition is entirely price-driven and not defensible)
- [ ] Debt covenants or credit rating agencies force accelerated cost cuts before restructuring takes effect
- [ ] No viable streaming technology path emerges within 6 months of search
- [ ] Franchisee defections exceed 20%, undermining the in-store return network that gives Total Access its competitive differentiation

### Kill Switch:
If any FAIL condition triggers, the board should immediately shift to an orderly wind-down of Total Access and pivot to a store-optimization + kiosk + selective-digital strategy. The restructuring plan must not become a sunk-cost trap.

## Strongest Counterargument and Rebuttal

**Counterargument:** "The restructured plan is a half-measure. Either Total Access is worth the investment or it isn't. Capping returns and raising prices will gut the competitive advantage that made the program work. Netflix will accelerate subscriber acquisition the moment Blockbuster flinches, and the streaming transition will make the entire disc-by-mail market irrelevant within 5 years anyway. Better to cut losses now and invest everything in store optimization and selective digital experiments."

**Rebuttal:** This argument is strategically coherent but relies on two assumptions that are not yet validated:

1. **That subscriber churn under modified terms will be catastrophic.** This is plausible but unproven. The in-store return experience is genuinely differentiated — no competitor offers it. Many subscribers may value the convenience enough to accept reasonable caps. The A/B test will resolve this within 90 days. Making a permanent strategic decision without this data is premature.

2. **That store optimization alone can sustain Blockbuster through the streaming transition.** This requires the physical rental market to remain large enough to support 5,700+ locations for 5+ years. Redbox kiosk expansion and streaming growth both compress this market from different directions. A store-only strategy is a bet that the market declines slowly enough for Blockbuster to extract sufficient value before it must shrink. Historical evidence from other retail format transitions (bookstores, record stores, video arcades) suggests these declines accelerate faster than incumbents expect.

The restructured Total Access plan is not a half-measure — it is a sequenced bet. It preserves competitive position while the two critical unknowns (subscriber elasticity and streaming viability) are resolved. If both resolve favorably, Blockbuster has a path to hybrid digital-physical leadership. If either fails, the kill switch triggers an orderly retreat. The alternative — killing Total Access today — forecloses the favorable scenario permanently with no option to reverse course.
