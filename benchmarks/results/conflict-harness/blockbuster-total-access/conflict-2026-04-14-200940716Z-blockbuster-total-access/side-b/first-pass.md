# Blockbuster Total Access Recommendation Memo

## Recommendation
Kill the hybrid strategy at its current funding level and replace it with a tightly scoped online transition plan. The board should stop subsidizing free in-store returns and free-rental economics that are destroying cash faster than they are building a durable advantage.

The core issue is not whether Total Access created competitive pressure. It did. The issue is whether Blockbuster can afford to keep buying that pressure long enough to convert it into a defensible position before the market shifts again. In Q3 2007, the answer is no.

## Why This Is The Right Decision
Total Access proved that Blockbuster's store footprint can be weaponized against Netflix in the short term. The program added 2 million subscribers in under a year, pushed Blockbuster online to roughly 3 million subscribers, and coincided with Netflix's first material growth stall. That is real evidence of product-market traction and competitive effect.

But the economics are untenable. The program is burning roughly $400 million per year while Blockbuster carries about $1.1 billion in long-term debt and has already posted an $85 million quarterly loss. A leveraged balance sheet cannot support a strategy that requires indefinite subsidy just to maintain customer appeal. The board is not choosing between growth and caution; it is choosing between a controlled retrenchment now and a forced retrenchment later.

The deeper strategic problem is timing. Even if Total Access keeps hurting Netflix's DVD-by-mail business, the category itself is likely nearing peak over the 2010-2012 window. At the same time, broadband penetration is moving toward a level that will make streaming progressively more viable, and Netflix has already launched Watch Instantly in January 2007. Blockbuster would be pouring capital into winning a late-stage physical-rental battle while its best-capitalized competitor is already building the next delivery model.

Blockbuster's 5,700-store footprint is therefore more liability than moat under the current design. In theory, stores offer local convenience and brand presence. In practice, Total Access turns stores into cost centers that absorb returns, create franchisee conflict, and cannibalize unit economics. The company is not using the footprint to create profitable network effects; it is using it to fund customer acquisition through operational leakage.

## Strongest Counterargument
The strongest case for continuing is that Total Access is the first strategy that genuinely rattled Netflix. If Blockbuster cuts back now, it may surrender the one differentiator Netflix cannot easily replicate: nationwide stores integrated with online service. With 47 million store-visit customers, Blockbuster could argue it still has a customer funnel Netflix lacks.

That argument is strategically serious but still insufficient. First, the program has not closed the scale gap: Netflix remains at 7.5 million subscribers versus roughly 3 million online for Blockbuster. Second, the advantage depends on costly in-store behavior that does not scale economically. Third, the benefit is tied to a physical format whose long-run outlook is weakening. A good strategy can be expensive for a period; this one is expensive in the exact part of the value chain that is likely to matter less over time.

## What The Board Should Do Instead
Blockbuster should end the current Total Access subsidy structure immediately and shift to a narrower transition strategy:

1. Remove free in-store-return economics that trigger the largest per-transaction losses.
2. Preserve an online subscription offering, but price it to stand on its own rather than to punish Netflix.
3. Use stores as cash-harvesting and customer-service assets, not as subsidized fulfillment nodes.
4. Stabilize franchisee relations by reducing direct cannibalization and clarifying store-level economics.
5. Reallocate scarce capital toward digital option value, including streaming partnerships or a smaller in-house streaming roadmap, rather than doubling down on peak-DVD economics.
6. Prepare for Redbox pressure by protecting convenience-oriented store categories selectively rather than trying to outspend every low-price entrant.

## Decision Frame
The board should kill Total Access as a full-scale hybrid growth strategy and replace it with a scaled-back online business designed for cash discipline and digital transition. The deciding logic is that the current program wins customers but does not create a financially durable advantage before the market migrates away from physical rental.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- The board needs cleaner cohort economics: retention, lifetime value, and attach rates for Total Access subscribers versus standard online subscribers.
- It is unclear how many of the 2 million added subscribers would remain after benefits are reduced.
- The pace and monetization curve of streaming remain uncertain in 2007, including content licensing feasibility for Blockbuster.
- Store-level profitability by company-owned versus franchise locations is necessary to judge how much of the footprint is worth preserving.
- Management should quantify whether a modified hybrid offer could retain most of the demand with materially better unit economics.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if the board accepts that liquidity preservation and strategic flexibility matter more than extending an expensive tactical blow against Netflix's current model.

Pass if management can execute a rapid redesign that stops the worst cash leakage without collapsing the entire online customer base.

Fail if the board believes Blockbuster can finance multiple years of heavy losses and simultaneously build a credible streaming position.

Fail if management cannot produce a near-term operating plan that protects store cash flow while preserving a smaller online foothold.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A 90-day transition plan with three components: revised online pricing and benefit design, store/franchise economic impact by segment, and a digital-options memo covering partnership, acquisition, or build paths for streaming.
