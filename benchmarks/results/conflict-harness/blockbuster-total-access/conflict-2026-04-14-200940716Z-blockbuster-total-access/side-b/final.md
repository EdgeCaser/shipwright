# Revised artifact

## Recommendation
Kill Total Access as a full-scale hybrid strategy in its current form. Do not continue funding the existing free-return/free-rental design at current levels. Preserve the online business only through an immediate redesign whose purpose is to stop the subsidy, not to keep buying share.

This is not a recommendation to abandon online. It is a recommendation to terminate a specific economic design that is consuming too much cash for a company with Blockbuster's balance sheet and time horizon.

## Core Judgment
Total Access proved two things at once. First, it created real competitive pressure: roughly 2 million subscriber adds in under a year, online scale to about 3 million, and a visible stall in Netflix's growth. Second, it did so with economics that Blockbuster cannot responsibly carry: about $400 million of annual incremental burn, roughly $1.1 billion of long-term debt, and an $85 million Q2 2007 loss.

The critique is correct that the original memo leaned too hard on the burn figure without explicitly separating the current design from all possible hybrid designs. That distinction matters. The right board decision is still to kill the current strategy because the board is being asked whether to continue funding Total Access at current levels, and the evidence says no. A redesign path can be explored, but it should be treated as a salvage test after termination of the current subsidy structure, not as justification for continuing the program unchanged.

## Why The Strategy Should Still Be Killed
The strongest argument for continuation is that Total Access may be the only move that materially unsettled Netflix. That should be taken seriously. But a durable advantage requires more than causing temporary fear in a competitor. For this board, "durable" should mean three things:

1. The advantage can be funded without open-ended cash burn.
2. The advantage materially closes the scale gap before the DVD-by-mail market peaks.
3. The advantage improves Blockbuster's position against the next format transition, not only the current one.

Total Access fails that test on the current evidence. Even after the strongest period of traction, Blockbuster remains well behind Netflix at roughly 3 million online subscribers versus 7.5 million. The advantage depends on store-based benefits that cost about $2 per return and generate franchise conflict. And the company is fighting hardest in a format likely to peak around 2010-2012 while Netflix has already launched Watch Instantly in January 2007 and broadband penetration is moving toward a much better streaming environment by 2010.

So the issue is not whether Total Access worked competitively. It did. The issue is whether Blockbuster can convert that traction into a self-funding strategic position before either liquidity or market timing runs out. On the evidence provided, it cannot.

## Middle Path, But Not Middle Funding
The board should explicitly reject a false binary between "continue full subsidy" and "exit online entirely." The correct middle path is:

- kill the current Total Access economics now;
- keep the online channel alive under revised pricing and benefits;
- run a short redesign window to test whether a modified hybrid can retain enough subscribers at sharply lower cost.

That redesign could include tiered return limits, reduced free-rental credits, co-pays on in-store exchanges, or narrower eligibility. But unless management can show in 60-90 days that the redesigned offer cuts burn to a level consistent with debt service and store economics, the board should not keep subsidizing the model.

## Strongest Counterargument
The best case for continuing is that sustained pressure might have forced Netflix into a capital-intensive response and given Blockbuster time to exploit its 5,700-store footprint and 47 million store-visit customers. That is plausible.

It is still not enough. Netflix had more subscribers, a cleaner balance between growth and business model, and was already investing in streaming infrastructure. Blockbuster, by contrast, was trying to win a share war with a leveraged balance sheet, internal franchise conflict, and a store network that under the current design acts as a cost amplifier. A strategy can be strategically promising and still be financially non-viable for the company attempting it. That is the case here.

## Board Action
The board should vote to end Total Access as currently designed and require management to deliver a 90-day transition plan with three outputs:

- a redesigned online offer with materially lower subsidy,
- store and franchise economics under the new design,
- a digital transition plan focused on partnerships or narrowly scoped streaming investment rather than full-scale DVD subsidy.

## Decision Frame
Kill Total Access at current funding and current design. Preserve online only as a redesigned, cash-disciplined transition business. The deciding logic is that Blockbuster has demonstrated demand creation but not a financeable path to convert that demand into durable advantage before the physical-rental market matures and streaming matters more.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- The exact subscriber retention curve under a lower-subsidy hybrid design is unknown.
- The board lacks a decomposed bridge from the reported roughly $400 million burn to specific levers that could be reduced quickly.
- The realistic cost, speed, and feasibility of a 2007 streaming partnership or build path for Blockbuster remain uncertain.
- Management has not shown whether the store footprint can be reconfigured into a profitable service and acquisition advantage rather than a return-processing liability.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if the board accepts that the relevant decision is whether to keep funding the current design, not whether online matters strategically.

Pass if management can redesign the offer within 60-90 days and prove materially improved unit economics without collapsing the subscriber base.

Fail if management argues for continued full-scale subsidy without showing a near-term path to sharply lower burn.

Fail if the board treats short-term competitive disruption of Netflix as sufficient proof of long-term strategic viability.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A board decision memo with a redesign gate: subscriber retention scenarios, unit-economics sensitivity by benefit design, franchise impact by store type, and a digital-option comparison covering partnership, acquisition, and limited internal streaming build.
