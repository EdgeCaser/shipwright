# Critique: finding-1

- Target Side: side_b
- Target Claim IDs: side_b-claim-1, side_b-claim-2
- Attack Type: evidence_gap
- Severity: high

## Claim Under Attack
Total Access demonstrated short-term competitive traction but not a durable, financeable advantage, and its $400M burn is incompatible with Blockbuster's balance sheet.

## Evidence Or Reason
Side B treats the $400M burn as a fixed, irreducible cost of the program but simultaneously lists as an open question whether the burn could be reduced through redesign rather than full termination. This is a critical gap: the memo recommends killing the strategy based on a cost figure it admits it hasn't decomposed. The $2 per in-store return is a design parameter, not a law of physics — tiered return limits, reduced free rental credits, or co-pay structures could preserve the competitive flywheel at a fraction of the current burn. Side B never models a middle path between full subsidy and full termination, creating a false binary. Furthermore, the claim that the advantage is not 'durable' ignores that Total Access achieved in under 12 months what Netflix took years to build (2M net subscriber adds while stalling a well-capitalized competitor). The memo provides no evidence for what 'durable' would look like or what threshold of subscriber growth would satisfy that standard — it simply asserts transience without defining the counterfactual. A strategy that forced Netflix's CEO to describe himself as 'ichiban scared' and froze Netflix's growth trajectory is dismissed as mere 'tactical' pressure without engaging the possibility that sustained pressure could have forced Netflix into a capital-depleting response of its own.
