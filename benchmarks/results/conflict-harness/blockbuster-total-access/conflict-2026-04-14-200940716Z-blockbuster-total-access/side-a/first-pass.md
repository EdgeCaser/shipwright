# Strategic Recommendation: Restructure Total Access into a Streaming-Bridge Strategy

**To:** Blockbuster Board of Directors
**From:** Strategy Advisory Group
**Date:** Q3 2007
**Decision:** Neither continue at current burn nor kill outright — restructure Total Access as a time-limited bridge to streaming, with immediate cost discipline.

---

## Decision Frame

The board faces a false binary. The real question is not "fund Total Access or cut it" but **"what is the highest-value use of Blockbuster's remaining financial runway before physical media declines?"**

Three facts anchor the frame:

1. **Total Access is the only competitive weapon that has ever made Netflix flinch.** Two million subscribers in under twelve months, Netflix growth stalling for the first time, and Hastings privately expressing fear — this is rare, validated competitive signal. Abandoning a strategy your strongest competitor fears is strategically incoherent unless you literally cannot fund it.

2. **The current burn rate ($400M/year) is unsustainable against $1.1B in long-term debt and quarterly losses of $85M.** Continuing unchanged is a solvency question, not a strategy question. The program must be restructured to reduce per-transaction cost while preserving subscriber momentum.

3. **Physical disc rental is a transitional business with a visible expiration date.** DVD-by-mail peaks 2010–2012. Broadband penetration moves from 50% to 70%+ by 2010. Netflix's January 2007 streaming launch — even with only 1,000 titles — is a directional bet on where the margin structure shifts. Blockbuster's 5,700 stores become liabilities, not assets, as this transition accelerates.

**Recommendation:** Restructure Total Access into a margin-disciplined subscriber acquisition engine that funds and accelerates a streaming pivot. Reduce the per-return subsidy, cap monthly in-store returns, and redirect $150M–$200M of the savings into streaming content licensing and technology.

---

## Core Claims

### Claim 1: Killing Total Access surrenders the only competitive position that pressured Netflix

Total Access is not a generic marketing spend — it exploits Blockbuster's sole structural advantage (physical store network) against a pure-play online competitor. The 2M subscriber additions demonstrate product-market fit for hybrid convenience. Netflix responded by cutting prices and increasing marketing spend, confirming the threat was real. Eliminating this program returns Blockbuster to competing on stores alone against Netflix's online model and Redbox's price point — a position with no strategic differentiation.

The franchisee resistance, while operationally real, reflects a principal-agent problem (franchisee economics vs. corporate strategy), not evidence the program lacks consumer value.

### Claim 2: The current cost structure is fixable without killing the program

The ~$2 per in-store return cost is a design parameter, not a law of physics. Restructuring options include:
- **Cap in-store returns** at 3–5 per month per subscriber (preserving convenience perception while limiting cost exposure)
- **Tiered pricing** that charges a modest premium for unlimited in-store returns
- **Redirect returned discs** to the online fulfillment pool rather than re-renting free (eliminating the lost-rental-revenue component)
- **Negotiate franchisee revenue-sharing** on incremental store traffic driven by Total Access returns

A 40–50% cost reduction from $400M to $200–240M/year is achievable while retaining the core value proposition.

### Claim 3: Freed capital must go to streaming, not store profitability

Refocusing on store profitability is a retreat into a declining business. Redbox is attacking from below ($1/night, grocery/convenience locations). Streaming is attacking from above (zero logistics cost, instant delivery). Stores cannot win a cost war against kiosks or a convenience war against streaming.

Blockbuster's 47M store-visit customers and 3M online subscribers represent a massive installed base for a streaming offering. The failed 2000 Enron Broadband deal shows Blockbuster recognized this trajectory early but executed poorly. The window to build streaming capability is 2007–2010, before broadband penetration and Netflix's content library make the gap insurmountable.

### Claim 4: Icahn's value-destruction framing mistakes the cost of transition for the cost of failure

Icahn's position — that online investment destroys value — assumes the store business has a durable standalone future. It does not. The choice is between spending to transition or spending nothing and presiding over a slower decline. Every dollar "saved" by cutting online investment extends the life of a business model with a visible expiration date by months, not years.

The correct financial framing: Total Access at restructured levels is the customer acquisition cost for building the subscriber base that a streaming business requires. The alternative — no online investment — yields a store-only business worth substantially less than current enterprise value within 3–5 years as disc rental declines.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

| Unknown | Why It Matters | How to Resolve |
|---|---|---|
| **Streaming content licensing costs at scale** | Determines whether Blockbuster can build a viable streaming library before cash runs out | Commission a 90-day analysis of studio licensing terms and costs for 5,000+ title library |
| **True franchisee attrition risk** | If franchisees defect en masse, store network advantage evaporates | Survey top 50 franchisees on restructured Total Access terms; model worst-case closures |
| **Debt covenant and refinancing capacity** | $1.1B debt may constrain ability to fund any transition | Engage investment bank to model restructuring scenarios and covenant headroom |
| **Consumer willingness to pay for tiered Total Access** | Pricing restructure viability is untested | A/B test tiered pricing in 3 DMAs over 60 days before national rollout |
| **Streaming technology build-vs-buy timeline** | In-house build may take too long; acquisition or partnership could accelerate | Evaluate 2–3 streaming technology acquisition targets (estimated $50–100M range) |
| **Redbox expansion trajectory** | Kiosk growth rate determines how fast store economics erode independently of online competition | Model Redbox's 2008–2010 growth against Blockbuster same-store-sales trends |
