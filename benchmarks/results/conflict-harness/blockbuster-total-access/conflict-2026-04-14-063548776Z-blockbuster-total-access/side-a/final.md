# Revised artifact

# Blockbuster Total Access: Kill The Current Program, Preserve A Narrow Online Transition

## Recommendation
Blockbuster should **kill Total Access in its current form** and replace it with a tightly capped transition offer. The core reason is not that DVDs become irrelevant immediately. It is that Blockbuster cannot afford to fight a multi-year subsidy war in physical media from a weaker balance sheet, with channel conflict and governance resistance, while also needing flexibility to invest in the next distribution model.

Total Access proved that the hybrid model can win customers and pressure Netflix. That is not the same as proving it is financeable or strategically durable for Blockbuster.

## Why This Is The Correct Decision

### 1. The economics are not survivable at current scale
Total Access added roughly 2 million subscribers in under a year and helped take Blockbuster Online to about 3 million subscribers. That validates demand. But the program is also burning about $400M annually while Blockbuster carries roughly $1.1B in long-term debt and just posted an $85M quarterly loss. That is the board-level fact pattern that matters most.

Even if DVD-by-mail remains a meaningful market through 2010-2012, Blockbuster would still need to finance several years of losses to exploit that window. The company is not positioned to do that safely. A strategy can be competitively effective and still be unfit for the owner of the asset.

### 2. The problem is not that physical media disappears tomorrow; it is that the moat is too expensive to hold
The critique is correct that Side A overstated the immediacy of streaming displacement. In Q3 2007, streaming is early: Netflix Watch Instantly launched in January 2007 with a limited catalog, broadband penetration is only around 50% of U.S. households, and DVD-by-mail may still grow until 2010-2012.

But that actually sharpens the recommendation rather than reversing it. If physical media has 3-5 more years of relevance, Blockbuster would need to fund 3-5 more years of a structurally expensive hybrid offer to convert tactical momentum into durable share gains. The case packet gives no evidence that Total Access unit economics improve enough to make that feasible. Instead, each in-store return imposes about $2 in handling and lost rental revenue, meaning growth deepens the subsidy burden.

So the issue is not "streaming makes stores worthless now." The issue is "the current moat requires too much cash to defend during the remaining DVD window."

### 3. The store footprint is real leverage, but not under free-exchange economics
Blockbuster's 5,700-store footprint is a genuine competitive asset in disc rental. It created a convenience advantage that Netflix could not match and clearly contributed to the pressure Total Access put on Netflix.

However, an asset only matters if it produces positive incremental economics. Under the current program, the store base is being used as a subsidized service layer: customers return discs in store, Blockbuster absorbs handling cost, and stores lose the chance to re-rent the returned title at full economics. That turns the footprint from an advantage into a transfer mechanism from store P&L to online subscriber growth.

The board should preserve the footprint as a transition asset, but stop using it in a way that destroys contribution.

### 4. Blockbuster lacks the coalition required for a long subsidy campaign
Even if the board believed the remaining DVD window justified aggressive investment, the organization is not configured to sustain it. Franchisees are hostile because the program cannibalizes store economics. Carl Icahn controls three board seats and views the losses as unsustainable. Leadership has already changed. Those conditions matter because a multi-year channel-transition strategy only works when capital providers and operating stakeholders accept near-term pain.

Blockbuster does not have that alignment. The likely result is not committed execution, but a half-supported strategy that consumes cash and then gets cut late.

## Strongest Counterargument, And Why It Still Does Not Change The Decision
The best counterargument is that Total Access found the first credible way to hurt Netflix, during a period when DVDs still likely have several years left. If Blockbuster pulls back now, it may surrender a rare chance to gain share before streaming becomes fully viable.

That is the strongest case for continuation, and it is real. But it still fails the board test because the relevant question is not whether the tactic works. It is whether Blockbuster can afford to press it long enough to win. The evidence provided says no: high annual burn, meaningful debt, current losses, franchise conflict, and investor resistance. A moat that requires years of funding from a capital structure that cannot support it is not a defendable board strategy.

## Board Directive
1. End Total Access in its current unlimited form.
2. Replace it with a capped online offer aimed at retaining the highest-value subscribers.
3. Remove or sharply restrict free in-store exchange economics that destroy store-level contribution.
4. Preserve online customer relationships and data, but require any ongoing hybrid offer to meet explicit loss caps and retention targets.
5. Reserve capital for a narrower digital transition path rather than spending the balance sheet on a full-scale DVD subsidy war.

## Decision Frame
**Decision:** Kill Total Access as currently designed; keep only a narrow, financially bounded transition version.

**Why now:** The key issue is not whether Total Access works competitively. It does. The issue is that Blockbuster cannot safely fund the several-year subsidy campaign likely required to convert that advantage into durable share gains.

**What must be true for this decision to be right:**
- Total Access unit economics cannot improve fast enough to offset the current subsidy burden.
- Blockbuster's debt load and loss profile limit its ability to finance a prolonged physical-media share war.
- The board values liquidity and optionality over an expensive attempt to maximize DVD-era share.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- Exact cohort retention and lifetime value for Total Access subscribers versus standard online subscribers.
- Whether repricing or capping the offer could preserve most of the competitive benefit at much lower cost.
- The time path by which streaming becomes a full substitute for the DVD catalog.
- Store-level contribution impact by market and by franchise versus company-owned footprint.
- The minimum capital required for a credible streaming partnership or entry option.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
**Pass for decision now:** yes. The board has enough evidence to stop funding the current version of Total Access.

**Fail for full operating-plan approval:** yes. Management still needs to produce a replacement plan with explicit economics, loss caps, and customer-retention assumptions.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A **24-month transition economics plan** comparing:
1. a capped hybrid retention model,
2. an online-only subscription model,
3. a partnership-led digital entry option.

That artifact should include cohort retention, store contribution impact, franchise effects, liquidity implications, and hard kill criteria.
