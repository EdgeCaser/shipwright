# Critique: finding-2

- Target Side: side_a
- Target Claim IDs: side_a-claim-1, side_a-claim-2
- Attack Type: evidence_gap
- Severity: high

## Claim Under Attack
Total Access's $400M annual burn is unsustainable and killing it preserves the company, while competitive effect against Netflix does not constitute enterprise value creation.

## Evidence Or Reason
Side A treats the $400M burn as a fixed, irreducible cost and dismisses the competitive effect as merely 'tactical,' but the artifact contains no analysis of what happens to Blockbuster's revenue trajectory if Total Access is killed. The case packet shows Blockbuster had 47M store-visit customers in a declining physical-rental market with Redbox undercutting at $1/night and Netflix pulling subscribers toward mail/streaming. Killing the only program that was growing Blockbuster's subscriber base and pressuring its primary competitor does not 'preserve liquidity' in any durable sense — it accelerates the core revenue decline that makes the debt load dangerous in the first place. Side A's own evidence gaps section admits it lacks 'precise cohort economics,' 'whether targeted pricing could reduce the burn materially,' and 'how much of the 47M store-visit base can be migrated.' Without this data, the claim that killing the program is safer than funding it is an assumption dressed as analysis. The artifact asserts the balance sheet 'cannot absorb' the subsidy but never models the counterfactual: a Blockbuster with no online growth engine, declining store traffic, Redbox eroding impulse rentals, and Netflix resuming uncontested subscriber acquisition. The $400M cost is visible; the revenue destruction from retreat is not quantified anywhere in the memo, which is a critical asymmetry in the argument.
