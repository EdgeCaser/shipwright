# Strategic Recommendation: Scale Back Total Access and Pivot to a Hybrid Streaming-Store Model

## Decision Frame

**Core question:** Should Blockbuster continue funding Total Access at ~$400M/year to press a competitive advantage in DVD-by-mail, or scale it back and redeploy resources toward a defensible long-term position?

**Recommendation: Scale back Total Access to a sustainable level and redirect freed capital toward streaming infrastructure and an optimized store footprint.** The exact sustainable spend level and exchange cap must be determined through rapid financial modeling and market testing (see Unknowns & Evidence Gaps), but the strategic direction — reduce burn on a declining-market program and invest in the streaming transition — is sound regardless of where the precise numbers land.

This is not a recommendation to kill the online business. It is a recommendation to stop winning a war whose battlefield is about to be abandoned.

### The Case for Scaling Back

**1. Total Access is winning a shrinking market.** The DVD-by-mail market is projected to peak between 2010-2012 and then decline. Broadband penetration is at ~50% today and projected to exceed 70% by 2010. Netflix has already placed its strategic bet on streaming (Watch Instantly, January 2007). Total Access is a brilliant tactical weapon aimed at a target that is moving. Spending $400M/year to capture share in a market with a 3-5 year runway is capital destruction, not capital investment.

**2. The burn rate is existential given Blockbuster's balance sheet.** With $1.1B in long-term debt and a Q2 2007 net loss of $85M, Blockbuster does not have the financial reserves to sustain a $400M/year program while simultaneously investing in the streaming transition. Netflix, by contrast, is profitable and can fund its streaming pivot from operating cash flow. Blockbuster cannot fight on two fronts. We must choose.

**3. The competitive stall on Netflix is temporary, not structural.** Total Access stalled Netflix growth because it offered a genuine value proposition: return online discs in-store for immediate gratification. But this advantage depends entirely on the store footprint — the same footprint that carries ~$1B in annual lease obligations. Netflix's response will not be to match Total Access; it will be to accelerate the streaming transition that makes the physical advantage irrelevant. We are forcing Netflix to execute its own strategy faster.

**4. Franchisee revolt is a real operational constraint.** Franchisees are not irrational — Total Access cannibalizes their unit economics by converting paid rentals into free exchanges. A program that alienates a significant portion of our distribution network creates execution risk that compounds the financial risk.

**5. Redbox is compressing the low-end of physical rental.** At $1/night, Redbox kiosks in grocery and convenience stores are attacking Blockbuster's casual-rental segment from below. Total Access addresses the high-frequency subscriber segment, but our store economics are being squeezed from both directions simultaneously.

### The Restructured Approach

- **Reduce Total Access to a financially sustainable subscriber tier.** The mechanism is capping free in-store exchanges (testing tiers of 3 and 5 per month against unlimited). The precise cost reduction and retention impact are unknown — this is acknowledged as the critical unknown in this recommendation. What the evidence does establish is that the current $400M/year burn is unsustainable on a $1.1B-debt balance sheet with quarterly losses of $85M, and that the market being won is in structural decline. The direction of the adjustment is clear even if the exact landing point requires empirical validation.
- **Redirect savings toward streaming capability.** License content, build or acquire streaming technology, and leverage the store footprint as a marketing and customer acquisition channel for the digital business — not as the business itself. The quantum of redirected capital depends on the Total Access restructuring results.
- **Rationalize the store footprint proactively.** Begin closing the bottom 15-20% of locations by profitability. Use remaining stores as competitive differentiators for customer acquisition and same-day fulfillment, not as the primary revenue engine.
- **Negotiate franchisee alignment.** Revised Total Access terms with capped exchanges give franchisees a sustainable model and restore cooperation.

### Counterarguments Addressed

**"Total Access is the only thing that has worked against Netflix — killing it surrenders the initiative."** This is the strongest counterargument. Total Access demonstrably stalled Netflix and proved Blockbuster could compete in online subscription. But sustaining a $400M/year program on a leveraged balance sheet to win a market with a declining long-term trajectory is not pressing an advantage — it is overextending into a retreat. The recommendation is not to kill the online business but to make it sustainable while pivoting investment toward the actual future battlefield.

**"Netflix streaming is nascent — only 1,000 titles, 50% broadband penetration."** True today. But the trajectory is unambiguous. Broadband is growing, content licensing will expand, and Netflix is investing its profits into this transition. By the time streaming reaches critical mass (2010-2012), Blockbuster needs to have a credible offering or it will be structurally locked out. That requires investment now, which requires freeing capital from Total Access.

**"Icahn's cost-cutting focus is short-termist."** Icahn is wrong about the direction (he wants to retreat to stores-only profitability) but right about the constraint (the burn rate is unsustainable). The correct synthesis is to reduce the burn while investing the savings in the streaming transition — not to retreat, but to advance on the right front.

**"Blockbuster's 47M store-visit customers are an unmatched asset."** They are — if we use them as a funnel into digital services. They become a liability if we use them to justify maintaining 5,700 locations at current cost structure while the market shifts underneath us.

**"The scaled-back economics are speculative — you don't know if capped exchanges retain subscribers."** Correct. The specific dollar figures for a restructured program are estimates, not validated operating models. This recommendation does not claim otherwise. What the evidence does establish with confidence is: (a) the current $400M/year burn is unsustainable against $1.1B debt and $85M quarterly losses, (b) the market being subsidized is in structural decline, and (c) the streaming transition requires capital Blockbuster cannot access without reducing Total Access spend. The precise restructuring parameters must be validated through A/B testing and financial modeling (see Unknowns below), but the strategic direction holds across any plausible retention scenario. Even if subscriber churn from capping is severe (60%+), maintaining an unsustainable burn in a declining market is worse than controlled subscriber loss paired with investment in the growth vector.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

1. **Total Access restructuring economics (CRITICAL).** The specific cost of a capped-exchange program and subscriber retention under modified terms are unknown. Prior estimates of ~$150M/year residual burn were illustrative, not evidence-based. This is the highest-priority unknown because the entire execution plan depends on finding a sustainable price point that retains enough subscribers to justify the program. *Mitigation: Immediately commission (a) finance team modeling of per-subscriber cost curves at 3, 5, and 7 exchange caps, and (b) A/B testing of capped plans in 2-3 test markets within 60 days. Decision gate: if no cap level achieves positive unit economics with <40% churn, escalate to full program wind-down.*

2. **Streaming content licensing costs and availability.** We do not have clear data on what it would cost Blockbuster to build a competitive streaming library. Netflix's first-mover deals may have locked up favorable terms. *Mitigation: Commission a 60-day content licensing feasibility study before committing streaming capital.*

3. **Blockbuster's technical capability to build streaming.** The failed Enron Broadband deal in 2000 and lack of subsequent streaming investment suggests a significant technology gap. Can Blockbuster build or acquire streaming capability fast enough? *Mitigation: Evaluate acquisition targets (MovieLink, CinemaNow) and partnership options (hardware OEMs, ISPs) within 90 days.*

4. **Redbox trajectory and impact on store economics.** Redbox kiosk expansion is accelerating but we lack granular data on per-store revenue impact. *Mitigation: Analyze same-store sales trends in markets with high Redbox density.*

5. **Debt covenant and refinancing constraints.** Can Blockbuster's balance sheet support continued online investment at any level while servicing $1.1B in debt? *Mitigation: CFO stress-test of revised plan against covenant thresholds and 2008-2009 refinancing windows.*
