# Recommendation: Kill Total Access in its current form; preserve the customer insight, not the subsidy

## Board Recommendation
Blockbuster should **kill Total Access as currently structured** and replace it with a sharply narrower hybrid offer that protects liquidity, stabilizes franchise relations, and funds a fast option on digital delivery. The current program has proved that Blockbuster's store network can create real competitive pressure, but it has **not** proved that this advantage can be monetized before the business model shifts toward streaming. The board's decision is not whether Total Access is strategically interesting; it is whether Blockbuster's balance sheet can carry a $400M annual subsidy in a shrinking physical-rental category while a larger, focused rival invests into the next format. The answer is no.

## Why This Is The Correct Decision
First, Total Access has demonstrated **market appeal but not economic viability**. The evidence is strong that the offer resonated: roughly 2 million subscribers were added in under a year, online subscribers reached about 3 million, and Netflix's growth stalled during the program's peak. That validates the core insight that combining online convenience with store returns creates differentiation. But the same packet says each in-store return costs about $2 in handling and lost rental revenue, producing roughly **$400M/year of incremental burn**. A customer proposition that wins share only by imposing losses of this scale is not yet a strategy; it is an expensive promotion.

Second, Blockbuster's **capital structure cannot absorb a prolonged format war subsidy**. The company already carries about **$1.1B in long-term debt** and posted an **$85M loss in Q2 2007**. In that context, continuing Total Access at current levels amounts to levering the company harder behind a business whose peak is expected in 2010-2012 and then declines. Boards should fund losses when they buy durable future cash flows or strategic control of the next platform. Here, the company would be funding losses mainly to defend share in a transitional channel while Netflix invests in streaming.

Third, the hybrid advantage is **time-limited**. Blockbuster's 5,700-store footprint is valuable only if stores remain the center of home entertainment convenience. The evidence points the other way: U.S. broadband is around 50% of households in 2007 and projected above 70% by 2010, Netflix launched Watch Instantly in January 2007, and DVD-by-mail is projected to peak within a few years. That means Total Access is fighting brilliantly for superiority in a format that is approaching maturity while the basis of competition is shifting toward digital delivery. Winning a temporary disc-rental battle while underinvesting in the next platform would be strategically hollow.

Fourth, the program is **organizationally destabilizing**. Franchisees see Total Access as cannibalizing store economics, and influential board representation aligned with Carl Icahn already views the online losses as value-destructive. A strategy that depends on burning cash, alienating channel partners, and splitting the board is difficult to sustain long enough to create an eventual payoff. Even if the strategy were directionally correct, Blockbuster lacks the internal alignment and financial slack required to execute it at current scale.

Fifth, competitive pressure is rising from **both ends of the market**. Netflix is scaling a subscription relationship and investing in streaming. Redbox is expanding aggressively into low-cost, high-convenience kiosk rentals. Total Access sits awkwardly between them: too expensive to out-economize Redbox, too anchored to physical retail to outrun Netflix's digital migration. That is a dangerous middle position.

## Strongest Counterarguments, And Why They Do Not Change The Answer
The strongest argument for continuing is that Total Access is the first strategy that visibly hurt Netflix. That matters. Reed Hastings being "ichiban scared," subscriber adds accelerating, and Netflix growth stalling all indicate Blockbuster found a real wedge. If Blockbuster retreats now, it may surrender the first credible offensive move it has made in years.

That argument still fails because **competitive effect is not the same as enterprise value creation**. Hurting Netflix is only worth paying for if Blockbuster can either convert the advantage into profitable scale or use it to bridge decisively into streaming. The case packet offers no evidence that current unit economics can improve fast enough, and the balance sheet leaves little room for a long learning curve.

A second counterargument is that the store network is a unique asset Netflix cannot replicate. That is true in the short run. But asset uniqueness is only valuable if the asset remains central to future customer behavior. As streaming adoption rises, the store network risks becoming a fixed-cost burden rather than a moat. The board should monetize and repurpose this asset selectively, not double down on it as the foundation of strategy.

A third counterargument is that scaling back signals weakness and hands initiative back to Netflix. That is also true tactically. But boards are not paid to maximize tactical aggression; they are paid to allocate capital rationally under uncertainty. Preserving liquidity and redeploying investment toward a narrower hybrid model plus digital option value is more defensible than continuing a broad subsidy the company may not survive.

## What Blockbuster Should Do Instead
Kill Total Access **in its current unlimited, heavily subsidized form**. Replace it with a limited hybrid tier designed to capture the highest-value use cases while capping in-store-return economics. Examples include restricting the number of free in-store exchanges, charging for premium hybrid access, or narrowing eligibility to markets and customer segments where store density and attachment rates are strongest.

At the same time, the board should redirect capital toward three priorities:
1. Stabilize the core store base and renegotiate franchise alignment around a model they can economically support.
2. Build or partner aggressively for digital delivery, even if initially subscale, because strategic relevance is shifting there.
3. Use stores as a customer-acquisition and convenience channel where they are genuinely advantaged, not as an excuse to subsidize unprofitable behavior nationally.

This path accepts a near-term loss of momentum in online subscriber growth, but it materially lowers the risk of a balance-sheet-driven collapse while preserving the option to compete in the next format.

## Decision Frame
**Decision:** Kill Total Access at current funding levels and relaunch a constrained hybrid offer.

**Why now:** The current model proves demand but consumes too much capital relative to Blockbuster's debt load, quarterly losses, franchise conflict, and the approaching shift from mail/store rental to streaming.

**What must be true for this decision to be right:**
- The current $400M annual burn is not rapidly reversible through pricing or operating improvements.
- Streaming adoption will accelerate fast enough that defending physical-rental share alone does not justify the subsidy.
- A narrower hybrid tier can preserve some differentiation without destabilizing the enterprise.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- Precise cohort economics of Total Access customers: retention, ARPU, attachment to store purchases, and lifetime value.
- Whether targeted pricing or benefit caps could reduce the $400M burn materially without collapsing demand.
- The true cost and timeline for a credible digital partnership or in-house streaming offer.
- How much of the 47M store-visit customer base can be migrated into a profitable cross-channel membership.
- Market-by-market evidence on where stores function as an asset versus a liability.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
**Pass if:** management can present within 60 days a restructuring plan that cuts hybrid-program losses sharply, preserves the best customers, secures franchisee cooperation, and reallocates capital toward digital capability.

**Fail if:** management proposes only modest cost trimming while preserving the core subsidy logic, or continues to treat subscriber growth as sufficient justification without proving path-to-profitability and digital transition readiness.
