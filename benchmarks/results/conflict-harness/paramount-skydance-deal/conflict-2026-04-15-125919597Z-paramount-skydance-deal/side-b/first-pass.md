# Paramount-Skydance Recommendation Memo

Paramount should accept the Skydance-led transaction now while using the 45-day go-shop to pressure-test price and terms. The committee's core job is not to preserve abstract optionality; it is to choose the highest-probability value-preserving path under deteriorating industry conditions. On the facts available in July 2024, Paramount is negotiating from a position of weakening leverage: linear television economics are structurally declining, advertising is soft, Paramount+ is not yet profitable, and the company is carrying nearly $15 billion of debt. In that context, fresh capital, balance-sheet support, and strategic control clarity are worth more than waiting for an uncertain superior alternative.

The Skydance package matters because it addresses both ownership and operating risk at once. The two-step structure resolves the controller issue through the National Amusements acquisition, provides roughly $4.5 billion of cash or stock for shareholders, and adds another $1.5 billion of balance-sheet support. That does not eliminate execution risk, but it does materially improve Paramount's ability to absorb continued streaming losses and manage decline in the legacy business. A committee considering rejection must answer a harder question: what specifically gets better by waiting, and on what timeline? The case packet does not show a fully financed, clearly executable superior path today.

The strongest argument for rejecting the deal is that the current process may underprice Paramount's assets. Paramount still owns a valuable library, distribution footprint, studio assets, and traditional TV cash flows. Other parties, including Apollo/Sony and later Edgar Bronfman Jr., have shown interest. Critics also have a real governance point: a controller-linked transaction can create justified concern that the process is serving the controller's exit rather than optimizing for minority shareholders. If a clean, higher, fully financed bid were likely to emerge quickly, waiting would be attractive.

That counterargument is still weaker than the case for signing now. First, the go-shop period preserves near-term optionality without forcing the committee to remain uncommitted indefinitely. Second, interested parties are not the same as executable alternatives; financing certainty, regulatory complexity, diligence readiness, and willingness to solve the controller issue all matter. Third, the independent-turnaround path is not a neutral baseline. It means carrying debt and streaming losses longer while operating in businesses that are structurally eroding. In a declining industry, time is not free; delay itself destroys bargaining power.

Skydance's strategic rationale is also directionally credible. The combination offers creative leadership, technology orientation, and fresh capital alongside Paramount's content library and distribution footprint. The committee should not overpay for narrative, but it should recognize that Paramount needs more than financial engineering. A buyer or partner that can recapitalize the company and articulate an operating thesis is more valuable than a purely theoretical price maximizer that may never close.

The practical recommendation is to approve the Skydance transaction now, keep the go-shop genuinely open, and use that window to improve economics or protections if alternative bidders materialize. The committee should focus on four negotiating points before final lock-in: maximize minority-shareholder economics, tighten governance protections, preserve fiduciary flexibility during go-shop, and obtain high-confidence financing and closing commitments. That approach captures the certainty value of the current deal while preserving the committee's ability to pivot if a clearly better offer becomes real.

## Decision Frame

Accept the Skydance-led deal now, subject to aggressive use of the go-shop and continued pressure on price, protections, and process integrity. The deciding judgment is that Paramount's downside from delay is more concrete and more immediate than the upside from waiting for a speculative superior outcome.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- Whether any alternative bidder can deliver a fully financed, higher-value, lower-risk proposal within the go-shop window.
- Whether the minority-shareholder economics are attractive relative to standalone intrinsic value under realistic rather than optimistic turnaround assumptions.
- The degree to which governance concerns can be mitigated through committee process, disclosure, and final deal protections.
- The true medium-term cash needs of the streaming business if profitability takes longer than expected.
- The execution credibility of Skydance's operating plan beyond the broad strategic narrative.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if:
- No clearly superior, fully financed alternative emerges during go-shop.
- Fairness and process protections are strong enough to defend the transaction for minority shareholders.
- Financing, balance-sheet support, and closing certainty remain firm.
- The committee can document that the standalone path carries higher expected risk-adjusted value destruction.

Fail if:
- A higher and cleaner bid with comparable or better closing certainty emerges.
- New facts show the process is materially compromised in favor of the controller.
- Skydance financing or strategic commitments weaken.
- Updated internal forecasts show standalone liquidity and turnaround prospects are materially better than currently implied.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A decision memo for the special committee that compares three executable paths on a risk-adjusted basis: signed Skydance deal, best credible alternative bid, and 24-month standalone turnaround. It should include financing certainty, regulatory risk, controller-resolution mechanics, minority-shareholder economics, and downside-case liquidity analysis.
