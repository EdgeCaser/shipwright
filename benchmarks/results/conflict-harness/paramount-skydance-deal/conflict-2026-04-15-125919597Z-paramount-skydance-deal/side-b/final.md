# Paramount-Skydance Recommendation Memo

Paramount should proceed with the Skydance-led transaction, but only as the current best executable path rather than as a proven full-value outcome. That distinction matters. On the July 2024 facts available here, the committee can defend signing because Paramount faces concrete near-term pressure: nearly $15 billion of debt, continued cable decline, a weak advertising market, and an unprofitable Paramount+ business. The alternative paths are not costless. Waiting for a better bidder or pursuing a standalone turnaround preserves upside in theory, but it also preserves exposure to operating erosion and financing strain in a structurally declining industry. The evidence packet shows real pressure; it does not show a clearly superior executable alternative today.

The strongest critique of signing is also real: the committee does not yet have a demonstrated valuation floor proving that Skydance is the best economic outcome for minority shareholders. Paramount still has valuable assets, including its library, studio, distribution footprint, and legacy-TV cash flows, and other parties such as Apollo/Sony and later Edgar Bronfman Jr. were discussed as alternatives. That means the right recommendation is not "sign because urgency alone settles value." It is "sign if, after a disciplined market check and risk-adjusted comparison, no higher-confidence superior path is actually deliverable." The case for Skydance is therefore comparative and procedural, not absolute.

On that comparative basis, Skydance has a credible edge. The transaction resolves the controller issue through the National Amusements acquisition, provides about $2.4 billion in cash for National Amusements, contemplates roughly $4.5 billion in cash or stock for shareholders, and adds another $1.5 billion to support Paramount's balance sheet. That package does not prove fair value in a strict intrinsic-value sense, but it does directly address the company's most immediate constraints: leverage, capital support, and strategic control uncertainty. By contrast, the case packet identifies interest from alternatives but does not establish a fully financed, clearly executable, regulatorily navigable topping bid.

The go-shop helps, but it should not be treated as proof that the market has been fully tested. A 45-day window may be enough to surface seriousness; it may not be enough to complete every layer of diligence, regulatory analysis, and controller-resolution work for a complex media transaction. The committee should therefore use the go-shop as one tool, not as a substitute for judgment. If credible parties cannot advance from interest to concrete, financeable proposals even with an open window, that is itself informative about the real availability of alternatives. If they can, the committee should pivot.

The recommendation is to approve the Skydance deal now as the leading executable option, while explicitly conditioning final commitment on a hard-nosed comparison against any superior proposal that emerges during the go-shop. The committee should not frame this as choosing certainty over value; it should frame it as choosing the highest-confidence value-preserving path visible on current evidence, while preserving a narrow chance to upgrade if a real alternative appears.

## Decision Frame

Approve the Skydance-led transaction as the best currently executable risk-adjusted path, but keep the recommendation conditional on a genuine market check during the 45-day go-shop. The key judgment is not that Skydance is definitively full value; it is that Paramount's downside from remaining exposed to debt, streaming losses, and industry erosion is presently more evidenced than the upside from waiting for an unproven superior alternative.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- Whether any alternative bidder can convert interest into a fully financed, higher-value, lower-risk proposal within the go-shop period.
- Whether Paramount's standalone intrinsic value under realistic operating assumptions exceeds the value and risk reduction embedded in the Skydance package.
- Whether the 45-day go-shop is sufficient to produce a true competitive outcome for a transaction with controller, financing, and regulatory complexity.
- The degree to which minority-holder protections and governance safeguards can reduce concern that the process favors the controller path.
- How much additional capital Paramount would need if streaming profitability is delayed further.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass if:
- No alternative bidder produces a clearly superior, fully financed, and executable proposal during the go-shop.
- The committee documents that standalone execution risk and balance-sheet pressure make delay a worse risk-adjusted option.
- Financing and the $1.5 billion balance-sheet support remain firm.
- Governance and process protections are strong enough to defend the outcome for minority shareholders.

Fail if:
- A higher-value bid with comparable or better closing certainty emerges.
- The committee cannot establish that Skydance is preferable on a risk-adjusted basis versus standalone or another bidder.
- Evidence shows the process is materially compromised in favor of the controller.
- Skydance's financing, economics, or strategic commitments deteriorate.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A risk-adjusted value comparison memo for the special committee covering three executable cases: Skydance signed path, best alternative bid, and 24-month standalone turnaround. It should quantify, at minimum, liquidity downside, financing certainty, controller-resolution mechanics, timing risk, and minority-shareholder economics under each path.
