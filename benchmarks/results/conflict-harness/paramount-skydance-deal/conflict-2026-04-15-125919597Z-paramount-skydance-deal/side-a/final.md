# Strategic Recommendation: Accept the Skydance Transaction
**To:** Paramount Global Special Committee
**Date:** July 2024
**Re:** Skydance merger — strategic recommendation

---

## Decision Frame

**Recommendation: Accept the Skydance deal now.**

The committee faces a deteriorating-asset negotiation in which Paramount's leverage erodes with every quarter of delay. The structural case for accepting is not that Skydance is an ideal partner — it is that Paramount's unassisted position carries compounding execution risk, and the go-shop window has not produced an alternative bid that demonstrably clears a higher bar.

The decision hinges on one core question: **Is there a credible path to higher value outside this transaction?** The available evidence weighs against that conclusion, for three compounding reasons — though the strength of that conclusion is bounded by material evidence gaps addressed below.

**1. The debt load and cash burn create severe execution risk without external capital.** Paramount carries ~$15B in debt while Paramount+ bleeds cash and linear TV cash flows are in secular decline. The Skydance structure injects $1.5B directly into the balance sheet and provides $4.5B in cash/stock to shareholders. Whether that capital is strictly necessary for survival depends on covenant headroom and maturity timing that the public record does not fully specify — but the direction of risk is clear. Delay increases the probability of forced asset sales at distressed prices or covenant-driven outcomes that remove the committee's negotiating leverage entirely.

**2. The alternative bidders did not table a bid that cleared the three-part bar.** A credible competing bid requires: (a) purchase of National Amusements to resolve the control structure, (b) sufficient capital to address the debt burden, and (c) regulatory clearability. Apollo/Sony showed interest but never tabled a binding offer addressing all three. The Sony path in particular creates broadcast license transfer complexity that could extend resolution by 18-24 months. Edgar Bronfman Jr.'s late-stage interest appeared undercapitalized relative to the balance sheet need. The critique fairly notes that the case packet does not definitively establish that alternatives *could not* have delivered on these dimensions — only that they did not do so during the go-shop. That distinction matters: the committee's fiduciary duty is to evaluate what was actually put forward, not to hold out for hypothetical bids that did not materialize. On the visible record, no alternative met the bar.

**3. Independent turnaround requires surviving to execute it.** An independent path requires either a dramatic improvement in streaming unit economics or successful deleveraging asset sales. Paramount+ (~67M subscribers) has not demonstrated a path to the 100M+ scale needed for self-sustaining profitability. Selling crown assets buys time but destroys the strategic rationale for the remaining entity. This is a structural constraint, not a certainty of failure — but it shifts the burden of proof onto those arguing for independence to show how the balance sheet survives the transition period.

**Strongest counterargument:** The deal undervalues Paramount's library and IP at a cyclical trough. AI-driven content licensing and a potential advertising recovery could materially improve valuation in 24-36 months.

**Why this does not override the recommendation:** Capturing that upside requires either (a) surviving to the recovery with the current balance sheet, or (b) a competing bidder who will pay for that optionality now. Neither condition is established in the visible record. The Skydance transaction allows shareholders to participate in recovery through the combined entity rather than betting the company on the trough-to-peak timing working out unassisted.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

The critique correctly identifies that the viability conclusion in claim-1 is bounded by evidence gaps. These are not minor — they affect how strongly the recommendation should be held:

- **Covenant headroom and maturity schedule:** If covenant triggers are 18+ months away and no near-term maturities are at risk, the urgency argument weakens. The committee should have this information; the public record does not surface it. The recommendation holds directionally but with lower confidence if the liquidity runway is longer than assumed.
- **Quality and genuineness of the go-shop:** We do not have full visibility into what terms Apollo/Sony or Bronfman actually put forward, or whether the committee conducted the go-shop with genuine openness. If a competing bid was suppressed or not seriously evaluated, that changes the calculus — and the governance critique becomes an economic critique, not just a process one.
- **Fairness opinion independence:** The committee's analysis depends on whether the financial advisor's opinion was structured to reach a predetermined outcome. This is unresolvable from public information but is the primary litigation risk.
- **Skydance's operational value-add:** The $1.5B balance sheet injection is real and quantified. The strategic synergy claims ("technology," creative leadership) are underspecified and should not be weighted heavily in the committee's analysis.
- **Alternative bidder capacity:** The case packet does not establish that Apollo/Sony or Bronfman definitively could not have structured a competing bid. It establishes they did not. The committee should document whether they were given a realistic path to acquire National Amusements, or whether the process was structured to foreclose non-Skydance outcomes.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**Decision readiness: CONDITIONAL PASS — accept, subject to governance safeguards and covenant confirmation.**

The transaction economics support acceptance on the visible record. The recommendation is *not* that independent continuation is impossible — it is that the alternative bids available during the go-shop did not constitute a superior offer, and the execution risk of continuing unassisted is material. The committee should proceed, but must:

1. Confirm covenant headroom and near-term maturity schedule internally before finalizing the acceptance recommendation. If liquidity runway is genuinely 24+ months, some evidence gaps in the urgency argument close, but the core transaction logic does not change.
2. Ensure the fairness opinion is defensible and independent.
3. Document the go-shop process rigorously to establish that alternatives were genuinely evaluated.
4. Negotiate structural protections that give public shareholders meaningful upside participation (CVRs tied to streaming milestones, anti-dilution provisions).
5. Require the $1.5B capital injection to be unconditional at signing.

**The core decision direction is clear.** No alternative bid cleared the three-part bar during a legitimate go-shop. The Skydance transaction is the only offer on the table that addresses the control structure, injects capital, and has a clear regulatory path. That combination, at this debt level and cash burn trajectory, makes acceptance the correct fiduciary outcome — with appropriate procedural documentation to survive shareholder scrutiny.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Governance defense memo documenting the go-shop process**

The transaction economics are sound on the visible record, but the committee's exposure is procedural. The most likely post-close litigation vector is process fairness, not economic outcome. The next artifact should:

- Document every bid, indication of interest, and preliminary discussion during the go-shop
- Articulate the criteria used to evaluate competing paths against the Skydance baseline, and confirm alternatives were given a genuine path to acquire National Amusements
- Explain why the controller transaction (National Amusements acquisition) was a structural prerequisite for any deal, not a Redstone preference
- Address whether Redstone's personal economics were properly walled off from the committee's evaluation of public company merger terms
- Include the covenant and maturity analysis that supports the urgency framing

This memo closes the evidence gaps identified above, protects the committee legally, and ensures the transaction record survives shareholder scrutiny.
