# Strategic Recommendation Memo

## Recommendation
Reject the Skydance-led transaction in its current form and continue the search for a better alternative during the available process window. The company is under real pressure, but that pressure does not by itself justify locking into a controller-led path that critics already question on governance and valuation fairness grounds when other interested parties exist and a go-shop period is available.

The central strategic issue is not whether Paramount needs capital and change; it does. The issue is whether this specific transaction is the best available way to deliver that outcome for all shareholders. On the evidence available, that has not been established strongly enough to justify immediate acceptance.

## Why This Position Is Correct
First, the process still contains live optionality. Other parties, including Apollo / Sony and later Edgar Bronfman Jr., had shown interest or were discussed as potential alternatives, and the deal included a 45-day go-shop period to solicit alternative bids. That means the committee is not choosing between this deal and a vacuum. It is choosing between accepting now versus using a still-open process to test whether a superior path exists.

Second, the current transaction has a structural conflict risk. The deal is built around the acquisition of National Amusements followed by a merger of Skydance and Paramount, and critics questioned whether the special committee was optimizing for all shareholders or primarily for the controller transaction path. When a transaction both resolves the controller situation and sets the terms for public shareholders, the committee should demand especially strong evidence that fairness and value are fully aligned. The packet does not establish that clearly enough.

Third, accepting now could cap upside precisely when strategic scarcity may matter. Paramount still has a major library, distribution footprint, and traditional TV assets that continue to generate cash even though those assets are structurally declining. Skydance itself argued that Paramount's assets are strategically valuable when paired with creative leadership, technology, and capital. If that strategic value is real, it is a reason to test the market more fully, not only a reason to sell quickly to the first actionable structure.

Fourth, the company is pressured but not yet shown to be out of options. Paramount carried nearly $15 billion of debt, faced weak advertising, continued cable declines, and Paramount+ had not yet reached profitability. Those facts make delay costly, but they do not prove that immediate acceptance of this specific deal is the highest-value response. A pressured seller should be careful not to convert urgency into under-optimization.

## Strongest Counterarguments
The strongest case for immediate acceptance is that the company needs capital support and strategic clarity now. The Skydance transaction contemplated about $4.5 billion in cash or stock for shareholders and an additional $1.5 billion to support Paramount's balance sheet. Rejecting the deal leaves Paramount exposed to continued streaming losses, debt pressure, and industry erosion. Those are serious risks.

That argument is real, but it still falls short of proving this deal should be accepted immediately. The company already has an active process, a go-shop window, and evidence of outside interest. The better interpretation is that Paramount should use that leverage while it still can. If no superior proposal emerges and no credible standalone plan can address the pressures fast enough, the committee can still return to the transaction or a similar solution with greater confidence that it exhausted alternatives.

## Decision Frame
The decision is whether to lock in strategic certainty now through the Skydance-led controller transaction, or preserve bargaining power long enough to test whether a better shareholder outcome exists. The recommended choice is to preserve optionality for the duration of the active process and reject immediate acceptance of the current deal terms.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
The biggest unknown is whether any alternative party can deliver a cleaner structure or better value with equivalent certainty of closing. The packet also does not establish whether the standalone turnaround path is credible enough to serve as a true fallback rather than only a negotiating posture. Another major gap is whether the current deal's governance and valuation concerns can be materially improved through renegotiation.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if the committee can use the go-shop and broader market interest to run a credible short-duration comparison of alternatives, including both other bidders and a concrete independent plan. Pass if that review can determine whether the current structure is in fact the best available outcome for all shareholders.

Fail if no alternative bidder proves credible, no standalone plan can address debt and ongoing losses, and the company determines that delay materially worsens its position without improving leverage. In that case, accepting the Skydance path may become the least-bad option.

## Recommended Next Artifact
Produce a board-ready alternatives memo comparing three paths on the same decision criteria: the current Skydance transaction, the best available third-party alternative, and a time-bound standalone turnaround plan. That memo should explicitly isolate shareholder value, governance fairness, balance-sheet impact, and execution risk so the committee can show that its recommendation is driven by the best available outcome rather than by controller-path convenience.
