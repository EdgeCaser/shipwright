# Strategic Recommendation Memo: Accept the Skydance Transaction
**To:** Paramount Global Special Committee
**Date:** July 2024
**Re:** Skydance merger — recommended acceptance

---

## Decision Frame

The committee faces a binary decision under time pressure: accept the Skydance-led transaction on current terms, or reject it and rely on the go-shop window to surface a materially superior alternative — or pursue an independent operational turnaround.

**Recommendation: Accept the Skydance transaction.**

The case rests on three pillars: (1) the company's financial position makes delay costly and risky, not optionality-preserving; (2) the go-shop period was the designated mechanism for surfacing superior bids — its outcome is the critical evidentiary question the committee must resolve before closing; and (3) the independent turnaround path is structurally implausible given the debt load and secular headwinds.

### Pillar 1 — The balance sheet forecloses the waiting strategy

Paramount carried nearly $15 billion in debt while operating in an environment of weak advertising revenue and accelerating cable subscriber decline. Paramount+ had not yet reached profitability. Traditional TV assets were generating cash but structurally declining. This is not a company with the runway to absorb a prolonged strategic process. Every quarter of delay compounds interest expense against a deteriorating EBITDA base. The Skydance deal injected approximately $1.5 billion directly to support the balance sheet — capital the company needed and could not readily source independently.

Waiting for a better offer is only rational if the company can sustain itself at current leverage while that offer materializes. The evidence does not support that assumption.

### Pillar 2 — The go-shop process was the optionality mechanism; its outcome determines the fiduciary analysis

The transaction included a 45-day go-shop period. Apollo/Sony and Edgar Bronfman Jr. were among the parties who had shown interest or were discussed as alternatives. A go-shop window is specifically designed to surface superior bids, and the committee's fiduciary obligation is to run that process rigorously — not to reject a committed offer in speculative anticipation of a better one that has not been tabled.

The critique correctly identifies the conditionality here: it would be too strong to assert that the go-shop's expiration proves "the market cleared." A short go-shop window in a controller-influenced process may fail to surface full-value alternatives even when genuine interest exists, particularly if potential bidders face structural barriers to diligence or financing. The committee cannot treat the absence of a binding bid as self-validating if process constraints suppressed participation.

What the go-shop outcome does determine is the strength of the acceptance recommendation. If the process was rigorous and no binding superior proposal emerged despite meaningful outreach, the case for acceptance is strong. If interested parties were constrained by process design rather than deal merit, the committee faces a harder fiduciary question. The recommendation to accept is therefore conditioned on the committee's ability to document that the go-shop was conducted in good faith with meaningful access.

Rejecting Skydance after an inconclusive go-shop would still leave the company in a deteriorating financial position without a committed partner — the baseline risk does not improve. The asymmetry favors acceptance unless the record shows a suppressed superior bid.

### Pillar 3 — Independent turnaround is structurally implausible

The independent path requires Paramount to simultaneously: service ~$15 billion in debt, fund continued streaming investment to reach Paramount+ profitability, absorb ongoing linear TV revenue erosion, and navigate the advertising cycle — without the capital infusion the Skydance deal provides. There is no evidence in the record that management had a credible self-funding path to streaming profitability under those constraints. The Skydance argument — combining creative leadership, technology, and fresh capital with Paramount's library and distribution footprint — addresses the specific gaps the independent path cannot close.

### Addressing the strongest counterarguments

**Governance and valuation fairness concerns are legitimate but not dispositive.** Critics questioned whether the special committee optimized for all shareholders or primarily for the controller transaction path. This concern deserves scrutiny and appropriate process rigor. The $2.4 billion acquisition of National Amusements and the approximately $4.5 billion in cash or stock for shareholders require independent fairness analysis. However, the committee's obligation is to evaluate whether accepting or rejecting the transaction serves shareholders — not whether the deal structure is aesthetically clean. A deal with governance tensions but real capital support may still be superior to rejection under financial duress, provided the process was defensible.

**The upside optionality argument fails on asymmetry.** Rejecting the deal preserves upside only if a better offer arrives before the financial position deteriorates further. The downside — continued streaming losses, debt pressure, and industry erosion without a committed partner — is both more probable and more severe than the upside of a hypothetical superior bid.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

1. **Go-shop outcome details (critical):** The record notes interested parties but does not confirm whether binding alternatives were submitted and evaluated, or whether no binding offer was tabled, or whether process constraints limited participation. This is the most consequential gap and the one on which the acceptance recommendation is most directly conditioned. The critique is correct that expiration of the go-shop window cannot by itself be read as market clearance.

2. **Valuation methodology:** The record does not specify what fairness opinion analysis underpinned the $4.5 billion shareholder consideration figure, or how it compared to independent DCF or comparable transaction analysis. Fairness opinion quality matters for fiduciary defense.

3. **Skydance integration assumptions:** The claim that Skydance brings "creative leadership, technology, and fresh capital" is asserted but not substantiated with specifics. The committee should have stress-tested the operating plan behind that claim.

4. **Debt covenant exposure:** The record notes the debt load but does not specify covenant thresholds or maturity schedule. If near-term maturities existed, urgency is higher; if maturities were long-dated, urgency is somewhat lower.

5. **Shari Redstone's alternative incentives:** National Amusements' controlling stake creates a principal-agent tension. The committee should confirm that the controller's incentives in accepting $2.4 billion for NAI did not structurally bias the go-shop process against a higher Paramount enterprise value.

---

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**Readiness assessment: Conditional Pass — accept contingent on go-shop process documentation.**

The recommendation to accept is sound if: (a) the go-shop period was conducted with meaningful outreach and access, and (b) no binding superior proposal emerged from that process. The committee must formally document both conditions before the board vote. The financial urgency is real — the debt load and operating losses are genuine constraints, not manufactured pressure. The committee does not have the luxury of treating this as a patient auction with unlimited runway.

If the go-shop record shows that interested parties faced structural barriers to participation, the committee faces a harder question and should seek additional independent advisory input before closing.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Go-shop outcome summary memo** — a factual record of which parties were contacted during the 45-day window, which executed NDAs, which submitted indications of interest, and which (if any) submitted binding proposals. Critically, this memo should also document any process constraints that may have limited participation (timing, diligence access, financing conditions) so the committee can assess whether the absence of a superior bid reflects genuine market judgment or process design. This document is the evidentiary foundation for the fiduciary defense of the acceptance decision and should be produced by the committee's financial advisors before the board vote.
