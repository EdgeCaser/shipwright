# Strategic Recommendation Memo: Accept the Skydance Transaction
**To:** Paramount Global Special Committee
**Date:** July 2024
**Re:** Skydance merger — recommended acceptance

---

## Decision Frame

The committee faces a binary decision under time pressure: accept the Skydance-led transaction on current terms, or reject it and rely on the go-shop window to surface a materially superior alternative — or pursue an independent operational turnaround.

**Recommendation: Accept the Skydance transaction.**

The case rests on three pillars: (1) the company's financial position makes delay costly and risky, not optionality-preserving; (2) the go-shop period was already exercised, and no superior binding offer materialized; and (3) the independent turnaround path is structurally implausible given the debt load and secular headwinds.

### Pillar 1 — The balance sheet forecloses the waiting strategy

Paramount carried nearly $15 billion in debt while operating in an environment of weak advertising revenue and accelerating cable subscriber decline. Paramount+ had not yet reached profitability. Traditional TV assets were generating cash but structurally declining. This is not a company with the runway to absorb a prolonged strategic process. Every quarter of delay compounds interest expense against a deteriorating EBITDA base. The Skydance deal injected approximately $1.5 billion directly to support the balance sheet — capital the company needed and could not readily source independently.

Waiting for a better offer is only rational if the company can sustain itself at current leverage while that offer materializes. The evidence does not support that assumption.

### Pillar 2 — The go-shop process was the optionality mechanism; it ran

The transaction included a 45-day go-shop period. Apollo/Sony and Edgar Bronfman Jr. were among the parties who had shown interest or were discussed as alternatives. A go-shop window is specifically designed to surface superior bids. The committee's fiduciary obligation is to run that process — not to reject a committed offer in speculative anticipation of a better one that has not been tabled.

If the go-shop period expired without a superior binding proposal, the rational inference is that the market cleared. Rejecting Skydance after an unsuccessful go-shop would leave the company in a worse negotiating position with any future party, because the strategic weakness would be more exposed and the financial position more deteriorated.

### Pillar 3 — Independent turnaround is structurally implausible

The independent path requires Paramount to simultaneously: service ~$15 billion in debt, fund continued streaming investment to reach Paramount+ profitability, absorb ongoing linear TV revenue erosion, and navigate the advertising cycle — without the capital infusion the Skydance deal provides. There is no evidence in the record that management had a credible self-funding path to streaming profitability under those constraints. The Skydance argument — combining creative leadership, technology, and fresh capital with Paramount's library and distribution footprint — addresses the specific gaps the independent path cannot close.

### Addressing the strongest counterarguments

**Governance and valuation fairness concerns are legitimate but not dispositive.** Critics questioned whether the special committee optimized for all shareholders or primarily for the controller transaction path. This concern deserves scrutiny and appropriate process rigor. However, the committee's obligation is to evaluate whether accepting or rejecting the transaction is in shareholders' best interest — not whether the deal structure is aesthetically clean. A deal with governance tensions but real capital support and strategic clarity may still be superior to rejection under financial duress.

**The upside optionality argument fails on asymmetry.** Rejecting the deal preserves upside only if a better offer arrives before the financial position deteriorates further. The downside — continued streaming losses, debt pressure, and industry erosion without a committed partner — is both more probable and more severe than the upside of a hypothetical superior bid.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

1. **Go-shop outcome details:** The record notes interested parties but does not confirm whether binding alternatives were submitted and rejected, or whether no binding offer was tabled. This is the most consequential gap — if a superior binding bid existed and was turned down, the recommendation requires reexamination.

2. **Valuation methodology:** The record does not specify what fairness opinion analysis underpinned the $4.5 billion shareholder consideration figure, or how it compared to independent DCF or comparable transaction analysis. Fairness opinion quality matters for fiduciary defense.

3. **Skydance integration assumptions:** The claim that Skydance brings "creative leadership, technology, and fresh capital" is asserted but not substantiated with specifics. The committee should have stress-tested the operating plan behind that claim.

4. **Debt covenant exposure:** The record notes the debt load but does not specify covenant thresholds or maturity schedule. If near-term maturities existed, urgency is higher; if maturities were long-dated, urgency is somewhat lower.

5. **Shari Redstone's alternative incentives:** National Amusements' controlling stake creates a principal-agent tension. The committee should confirm that the controller's incentives in accepting $2.4 billion for NAI did not structurally bias the process against a higher Paramount enterprise value.

---

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**Readiness assessment: Conditional Pass — accept with go-shop confirmation.**

The recommendation to accept is sound if the go-shop period ran without a superior binding offer emerging. The committee should formally confirm and document that outcome before closing. If the go-shop produced a superior offer that was not fully evaluated, that changes the analysis and requires committee re-examination with independent advisors.

The financial urgency is not manufactured — the debt load and operating losses are real constraints. The committee does not have the luxury of treating this as a patient auction with unlimited runway.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Go-shop outcome summary memo** — a factual record of which parties were contacted during the 45-day window, which executed NDAs, which submitted indications of interest, and which (if any) submitted binding proposals. This document is the evidentiary foundation for the fiduciary defense of the acceptance decision and should be produced by the committee's financial advisors before the board vote. If no binding superior proposal emerged, that memo closes the optionality argument decisively.
