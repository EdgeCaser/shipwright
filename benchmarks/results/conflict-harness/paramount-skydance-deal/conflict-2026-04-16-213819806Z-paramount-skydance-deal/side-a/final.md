# Strategic Recommendation Memo: Accept the Skydance Transaction

**To:** Paramount Global Special Committee  
**Date:** July 2024  
**Re:** Skydance merger — recommendation to proceed

---

## Decision Frame

The committee faces a binary choice under time pressure: accept a structured transaction that provides immediate capital relief and strategic clarity, or preserve optionality at the cost of continued exposure to structural deterioration.

**Recommendation: Accept the Skydance transaction.**

The case for acceptance rests on two primary interlocking realities and one conditional observation about the go-shop process.

**1. The balance sheet cannot sustain a waiting strategy.** Paramount carries nearly $15 billion in debt. Paramount+ has not yet reached profitability. Traditional television assets still generate cash but are structurally declining. This combination — leveraged capital structure, negative-contribution streaming unit, and eroding core business — means that time is not neutral. Every quarter spent searching for a better outcome is a quarter in which the debt burden compounds and the linear TV asset base deteriorates further. Waiting is not a free option; it has a measurable cost that the current capital structure cannot easily absorb.

**2. The Skydance terms provide concrete, near-term balance sheet repair.** The transaction contemplates approximately $1.5 billion in fresh capital to support Paramount's balance sheet, plus the broader $4.5 billion cash-or-stock structure for shareholders. This is committed capital available at close, not a speculative future benefit. In the context of a $15 billion debt load and an unprofitable streaming operation, this injection materially changes the risk profile for operating management going forward. The independent turnaround path offers no equivalent committed capital source.

**3. The go-shop period surfaces available alternatives — but its conclusions require scrutiny.** The 45-day go-shop window allowed the special committee to solicit competing bids. Interest from Apollo/Sony and subsequent engagement from other parties arose within this window. However — and this is an important qualification — the absence of a binding superior offer at go-shop close does not by itself prove that Skydance was the best available outcome. The case packet does not reveal the process design, bidder access terms, financing deadlines, or whether the two-step controller structure created friction that discouraged rival bids. A structurally deterrent process and a genuinely open market test can produce the same observable output: no competing binding offer. The committee cannot rely on the go-shop result alone as validation.

What the go-shop result does establish is narrower: no competing party was prepared to submit a binding proposal on terms superior to Skydance within the window provided. That is a necessary condition for accepting the deal — but the committee's confidence in the result should be proportional to its confidence in process integrity, not in the negative outcome alone.

**Position on the strongest counterargument:** Critics raised legitimate concerns about governance, valuation fairness, and whether the special committee's process was optimizing for all shareholders or primarily for the controller transaction path. These concerns deserve serious weight — and the go-shop qualification above strengthens rather than weakens the case for taking them seriously. The correct response is to ensure the process record is clean: genuine bidder access, no structural deterrents embedded in go-shop terms, and a fairness opinion that addresses the specific valuation concerns raised. If that record is clean, the committee's fiduciary duty is satisfied. If it is not, the committee faces legal and governance exposure that accepting the deal will not resolve.

The recommendation to accept still holds because claims 1 and 2 are independent of the go-shop process quality. Even if the go-shop had structural flaws, the capital structure argument remains: Paramount's debt load and streaming losses create a deteriorating baseline from which no alternative bidder has offered to rescue the company. Absent a credible competing proposal, the committee's choice is between a flawed-but-funded transaction and an unfunded independent path. The former is preferable.

---

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

The following material uncertainties should be acknowledged:

- **Go-shop process integrity:** The case packet does not reveal bidder access terms, financing deadlines, or whether the two-step controller structure deterred competing bids. The committee's confidence in the go-shop conclusion depends on this record.
- **Alternative bid quality:** The evidence notes Apollo/Sony interest and Bronfman engagement, but does not establish that any alternative bidder submitted a binding proposal the committee could evaluate on equivalent terms.
- **Streaming trajectory:** Paramount+ has not reached profitability, but the evidence does not specify the projected timeline or capital requirement under either path.
- **Debt covenants and near-term liquidity:** The $15 billion debt figure is stated, but the maturity schedule, covenant structure, and any near-term refinancing requirements are not revealed. The urgency calculus depends on this.
- **Skydance integration thesis:** Skydance argued it could combine creative leadership, technology, and capital with Paramount's assets. The evidence does not evaluate whether this thesis is credible or supported by prior performance.
- **Valuation fairness opinion:** The evidence notes critics questioned valuation fairness but does not include the underlying financial analysis.

---

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**Readiness assessment: Conditionally ready to proceed, subject to process record review and go-shop completion.**

The transaction passes on strategic logic and capital structure necessity. The committee is ready to recommend acceptance provided:

1. The go-shop period closes without a superior proposal that meets or exceeds the Skydance terms on a risk-adjusted basis — AND the committee can document that the process was genuinely open and did not structurally deter competing bids.
2. A credible fairness opinion is in hand addressing the valuation concerns raised by critics.
3. Governance terms in the post-close structure adequately protect minority shareholders from future controller conflicts.

If these conditions are met, the committee should proceed. If the go-shop record reveals process flaws, the committee should either renegotiate terms to improve minority protections or accept the legal exposure risk with eyes open.

**Failure modes to monitor:**
- A competing bid that offers equivalent capital support but superior governance terms should trigger renegotiation of governance provisions, not automatic rejection.
- If the $1.5 billion balance sheet support is structured with conditions that limit availability during integration, the committee should push for unconditional availability.
- If the process record shows the two-step controller structure was embedded in go-shop terms in a way that deterred bids, this is the most significant legal exposure point and should be surfaced before close.

---

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Go-shop process and outcome analysis:** A structured evaluation examining (1) process integrity — bidder access terms, financing deadlines, any structural features that may have deterred competing bids; (2) comparative bid terms — any alternative proposals received versus Skydance on total capital delivered, balance sheet support, governance structure, and strategic integration credibility; and (3) minority shareholder protection adequacy in the post-close governance structure. This artifact allows the committee to make a final recommendation with documented comparison and a defensible process record, rather than relying on the Skydance transaction as the default by elimination.
