# Strategic Recommendation Memo: Google Ad-Tech Remedies
**To:** Alphabet and Google Leadership
**From:** Strategy Advisory
**Date:** May 2025
**Subject:** Remedies Strategy — Behavioral Remedies vs. Structural Separation

---

## Recommendation

**Fight aggressively for behavioral remedies only.** Do not proactively offer structural separation of AdX or DFP/Google Ad Manager. The legal record does not compel divestiture, the business case against breakup is strong, and a well-structured behavioral remedy package can satisfy the court's mandate while preserving Google's integrated ad-tech stack.

---

## Decision Frame

### The Core Strategic Question
The framing of "fight or fold" misrepresents the actual decision space. The real question is: **what remedies would a court actually uphold, and does proactive structural concession improve or worsen Google's position across its full regulatory portfolio?**

On both dimensions, behavioral remedies are the correct answer.

### Why Behavioral Remedies Are Legally Defensible

Judge Brinkema's April 2025 findings were specific: Google illegally maintained monopoly power in **publisher ad servers** and **ad exchanges**. The findings did not conclude that the integrated business model itself is unlawful — only that specific exclusionary practices maintained illegal market power.

Remedies law requires proportionality. Courts must fashion relief that is "related to the violation" and no broader than necessary to cure the harm (see *United States v. Microsoft Corp.*, D.C. Cir. 2001). DOJ's divestiture demand faces a high bar: it must demonstrate that behavioral remedies are insufficient to restore competition. Google's legal team should contest this premise directly and vigorously.

The behavioral remedy package Google should propose includes:
1. **Real-time bid transparency:** Mandate auction-clearing price disclosure to publishers across all exchanges
2. **Header bidding parity:** Commit to no preferential treatment for Google Ads in AdX auctions
3. **API interoperability:** Open DFP yield management APIs to certified third-party exchanges on equal terms
4. **Independent compliance monitor:** Accept a court-appointed technical monitor with audit rights — a credibility signal that costs little but demonstrates good faith

This package directly addresses the findings (non-transparent auction mechanics, preferential routing) without requiring structural destruction of a profitable, integrated business.

### Why Proactive Structural Concession Is the Wrong Move

**Argument 1: It sets a catastrophic precedent across Google's regulatory portfolio.** Google faces concurrent antitrust proceedings in search, app stores, and platform access. Volunteering a breakup in ad-tech signals willingness to accept structural remedies as an instrument of regulatory resolution. This weakens Google's negotiating posture in every subsequent proceeding. Regulatory opponents will cite the ad-tech concession as evidence that Google acknowledges structural separation is proportionate — precisely the concession Google cannot afford to make.

**Argument 2: The business case against separation is strong and documentable.** Publisher yield management and exchange liquidity are tightly coupled. DFP's value to publishers derives substantially from its ability to integrate real-time demand signals from AdX alongside other demand sources. Breaking this integration does not restore competition — it destroys a functioning market mechanism that publishers depend on. Google's remedy trial strategy should lead with publisher impact testimony: a divestiture that harms the ostensible beneficiaries of antitrust enforcement is difficult for a court to justify.

**Argument 3: Structural separation creates irreversible execution risk with no guaranteed regulatory benefit.** Divesting AdX and DFP does not neutralize the broader antitrust threat — it simply removes profitable assets while leaving Google exposed to continued regulatory scrutiny in search and elsewhere. The theory that "accepting one breakup reduces regulatory overhang globally" is speculative and historically unsupported. Microsoft's consent decree in 2001 did not end its antitrust exposure; it simply shifted the terrain.

**Argument 4: Litigation timeline is favorable.** The remedies trial is scheduled for late 2025. Google's legal team has time to build a complete record on behavioral remedy sufficiency. Rushing to structural concession before the trial forfeits a legitimate legal process that may well produce a more favorable outcome.

### Acknowledging the Strongest Counterarguments

**Counterargument A: Prolonged litigation extends uncertainty and management distraction.**
True, but the alternative is worse. Voluntarily divesting $20B+ in integrated infrastructure to shorten a legal proceeding by 12-18 months is not a rational trade. Management attention is better spent building the behavioral remedy case than executing a complex divestiture under adversarial conditions.

**Counterargument B: Fighting hard emboldens regulators and worsens public perception.**
This conflates litigation strategy with regulatory posture. Google can fight hard in court while simultaneously communicating a genuine commitment to behavioral reform. The behavioral remedy package described above is substantive, not cosmetic — it would materially change auction dynamics. That is a credible narrative.

**Counterargument C: A court could impose harsher structural remedies if Google "stonewalls."**
This assumes the court views vigorous legal defense as bad faith. Courts do not. Google is entitled to contest remedies that exceed the scope of findings. A well-prepared, evidence-heavy behavioral remedy submission is not stonewalling — it is the proper exercise of the adversarial process.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

1. **Judge Brinkema's remedial philosophy:** Does she have a track record favoring behavioral or structural remedies in complex tech cases? This is a critical input to trial strategy and is not fully established in the evidence available.

2. **DOJ's internal coalition strength:** Is the structural remedy demand a genuine DOJ priority or a negotiating position? The answer affects whether a credible behavioral offer could settle before trial.

3. **Publisher sentiment data:** How many major publishers would publicly support or oppose divestiture? Publisher testimony at the remedies trial is potentially decisive. We do not have reliable survey data on publisher preferences as of this memo.

4. **Buyer market response:** Would advertisers view a divested AdX as a more or less trustworthy auction environment? If advertisers believe a standalone AdX would have less liquidity, their opposition strengthens Google's case.

5. **Appellate likelihood:** If behavioral remedies are ordered but later overturned on appeal by DOJ, what is the timeline and cost? Conversely, if structural remedies are ordered, what is the realistic appellate path and duration?

6. **Political environment:** The May 2025 DOJ posture reflects the current administration's antitrust priorities. Is there any evidence of political moderation or hardening on tech structural remedies that would affect settlement dynamics?
