# Revised artifact

## Recommendation
Proactively move toward structural separation of AdX and DFP / Google Ad Manager, while using the remedies process to shape scope, sequencing, and customer protections rather than fighting exclusively for behavioral remedies.

This recommendation does not depend on claiming that voluntary separation will clearly improve outcomes in adjacent antitrust matters. The stronger basis is narrower and better grounded in the packet: Google has already lost on liability in two ad-tech markets, the DOJ has already proposed divestiture of the core assets tied to those markets, the DOJ argues divestiture is necessary to restore competition, and a remedies trial is still ahead. In that posture, an all-in defense of the integrated stack risks leaving Google with less control over remedy design if structural relief remains live. A proactive shift gives Google a better chance to influence scope, transition planning, interoperability, and continuity for publishers and advertisers.

## Why This Position Is Correct
The central strategic fact is that structural relief is no longer hypothetical. The court found illegal maintenance of monopoly power in publisher ad servers and ad exchanges, and the DOJ responded by seeking divestiture of AdX and DFP / Google Ad Manager. Google can still argue that breakup is excessive and that behavioral remedies are more appropriate, but the company is now operating inside a remedies phase where structural separation is a serious live outcome.

That matters because the choice is not between a painless status quo and a painful breakup. The real choice is whether to keep defending full integration as the primary objective, or to start shaping the terms of a remedy that may otherwise be imposed with less input from Google. A proactive posture is strategically stronger because it preserves room to negotiate sequencing, interoperability, access to bidding information, and customer safeguards rather than treating those protections as fallback requests after losing the core fight.

The counterargument remains real. Google says forced divestiture goes beyond the court's findings, has weak legal grounding, and would harm publishers and advertisers that rely on integrated tools. The packet also states that fighting for conduct remedies could preserve valuable integration, data advantages, and customer relationships while avoiding a costly breakup of a still-profitable business stack. Those are meaningful advantages, and they are the best case for resisting structural relief.

Even so, they are not enough to justify an exclusively behavioral strategy. The packet also states that a prolonged legal fight could extend uncertainty, embolden regulators, and worsen the perception that Google will resist meaningful reform. Against a live DOJ divestiture request and a pending remedies trial, that risk is concrete enough to matter. Structural separation carries execution risk, strategic leakage, and near-term revenue pressure, but those are implementation risks that Google can at least try to shape. An all-out conduct-only posture instead concentrates risk in a remedies contest where Google may preserve principle but lose control.

## Decision Frame
Choose controlled structural separation of the core ad-tech assets as the lead strategy. Continue arguing for operational protections and transitional conduct commitments, but do not anchor the company on preserving the integrated ad-tech stack at all costs.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
The packet does not show how the remedies court will weigh structural relief against behavioral remedies.

The packet does not establish how much harm separation would cause publishers and advertisers relative to the benefits of restored competition.

The packet does not resolve whether a proactive restructuring stance would materially improve Google's position outside this case, so that should be treated as a possible secondary benefit rather than the core rationale.

The operational difficulty of separation, including transition design and near-term business impact, is not specified.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if leadership is willing to optimize for shaping a plausible structural outcome rather than defending full integration as the overriding goal.

Pass if Google can define transition safeguards for publishers and advertisers, including interoperability and continuity protections.

Fail if leadership concludes that preserving integration, data advantages, and existing customer relationships outweighs the value of reducing remedies-phase risk.

Fail if the company cannot produce a credible separation design that limits avoidable disruption.

## Recommended Next Artifact
Prepare a remedies-phase decision memo that compares two executable paths: proactive structural separation with transition safeguards, and conduct-remedies-only litigation. The memo should force explicit choices on scope, sequencing, interoperability commitments, customer continuity protections, and acceptable downside if the court still orders divestiture.
