# Strategic Recommendation: Fight for Behavioral Remedies

**To:** Alphabet and Google Leadership
**Re:** Ad-Tech Remedies Strategy — May 2025
**Classification:** Internal Strategy

---

## Decision Frame

**Recommendation: Pursue behavioral remedies aggressively. Do not proactively offer or accept structural separation of AdX or DFP / Google Ad Manager.**

The core strategic question is whether a proactive move toward divestiture reduces enough long-term risk to justify the near-term execution costs and strategic leakage. It does not. The legal case for forced divestiture is weak, the remedies trial has not yet concluded, and the business case for preserving integration is strong. Voluntarily dismantling profitable, integrated infrastructure before a court requires it is poor strategy.

**The decision logic rests on three pillars:**

1. **Legal exposure does not compel divestiture.** Judge Brinkema's April 2025 finding established monopoly maintenance in publisher ad servers and ad exchanges — it did not mandate a specific remedy. Google has correctly argued that the DOJ's proposed divestitures exceed what the findings legally support. That argument is substantive and worth pressing. Surrendering assets before the remedies trial forecloses a legitimate legal defense.

2. **Integration has real customer value.** Google's argument that publishers and advertisers depend on integrated tooling is not spin — it is an accurate description of how the ad stack functions. Behavioral remedies such as improved interoperability and bid-data transparency can address the court's competition concerns without destroying the operational architecture that customers use. A conduct remedy that opens access without forcing divestiture addresses the harm the court identified.

3. **Proactive separation creates strategic losses that cannot be recovered.** Structural separation carries execution risk, strategic leakage, and near-term revenue pressure. More importantly, once assets are divested under a consent framework, the decision is irreversible. The company loses the option value of defending its position. Given that a remedies trial is already scheduled for 2025, waiting for that outcome costs little and preserves all options.

**The counterargument that must be taken seriously:** A prolonged legal fight extends regulatory uncertainty, may embolden regulators in parallel proceedings (search monopoly, app store, platform scrutiny), and risks the perception that Google will resist any meaningful reform. That reputational cost is real. However, the answer to reputational risk is a credible, substantive behavioral remedy package — not preemptive divestiture. Offering robust conduct remedies demonstrates good faith without sacrificing the structural integrity of the business.

---

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- **Remedies trial outcome:** The trial is scheduled for later in 2025 but has not concluded. The court's receptivity to behavioral versus structural remedies is unknown. If the judge signals strong preference for structural relief, the calculus shifts.
- **DOJ negotiating posture:** Whether the DOJ would accept a well-structured behavioral remedy package in lieu of divestiture is not established. The current packet shows DOJ has proposed divestiture, not whether it has ruled out alternatives.
- **Cross-proceeding interaction effects:** The search monopoly proceedings and other antitrust actions are ongoing. How remedies in the ad-tech case affect those proceedings — or create precedent the government exploits elsewhere — is not quantified here.
- **Customer dependency depth:** Google asserts publisher and advertiser reliance on integrated tooling, but the degree to which customers could adapt to separated tools is not specified in the available evidence. This matters for how persuasive the harm-to-customers argument will be to the court.
- **Divestiture buyer landscape:** If separation were ultimately ordered, whether viable buyers exist for AdX and DFP / Google Ad Manager at values that limit balance-sheet damage is unknown.

---

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**This recommendation is ready to act on, with one conditioned gate.**

- **Pass condition:** Google enters the remedies trial with a fully specified behavioral remedy proposal — including concrete interoperability commitments and bid-transparency mechanisms — rather than a general objection to divestiture. A credible alternative must be on the table.
- **Fail condition:** Google litigates purely as a blocking strategy without a substantive conduct remedy offer. That posture will lose both in court and in public perception, and will strengthen the government's argument that only structural remedies can produce compliance.

The recommendation to fight for behavioral remedies is only defensible if the behavioral remedy package is genuinely responsive to the monopoly maintenance harms the court identified. Half-measures invite the structural outcome this strategy seeks to avoid.

---

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Behavioral Remedy Term Sheet** — a detailed specification of the interoperability and bid-transparency commitments Google is prepared to offer, structured as a court-submittable proposal. This document should define measurable obligations, enforcement mechanisms, and a monitoring framework. It is the instrument that converts this strategic recommendation into a legally viable position at the remedies trial.
