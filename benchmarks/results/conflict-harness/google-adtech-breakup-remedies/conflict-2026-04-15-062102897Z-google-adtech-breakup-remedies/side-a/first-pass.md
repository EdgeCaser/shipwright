# Strategic Recommendation: Fight for Behavioral Remedies, Resist Structural Separation

**To:** Alphabet / Google Leadership
**Re:** Ad-Tech Antitrust Remedies Strategy — May 2025
**Classification:** Privileged and Confidential

---

## Decision Frame

**Recommendation:** Google should contest DOJ's divestiture proposal vigorously at the remedies trial and advocate for a robust behavioral remedy package — not proactively offer structural separation of AdX or DFP/Google Ad Manager.

**The core logic:** Judge Brinkema found illegal maintenance of monopoly power in two specific markets. She did not find that the integrated stack itself is an antitrust violation, nor that integration caused the harm. Divestiture is an extraordinary remedy — courts require a close nexus between the violation and the remedy. That nexus is weak here, which gives Google strong appellate ground. Proactive separation concedes that nexus before the legal argument is lost, forfeits a profitable and strategically load-bearing business, and sets a precedent that emboldens regulators in the search and Play Store proceedings simultaneously.

**What this is NOT:** This is not a recommendation to stonewall or minimize reform. Google must offer a credible, operationally enforceable behavioral remedy package — one that demonstrably changes market dynamics — or the remedies trial judge will conclude that conduct remedies are toothless and greenlight divestiture anyway.

**Decision criteria used:**
1. Legal defensibility of DOJ's divestiture theory
2. Strategic value of integrated ad-tech stack (data, margin, publisher relationships)
3. Contagion risk to parallel antitrust proceedings
4. Execution risk of separation vs. prolonged litigation
5. Near-term revenue and investor signal

---

## Position Argument

### 1. The Legal Bar for Divestiture Is High and the DOJ Has Not Cleared It

Under *United States v. Microsoft* (D.C. Cir. 2001) and *United States v. du Pont* precedent, structural remedies must be the least restrictive means sufficient to cure the violation. The court found monopoly maintenance — anticompetitive conduct in how Google leveraged its position — not that vertical integration between publisher ad servers, ad exchanges, and buy-side tools is per se illegal.

Google's conduct violations are specific: preferential auction design (Last Look, Project Jedi Blue arrangements), opacity in bid data, and self-dealing between AdX and DFP. Each of these is remediable through targeted behavioral orders: real-time bid parity disclosures, auction neutrality requirements, API interoperability mandates, and independent monitoring. A structural remedy that breaks up a vertically integrated system addresses more than was found to be illegal — that overreach is reversible on appeal.

**Implication for strategy:** Enter the remedies trial with a prepared, detailed behavioral remedy package that maps each specific violation to a specific, verifiable conduct obligation. Do not give the court a vacuous commitment to "greater transparency." Give it operationally specified, monitored, and enforceable obligations. This shifts the burden: if the DOJ still insists on divestiture after a credible behavioral package is on the table, the DOJ must explain why conduct remedies are inadequate — a harder argument.

### 2. The Integrated Stack Has Compounding Strategic Value That Separation Destroys Irreversibly

AdX and DFP/Google Ad Manager are not isolated profit centers — they are the data feedback loop that makes Google's buy-side (DV360, Google Ads) more effective, which in turn makes Search advertising more valuable to buyers. The integration produces:

- Cross-market signal quality unavailable to standalone DSPs or SSPs
- Publisher relationships that anchor Google's measurement infrastructure
- A loss-leader pricing capability that cross-subsidizes margin across the stack

Separation does not merely remove a profitable segment. It degrades the entire advertising data flywheel that supports Search revenue — Google's core. Even a "clean" divestiture at a favorable valuation leaves a structurally weakened buyer-side platform competing against a now-independent AdX/DFP with degraded data inputs. The risk is not just near-term revenue — it is permanent impairment of competitive position in programmatic, which is still a growth market globally.

### 3. Proactive Separation Accelerates Contagion to Search and Platform Cases

Google is simultaneously fighting major antitrust proceedings in Search (Judge Mehta's remedies phase) and facing ongoing scrutiny of the Play Store. Voluntarily offering structural separation in ad-tech sends a signal that Google accepts the DOJ's theory that its market positions are illicitly maintained and that breakups are a legitimate remedy class. This is admissible precedent in other proceedings, and it hands regulators a playbook.

The argument that separation "reduces regulatory overhang" is only true if separation satisfies regulators permanently — it does not. The FTC, state AGs, and international regulators will interpret a proactive offer as confirmation that structural remedies work and will immediately apply the same logic to Search. The overhang does not reduce; it spreads.

### 4. Litigation Uncertainty Is Manageable; Separation Risk Is Permanent

The standard argument for proactive separation is that prolonged litigation extends uncertainty. This is true but overstated. The remedies trial timeline is defined. Appeals are structured. The actual period of uncertainty is bounded — likely 2–4 years before final resolution. During that period, AdX and DFP continue operating normally; there is no injunction on operations pending remedy determination.

Contrast this with separation: divestiture is a multi-year operational exercise with execution risk (customer defection, talent loss, system decoupling), permanent strategic leakage (a now-independent AdX is a better-capitalized competitor), and an irreversible outcome. Litigation risk has a resolution path. Structural separation does not.

## Counterargument Engagement

**Strongest counterargument: A judge who already found illegal monopoly maintenance will not be moved by behavioral promises; she will see conduct remedies as Google asking to self-police the same conduct she just condemned.**

This is the most serious objection. The answer is not to discount it but to address it operationally. The behavioral package must include:
- An independent technical monitor with real access to auction logs, not just high-level reports
- Mandatory API interoperability with header bidding partners at parity with AdX's own pipe
- A whistleblower-accessible complaint mechanism with defined DOJ escalation rights
- Automatic disgorgement triggers if auction neutrality violations are detected

This is not self-policing. This is a court-supervised structural intervention in how auctions run — it just stops short of ownership separation. The distinction matters legally and operationally.

**Second counterargument: Proactive separation signals strength and earns goodwill with regulators, courts, and the public.**

The empirical record does not support this. AT&T's proactive structural concessions in prior merger proceedings did not reduce long-term regulatory pressure. Microsoft's eventual settlement in its antitrust case came after litigation revealed the limits of DOJ's structural case, not before. Goodwill from voluntary breakups is short-lived; regulatory appetite is not.

**Artifact:** Behavioral Remedy Term Sheet

**Contents should include:**
1. Specific auction neutrality obligations (defined technically, not as principles)
2. Interoperability API specifications and access tiers

4. Data access provisions for third-party DSPs/SSPs with defined latency and format requirements
5. Disgorgement and penalty mechanism for detected violations
6. Sunset review provision (e.g., 5 years with DOJ right of renewal)

**Owner:** Legal + Engineering leads, with Business Strategy review for commercial impact modeling
**Deadline:** 6 weeks before remedies trial date

This document, if credible, is the primary litigation asset at trial and the strongest argument against divestiture.
