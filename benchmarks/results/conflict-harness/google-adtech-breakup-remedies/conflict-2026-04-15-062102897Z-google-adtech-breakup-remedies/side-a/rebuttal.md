- Target Side: side_b
- Target Claim IDs: side_b-claim-1
- Attack Type: evidence_gap
- Severity: high

## Claim Under Attack
Google should oppose immediate structural separation and instead pursue a conduct-first remedy strategy with strong, enforceable concessions and contingency planning.

## Evidence Or Reason
Side B's central claim rests on the assumption that a court will find sufficiently concrete behavioral remedies credible enough to forgo divestiture — but it cites no precedent or legal authority supporting this outcome. The case packet establishes that Judge Brinkema found illegal maintenance of monopoly power in two distinct markets. In monopoly-maintenance cases (as opposed to mere anti-competitive conduct), courts have historically viewed structural remedies as the primary restorative tool precisely because behavioral remedies leave the monopolist in control of the mechanism of foreclosure. Side B acknowledges this risk in its own counterarguments section ('courts may see conduct remedies as inadequate after monopoly findings') but then treats the solution — making conduct remedies 'concrete enough' — as self-evidently achievable. No evidence is offered for what level of specificity would satisfy this particular court, no comparable case is cited where conduct remedies succeeded post-monopoly-finding in a two-sided platform market, and no probability estimate is provided for the court accepting conduct-only remedies. The claim's logical chain therefore contains a critical unsupported assumption: that judicial acceptance of behavioral remedies is achievable through remedy design quality alone, independent of the legal posture the court has already signaled by accepting the DOJ's framing. Without this evidentiary support, the entire conduct-first strategy rests on conjecture about judicial receptivity rather than evidence.
