# Critique: finding-1

- Target Side: side_b
- Target Claim IDs: side_b-claim-1
- Attack Type: evidence_gap
- Severity: high

## Claim Under Attack
Google should pivot from an all-in conduct-remedies fight toward a negotiated structural separation of key ad-tech assets.

## Evidence Or Reason
Side B's pivot recommendation rests on the untested assumption that Google would retain meaningful negotiating leverage to shape the scope, sequencing, and governance of any structural remedy. But the case record cuts against this. The DOJ has already publicly committed to divestiture of both AdX and DFP / Google Ad Manager — not a partial or staged separation — and courts that have found illegal maintenance of monopoly power in two distinct markets have little structural incentive to accept a defendant-engineered package as a substitute for what the plaintiff explicitly requested. Side B cites no historical precedent where a defendant who proactively offered structural separation in a monopoly-maintenance case successfully narrowed the remedy scope relative to the government's opening position; in practice, early concession on the structural question often shifts the debate to how much more the government can extract, not how much less. The claim also assumes that 'negotiated' and 'proactive' are synonymous with 'controlled,' but if the court views Google's pivot as a litigation tactic rather than genuine reform, that framing advantage evaporates. The evidence gap is material: Side B does not establish that Google has any credible mechanism to bind the DOJ or the court to remedy terms favorable enough to make proactive separation preferable to litigating proportionality — which remains a live legal argument with genuine force given that the liability finding was confined to specific markets, not the full ad-tech stack.
