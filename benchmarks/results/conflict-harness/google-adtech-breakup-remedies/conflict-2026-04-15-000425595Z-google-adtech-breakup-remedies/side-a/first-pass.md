# Strategic Recommendation: Fight for Behavioral Remedies, Reject Structural Separation

**To:** Alphabet / Google Leadership
**From:** Strategy Advisory
**Date:** May 2025
**Re:** Ad-Tech Antitrust Remedies — Recommended Posture

---

## Decision Frame

**Recommendation: Aggressively defend behavioral remedies only. Do not preemptively offer or accept structural divestiture of AdX or DFP/Google Ad Manager.**

The core strategic question is not whether Google can survive a breakup — it probably can. The question is whether proactive structural separation materially reduces total regulatory and competitive risk, or whether it surrenders valuable assets and precedent for a discount on uncertainty that never fully materializes.

The answer is the latter. Here is why:

### 1. The Legal Ceiling Has Not Been Reached

Judge Brinkema's April 2025 finding established monopoly power in publisher ad servers and ad exchanges — not in the full ad-tech stack, not in search advertising, and not as a consequence of a vertically integrated design per se. The court found illegal *maintenance* of monopoly, not that integration itself is unlawful. DOJ's proposed divestitures exceed the scope of the finding. Courts must calibrate remedies to violations; forcing divestiture of AdX and DFP would require the court to accept the DOJ's theory that only structural separation can terminate the monopoly. That is a contested empirical claim the DOJ must prove at the remedies trial — and Google has a credible counter-narrative.

Giving up AdX and DFP before that trial concedes the DOJ's framing. It forfeits the legal argument without requiring the government to win it.

### 2. Behavioral Remedies Are Legally Sufficient and Practically Meaningful

The specific violations — preferential treatment of Google's own buy-side tools in AdX auctions, opacity in unified pricing rules, Project Jedi-style information advantages — are addressable through conduct remedies:

- **Real-time bid transparency:** Require AdX to expose clearing prices and auction mechanics to all SSP participants on equal terms.
- **Interoperability mandates:** Require DFP to give non-Google SSPs and DSPs the same integration depth as Google's own tools.
- **Firewall enforcement:** Prohibit Google's buy-side teams from accessing publisher floor data not available to rivals.
- **Independent monitoring:** Appoint a technical compliance monitor with audit rights over auction mechanics.

These remedies directly address the specific anticompetitive conducts identified. They do not require a court to substitute its judgment about the right market structure for that of market participants.

### 3. Divestiture Creates Durable Strategic Damage That Conduct Remedies Do Not

AdX and DFP are not peripheral assets. They are the connective tissue between Google's buy-side (DV360, Google Ads) and the open web's publisher monetization layer. Separating them:

- **Severs first-party data linkages** that improve campaign performance across Google's full stack — a meaningful quality advantage that is currently lawful.
- **Creates a well-capitalized competitor** with deep knowledge of Google's auction mechanics, pricing models, and publisher relationships, purpose-built to exploit that knowledge.
- **Triggers a talent and product exodus** as the divested entity immediately competes for the same engineering and sales teams.
- **Sets a structural precedent** that will be cited in parallel proceedings (search, app stores, AI distribution) to argue that behavioral remedies are categorically insufficient for Google.

The last point is underweighted in most analyses. A consent-to-divest in ad-tech does not quarantine the regulatory problem — it creates a template argument for DOJ and state AGs in every other proceeding.

### 4. The "Regulatory Goodwill" Thesis Is Historically Weak

The argument for proactive separation holds that demonstrating good faith will reduce regulatory pressure elsewhere. The empirical track record does not support this. Microsoft's browser and middleware concessions did not prevent years of additional compliance proceedings. Meta's data-portability concessions did not forestall FTC action. In antitrust enforcement, demonstrated willingness to divest is read not as goodwill but as an admission that the asset was the problem — inviting further scrutiny of adjacent assets.

Regulatory overhang reduction requires either winning in court or negotiating a consent decree with precise, limited scope. Neither requires preemptive divestiture.

### 5. Management Bandwidth Is a Real But Manageable Cost

The strongest argument for settlement is that prolonged litigation consumes executive attention during a critical AI competition window. This concern is legitimate but overstated. The remedies trial is a finite proceeding. Google has deep enough legal and policy infrastructure to manage parallel proceedings without material distraction to the AI product roadmap. By contrast, executing a complex divestiture of two interconnected enterprise platforms — separating engineering teams, contractual relationships, data systems, and go-to-market — is a multi-year operational project that consumes far more internal bandwidth than continued litigation.

**The fight is cheaper than the breakup, not more expensive.**

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**This recommendation is ready to act on with the following conditions:**

**Pass (proceed with behavioral-only posture) if:**
- Legal team assesses >40% probability of defeating structural remedy at trial
- Economic experts can produce credible empirical models showing behavioral remedies restore competition
- Publisher coalition outreach confirms meaningful third-party support for integrated tooling
- No parallel proceeding (search, app stores) reaches a structural-remedy ruling before the ad-tech remedies trial concludes

**Fail / revisit if:**
- Remedies trial preliminary signals suggest court is leaning toward structural separation regardless of Google's arguments
- A major publisher group publicly endorses divestiture, undermining the "publisher harm" narrative
- Search-monopoly proceedings produce a structural ruling that creates binding precedent before ad-tech trial concludes
- DOJ presents economic modeling that Google's own economists cannot rebut on market restoration under conduct remedies

**Red line:** Do not offer voluntary divestiture as a negotiating chip. Once offered, it cannot be retracted. Any settlement discussion should be limited to conduct remedy scope and monitoring mechanisms.

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Behavioral Remedies Term Sheet** — a detailed specification of the interoperability, transparency, and firewall conduct remedies Google is prepared to accept, designed to:
1. Directly address each specific violation finding
2. Include enforceable, measurable compliance metrics
3. Propose an independent technical monitor with defined audit scope
4. Be presentable to the court as a credible alternative to structural separation

This artifact serves dual purpose: it is the offer Google makes at the remedies trial, and it is the public-facing proof that Google is not stonewalling reform — only insisting that reform be proportionate to the violation.

Secondary artifact: **Publisher Coalition Brief** — coordinate amicus filings from major publishers (NYT, Condé Nast, News Corp) attesting to integration dependency, to counter DOJ's claim that the market is better served by separation.
