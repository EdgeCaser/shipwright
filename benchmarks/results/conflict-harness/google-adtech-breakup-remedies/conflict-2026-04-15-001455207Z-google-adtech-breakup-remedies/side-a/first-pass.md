# Strategic Recommendation: Fight for Behavioral Remedies — Structural Separation Is the Wrong Concession

**To:** Alphabet / Google Executive Leadership
**From:** Strategy Advisory
**Date:** May 2025
**Re:** Ad-Tech Remedies Response — Recommended Position

---

## Decision Frame

**The strategic question:** Should Google fight aggressively for behavioral remedies only, or proactively offer structural separation of AdX and DFP / Google Ad Manager to reduce regulatory overhang?

**Recommended position:** Fight for behavioral remedies only. Do not offer or accept structural separation.

**Rationale in one sentence:** Proactive divestiture would surrender assets worth more integrated than separated, set a capitulation precedent across all Google antitrust fronts, and deliver no guaranteed regulatory peace in return.

### Why This Is the Right Frame

This decision must be evaluated across three dimensions simultaneously:

1. **Legal probability** — What is the realistic likelihood that a court orders structural separation versus accepts conduct remedies?
2. **Strategic cost asymmetry** — What do we lose by fighting versus conceding, and are those losses symmetric?
3. **Precedent contagion** — How does this decision affect Google's posture in the search monopoly case, app store proceedings, and future regulatory engagements?

Each dimension favors the behavioral-remedy position.

---

## The Case for Behavioral Remedies

### Claim 1: Structural separation exceeds the legal remedy standard and courts know it

Judge Brinkema found illegal maintenance of monopoly power in publisher ad servers and ad exchanges — she did not find that the *integration itself* was the unlawful act. Structural remedies are legally appropriate when the conduct and the structure are inseparable, or when conduct remedies have already been tried and failed. Neither condition is met here:

- Google has not been subject to a prior failed consent decree in ad-tech
- The court's findings center on auction manipulation and self-preferencing behaviors, not on the existence of vertical integration per se
- The legal standard from *United States v. Microsoft* (2001) holds that structural relief requires a "sufficient causal nexus" between the structural remedy and the violation — that nexus is weaker here than DOJ's framing suggests

The DOJ's divestiture proposal is an opening bid in a negotiation, not a settled legal outcome. Courts have historically been reluctant to impose breakups absent clear failure of conduct alternatives. Google's legal team has real ground to stand on.

**Strength of this claim:** High. Legal precedent for software/platform structural remedies post-breakup is thin. The DOJ faces a higher evidentiary bar than its press framing implies.

### Claim 2: AdX and DFP integration creates genuine efficiency value — separation destroys it, not just for Google but for publishers

The integrated ad-serving and exchange stack generates real value through:

- Unified auction clearing that reduces latency and improves fill rates for publishers
- Cross-signal optimization that only works with integrated data pipelines
- Single-vendor support simplicity that small and mid-size publishers especially depend on

If AdX and DFP are separated — especially into independent ownership — the technical and operational integration breaks. Transition costs fall on publishers, not just Google. This is a genuine counterargument Google can make to the court with publisher testimony. A separated AdX would need to rebuild publisher relationships under a new operator, creating a transition trough that hurts the very parties the remedy is designed to protect.

**Strength of this claim:** Medium-High. Publisher dependency is real but has limits — large publishers (News Corp, Condé Nast) already run competitive stacks. Smaller publishers are more exposed.

### Claim 3: Proactive divestiture emboldens regulators across all fronts without buying regulatory peace

This is the most important strategic consideration and the most underweighted one in internal discussions that frame this as "just the ad-tech case."

Google is simultaneously facing:
- The DOJ search monopoly remedies proceeding (potentially far larger strategic stakes)
- App store and Play Store antitrust scrutiny
- EU Digital Markets Act compliance obligations
- Congressional pressure on AI market concentration

A voluntary structural concession in ad-tech does not purchase goodwill on these other fronts. Regulators and plaintiffs interpret concession as admission of the underlying theory — that Google's vertical integration is itself harmful and should be unwound. If Google accepts structural separation in ad-tech, the DOJ will cite that capitulation in search remedy proceedings as evidence that Google acknowledges the breakup model is appropriate.

The precedent contagion risk is severe. The search business — not ad-tech — is the strategic core of Alphabet's revenue and the foundation of its AI pivot. Any structural weakening of the precedent wall around search could cost orders of magnitude more than the ad-tech assets themselves.

**Strength of this claim:** High. This is the single most decision-relevant factor and it argues strongly against any proactive structural offer.

### Claim 4: Behavioral remedies can be designed to satisfy the court's concerns without destroying integration value

A credible behavioral remedy package can include:

- **Auction transparency requirements:** Real-time disclosure of auction parameters, bid floors, and clearing logic to third-party exchanges and DSPs
- **Mandatory interoperability:** Open access APIs for publisher ad servers to connect to competing exchanges on equal terms
- **Firewall commitments:** Structural information barriers preventing AdX from receiving non-public signals from DFP
- **Independent compliance monitor:** Court-appointed technical monitor with access to audit auction mechanics quarterly
- **MFN prohibition:** Commitment not to require publishers to use DFP as a condition of accessing AdX, and vice versa

This package addresses the specific behaviors the court found unlawful — self-preferencing, information asymmetry, and exclusionary tying — without dismantling the integration.

**Strength of this claim:** Medium. The DOJ will argue behavioral remedies are unenforceable and insufficient. Google must pre-answer this with specificity and propose a strong compliance mechanism. Vague behavioral commitments lose in court.

## Acknowledging the Strongest Counterarguments

### Counterargument 1: "A prolonged fight extends uncertainty and management distraction during a critical AI window"

This is real. Legal proceedings through 2025-2026 will consume executive bandwidth, legal resources, and reputational capital at exactly the moment Google needs to move fast on AI infrastructure and product. There is a legitimate opportunity cost.

**Why our position is still correct:** The alternative — proactive divestiture — does not actually end the uncertainty. Any divestiture negotiation involves years of execution, regulatory approval of the buyer, operational separation, and ongoing compliance monitoring. The uncertainty timeline is not materially shorter. And we would have surrendered assets to obtain that timeline. Fighting on the merits while proposing a serious behavioral remedy package is the cleaner path.

### Counterargument 2: "AdX and DFP are mature, lower-growth assets — cutting them loose frees capital and focus for AI"

Some internal analysis suggests the ad-tech stack is worth more as optionality than as actual revenue given the secular headwinds from privacy regulation and cookie deprecation.

**Why our position is still correct:** Even if that analysis is correct, the *value of AdX and DFP* is secondary to the *precedent value of not conceding structural breakup theory*. We should not make the divestiture decision based on asset valuation while the legal fight is active — doing so signals that Google agrees the integrated structure was anticompetitive. Divest on our own strategic terms after the case is resolved, not as a concession under pressure.

### Counterargument 3: "Good-faith restructuring could generate press goodwill and soften Congressional pressure"

**Why our position is still correct:** Regulatory goodwill from a structural concession has a half-life measured in weeks. The DOJ does not reward voluntary compliance with reduced scrutiny on other fronts — the incentives run the other way. Congressional members who want to run on "breaking up Big Tech" will use the concession as evidence that more breakups are warranted, not less. The goodwill theory has repeatedly failed empirical test in platform antitrust (see Meta's WhatsApp/Instagram acquisitions, Amazon's seller data settlements).

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

| Unknown | Why It Matters | How to Resolve |
|---|---|---|n| Judge Brinkema's appetite for structural vs. conduct remedies | Determines legal risk at remedies trial | Review her prior rulings and appointment history; get outside counsel read on her remedy preferences |
| DOJ's actual bottom line vs. opening bid | Divestiture demand may be negotiating posture | Informal channel reads through ex-DOJ contacts |
| Publisher testimony distribution | If major publishers testify *for* divestiture, behavioral remedy defense weakens | Conduct private publisher pulse survey before trial prep |
| Parallel EU/DMA enforcement timing | EU remedy could set a de facto structural standard before U.S. trial resolves | Track DMA compliance timeline closely; coordinate remedy framing across jurisdictions |
| Buyer market for AdX/DFP if divestiture ordered | Forced sale to a weak buyer could be worse than operating it | Do confidential M&A read on viable acquirers even if we're not planning to sell |

**Critical gap:** We do not yet have a strong quantified model of the revenue impact of the specific behavioral remedies we'd propose. We need that before the remedies trial to show the court our proposal has teeth, not just optics.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**Ready to proceed on:**
- Filing legal briefs arguing behavioral remedies are the appropriate remedy
- Developing a detailed, specific behavioral remedy proposal for court submission
- Mobilizing publisher-side testimony on integration value

**Not ready to proceed on:**
- Any public statement about potential divestiture willingness — this sets a floor that negotiators will anchor to
- Any internal restructuring moves that could be characterized as divestiture preparation

**Blocking dependencies:**
- Outside counsel needs to deliver a remedies-trial probability analysis by June 2025
- Publisher relations team needs a 30-day brief on who will testify and what they'll say
- Policy team needs a cross-jurisdiction risk map connecting ad-tech remedy precedents to search case exposure

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Behavioral Remedy Package Design Document**

The next deliverable should be a detailed specification of the behavioral remedy package Google will propose at the remedies trial, including:

1. Specific interoperability and transparency requirements with implementation timelines
2. Proposed compliance monitoring mechanism (independent technical auditor, audit frequency, escalation path)
3. Quantified analysis of how each proposed remedy addresses each specific finding in Judge Brinkema's liability ruling
4. Publisher and advertiser impact modeling (showing behavioral remedies preserve value that structural separation destroys)

This document should be drafted at trial-brief quality, not internal memo quality — it will inform the public legal record.

**Audience:** Outside antitrust counsel + policy leadership
**Format:** Legal strategy brief with technical appendix
**Deadline:** 45 days before remedies trial opening

---

## Bottom Line

Fight for behavioral remedies. The legal standard does not require structural separation. The strategic precedent of capitulating does. Design the behavioral remedy package with enough specificity and accountability that the court has a credible alternative to divestiture. Do not signal divestiture willingness under any channel — that signal cannot be unsent.
