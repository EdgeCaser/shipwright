# North Star Metric Recommendation: Resolving the Active User Conflict

## Decision Frame

Leadership has requested a single active user number. That request cannot be honored as stated until the organization resolves a prior question: **what user behavior, if it increases, most reliably predicts the outcomes the business is accountable for?**

Three definitions currently exist across the finance, product, and growth teams. Each encodes a different theory of value:

- The **finance team's definition** is most likely anchored to billing, contract status, or revenue recognition thresholds — counting users the business has already captured economically.
- The **product team's definition** is most likely anchored to feature engagement or session depth — counting users who are extracting meaningful value from the product.
- The **growth team's definition** is most likely anchored to acquisition funnel entry or retention cohort membership — counting users the business is working to develop.

A 40% variance in outcomes is not a measurement error. It means these teams are making fundamentally different claims about what "using the product" means. Selecting one definition without organizational alignment will not resolve the conflict — it will suppress it until the next planning cycle, forecast review, or board presentation.

**Three structural questions must be answered by leadership before a single number can be committed:**

1. Is the north star metric intended to drive internal prioritization, external reporting, or both? These use cases have different tolerance for definitional complexity.
2. Which team's accountability model does the metric need to govern — or does committing to a shared north star require a new cross-functional accountability structure?
3. Is leadership prepared to accept that the chosen definition will show one of three states — growing, flat, or declining — and act on that signal regardless of which teams' narratives it validates?

Without answers to these questions, any single number issued here is a placeholder that each team will continue to interpret through its own definition.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

| Gap | Why It Matters |
|-----|----------------|
| Exact operational definitions for each team's "active user" | Cannot evaluate overlap or reconcile without knowing the precise event, session window, or action threshold each team counts |
| Correlation between each definition and revenue or retention outcomes | The defensible definition is the one with the strongest predictive validity — this cannot be determined without historical analysis |
| Whether the 40% variance is stable or shifts by segment, time period, or product area | Variance that fluctuates seasonally or by cohort changes the risk profile of any single committed definition |
| Leadership's actual use case for the metric | A board-level narrative number has different requirements than an operational metric used for sprint prioritization or resource allocation |
| Whether any definition is already committed externally | Investor materials, partner contracts, or regulatory filings may have locked in a specific active user definition, which constrains the solution space regardless of internal preference |

No external benchmarks, industry data, or prior analysis is present in the case materials. All claims above are structural inferences from the scenario framing, not empirical findings.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**This recommendation is NOT ready to commit a single number.**

| Readiness Condition | Status |
|---------------------|--------|
| Agreed operational definition tied to a specific triggering behavior and time window | Not met |
| Historical backtest showing which definition correlates with the business outcomes leadership is accountable for | Not met |
| Leadership acknowledgment that one definition will show decline and commitment to act on it | Not confirmed |
| Cross-functional sign-off that the chosen metric governs resource allocation and OKR scoring | Not met |
| Audit of external commitments that may constrain the definition | Not completed |

Issuing a premature single number without these conditions met produces a metric each team will immediately reinterpret through its own definition. The 40% conflict resurfaces at the next planning cycle with the added problem that leadership has now implicitly endorsed a number no one fully trusts.

**What leadership can have now:** a structured decision process that produces a committed, defensible definition within one working session — not a number that defers the conflict under a veneer of resolution.

---

## Recommended Next Artifact

**A metric arbitration brief** — a one-page document prepared before a 60-minute cross-functional working session, containing:

1. Each team's definition stated in precise operational terms: the triggering event, the counting window, the minimum threshold
2. The outcome each definition produces for the same historical time period — the three concrete numbers behind the 40% gap, shown side by side
3. The decision framed as a question of business theory: which user behavior predicts the outcomes leadership is actually accountable for?
4. Two or three candidate north star options with explicit tradeoffs — not a single recommendation — so the working session produces a decision rather than ratifying a staff recommendation

The working session requires one representative from each team with definitional authority and one executive with the power to commit. The output of that session — not this document — is the north star metric.

**Parallel action:** Audit external commitments immediately. If any existing materials have locked in a specific active user definition, that definition is a floor constraint and the internal debate narrows to fit within it. This audit takes hours and changes the decision space significantly if it surfaces a conflict.
