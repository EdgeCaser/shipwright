# Recommendation Memo: Preserve Tight Integration for Now

## Recommendation
Intel should preserve tighter integration rather than push further toward structural separation and outside funding in the near term. The board can keep the foundry subsidiary structure as a management and reporting tool, but it should not treat deeper separation as the primary answer to the turnaround while execution is still fragile.

The core reason is sequencing. Intel is already managing a difficult recovery: foundry losses were substantial in 2023, foundry spending had been running at roughly $25 billion per year, the company was pursuing layoffs and a roughly $10 billion cost-reduction plan, and parts of the European expansion were already being paused or delayed. Adding more governance distance and financing complexity now risks making execution harder at the moment Intel most needs operational coherence.

The strongest argument for deeper separation is real: a more independent foundry structure could unlock outside capital, produce cleaner economics, and preserve optionality for a later spin or carve-out. That matters because investor confidence had been badly damaged, and the foundry strategy is both capital intensive and central to the revival narrative. But optionality is not the same as urgency. Intel has already created a separate entity with its own operating board and the ability to evaluate outside funding. That step captures much of the benefit of increased strategic flexibility without forcing the company into a more disruptive structural move before the turnaround has proven itself.

A tighter integrated model also protects an important strategic logic: Intel's differentiated story is that design and manufacturing can reinforce each other. Further separation would raise questions about whether the company still believes in that model. That is especially risky when Intel is already under pressure in key markets, trailing in AI acceleration, and depending on the foundry strategy to anchor both commercial credibility and geopolitical relevance. The expanded relationship with Amazon Web Services and the support tied to CHIPS Act and Secure Enclave funding both strengthen the case for keeping the foundry effort credible, but they do not require Intel to move faster into a more detached ownership structure now.

The board should therefore hold the line on operational independence where it improves transparency and discipline, while resisting a faster march toward outside capital or a broader carve-out until execution improves and the turnaround is on firmer footing.

## Decision Frame
The decision is not whether the foundry business should have sharper accountability; Intel has already moved in that direction. The real choice is whether to accelerate toward a more separated capital structure now, or keep the foundry business strategically and operationally close while the company stabilizes. The better choice is to keep integration tighter for now and revisit external capital only after Intel shows more credible progress on losses, execution, and turnaround stability.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
The packet does not establish what specific outside investors would fund, on what terms, or with what governance rights. It also does not show whether customers would view a more separated foundry as materially more credible than the current subsidiary structure. Another unresolved issue is whether tighter integration is still producing real product-and-manufacturing advantages in practice, rather than only in theory. Those unknowns matter because they determine whether deeper separation would create net strategic value or mainly add distraction.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if the board wants to reduce complexity during the turnaround, preserve the integrated strategic model, and use the new subsidiary structure primarily for transparency, accountability, and future optionality. Pass if management can keep key U.S. projects moving while controlling spending and maintaining customer momentum.

Fail if Intel has near-term access to clearly attractive outside capital that materially improves resilience without undermining execution or strategic control. Fail if major customers or government stakeholders signal that the current level of separation is insufficient to support confidence in the foundry business.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A board decision memo comparing three explicit operating states over the next 12 to 18 months: maintain the current subsidiary structure without external capital, raise minority outside capital with constrained governance rights, or prepare for a broader carve-out. That memo should specify decision triggers, governance implications, execution risks, and what evidence would justify moving from one state to the next.
