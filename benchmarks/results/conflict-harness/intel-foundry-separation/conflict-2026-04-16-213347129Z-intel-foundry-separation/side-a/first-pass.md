# Strategic Recommendation Memo: Proceed with Foundry Structural Separation

**To:** Intel Board of Directors  
**From:** Strategy Advisor  
**Date:** September 2024  
**Re:** Foundry Separation Decision

---

## Decision Frame

**Recommended position: Proceed with structural separation and pursue outside capital for the foundry business.**

The board is not choosing between a risky path and a safe one. It is choosing between two forms of risk. Tighter integration preserves simplicity but concentrates all capital risk on Intel's balance sheet, compounds investor skepticism, and forecloses strategic options at precisely the moment when flexibility has the highest option value. Structural separation, by contrast, unlocks external capital, forces cleaner economic accountability on the foundry unit, and preserves the long-term possibility of a spin or carve-out if the turnaround stalls.

The core logic: a foundry business losing roughly $7 billion in operating income in a single year, with no break-even expected before 2030, cannot be nursed back to health solely through internal cross-subsidy from a design business that is itself under pressure. The foundry needs its own capital structure, its own investors who price foundry risk correctly, and its own governance discipline. Keeping it fused to the product business does not make the losses go away — it just obscures them and chains Intel's equity story to a multi-year drag that the market has already repriced severely.

**What separation buys:**

1. **Capital access without further balance sheet impairment.** Intel has been spending roughly $25 billion per year on the foundry business. That pace is unsustainable from a single corporate balance sheet while simultaneously executing a $10 billion cost-reduction plan and defending market share in core product segments. A subsidiary structure with its own operating board creates a credible vehicle for outside investment — sovereign wealth funds, CHIPS Act co-investors, or strategic partners who want a U.S.-based advanced manufacturing option but will not invest directly into a conglomerate balance sheet.

2. **Geopolitical leverage becomes financeable.** The U.S. government has provided major support under the CHIPS Act and Secure Enclave funding specifically because the foundry strategy is geopolitically important. That political support is an asset — but it is most valuable when it can be paired with outside capital rather than used to subsidize internal cross-subsidy indefinitely. A standalone foundry entity is a cleaner recipient of public-private co-investment.

3. **The AWS relationship signals external demand exists.** The expanded custom-chip relationship with Amazon Web Services is not just a revenue event — it is a proof point that the foundry business has external customers willing to commit. That changes the investment thesis for outside capital from speculative to partially de-risked. A structurally separate foundry can present that relationship as anchor demand to prospective investors in a way that a division buried inside a larger P&L cannot.

4. **Optionality has asymmetric value here.** If the turnaround succeeds, a separate foundry subsidiary can be reintegrated or kept independent — both are good outcomes. If the turnaround fails or stalls, a separate entity can be sold, spun, or recapitalized without triggering a full corporate restructuring. Tight integration eliminates that second path entirely. Given the uncertainty of a multi-year turnaround, preserving optionality is worth governance complexity.

**Strongest counterargument and why it does not change the recommendation:**

The most serious objection is that Intel's differentiated model — the claim that design-manufacturing integration produces better products — requires tight coupling between the two businesses, and that separation structurally weakens that coupling. This is a real concern. If separation degrades the design-manufacturing feedback loop, Intel loses its theoretical advantage over pure-play foundries without gaining the cost structure of a pure-play.

However, this argument proves too much. Intel has been pursuing IDM 2.0 — integrated design and manufacturing — while simultaneously losing share and watching its stock fall nearly 60% in 2024. The integration thesis has not protected Intel's competitive position. More practically, structural separation via a subsidiary model does not require operational separation: Intel's product teams can remain the foundry's preferred or captive customer, transfer pricing can be managed, and shared technology roadmaps can persist. What changes is the capital structure and governance — not necessarily the engineering collaboration.

The risk of separation damaging execution focus is real but manageable. The risk of not separating — remaining capital-constrained on a $25 billion per year investment program with a 2030 break-even horizon — is structural and not manageable through better execution alone.

---

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

The recommendation above is made with meaningful uncertainty across several dimensions the board should surface before committing to execution details:

- **Outside capital appetite is unconfirmed.** The case for separation assumes that credible outside investors exist at acceptable dilution rates. This has not been tested. If sovereign wealth funds and strategic partners will not invest at terms that preserve Intel's control and roadmap continuity, the capital access rationale weakens substantially. The board needs a parallel track: a private market sounding process to validate investor interest before announcing separation as a financing mechanism.

- **Transfer pricing and captive economics are unresolved.** If Intel's product division is both the foundry's largest customer and its controlling shareholder, transfer pricing disputes will be a persistent governance friction. The arm's-length economics that make separation credible to outside investors may conflict with the preferential economics that make the foundry attractive to Intel's own design teams.

- **The 2030 break-even timeline is a projection, not a commitment.** Foundry losses were expected to peak in 2024, but the trajectory beyond that depends on yield improvement, customer ramp, and competitive dynamics that are not fully in Intel's control. Outside investors will stress-test this timeline aggressively. The board needs a range of scenarios, not a single path.

- **The Poland and Germany pause signals resource prioritization uncertainty.** Intel has already paused or delayed fabrication expansion outside the U.S. This suggests the capital program is being managed reactively rather than strategically. A separated foundry entity will face immediate pressure from investors and customers to articulate a coherent global capacity strategy — which Intel does not yet appear to have locked down.

- **CHIPS Act conditionality.** Government support under the CHIPS Act may carry conditions about ownership structure, foreign investor participation, or technology transfer that constrain how freely Intel can bring in outside capital. The board needs legal and policy clarity on what outside investment structures are permissible before committing to a separation thesis that depends on that capital.

---

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**Decision readiness: Conditional pass — proceed to term sheet and investor sounding, not to full structural commitment.**

The strategic logic for separation is sound and the recommendation is defensible. However, the execution depends on two conditions that are not yet confirmed:

1. **Investor appetite test:** Conduct a confidential market sounding with 5-8 credible outside investors (sovereign wealth funds, strategic industrials, infrastructure funds) within 90 days to validate that capital is available at acceptable terms. If no credible investor emerges at acceptable dilution and governance terms, the capital access rationale for separation disappears and the board should revisit integration.

2. **CHIPS Act legal review:** Obtain written guidance from counsel and government liaisons on permissible ownership structures for Secure Enclave and CHIPS Act recipients within 60 days. If outside investment is structurally constrained by government conditionality, the financing model needs redesign before separation proceeds.

If both conditions are met, the board should authorize full separation planning. If either condition fails, the board should pause and reassess rather than proceed on the current thesis.
