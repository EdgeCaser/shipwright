# Revised artifact

## Recommendation
Intel should preserve tight operating integration while using the newly created foundry subsidiary as a selective capital-access and transparency vehicle, not as a trigger for accelerated structural separation now.

The strongest evidence in the packet supports a sequencing argument rather than a claim that separation itself would necessarily worsen performance. Intel has already created a separate foundry entity with its own operating board and the ability to evaluate outside funding, which gives the board real optionality without forcing a broader break in the operating model. That matters because the case for going further now is still incomplete: the packet shows the potential upside of outside capital, cleaner economics, and spin or carve-out optionality, but it also explicitly warns that further separation could add governance complexity and weaken the integrated design-and-manufacturing model. On this record, the board has enough evidence to preserve flexibility, but not enough to treat deeper separation as the clearly superior next step.

Intel's financial and execution strain still matters, but as a reason to avoid overcommitting to an unproven structural move, not as proof that separation would itself be destabilizing. The company is carrying heavy foundry investment, large foundry losses, layoffs, a cost-reduction plan, and fabrication-footprint changes while trying to restore credibility. That context makes cleaner capital access attractive, but it does not establish that deeper separation is the decisive remedy. Since the current subsidiary structure already allows Intel to explore outside funding, the board should use that structure to test capital options and improve reporting discipline before taking on additional structural complexity.

## Counterarguments
The strongest counterargument is that Intel's losses, spending burden, and stock decline mean the company should move faster toward structural separation so the foundry business can access outside capital sooner. That argument is plausible on this record. The problem is that the packet does not show what capital is actually available, on what terms, or whether a deeper separation would improve execution enough to justify the added governance burden. The evidence supports optionality; it does not yet prove that acceleration is the better choice.

A second counterargument is that Intel is already making major changes, so another structural move should not be treated as uniquely disruptive. That critique is fair. The safer conclusion is not that separation is clearly too disruptive, but that the evidence does not justify assuming either path is operationally easy. In that setting, preserving the announced subsidiary structure while delaying a stronger separation commitment is the more defensible board posture.

## Decision Frame
Choose controlled optionality over accelerated separation. Keep the foundry subsidiary and preserve the ability to raise outside capital, but do not commit to deeper structural separation until the board has clearer evidence on capital availability, governance effects, and the cost of weakening integration.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- What outside-capital options are realistically available under the new subsidiary structure, and on what terms?
- How much governance complexity would deeper separation add beyond the current subsidiary model?
- How important is Intel's integrated design-and-manufacturing model to the turnaround over the next several years?
- How durable are the customer and geopolitical benefits supporting the foundry strategy?

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if the board's goal is to preserve financing flexibility and strategic optionality without committing to a larger structural move that the current evidence does not yet validate.

Fail if the board has additional evidence, not present here, showing that near-term outside capital requires deeper separation or that tighter integration is materially blocking foundry progress.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A board decision memo comparing the current subsidiary-with-integration path against a deeper separation path, focused on three unresolved questions: outside-capital feasibility, governance consequences, and the strategic value of maintaining tighter design-manufacturing integration.
