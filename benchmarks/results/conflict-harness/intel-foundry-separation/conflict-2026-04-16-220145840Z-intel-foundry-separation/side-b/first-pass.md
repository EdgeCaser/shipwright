# Recommendation Memo: Preserve Tight Integration While Using the New Subsidiary Structure Selectively

## Recommendation
Intel should not push aggressively toward deeper structural separation or near-term outside funding for the foundry business. It should keep the new subsidiary structure as a financing and reporting tool, but preserve tight operating integration while the turnaround remains fragile.

This is the better course because Intel is already managing a costly turnaround, heavy foundry losses, layoffs, cost reductions, and changes to its fabrication footprint at the same time. Adding another major structural transition now would increase execution risk precisely when the company needs management attention on operational recovery, customer delivery, and restoring credibility.

## Why This Position Is Correct

### 1. Intel's immediate problem is execution under strain, not just capital structure
The foundry business has required roughly $25 billion per year in spending in each of the prior two years, while Intel disclosed that the business lost about $7 billion in operating income in 2023 on roughly $18.9 billion in sales. Intel had also said those losses would likely peak in 2024 and not break even until sometime before 2030. At the same time, the company was pursuing layoffs and a roughly $10 billion cost-reduction plan, and its stock had lost nearly 60% of its value during 2024 before the announcement. That combination points to an enterprise already under severe execution and credibility pressure. The board should be careful not to layer governance disruption onto an already stressed operating system. 

### 2. Further separation risks undermining one of Intel's core strategic distinctions
A tighter split could create cleaner economics and more optionality, but it also raises a central strategic question: whether Intel can still integrate design and manufacturing as a differentiated model. That is not a side issue. If Intel weakens that link during a turnaround, it may sacrifice one of the few strategic narratives that can still justify enduring foundry losses and large capital commitments.

### 3. The current structure already captures much of the upside without forcing the downside
Intel has already said it will create a separate entity for the foundry business with its own operating board and the ability to evaluate outside funding. That is enough to improve transparency, test investor appetite, and create optionality for future capital actions. The board does not need to force a faster or broader separation now. It can preserve the option value of outside capital while keeping day-to-day execution aligned.

### 4. Geopolitical and customer credibility are better served by stability than by added complexity
The foundry strategy is not only a financial project. It is geopolitically important because Intel received major U.S. government support under the CHIPS Act and additional Secure Enclave funding. Intel also announced an expanded custom-chip relationship with Amazon Web Services, which supports the foundry narrative. Those are meaningful signals, but they strengthen the case for stable execution rather than more organizational disruption. Intel needs to show that it can build, deliver, and govern this strategy reliably.

## Strongest Counterarguments, and Why They Do Not Change the Recommendation

### Counterargument: Outside capital is necessary because the foundry business is too expensive for Intel to fund alone
This is the strongest argument for deeper separation. The spending burden is very large, the losses are material, and cleaner economics could help attract capital.

Response: the board should preserve this option, but not rush into it as a strategic centerpiece before the turnaround is more stable. A structure that allows Intel to evaluate outside funding already exists. The right sequence is to improve execution, prove operating discipline, and use the subsidiary framework to explore capital sources from a stronger position.

### Counterargument: A more independent foundry business could improve transparency and investor confidence
Cleaner reporting and strategic optionality could help address skepticism, especially after the stock decline.

Response: transparency is valuable, but transparency alone does not solve the core problem if operational performance remains weak. If deeper separation creates confusion over accountability or slows decision-making, investor confidence may worsen rather than improve.

### Counterargument: Foundry is central to the revival plan and should be structured to maximize its standalone potential now
If foundry is the future, the company should optimize for that future early.

Response: the board should optimize for survival of the turnaround first. A future spin or carve-out is more credible after Intel shows the model can execute with discipline. Optionality is valuable; premature commitment is not.

## Decision Frame
Choose controlled optionality over accelerated separation. Keep the subsidiary structure, preserve the ability to raise outside capital, but hold operating integration tight until the turnaround shows clearer evidence of execution stability.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps
- The board needs a clearer view of how much outside capital is realistically available under the new structure and on what terms.
- It remains unclear how much governance complexity a deeper separation would add in practice versus the current subsidiary model.
- The packet does not establish how dependent Intel's product-side recovery is on close design-manufacturing integration over the next several years.
- The durability and scale of customer demand implied by the AWS relationship are not specified.

## Pass/Fail Readiness
Pass if the board wants to preserve financing flexibility while minimizing added execution risk during the turnaround.

Fail if the board has evidence, not included here, that Intel cannot sustain the foundry investment path without near-term outside capital or that tighter integration is already impeding customer adoption and capital formation.

## Recommended Next Artifact
A board decision memo comparing three paths over the next 12 to 24 months: maintain the current subsidiary-with-integration model, pursue minority outside capital under the new structure, or prepare a fuller carve-out. That memo should focus on governance implications, capital access, execution burden, and impact on Intel's integrated design-manufacturing advantage.
