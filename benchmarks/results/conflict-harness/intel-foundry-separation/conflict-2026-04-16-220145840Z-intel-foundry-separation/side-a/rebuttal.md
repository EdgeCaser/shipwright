# Critique: finding-1

- Target Side: side_b
- Target Claim IDs: side_b-claim-1
- Attack Type: evidence_gap
- Severity: high

## Claim Under Attack
Intel should avoid pushing toward deeper structural separation now because the turnaround is already under significant financial and operational strain.

## Evidence Or Reason
Side B treats financial strain as a reason to delay separation, but the causal arrow may run the other way: the $7B operating loss on $18.9B foundry revenue and the 60% stock decline are precisely the conditions that make continued full integration untenable. Side B never establishes a mechanism by which preserving tight integration actually reduces that strain — it asserts execution focus benefits without evidence that separation is the variable causing distraction. In fact, the evidence in the packet (paused European expansion, $10B cost-reduction plan, layoffs) shows Intel was already executing major structural changes simultaneously. The claim that separation adds decisive complexity relies on an unexamined assumption that the current integrated model is the stable baseline — when the baseline is already a multi-front crisis, adding a cleaner capital structure is as plausible a stabilizer as it is a destabilizer. Side B acknowledges this in its own Unknowns section ('it remains unclear how much governance complexity a deeper separation would add in practice') but does not treat that uncertainty symmetrically — it discounts separation risk while treating integration continuity as the safe default without evidence.
