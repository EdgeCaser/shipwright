# Strategic Recommendation: Proceed with Foundry Separation

**To:** Intel Board of Directors  
**From:** Strategy Advisor  
**Date:** September 2024  
**Subject:** Foundry Structural Separation — Recommended Path Forward

---

## Decision Frame

The board faces a binary with long tails. The integrated model is not a stable holding position: the foundry business lost approximately $7 billion in operating income in 2023 on roughly $18.9 billion in sales, has consumed roughly $25 billion per year in capital for two consecutive years, and is not projected to break even until sometime before 2030. Intel's stock has lost nearly 60% of its value in 2024. The turnaround is not fragile because separation is being attempted — it is fragile because the current integrated model has already destroyed shareholder value at scale.

The correct frame is not "integration vs. separation." It is: **which structure best sustains the foundry through its loss years while preserving Intel's long-term optionality?**

**Recommended position:** Proceed with structural separation into a standalone subsidiary with its own operating board, pursue outside capital evaluation, and use the subsidiary structure to draw a hard line between foundry economics and the products business. Do not reverse course.

---

## Core Claims

### 1. The integrated balance sheet can no longer absorb foundry losses without existential risk to the products business

At roughly $25 billion per year in foundry-side capital expenditure and a $7 billion operating loss in a single year, the foundry is consuming resources that the products business — which faces its own competitive pressure from Nvidia in AI acceleration and AMD in CPUs — cannot continue to subsidize indefinitely. The $10 billion cost-reduction plan and layoffs signal that Intel has already reached the limits of what the integrated P&L can absorb. Keeping the foundry fully consolidated means future cost crises fall on a single balance sheet, with no mechanism to ring-fence the damage.

A subsidiary structure with its own operating board creates a distinct capital structure, enabling outside investors or strategic partners to fund foundry losses without directly diluting Intel's core business equity holders.

### 2. Outside capital is only accessible through structural credibility — and structural credibility requires real separation

No sophisticated outside capital provider will commit to a foundry investment that lacks governance independence, clean economics, and a credible exit path. The subsidiary structure is a prerequisite, not a downstream option. The AWS expanded custom-chip relationship demonstrates that external partners are willing to engage with Intel's foundry narrative — but scaling that into equity or structured capital requires a legal and governance entity that can offer those terms. Half-measures (a subsidiary in name only, with fully consolidated P&L) will not unlock this capital.

### 3. CHIPS Act and Secure Enclave funding create a geopolitical floor that makes abandonment costlier than continuation

The U.S. government has made significant commitments to Intel's foundry strategy under the CHIPS Act and through Secure Enclave designation. These are not passive grants — they carry implicit obligations around domestic manufacturing capacity and national security supply chain. Walking back the foundry strategy, or visibly failing to execute on it, risks those relationships and may trigger clawback provisions or reputational damage with the government as a long-term customer and partner. Separation, by contrast, can be framed as strengthening the foundry's independence and durability — a positive signal to government stakeholders.

### 4. The geographic rationalization already underway is consistent with separation, not integration

Intel paused fabrication expansion in Poland and Germany while keeping key U.S. projects moving. This is the behavior of a team trying to right-size capital commitments to a loss-making business — precisely the kind of discipline that a subsidiary with its own operating board would institutionalize. Reintegrating these decisions under a unified corporate structure would remove the accountability mechanism that is already producing better capital allocation.

---

## Strongest Counterargument and Why It Does Not Hold

**Counterargument:** Further separation adds governance complexity, weakens execution focus during a fragile turnaround, and undermines Intel's integrated device manufacturer (IDM) differentiation — the core thesis that design and manufacturing together produce better outcomes than fabless + foundry separately.

**Response:** The IDM thesis has merit in the abstract, but Intel's recent execution record does not support it as a reason to delay separation. The integrated model did not prevent the $7 billion foundry operating loss, did not prevent the 60% stock decline, and did not produce the AI acceleration leadership that Nvidia has captured. IDM differentiation is a product-era argument; it applies when a company's own chip designs are the primary foundry customer and when those designs are winning. Intel is in a different situation: it needs external customers to justify foundry scale, and external customers need governance separation to trust Intel as a neutral foundry partner. Maintaining tight integration is actively hostile to the external customer acquisition the foundry requires to reach break-even.

Governance complexity is real but manageable. Intel is not spinning the foundry out — it is creating a subsidiary with an operating board. That is a well-understood structure. The complexity cost is lower than the cost of continued balance sheet consolidation of a multi-billion-dollar annual loss.

---

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- **Outside capital appetite:** The packet establishes that outside capital is theoretically available to evaluate, but does not confirm that any capital provider has expressed binding interest. The recommendation holds regardless, because the subsidiary structure is necessary to test that market — but the board should not assume capital will materialize on favorable terms.
- **Break-even timeline confidence:** The projection that losses will peak in 2024 and break-even will occur "sometime before 2030" is a wide band. The board should probe what assumptions drive that range and what would cause it to extend further right.
- **Customer concentration risk:** The AWS relationship is cited as a positive signal, but one expanded relationship does not validate the external foundry thesis at scale. The board needs visibility into the pipeline beyond AWS before treating external demand as confirmed.
- **Government commitment conditionality:** The packet references CHIPS Act support and Secure Enclave funding but does not specify whether those commitments are contingent on maintaining a particular ownership or governance structure. Legal review of those conditions is a prerequisite to any capital-raise.

---

## Pass/Fail Readiness

| Criterion | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Does the integrated model have a viable path without foundry separation? | No — the balance sheet cannot sustain current loss rates without structural relief |
| Is the subsidiary structure sufficient to unlock outside capital? | Necessary but not sufficient — governance is a prerequisite, not a guarantee |
| Does CHIPS Act exposure support or complicate separation? | Supports — geopolitical importance creates a floor, not a ceiling |
| Is the IDM counterargument disqualifying? | No — IDM differentiation requires winning designs; Intel's current competitive position limits its force |
| Is the governance complexity objection disqualifying? | No — subsidiary structure is well-understood and the complexity is bounded |

**Overall readiness:** Proceed. The risks of separation are real but bounded and manageable. The risks of non-separation are existential and compounding.

This artifact converts the strategic recommendation into an actionable governance design the board can pressure-test with advisors and potential capital partners.
