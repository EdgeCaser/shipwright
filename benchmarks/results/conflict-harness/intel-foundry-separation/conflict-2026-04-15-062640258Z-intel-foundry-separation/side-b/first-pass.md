# Strategic Recommendation Memo

Intel should continue toward structural separation of the foundry business, but stop short of a near-term full spin. The right move in September 2024 is a ring-fenced subsidiary model with its own board, transparent economics, and authority to raise outside capital, while preserving operational coordination with Intel Products through a tightly governed transition model.

The core reason is capital efficiency. Intel's turnaround cannot succeed if the board treats foundry losses as a normal extension of the integrated model. Intel disclosed that foundry lost about $7 billion in operating income in 2023 on roughly $18.9 billion of sales, had been spending around $25 billion per year on foundry in the prior two years, and did not expect breakeven before sometime before 2030. At the same time, the company was cutting costs, laying off staff, and had seen its stock fall nearly 60% during 2024 before the separation announcement. That combination means the status quo integrated structure is no longer just strategically elegant; it is financially destabilizing. A more independent foundry structure gives the board three things the current model does not: cleaner accountability for foundry economics, a credible path to third-party capital, and strategic optionality if a later carve-out becomes necessary.

This position is stronger because Intel's foundry effort is not merely a discretionary adjacency. It has strategic and geopolitical weight. Intel received major U.S. government support under the CHIPS Act and additional Secure Enclave funding, and it kept core U.S. fab projects moving even while pausing or delaying some projects in Poland and Germany. Intel also expanded its custom-chip relationship with AWS, which supports the external-customer foundry thesis. These facts argue against retreat. They support separation as a financing and governance mechanism to preserve the foundry strategy, not abandon it.

The strongest counterargument is execution risk. Intel is already in a fragile turnaround, trailing badly in AI acceleration and under pressure in core markets. Further separation can add governance overhead, create internal friction, and weaken one of Intel's few potentially differentiated claims: coupling design and manufacturing. That risk is real, but it is still the wrong conclusion. Integration without financial discipline is what created the present credibility problem. The answer is not to preserve a blurred structure; it is to separate capital and reporting while preserving technology, capacity, and roadmap coordination through explicit intercompany agreements, shared-node governance, and board-level milestone gates.

The board should therefore authorize the subsidiary structure as the default path for 2025-2027, pursue outside capital only if it lowers Intel's effective funding burden without compromising U.S. strategic control, and defer any full spin decision until foundry demonstrates both customer traction and improving unit economics. In other words: separate enough to fund and govern the business properly, but not so far that Intel loses coordination during the turnaround.

## Decision Frame

Decision: Continue structural separation of Intel Foundry into a standalone subsidiary with its own board and external funding option, while preserving controlled operational integration with Intel's product businesses.

Why this is the right call:
- Intel's foundry losses, capex burden, and delayed breakeven profile make full balance-sheet integration too expensive to defend.
- A ring-fenced structure improves economic transparency and investor credibility.
- Outside capital and strategic partners matter more now because Intel's equity value and financial flexibility were severely impaired in 2024.
- CHIPS-linked national-security relevance and AWS customer momentum make foundry too important to abandon, but too costly to fund indefinitely under the old model.

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

- Whether outside investors would fund the foundry subsidiary on acceptable terms in late 2024 or 2025.
- How much governance friction the new structure would create in node planning, capacity allocation, and product-launch coordination.
- The true pace of third-party foundry customer adoption beyond announced relationships.
- Whether foundry losses actually peak in 2024 as management indicated.
- The degree to which government support is contingent on specific ownership, control, or investment structures.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

Pass conditions:
- Intel can establish clear transfer-pricing, capacity-allocation, and technology-sharing rules between Products and Foundry.
- External capital can be raised on terms that reduce funding pressure without undermining strategic control.
- Management commits to milestone-based oversight on foundry margin improvement, customer wins, and fab-utilization progress.
- U.S. government stakeholders remain supportive of the structure.

Fail conditions:
- Separation materially disrupts product execution or weakens Intel's design-manufacturing coordination.
- Outside capital is only available at punitive valuations or with restrictive terms.
- The subsidiary structure becomes mainly cosmetic, without true cost transparency or capital relief.
- Foundry demand signals deteriorate enough that the business no longer supports a credible path to scale.

## Recommended Next Artifact

A board decision memo for 2025 capital allocation that compares three paths on a common scorecard: integrated status quo, ring-fenced subsidiary with minority outside capital, and staged carve-out. It should include funding scenarios, governance design, customer-traction thresholds, and national-security constraints.
