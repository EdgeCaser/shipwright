# Strategic Recommendation: Proceed with Foundry Separation, With Conditional Pacing

**To:** Intel Board of Directors  
**From:** Strategy Advisor  
**Date:** September 2024  
**Subject:** Foundry Structural Separation — Recommendation Memo

---

## Decision Frame

**Recommendation:** Proceed with the standalone foundry subsidiary structure and pursue outside capital — but gate capital-raise timing on operational milestones, not market windows.

The core question is not whether separation is directionally correct. It is. The question is whether Intel can afford the governance and execution overhead of separation *right now*, while the turnaround is still fragile. The answer is that Intel cannot afford *not* to separate, because the alternative — a fully integrated model burning $25B/year in foundry capex against a balance sheet already punished by a 60% stock decline — leaves the board with no levers if the turnaround stalls.

**The integration-preservation argument is a false safety.** Staying integrated does not reduce foundry losses; it only hides them inside a consolidated P&L. The $7B operating loss in 2023 on $18.9B in revenue is not a rounding error — it is a structural drag that will continue to punish Intel's cost of capital and management bandwidth regardless of how the org chart is drawn. Separation forces discipline that integration defers.

**The strategic logic for separation rests on four pillars:**

1. **Capital access without diluting the parent.** Foundry investment cycles are decade-long. Intel's existing balance sheet cannot sustain $25B/year in foundry spending while simultaneously funding design competitiveness in AI and PC. A standalone subsidiary can raise project finance, sovereign wealth participation, or strategic partnership capital that the parent cannot access on comparable terms. The AWS relationship announced alongside the separation is exactly this playbook — a customer becomes a structural anchor.

2. **Geopolitical capital is real and time-limited.** CHIPS Act funding and Secure Enclave designation give Intel a positioning window that competitors cannot replicate. This window is tied to Intel remaining a credible U.S.-based foundry. A foundry that looks financially unviable will erode Washington's confidence faster than a foundry that is structurally separated but capitalized. Separation signals commitment and creates a fundable vehicle for government co-investment.

3. **Cleaner economics force operational accountability.** As long as foundry losses are consolidated, there is no clean mechanism for the board to hold the foundry business to a return on invested capital standard separate from the design business. A standalone operating board with its own P&L creates that accountability surface. The 2030 break-even target becomes a commitment, not an aspiration.

4. **Optionality is asymmetric.** A separated foundry can be recombined, partially sold, JV'd, or taken public depending on how the next three years unfold. An integrated foundry that has not been separated cannot easily be separated under pressure. The option to separate is worth more now than after a crisis forces it.

**Strongest counterargument:** Integration is Intel's only remaining differentiated narrative. TSMC and Samsung do not design chips; AMD and Qualcomm do not fab them. Intel's IDM 2.0 thesis is that tight design-manufacturing feedback loops produce better silicon economics over time. Separation structurally weakens that loop and may signal to customers and engineers that Intel has conceded the IDM thesis. If the separation is read as a prelude to a full spin, it could accelerate talent attrition in both divisions and create customer uncertainty about roadmap continuity.

**Why the counterargument does not change the recommendation:** The IDM integration advantage is real but it is not being realized at current execution velocity. Process node delays (Intel 4, Intel 3) have already damaged the narrative. The question is not whether integration is theoretically valuable — it is whether Intel can fund and execute the foundry investment required to make integration valuable without outside capital. The answer is no. A separated foundry that retains design services agreements and shared roadmap governance preserves the meaningful parts of IDM while unlocking the capital required to actually execute it.

---

## Unknowns & Evidence Gaps

1. **Outside investor appetite at realistic valuations.** The memo assumes outside capital is accessible on terms that do not further dilute Intel's strategic control. This is unverified. Sovereign wealth funds (ADIA, Mubadala, GIC) are the most likely institutional anchors, but their return requirements and governance expectations are not public. If available capital requires giving up foundry customer data access or process IP licensing, the calculus changes.

2. **AWS relationship depth and exclusivity.** The expanded AWS custom-chip announcement is strategically important but underspecified. Is this a committed volume agreement or a design engagement? Volume commitments are balance-sheet-relevant; design engagements are not. The foundry business needs anchor customer revenue, not anchor customer logos.

3. **Germany and Poland pause — strategic signal or cost management?** The European fab pause is ambiguous. If it reflects a deliberate prioritization of U.S. geopolitical positioning, it is defensible. If it reflects a liquidity constraint that is being papered over with strategic language, it is a leading indicator of deeper financial stress that would argue for faster, not slower, outside capital access.

4. **Pat Gelsinger's continuity.** The separation is architecturally tied to Gelsinger's IDM 2.0 thesis. If leadership changes before the foundry subsidiary reaches operational independence, there is execution risk that a new CEO reverses course mid-separation — the worst of both worlds. The board needs a separation governance structure that is not CEO-dependent.

5. **Foundry customer pipeline beyond Intel products.** Intel's foundry revenue of $18.9B is predominantly internal. External customer revenue remains thin. The separation thesis requires external customers to view Intel Foundry as a credible alternative to TSMC. Current evidence does not support that this customer pipeline exists at scale.

## Pass/Fail Readiness

**The separation passes the strategic readiness test on three of five criteria:**

| Criterion | Status | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Capital need is genuine and cannot be met internally | **Pass** | $25B/year capex against deteriorating balance sheet is unsustainable |
| Geopolitical anchoring creates durable outside-capital rationale | **Pass** | CHIPS Act + Secure Enclave provide non-market capital access |
| Operational governance structure exists to run standalone | **Conditional** | Operating board announced but management team depth unconfirmed |
| Anchor customer revenue justifies foundry standalone economics | **Fail** | External revenue too thin; AWS deal unquantified |
| Leadership continuity through separation transition | **Fail** | CEO-dependent architecture is a governance risk |

**Decision: Proceed with separation structure, but treat capital raise as milestone-gated, not calendar-gated.** Announce the subsidiary. Establish the operating board. Execute the AWS relationship as a reference anchor. Gate the external capital raise on: (a) 2+ additional named external customers with committed revenue, and (b) confirmation of foundry leadership team that is not dependent on Gelsinger's tenure.

---

## Recommended Next Artifact

**Foundry Subsidiary Operating Charter** — a one-page term sheet that defines: (1) what Intel parent retains vs. delegates to the foundry operating board, (2) the process IP and customer data governance fence between the two entities, (3) the financial milestones that trigger the external capital raise, and (4) the recombination/exit conditions under which the board would reverse course. This document is the governance spine that makes the separation credible to outside investors, customers, and employees without over-committing to a structure that cannot be unwound if circumstances change.
